Frank Bruni

July 25, 2024

Frank Bruni

Mr. Bruni is a contributing Opinion writer who was on the staff of The Times for more than 25 years.

It’s always a good idea to pay attention to Nancy Pelosi and to judge where things are going by which direction she’s nudging them in, but that was especially true over the past few weeks. When she started sending President Biden signals that he should reconsider his candidacy, I felt almost certain that he’d eventually do so, because she was both giving other Democrats permission to break ranks with him and letting him and his allies know the extent and inevitability of the rebellion.

And I realized that Vice President Kamala Harris wouldn’t face any competition for the Democratic nomination when Pelosi endorsed her. That was the sign.

In other words, Pelosi, a woman who so ably foiled Donald Trump when he was president and she was House speaker, helped make it possible — and then confirmed — that another woman, Harris, would lead the charge to foil him from becoming president again.

It’s a veritable Hollywood movie. Women are scripting what will happen to Trump, who has treated women with such cruelty and contempt.

They still don’t enjoy full equality with men in America, but we sure have been leaning on them lately to save American democracy. The appallingly stymied attempts to hold Trump responsible for his crimes have rested largely on the efforts of women, a few of whom did vanquish him in court, as my Times Opinion colleague Jessica Bennett noted in an essay in April. She did a roll call of his pursuers: “Letitia James. Fani Willis. E. Jean Carroll, and her lawyer Roberta Kaplan. And, of course, Stormy Daniels. The five women who are living rent-free in Mr. Trump’s mind these days.”

I’d add “crazy Nancy Pelosi” — Trump is still regularly calling her that, unable to purge her from his thoughts — to the list. (Jessica wrote about Pelosi in an essay this week.) Also former Representative Liz Cheney: Nobody on the House panel investigating the events of Jan. 6, 2021, was more forceful or impassioned than she was in exposing Trump’s actions and inaction on that day. She, too, squats somewhere in Trump’s gray matter, so much so that he recently amplified social media posts that accused her of treason and urged that she be subjected to a televised military tribunal.

Jessica made her “rent-free” observation on the eve of Trump’s trial in Manhattan for falsifying business records to cover up an alleged dalliance with Daniels, and that proceeding has been so completely overtaken and overshadowed by subsequent events that it might as well have happened in the Ice Age. Fresher developments include Biden’s cataclysmic debate performance, many Democrats’ demands that he end his campaign and their despair when he resisted, talking about all he’d accomplished and how nobody was a more potent foe for Trump.

Who do you suppose was tasked with taking on that male vanity? Women! Jill Biden was exhorted to make everything right. Or it was Valerie Biden’s job. Or Pelosi’s. Hunter Biden was reportedly hunkered down with Dad, but did anyone demand that he tame the paterfamilias? Not that I heard or read. Women were given the heaping measure of responsibility, though they often get only a soupçon of credit.

I do sense progress. When people discussed who might be a stronger Democratic nominee than Biden and the names of Gov. Gretchen Whitmer of Michigan and Commerce Secretary Gina Raimondo rightly came up, few dissenters questioned their electability on the ground that they’re women.

And Harris’s doubters on the Democratic side don’t focus on her gender. Some Democrats don’t want her to pick another woman as a running mate — they worry that such a ticket might be a milestone too far — but they’re not afraid that a woman on top is a mistake in the battle against Trump.

Maybe, just maybe, it’s exactly the way to go. Women’s reproductive rights are in the foreground of this presidential election, Harris is practiced and eloquent in her defense of them, and that could widen a gender gap in a way that works to Democrats’ advantage. Women voters could be the barricade between Trump and that first-day dictatorship.

Also, as my Times colleagues Maggie Haberman and Jonathan Swan wrote recently, and as I discussed with two prominent Democrats in a conversation published on Wednesday, women opponents bring out the ugliest in Trump. Harris will quickly take up residence with Pelosi, Cheney and the gang.

And Trump will have to build an annex, maybe to his frontal or occipital lobe, to accommodate the sorority.

For the Love of Sentences

We’ll soon get to politics — how can we not? — but let’s treat ourselves to other fare first.

In an essay in The Times about deciding to end his distinguished reign as the newspaper’s chief restaurant critic, Pete Wells reflected on the physical toll of the job. “One thing we almost never bring up, though, is our health,” he wrote, referring to all restaurant critics. “We avoid mentioning weight the way actors avoid saying ‘Macbeth.’” He also recalled a colleague’s caveat that every lofty Times job is rented formal wear, not permanent threads: “It’s time to return the tux. I’ve had the trousers let out a few inches, but a tailor can take them in again. As for the stain on the jacket, that’s just pork fat. I think it adds character.” (Thanks to Mark Zimmerman of Melville, N.Y., and Joe Weinstein of Bedminster, N.J., among others, for nominating passages of Pete’s farewell.)

Also in The Times, Emmett Lindner documented the resurgent appeal of the movie “The Blob,” which became a cult classic after its 1958 release: “In Phoenixville, Pa., where much of ‘The Blob’ was shot, thousands of fans gathered at the 25th annual Blobfest over the weekend to celebrate with ooze and ahhs.” (Bob Bliwise, Durham, N.C.)

And Amy Nicholson remarked on a tonal oddity involving interviewees in “Sorry/Not Sorry,” a new documentary about the allegations of sexual misconduct against Louis C.K.: “These talented women — Jen Kirkman, Abby Schachner and Megan Koester — tell their stories with charm and humor over a mischievous, overkill score that would be better suited to an outright comedy about a dowager poisoning her rival’s plum tart.” (Mark Mushkat, Los Osos, Calif.)

In The New Yorker, Justin Chang summarized key plot developments of “Twisters,” a cyclone-minded new action movie: “A car bearing two unlucky (if not terribly sympathetic) passengers gets sucked up into the sky. We don’t see their fate, but we can assume they’re toast — or, rather, funnel cake.” (Bill Combs, Basye, Va.)

In The Washington Post, Carolyn Hax considered whether a reader’s discontent qualified as depression: “Could be. Or inertia. Or fatigue. Or boredom. Or a domestic-rut trail mix of them all.” (Paula Mackey, St. Paul, Minn.)

OK, politics! A whole lot has happened since the previous newsletter, so we’ll let this go a little longer than usual:

In his newsletter, Sam Harris marveled two weeks ago at Biden’s and his closest advisers’ refusal, at that point, to end his re-election campaign: “They are not merely courting disaster now — they are having tantric sex with it.” (Rick Malone, Tampa Bay, Fla.)

In The Atlantic, David Frum appraised Biden’s big news conference on the last day of a NATO meeting in Washington: “Art restorers use the term ‘photodegradation’ to describe the process by which a painting fades. The colors remain present; they just become less vivid. That’s the Joe Biden story.” (Ric Wilson, Cape Town, South Africa, and Mary Moore, Milwaukee)

Also in The Atlantic, Tom Nichols revisited the book that made JD Vance famous: “He wrote ‘Hillbilly Elegy’ after he gained a Yale Law degree and became a multimillionaire in Silicon Valley, and the book has more than a whiff of self-satisfaction. His observations struck some critics as the smugness of a man who escaped a shipwreck and now has some thoughts about the swimming techniques of the people behind him who drowned.” (Christopher Baker, San Francisco, and Peggy Sweeney, Oviedo, Spain, among others)

And Helen Lewis tracked Vance’s performance as a running mate so far, noting the wan reception to a joke he made about liberals no doubt finding his consumption of Diet Mountain Dew racist: “The room did not go wild. It went semidomesticated at best.” (Kim Knapp, Duluth, Ga.)

In Slate, Jim Newell begged to differ with Trump’s claim that Vance’s beard made him look like a young Abraham Lincoln: “Hmm. Maybe if you stretched Lincoln’s face from portrait to landscape orientation and got A.I. Lincoln to rail passionately against Ukraine instead of slavery, we could see it? A little?” (Rebecca Naidis, The Hague)

In his newsletter, Dave Pell compared the current president to his predecessor: “Joe Biden reminds us of our mortality. Donald Trump reminds us of America’s.” (Paula Huguenin, Collex, Switzerland)

To return to The Washington Post, David Von Drehle recalled Biden’s resentment at being shoved aside for Hillary Clinton’s 2016 candidacy: “As though party politics were a family minivan and Joey called shotgun but his sister got the seat.” (Patrisha Bronzell, Hartsdale, N.Y., and Terry Burridge, Arlington, Va.)

Also in The Post, Monica Hesse flashed back to Kamala Harris’s days in the Senate and her questioning of Brett Kavanaugh: “The version of Harris that always struck me as the most authentic and the most reassuring was the one we were introduced to in 2018, when Donald Trump’s Supreme Court pick turned up on Capitol Hill with the confidence of an altar boy who’d never before had to account for some missing Communion wine.” (Martha Kaltenbach, Akron, Ohio, and Robert Meadow, Los Angeles, among others)

And to return to The Times, Lisa Lerer and Katie Glueck examined a laughably overheated political ritual: “Efforts to select a vice-presidential nominee always incite a firestorm of unfounded predictions stoked furiously by busybodies and blowhards. There are, of course, the infamous lists — real and speculative — of prospective candidates, which fuel breathless reporting on the possibilities. Planes are tracked, schedules are scoured and even minor players suddenly transform into figures ‘close to the candidate.’” (Michael Silk, Laguna Woods, Calif.)

Also in The Times, Bret Stephens found a perfect metaphor for a certain potentate’s use of social media: “With his every tweet, Trump’s presidency felt like a diesel engine blowing black soot in the face of the country.” In a subsequent article, Bret weighed in on Trump’s climactic remarks in Milwaukee: “In its length, the speech was worthy of Fidel Castro. In its depth, of Justin Bieber.” (Pam Pifer, Bothell, Wash., and Peter Braverman, Bethesda, Md., among many others)

On a Personal Note

I owe you an apology: I didn’t send out a newsletter last Thursday, and I hadn’t announced that I’d be taking a week off. That’s because I hadn’t planned to. But then I went and shut a kitchen door on my right hand, slicing into the ring finger deeply enough that a friend and I spent much of the next half-hour cleaning blood splatter off nearby cabinets. Good times.

A visit to urgent care the following morning determined that stitches weren’t in order — my fingernail was an impediment and the bleeding had mostly stopped — but that I’d broken my fingertip. I returned home bandaged, splinted and, well, mortified. I’d done this to myself, after all, then ignored it all night long.

I couldn’t type. I’d never trained myself to dictate. (Memo to self: Get on that.) So I sent word to editors that I was on injured reserve, to borrow a football term. And I reflected on my special talent for hurting myself.

My mother loved the story of how, even before I took my first step, I broke one of my legs by dislodging a gate at the top of the stairs and tumbling down the steps.

When I was about 8, I fell off a skateboard and broke one of my feet just days before our family’s big trip to Disney World. Like some child emperor, I was transported through the park, my parents using their muscle to wheel me from one amusement to another.

There were fractured vertebrae in my teen years, courtesy of a clumsy plummet from a friend’s treehouse. For a chunk of that school year, I wore a tightly laced canvas-and-steel back brace that looked like a corset designed by Torquemada.

“A klutz,” my mother called me. She tried to make that sound like a term of endearment. She failed.

The child becomes the man. During a run in late April, I tripped — maybe on a stone, probably on nothing — and hurtled forward, stopping my fall with outstretched arms. The pain in my wrists went away only two weeks ago, just in time for my finger to pipe up.

Here’s the worst part: I shut my other hand in the same door less than a year ago. That time, there was no blood, no break, just intense pain, followed by a monthlong sequence during which the affected fingernail went from purple to black to gone. That should have taught me about that door. But I am, apparently, uneducable.

I like to end these newsletter entries with a moral, so I’ll give it a whirl. We are damage-prone creatures, all of us. OK, some of us. OK, me. We should take better care. We should keep gauze and surgical tape and Neosporin on hand. I had none of the above. So I slept that first night with thin, inadequate Band-Aids and two layers of athletic socks swaddling my oozing hand. I was a ragtag patient and misbegotten medic both. Then I healed. Even we klutzes can manage that.
