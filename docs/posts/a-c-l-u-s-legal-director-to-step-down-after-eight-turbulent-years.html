A.C.L.U.’s Legal Director to Step Down After Eight Turbulent Years

SIDEBAR

As the Supreme Court veered to the right, David D. Cole oversaw hundreds of lawsuits against the Trump administration and helped secure more wins than losses before the justices.

July 15, 2024, 5:03 a.m. ET

It was the spring of 2016, and the American Civil Liberties Union was looking for a new national legal director. David D. Cole, a law professor at Georgetown, recalled the pitch.

“Just think what it’d be like to lead the A.C.L.U. and its Supreme Court practice under a liberal majority Supreme Court,” Mr. Cole said a little ruefully, recalling what Anthony Romero, the group’s executive director, had said over lunch.

Eight years later, as he prepares to step down from the position, Mr. Cole reflected on what turned out to be a very different tenure. His grueling run included significant victories before an increasingly conservative Supreme Court, more than 400 lawsuits against the Trump administration and dueling complaints from critics who said the A.C.L.U. was either insufficiently or excessively committed to defending free speech.

Things looked different in 2016, when there was a good prospect that the Supreme Court would soon have a liberal majority for the first time in decades.

Justice Antonin Scalia died that February. Even if Senate Republicans were to succeed in blocking President Barack Obama’s pick for a successor, as they did, Hillary Clinton seemed likely to win the election and to name a fifth liberal justice.

“And then Nov. 8 of 2016 happened,” Mr. Cole said, referring to that consequential election, “and the job changed dramatically.”

The new president, Donald J. Trump, promptly started testing legal norms. And instead of moving to the left, the Supreme Court regained and then expanded its conservative majority, as Mr. Trump appointed three new justices.

In his first week in office, Mr. Trump announced a ban on travel to the United States from several largely Muslim nations.

“That Friday night, we filed the first challenge to the Muslim ban,” Mr. Cole said. “I think we had at one point 15 or 16 Muslim ban challenges pending and in various federal courts.”

In 2018, the court upheld a later version of the ban. The vote was 5 to 4, with Mr. Trump’s first appointee, Justice Neil M. Gorsuch, in the majority.

That first week of the Trump administration set the tone and pace for Mr. Cole’s first four years. “We had 400,000 members before Trump was elected,” he said. “On that single weekend, when we got the first injunction against the Muslim ban, we got 340,000 new members, so we almost doubled in size in one weekend.”

Mr. Cole has deep roots in civil liberties litigation. After graduating from Yale Law School and clerking for a federal judge, he joined the Center for Constitutional Rights, a scrappy leftist advocacy and litigation group.

He described how his old employer differed from his new one.

“I think C.C.R. saw itself as the legal arm of the progressive movement,” he said, “whereas the A.C.L.U. defends the Bill of Rights. We defend those principles on behalf of those who are furthering progressive ends and on behalf of those who are countering progressive ends.”

There is a role for both kinds of groups, he said, but there are important differences.

“When I was at C.C.R., I used to say proudly that the difference between C.C.R. and the A.C.L.U. is, we would never represent the Nazis marching in Skokie,” he said, referring to a planned 1977 march by the American Nazi Party in Skokie, Ill., a home to many Holocaust survivors. “And now that I’m at the A.C.L.U. and somewhat older, I say that the difference is that we would represent the Nazis marching in Skokie.”

That commitment to free speech was reflected in the A.C.L.U.’s decision to represent the National Rifle Association in the Supreme Court in a First Amendment challenge to what the gun rights group said was a New York official’s efforts to blacklist it. The A.C.L.U.’s decision to take on the representation angered some in the civil rights community, including the New York affiliate of the A.C.L.U.

Mr. Cole argued the case in March and won unanimously two months later.

His previous two arguments also yielded victories. In 2021, he persuaded eight members of the court — everyone but Justice Clarence Thomas — that a Pennsylvania school district had violated the First Amendment by punishing a student for a vulgar social media message sent while she was not on school grounds.

And in 2019, he argued on behalf of a transgender woman in one of a pair of consolidated cases that produced a landmark decision protecting L.G.B.T.Q. employees from workplace discrimination. The vote was 6 to 3, with Justice Gorsuch writing the majority opinion.

Mr. Cole has done an informal tally, finding that his nonprofit organization’s track record has been reasonably strong. Over the past four years in argued cases, counting matters in which it represented a party or filed a supporting brief, the A.C.L.U. was on the winning side 38 of 68 times.

In many ways, the A.C.L.U.’s counterpart these days is Alliance Defending Freedom, a conservative Christian group that has won a series of important cases at the Supreme Court. The group’s president and general counsel, Kristen Waggoner, said that Mr. Cole was a respected adversary.

“While we don’t agree with David on much, we had one important point of commonality: a commitment to civil dialogue,” she said. “I have always found him a formidable, gracious and kind advocate. We hope his successor at the A.C.L.U. is someone as committed to respectful discourse.”

The two lawyers faced off in the Supreme Court in Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado Civil Rights Commission, which concerned a baker who objected to creating custom cakes for same-sex weddings. Mr. Cole represented a gay couple the baker had turned away, and the case ended inconclusively.

But a sequel last year, involving a web designer represented by Ms. Waggoner’s group, ruled that businesses engaged in expression have a First Amendment right to refuse to create messages endorsing same-sex marriage despite a state law that forbids discrimination against gay people. The A.C.L.U. had filed a brief urging the court to rule the opposite way.

Nico Perrino, the executive vice president of the Foundation for Individual Rights and Expression, said that Mr. Cole “falls within the lineage of the free speech advocates at the A.C.L.U. that I greatly admire.” But he added that the many agendas of the group sometimes required it to balance competing values.

An authentic and unwavering commitment to free expression, Mr. Perrino said, required supporting the web designer.

Mr. Cole is returning to Georgetown but will stay at the A.C.L.U. until his successor is chosen. That person will face a different litigation landscape than the one Mr. Cole encountered in 2016.

“It has become a more challenging environment to defend civil rights and civil liberties as a result of the Trump appointees, particularly on the Court of Appeals and in the Supreme Court,” he said.

Still, he said, “I don’t leave with any concern about the A.C.L.U.’s ability to fight back.”
