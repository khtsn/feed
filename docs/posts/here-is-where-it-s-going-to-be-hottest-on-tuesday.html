July 16, 2024, 5:30 a.m. ET

In most areas of the United States, the warmest day of the year is typically recorded in July. So it’s no surprise that most weather maps are red hot on Tuesday, with temperatures expected to reach above 100 in a strip from the Southwest to the Mid-Atlantic.

It may feel even hotter in the East, where another day of sweltering conditions will push the heat index well into the triple digits. The beginning of a gentle reprieve from the widespread heat wave could begin for some as early as Tuesday night or Wednesday.

The heavy air will make it feel even hotter, with the heat index pushing near 110 for some locations, like Washington, D.C. The city is expected — for a third day in a row — to top 100 degrees in the afternoon, after reaching 101 on Sunday and Monday. Forecasters again issued an excessive heat warning for the nation’s capital on Tuesday, predicting a temperature of 102 and a much higher heat index.

After sweating through natural steam room conditions Monday, cities from Atlanta to Boston could again see major-to-extreme heat risk conditions Tuesday afternoon. New York may even wither in the hottest temperatures of the summer.

Evenings may not offer much respite. Forecasters with the Weather Prediction Center warned that warm overnight lows in many areas Tuesday into Wednesday will provide little relief.

From Tuesday into Wednesday, temperatures across the Northern Plains are expected to regulate back to normal and, even dip slightly below average in some areas. Portions of the Midwest will also be at risk of major-to-extreme heat on Tuesday, but showers and thunderstorms arriving ahead of cooler air could keep temperatures lower on Wednesday.

In ensuing days, an air mass of unseasonably cooler air out of Canada will bring progressive relief as it sweeps through the eastern half of the U.S. Those milder temperatures should reach most in the South and along the East Coast by Thursday and Friday.

But as the heat subsides in the East, another persistent heat wave will begin building again out West. Record-setting high temperatures will again be possible, though it will not necessarily be as hot as last week’s temperatures, which set record highs in Palm Springs, Calif., and Las Vegas.

And although it might not be as scorching at first, the above-average temperatures out West could carry on into August, the Climate Prediction Center warned in its outlook. The extensive hot temperatures could also result in a prolonged period of enhanced wildfire danger.
