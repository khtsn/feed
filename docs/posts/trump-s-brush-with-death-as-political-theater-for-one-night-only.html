Donald J. Trump used his convention speech to recount the attempt on his life in dark detail. “Bullets were flying over us, yet I felt serene,” he said.

Shawn McCreesh

Reporting from Milwaukee

  • July 19, 2024, 2:26 a.m. ET

In his first speech since a gunman tried to kill him, Donald J. Trump stood before an arena of supporters and began, simply, with what he knew they wanted to hear.

He told the tale.

It was a theatrical, dark and, at times, quasi-religious retelling. Mr. Trump spoke slowly, paused purposefully, and even re-enacted the way he had turned his head just as a would-be assassin’s bullet sliced through his ear.

He would eventually slip into a version of his familiar stump speech, and the drama dissipated. But for those first 15 minutes, the former president delivered a campaign speech like no other — a remarkable narration of a near-death experience packaged as slick political theater.

And he knew how to hype it, telling his audience at the top that it was a one-night only, special event. “So many people have asked me what happened, ‘Tell us what happened, please,’” he said. “And therefore, I will tell you exactly what happened, and you’ll never hear it from me a second time. Because it’s actually too painful to tell.”

He began. “It was a warm, beautiful day in the early evening,” he said, and his campaign “was doing really well” and “everybody was happy” and he was “speaking very strongly, powerfully, and happily.”

His voice became solemn and the crowd sat transfixed as he told them of bullets flying and “absolutely blood all over the place.” Giant photos taken that day showing his face, an inch from the ground and streaked with blood, filled the jumbo screens surrounding the stage. Behind him was an image of the White House north portico — lest viewers forget he was a once, and perhaps future, president.

Mr. Trump framed the episode as a communal experience, a reminder of the adamantine bond he has with his followers. He marveled at how the crowd in Butler, Pa., on Saturday would not forsake him, even as they were being fired upon. “This massive crowd of tens of thousands of people stood by and didn’t move an inch,” he explained. “This beautiful crowd, they didn’t want to leave me. They knew I was in trouble.”

“Bullets were flying over us,” he said, “yet I felt serene.”

There was an odd note in his timbre, something sullen and uncharacteristically vulnerable — and also inviting — in the way he said, “I’m not supposed to be here tonight. Not supposed to be here.”

The arena instantly answered. “Yes you are! Yes you are!”

“Thank you,” he paused. “But I’m not. And I’ll tell you. I stand before you in this arena only by the grace of almighty God.”

Mr. Trump recounted what was going through his mind in the moment before he popped back up with his fist raised, making an image that was instantly seared into political history. He saw a look of “great sorrow” on faces in the crowd, he said. “I wanted to do something to let them know I was OK.”

He then turned to talk about others who were shot. Three men were hit that day, including a volunteer firefighter named Corey Comperatore, who died. “They were serious Trumpsters, I want to tell you,” he said. “They were serious Trumpsters and still are. But Corey, unfortunately we have to use the past tense. He was incredible.”

He turned around and approached a display holding Mr. Comperatore’s firefighter’s jacket, his helmet placed atop it. Mr. Trump gripped the jacket’s shoulders, bent his head down and kissed the helmet.

He asked for a moment of silence. Down on the floor, the delegates from Texas removed their cowboy hats.

Mr. Trump sounded prayerful as he told them that “there is no greater love than to lay down one’s life for others.”

The crowd responded: “We love Trump. We love Trump.”
