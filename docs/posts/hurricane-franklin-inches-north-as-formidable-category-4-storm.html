Judson Jones and Hogla Enecia Pérez

Judson Jones is a meteorologist and reporter for The Times. Hogla Enecia Perez reported from the Dominican Republic.

Aug. 29, 2023, 5:46 a.m. ET

Hurricane Franklin, the strongest storm of the 2023 Atlantic hurricane season, was producing life-threatening surf and rip currents along the coasts of Bermuda and the East Coast of the United States early Tuesday, but the eye of the Category 4 storm was expected to stay well away from land.

As of 5 a.m. Eastern, Franklin was about 385 miles west-southwest of Bermuda and moving north-northeast toward the island, the National Hurricane Center of the United States said in an advisory. Bermuda was under a tropical storm watch, indicating that tropical storm conditions were possible, typically within 48 hours.

Franklin was producing maximum sustained winds of about 140 miles per hour, the Hurricane Center said, making it a “formidable” Category 4 storm on the wind scale used to measure tropical cyclones in the Atlantic, the center said. A major hurricane has sustained winds of 111 m.p.h. or greater, corresponding to a Category 3, 4 or 5 hurricane; Category 5 ones have winds of at least 157 m.p.h.

Franklin was expected to weaken later on Tuesday and pass well northwest of Bermuda on Wednesday. But tropical storm conditions were still possible in Bermuda over the next few days, the Hurricane Center said. The Bermuda government warned early Tuesday that Franklin would likely produce gusty showers, dangerous swells and rip currents.

The life-threatening surf and rip currents along the East Coast of the United States were also expected to spread up the coastlines of the United States and Canada on Tuesday and Wednesday.

Franklin left at least one person dead in the Dominican Republic and hundreds of thousands of homes without power or potable water last week. At one point, 350,000 homes were without power, and more than 1.6 million did not have potable water.

Another dangerous hurricane, Idalia, was near Cuba early Tuesday. It was expected to reach the Gulf Coast of Florida later in the day or early Wednesday, forecasters said. The Bermuda government said it was monitoring that storm, too.

The Atlantic hurricane season started on June 1 and runs through Nov. 30.

In late May, the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration predicted that there would be 12 to 17 named storms this year, a “near-normal” amount, forecasters said. On Aug. 10, NOAA officials increased its estimate to 14 to 21 storms.

There were 14 named storms last year, coming on the heels of two extremely busy Atlantic hurricane seasons in which forecasters ran out of names and had to resort to backup lists. (There were a record 30 named storms in 2020.)

This year features an El Niño pattern, which started in June. The intermittent climate phenomenon can have wide-ranging effects on weather around the world, and it typically impedes the number of Atlantic hurricanes.

In the Atlantic, El Niño increases the amount of wind shear, or the change in wind speed and direction from the ocean or land surface into the atmosphere. Hurricanes need a calm environment to form, and the instability caused by increased wind shear makes those conditions less likely. (El Niño has the opposite effect in the Pacific, reducing the amount of wind shear.)

At the same time, this year’s heightened sea surface temperatures pose a number of threats, including the ability to supercharge storms.

That unusual confluence of factors has made making storm predictions more difficult.

Four names from the 2017 list do not appear this year because they have been retired.

They are: Harvey, Irma, Maria and Nate.

Names are retired when a storm causes history-making destruction or death and reusing the name would be insensitive to people who were affected by the storm.

There is consensus among scientists that hurricanes are becoming more powerful because of climate change. Although there might not be more named storms overall, the likelihood of major hurricanes is increasing.

Climate change is also affecting the amount of rain that storms can produce.

In a warming world, the air can hold more moisture, which means that a named storm can hold and produce more rainfall, as Hurricane Harvey did in Texas in 2017, when some areas received more than 40 inches of rain in less than 48 hours.
