Oregon Fugitive Found After Having Used a Stolen Identity for 30 Years

U.S.|Oregon Fugitive Found After Having Used a Stolen Identity for 30 Years

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/07/18/us/oregon-fugitive-false-identity.html

The escaped prisoner was living in Macon, Ga., for over a decade and was using the identity of a child who died in Texas in 1962, according to the U.S. Marshals Service.

July 18, 2024, 5:43 p.m. ET

A fugitive who escaped from an Oregon prison 30 years ago was found and arrested on Tuesday in Georgia, according to the U.S. Marshals Service, who said he had been using a dead child’s identity to evade authorities.

Steven Craig Johnson, 70, fled from a work crew in 1994 while sentenced at Mill Creek Correctional Facility, a minimum security prison in Salem, Ore., which has since closed. The U.S. Marshals Service said that its agents confronted Mr. Johnson, a convicted sex offender, on Tuesday in the apartment complex where he had been living since 2011 in Macon, Ga., under the alias “William Cox.” The identity he stole belonged to a child who died in Texas in 1962, the Marshals said.

Mr. Johnson is a pedophile and presented “a high probability of victimizing pre-teen boys,” according to a “Wanted” flier posted by the Oregon Department of Corrections. Investigators believe that he obtained the deceased child’s birth certificate and Social Security number in 1995 while he was in Texas. They believe he first obtained a driver’s license under the stolen identity in Texas. He obtained a Georgia license in 1998, the Marshals said.

Evidence shows he had family connections in Texas, but there is no evidence that family members assisted him, said Chris Wright, a senior inspector for the U.S. Marshals’ Southeast Regional Fugitive Task Force, in an interview.

It does not appear that Mr. Johnson had connections in Georgia, but a building manager of the apartment complex where he was living told Mr. Wright that Mr. Johnson had one friend who would visit him. The building manager also said that Mr. Johnson was known to his neighbors as William Cox or “Bud.” When U.S. Marshal officers went to question him, they had no indication that he was employed, but upon his arrest Mr. Johnson mentioned he had a pension.

Before his escape in 1994, Mr. Johnson was serving a state sentence in Oregon for three counts of sex abuse and one count of attempted sodomy. He was up for parole in 1995, but he had some disciplinary issues in the prison that could have complicated his ability to get parole, according to Mr. Wright.

The Oregon Department of Corrections requested in 2015 that the U.S. Marshals adopt the case. According to Mr. Wright, in partnership with the U.S. Marshals, the Diplomatic Security Service, an arm of the State Department, employed a new investigative technology, which helped investigators find new leads on the case this year. Mr. Wright would not disclose details on the new technology to protect the secrecy of future investigations using the method.

The Oregon prison in which Mr. Johnson was serving his sentence was an unfenced facility that housed 290 prisoners who were within four years of release. The facility was closed permanently in 2021 at the order of former Gov. Kate Brown.

Mr. Johnson is being held in Bibb County Jail in Macon, Ga., and is awaiting extradition to Oregon.
