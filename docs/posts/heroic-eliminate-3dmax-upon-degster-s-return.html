HEROIC eliminate 3DMAX upon degster's return

The Ezra Klein Show

Aug. 9, 2024, 5:04 a.m. ET

The remarkable thing about the past couple of months in politics has been watching the Democratic Party act like something we have not seen for a long time — a political party. A party that makes decisions collectively. A party that does hard things because it wants to win. A party that is more than the vehicle for a single — usually — man’s ambitions.

But parties are made of people. And in this case, the party was in particular made of a person, Nancy Pelosi, one of the longest-serving House speakers, the first female speaker, and — it sometimes feels — one of the last people left in American politics who knows how to wield power and knows why she wants to do so.

This is an edited transcript of our conversation.

We’re talking on the day that Vice President Kamala Harris named Tim Walz as her vice-presidential pick in the campaign. He is the first former House member to be on a Democratic ticket since Al Gore. What was he like in the House?

He was remarkable in the House. He came winning a Republican seat. So he was a red to blue candidate. He came as the longest-serving noncommissioned officer in the military ever to serve in the Congress. So he was on a path of veterans affairs and the rest. He came having worked in farming as a child and so rural America was a big priority for him.

He’s very popular in the House. Members are so excited about him because he’s a wonderful person. He called me right after this, shall we say, opportunity arose. Told me: ‘‘I know how to make this case. I know how to differentiate. I can get this done. I’m putting myself out there.’’ And, you know, here he is, governor of Minnesota, and he’s putting himself out there.

And then he comes up with “weird,” which becomes viral, and here he is. So, I have to give him a lot of credit for not only being a great governor, and values-based, and visionary, and all that, but being quite an adept politician.

You make the argument in the book that members of the House are more retail-oriented. What do you mean by that?

We have to run every two years. And that’s as our founders intended — that we would be close to the people and run every two years. Ours is, shall we say, a little more rough-and-tumble, a little more impatience to get things done and a little more closeness, whatever the word is, to constituents, our bosses. We consider them our bosses.

One of the ways Walz has changed the campaign quickly is the injection of the word and the idea of weirdness into it. You have a very funny chapter on Donald Trump that is largely about him being very weird in conversations with you. What have you thought about the bringing of weirdness as an attack into the campaign? And how well does it describe your experience of Trump and the Republicans who have followed him?

I think “weird” is too complimentary a word for them. Weird is weird. But wrong is even different. It’s a good word. It’s on the path. But I think “dangerous” is probably — but it’s not as, shall we say, appealing a word. It sounds confrontational, but I think they’re very dangerous. Some weird people may be dangerous, but not all.

It strikes me that one reason it’s connected in the way it has is that it’s deflating. I think in many ways, Joe Biden often wanted Trump’s threat to be the center of the campaign. And this feels like an interesting inversion. It’s about making them smaller, pushing them more to the side, grabbing hold of normalcy, not making them into a sort of unstoppable supervillain.

My understanding is that Trump does not like to be laughed at, and this made people laugh at them. That’s why he had a thing about me, because I was making people laugh at him.

JD Vance’s initial attack on Walz was that Walz is a San Francisco-style liberal. I happened to talk with Walz a week ago, and he told me that he had actually just been to San Francisco for the first time a few weeks ago. You’re a San Francisco liberal, and you were a very successful speaker of the House. Kamala Harris, a San Francisco politician and the vice president, is now the presumptive nominee. Gavin Newsom is a San Francisco politician. San Francisco actually has a pretty strong record right now of training politicians who become national figures, who seem quite adept, in power, at throwing a punch or taking a punch. Why has San Francisco trained so many politicians to be so adept at wielding both power and media?

Well, it’s all about the why. Why are you even into this? San Francisco is a place that is an intellectual resource, whether we’re talking about saving the planet, whether you’re talking about L.G.B.T.Q. rights, whether you’re talking about a one’s right to choose. You name any subject, and there is a bedrock of support for it — and again, intellectual resource as well as strategic thinking about how to get things done. It’s highly educated, the whole region, beautifully diverse in terms of people. In my district, we have every religion, or none. We have every political thought. And a lot of success, but a lot of need. So when it comes to the politics, it’s a network of allegiances, and it’s not easy. It’s competitive, and there are loyalties. And so you have to get through all of that.

And look at Kamala. She ran against the incumbent [Terence Hallinan, the San Francisco district attorney]. I was actually for him, because he was for me. And his family, the Hallinan family, was a major Democratic progressive family. And she ran against him. She won. So she had courage to go forward. And then when she ran for state attorney general, it was a very tough race. But she figured out a way.

So when people talk about her, I say: “You know what? She’s not only a person of deep faith, which I personally admire in her — and her commitment to public service. She knows what she cares about, and she fights for it. And politically she’s astute, but you don’t realize it.” And then she got to be vice president. And as soon as there was an opportunity — we thought there could be an open opportunity if people wanted to run, and they could have — but she locked it down right away.

Tell me about how she did that. She coalesced the party very rapidly.

None of us had any idea Biden would announce his withdrawal that Sunday. Well, I didn’t have any idea. Most people didn’t. So when he did that, the thought was everybody wanted an open process. Let’s see the bench of the Democrats and let them compete and see what they can attract. But when he endorsed her, then it was, are you with Kamala or not? And she moved quickly — again, a sign of her adroitness, in terms of being politically astute.

Our goal is to make sure that Donald Trump never sets foot in the White House. Because he’s beyond weird. I won’t go into all the adjectives. When you make a decision about your goal, you have to make every decision in favor of reaching that goal. But the most important part of the decision is the candidate and the campaign. And it didn’t seem as if there was a campaign in place, and it was discouraging to people.

So when an opportunity presented itself, people grasped it because it was, well, I’d like to see an open thing. But then when it got in, we’re running with the ball. I always say to them, I have the three M’s, but we have the three No’s — no wasted time, no underutilized resources and no regrets the day after the election.

So that decision about the campaign or, per se, the person, was the critical thing. And when that changed, the people who had been discouraged were overwhelmingly —

Watching the pent-up energy express itself has been something.

It’s beautiful to behold. Volunteers, small-donor contributions, just a whole different thing. Now we have to sustain it.

I wanted to read a quote from your book that caught my eye. It is about how you are as a speaker. And you say that, as speaker: “You must demonstrate a plan, and you must act without hesitation. The minute you hesitate, your options are diminished. The longer you wait, the more your options are diminished.” Tell me about that principle.

Being a member of Congress, you have to be deductive, study it, learn it. Hearings, constituent meetings. But sometimes when you’re the leader, or you’re the speaker, or an executive like the president of the United States, or a governor, you have to act intuitively. And the minute you hesitate, everybody starts to protect their interest, which reduces your success.

Act. Nine out of ten times, you’ll be right. That’s not a bad average.

Maybe nine out of ten times, you’ll be right. I’m not sure we all have that average.

Well, you have to know yourself. They say, trust your gut. Well, what’s your gut unless you know what you’re talking about? You know the appetite of the voters. You have listened. You don’t start listening that day; you might take a quick tally to make sure everybody knows the direction you’re going in. But I completely subscribed to, you act, you’ll prevail. And they know you’re going to act, so they won’t mess with you. [Laughs] I mean, I should say it in a nicer way than that. They know you’re going to act, so they might curb their enthusiasm until another time.

Well, when people believe you’re going to act, it changes their strategy.

It does. They want to negotiate for another day, and that’s always leverage, too. They have leverage, you have leverage, because they may want something another day.

You did something over the past month that I actually found sort of remarkable and inexplicable. On July 8, Joe Biden sent a letter to Democratic members of Congress. And he said: The debate about me running is over. I’m running. I was voted for by 14 million people in the Democratic primary. Are you trying to take away their voice? We’re done with this.

Two days later, you went on “Morning Joe” and said, “It’s up to the President to decide if he is going to run. We’re all encouraging him to make that decision because time is running short.” House Democrats had texted me all that day before. They said, it’s over, it’s a fait accompli, he’s quelled this. You walked out and reopened the space for deliberation by saying something that, in a way, didn’t make logical sense — he had made his decision. Then everybody else picked up your template. Where did that come from?

Well, I wanted to see a campaign that could win. Because I had made a decision that I stayed in Congress to defeat what’s his name, because I think he is a danger to our country. It’s not like a Bob Dole or a George Bush or something like that, where you have a difference of opinion. It’s patriotism — or autocracy or democracy. It’s a different thing.

I actually was on that show because I was bringing Svetlana so that people could hear about Belarus. That was our main purpose that morning. I didn’t accept the letter as anything but a letter. I mean, there are some people who are unhappy with the letter. Let me say it differently. Some said that some people were unhappy with the letter. I’ll put it in somebody else’s mouth. It didn’t sound like Joe Biden to me. It really didn’t.

My main purpose, though, was to say, “Stop saying things.” Because the president has NATO here. If you have something to say, save it for later, until they’re gone. Because this is a big deal — the summit of NATO, which he has strengthened, grown, and now is hosting. And they’re doing great things, and he’s the center of it all. Save your comments for later.

But the thing that was happening in that moment was that, as time was moving forward, the space for options was diminishing. That’s what it felt like. And you sort of flip that and reopen the question of deliberation. I’ve actually seen you do this a few times. I remember watching you do this with the Affordable Care Act, after Scott Brown won Ted Kennedy’s Senate seat, and it felt like the bill was collapsing. You have a tendency, when a thing is beginning to fall apart, to simply assert that it isn’t and to reopen people’s imagination about the options. You talked about that in terms of intuition. How do you know when something is breaking, and how do you know when it can actually be held together?

I see everything as an opportunity, no matter what it is — an opportunity for change, an opportunity to grow, whatever it is.

So when we did the Affordable Care Act, the press said, now you’re dead. I said: “No, no. We are not passing up an opportunity of a generation that people have been trying to do, presidents have been trying to do for a hundred years.”

For people who don’t remember this, Ted Kennedy dies. Scott Brown, a Republican, wins in Massachusetts. And it felt to people like the Affordable Care Act was going to die. And the feeling was that you pulled that back from the brink.

When the Senate bill, which was a real stinkeroo — I mean, there was no way we were going to vote for it. But finally they passed it, so at least they had a vehicle. And we had our vehicle, and we’re all set to go to conference the next day after the election. Then boom, you’re dead.

But the conference that we were engaged in prepared us for what we would put in reconciliation. I knew we had reconciliation. So we didn’t need 60 votes, but we needed to have something that our House Democrats would vote for. So the consensus that was built in conference prepared us for something that we could do in reconciliation and pass. Because there was no way —

Yeah, but you needed to stiffen your members to pass even that. A lot of them wanted to flee.

No, they didn’t want to pass it at all. I said, it stinks because see what we had to do, which was worse because of reconciliation, which is weird. People kept saying to me, “Just pass the Senate bill.” I said, “I wouldn’t even vote for that thing, much less bring it up. And my members are not voting for it. It’s a terrible bill. You have missed an opportunity and you don’t have things in there that you should. I don’t vote for a bill for what it doesn’t have, but I’m not voting for it if I don’t like what it does have.”

But then it came to us, and this was horrible. Horrible. But the parliamentarian says that you cannot pass your bill, the Senate bill, and add your amendments until this bill is signed into law. That meant I had to go to the members and say: “That stinking, lousy, rotten, nothing bill from the Senate, we’re going to have to vote for it so it gets signed into law. Then Harry Reid gets these passed. We stick it here, and then we have a victory.”

“You can’t make us.”

I said, “You know what? I hate the bill.” But the President was on the line, Harry Reid was on the line, and I was on the line. And they voted for the stinking, lousy bill that I was never going to bring up, that I was never going to vote for. But we had to do it. Send it to the President, he signs it, it comes back, we amend it, they pass it, and then we have the success that we went out there for.

I was sorry we never could get the public option because the Senate wouldn’t do it. The public option would have saved us money, but we couldn’t get it. But we put provisions in the bill that compensated for that. So it wasn’t so bad, but it wasn’t as good as it could be.

And as far as the reconciliation piece, I guess they just didn’t think we would do it. I don’t know what they thought, but we were not not going to pass the Senate bill. And President Obama always stuck with us. I’m not saying all of his people did, but the president, always. He was wonderful.

Before you were in Congress, you were the state chair of the California Democratic Party.

I thought I was the queen of the world. The biggest party in the country. Grass-roots organizing, owning the ground with your message, developing the candidates. Oh my God. It was heaven.

You’re a party person, which not everybody is these days. One of the most remarkable things to me about covering the Democratic Party over the past couple of months has been watching it do something collectively in very difficult, uncertain circumstances.

I was sitting with a Republican, a significant sort of experienced Republican, and I was asking him, “Why were they so surprised by the switch? Why was there so little talk of Kamala Harris at the Republican convention?” And he said, “We never thought they would do it.”

The Democratic Party has held together as an organization, an institution in recent years, not just in the past couple of months, in a way the Republicans haven’t. Tell me about how you have kept Democrats thinking and acting like a party. How you’ve empowered them to do that and maybe how they’ve empowered you to sort of act with power instead of the party.

The attitude that they gave me. I see myself as a weaver at a loom. And we have all these members and beautiful diversity. Seventy percent of our caucus are women, people of color, L.G.B.T.Q. That’s not the only diversity. It’s geographic. It’s generational. It’s everything that you can think of. It’s philosophy and the rest. But they know why they’re Democrats.

And you make everybody know that by telling them, “What I’m weaving here depends on all of you.” Treating each one with respect. “Every thread makes a mosaic, a tapestry that is so beautiful. And you’re part of that.”

So if we are not unanimous on something, that’s OK. Just as long as we have the consensus to proceed. So you may not be part of the unanimity on a given issue or even part of the consensus, but respect it. Because tomorrow is another day and your issue — something you’re more interested in regionally, whatever it is — and that tapestry will be there for you.

But it should be true for Republicans that the shifting coalitions benefit them over time. It should be true for Republicans that they have interests that weave in and out. It has become less true, right?

Yes, but if you don’t believe in governance and you don’t believe in science — it’s very hard to find leverage with people who don’t have really any beliefs or any agenda. It’s hard to negotiate with somebody who wants nothing. And so, it’s respect, it’s listening, it’s building off the information that people have. It’s not anything coming from the top down. And there’s one word that I attribute to my caucus, which is courage. They have the courage to go out and make the vote. They believe that this is what we need to do. Because, you know, the other side is going to go out and paint the thing like it’s poo-poo and caca when we think it’s chocolate ice cream.

Bernie Sanders and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez often argue with where the Democrats end up, but they often vote for the bill in the end. Sanders was there on the Affordable Care Act in every vote people needed. He wasn’t shutting the government down like Ted Cruz. Is the thing that broke the Republican Party’s ability to act as a coherent institution the fact that they just don’t believe in or want enough from the government?

One of the things I love is being a legislator. It’s really hard work. I don’t say that as a complaint. I love it. But people are working all the time developing policy, whether they start with a bill and or take it through the process, take it home, listen to constituents at home, see what the possibilities are on the other side of the aisle, the other side of the Capitol.

So it’s a triumph when you pass a bill. It’s a triumph, especially if it’s going to be signed into law. But you have to have goals. You have to have a why. Come back to the why. And you have to have respect for other people’s views and who’s going to take the lead on something.

You told a story I really liked in the book about getting your members to vote for the defense authorization bill that included the repeal of Don’t Ask, Don’t Tell.

It was hard because not all the Democrats, shall we say, were as fully on board as we would have liked them to be. But we did get the number of votes for an amendment to the defense authorization bill. And Don’t Ask, Don’t Tell comes up. We passed the bill. “Oh, my God.” Even the Democratic chair of the committee was against it. But we passed it.

So I said to Barney Frank, Anna Eshoo, everybody you can think of who was lefty — “This is pretty exciting. You made history today. We repealed Don’t Ask, Don’t Tell.” And I said, “Now, you’re going to make it twice.”

“Why?”

“You’re going to vote, for the first time, for a defense authorization bill.”

“Don’t ask us to do that. We will never, ever do it.”

I said, “Well, you like the repeal of Don’t Ask, Don’t Tell. We don’t have it unless we pass a bill.”

“Well, what makes you think you know everything? The Republicans always vote for the defense bill.”

“But not today.”

“How do you know?”

“Because I know. Because I’m the speaker. I know they ain’t going to vote for this bill.”

“How do you know?”

I said, “I can see it in their eyes. I know them. They ain’t voting for the bill.”

Nine Republicans voted for the bill. That means Republicans overwhelmingly voted against it. Now, we had some trouble in the Senate, so we had to split it up. But we got it passed, thanks to Harry Reid. So, I said, “See, you made history twice in one day.”

You talk in the book about what makes a good speaker. You say strategic know-how, you say a policy background, and then you say, above all else, intuition. You’re known as somebody who does know if the votes are going to be there. A lot of people don’t. Where does that intuition come from? How do you develop that?

There are many who do have it, so it’s not just me. My whole thing with the members is they know I didn’t want to run for any other office. I love the House. You’re close to your constituents. There’s nothing more thrilling to me than to walk on the floor and say, “I speak for the people of San Francisco.” No matter what other honors may be bestowed upon me.

You’re always observing and learning and respecting and listening to what you think the other side might do.

I think you’re underselling this a little bit. There’s something you did institutionally, organizationally, that kept your finger on the pulse of it. How do you think about that?

I’ve spent a long time in committee work, appropriations and intelligence, even some time on ethics. I was a dedicated committee member. I had no interest in running for leadership. Why would I want to do that when I could be doing appropriations or intelligence? So you get to know how the system works and what lights whose fire and the rest.

You’re a real partisan of the House. You quote John Dingell, the late, great member of the House, saying, “The Republicans are the opposition, but the Senate is the enemy.” If there were no Senate — or at least if there were no Senate filibuster — if the House was what governed America, how would America be different today?

Well, I’m not advocating for that, but I do think the compromise that our beautiful, visionary founders had in giving every state two votes, that was a compromise that created America.

Then they decide that it’s going to be 60 votes. So we’re saying 40 percent wins? This is ridiculous. And this is what we have to fight. Immigration, women’s right to choose, raise the minimum wage, all these kinds of things. 51 votes like that.

And people get frustrated: “Why can’t you do what you said you’re going to do?” Well, we do it in the House, but they don’t do it in the Senate.”

The cynicism creeps in, and understandably so. Democrats will run this year on protecting and reinstating through law the protections of Roe v. Wade. But if they win, they’re not going to have 60 votes in the Senate, even if they do keep the Senate, which means that they can’t really do it because —

They have to get rid of the filibuster or else everything is: the rescue package, 51 votes. Inflation Reduction Act, 51 votes.

Even the CHIPS Act. All the Republicans want the CHIPS Act. So much excitement for the CHIPS Act. Some of the Republicans were saying to me we’d probably get, like, 35 Republican votes. Because they don’t want Biden to have any success. Some of the Democrats thought that we were giving money to corporate America or something like that, so we couldn’t count on everybody.

The night before the vote, the ranking member of the Science, Space, and Technology Committee makes a beautiful presentation about the bill and the bipartisanship that helped shape the STEM parts of it and the education.

Ten o’clock, I’m talking to Chuck Schumer on the phone, and I get a thing on my phone that says Kevin McCarthy just said nobody can vote for the bill. And the ranking member has to argue against the bill. Which is a shame — they should at least let somebody else manage the opposition when this guy had just made this beautiful speech in favor.

So I said to Chuck, “Oh, we just lost all the Republican votes.” I said, “It’s 10 o’clock at night. Let me just tell my staff to tell the Republicans to go to hell. We’re going to pass the bill without them.” That was my message. I don’t usually use foul language, but that was ridiculous.

So I didn’t have the votes at that moment, but I figured out one way or another I had the leverage to get them. So we get the votes. When we get to 218, something like 24 Republicans came on board. But not until we passed it ourselves. That’s how bad they are. They wouldn’t even vote for the CHIPS Act to make us self-reliant as a country.

I just have to tell you this, because this one just defies everything: They don’t want Roe v. Wade, right? We bring a bill to the floor, by Kathy Manning of North Carolina, that women have a right to contraception.

Members said to me, “You’re letting them off the hook. They’re going to look good voting for this.” Eight Republicans voted for it, 195 voted against it — some of them women. Now, is that our country? Is that freedom? Does the public know how dangerous they are? They’re against abortion; they should love contraception, right?

You wrote a book about the art of power. Trump wrote a book years ago called “The Art of the Deal.” You’ve watched Donald Trump working on deals. What would you tell the American people about what Trump does and doesn’t know about how to make a deal?

If you’re going to make a deal, you have to be true to your word. You have to be honest. And he doesn’t have an honest thread in his body.

What do you think happens if Trump wins a second term? The race is very close. It’s possible. Trump was dealing in Congress with a Republican Party that had not fully bought into him in 2017. It’s a very different Republican Party now. What is possible if he and the Republicans win in 2024?

I can’t envision it. First time people thought, well, maybe he’ll respect the institution or the presidency. Little did we know he didn’t, but we thought he might. So let’s give him the benefit of the doubt. We all want our president to succeed, whoever he may be. But then we saw how vile, how disgusting, how crooked he was.

So now we have to make sure he doesn’t win. And anybody who said, well, I didn’t like Hillary Clinton because of this, or I didn’t like — you know what? Kamala’s the person. He’s the other one. That’s the choice.

We have to make sure he doesn’t win. I can’t even envision a situation where he would win. If he were to win and we didn’t win the House, imagine how horrible it would be. I can hardly sleep at night as it is. But that would be unthinkable, impossible for our country. We just have to do it all. Own the ground.

Meantime, Joe Biden, just a remarkable president of the United States. We have to make sure his legacy, which is our legacy — we passed that, and a lot of my members had to take tough votes for it. So we want it to be recognized, but more importantly, we want people to avail themselves of the goodness of it all. And that’s that.
