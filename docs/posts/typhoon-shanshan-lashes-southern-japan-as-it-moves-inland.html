Typhoon Shanshan Lashes Southern Japan as It Moves Inland

The storm weakened after making landfall, but the authorities reported three deaths and warned of potential landslides and floods.

Published Aug. 28, 2024Updated Aug. 29, 2024, 1:45 a.m. ET

Typhoon Shanshan lashed southern Japan with record rainfall and powerful winds on Thursday, after killing at least three people, knocking out power for hundreds of thousands and forcing almost a million evacuations.

The storm, the strongest to hit Japan this year, packed winds of up to 86 miles per hour on Thursday afternoon, equivalent to those of a Category 1 hurricane, according to the U.S. Navy’s Joint Typhoon Warning Center.

Shanshan had peaked at Category-4 strength before making landfall around 8 a.m. on Kyushu, the southernmost of Japan’s main islands, according to the Japan Meteorological Agency. It was forecast to move slowly north through Kyushu on Thursday, then shift east on Friday and Saturday, moving further inland.

The storm was weakening as it moved slowly inland, but the authorities warned of potential landslides and floods.

“This is one of the biggest typhoons in recent years, for a prefecture that experiences many typhoons every year,” Kensei Tomisako, a disaster response official in Satsumasendai, said in an interview.

The typhoon has brought record rainfall. Some parts of Kyushu recorded 2.6 feet of rain within 48 hours, forecasters said. The storm, moving north at just 7 m.p.h. on Thursday afternoon, lashed some areas with rain for hours.

Three people died after a landslide on Tuesday buried their home in Gamagori, a city in central Japan that was hit by heavy rain, the local government said early Thursday.

About a million people were under evacuation orders on Thursday, more than 40 people were injured in the storm, and one person was missing, according to Japan’s public broadcaster, NHK. Almost 236,000 households were without power, service provider Kyushu Electric Power Transmission and Distribution said.

Japan Airlines, one of Japan’s largest airlines, canceled all flights to and from Nagasaki and seven other cities in Shanshan’s path on Thursday. All Nippon Airways canceled all flights that had been scheduled at Kansai International Airport for Friday.

Rail service was suspended on Japan’s high-speed Shinkansen network on Thursday for all of Kyushu, and on many of the train lines linking major cities in western Japan, including Osaka, Kyoto and Hiroshima.

On Wednesday, the authorities issued rare emergency warnings for the storm in Kagoshima Prefecture, indicating that a large-scale disaster was possible, according to the Japan Meteorological Agency. On Thursday morning, they downgraded the warnings.

Toyota announced that it would pause production at all 14 of its Japan factories starting Wednesday evening to protect its workers. On Thursday morning, the carmaker said that it would extend the suspension until Friday for all but one of the factories.
