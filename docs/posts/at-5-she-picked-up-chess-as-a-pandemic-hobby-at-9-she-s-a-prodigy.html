By Isabella Kwai

July 18, 2024Updated 1:32 p.m. ET

Since learning chess during a pandemic lockdown, Bodhana Sivanandan has won a European title in the game, qualified for this year’s prestigious Chess Olympiad tournament, and established herself as one of England’s best players.

She also turned 9 in March. That makes Bodhana, a prodigy from the London borough of Harrow, the youngest player to represent England at such an elite level in chess, and quite possibly the youngest in any international sporting competition.

“I was happy and I was ready to play,” Bodhana said in a phone interview, two days after she learned that she had been selected for this year’s Olympiad, an international competition considered to be the game’s version of the Olympics.

The fourth-grader, who learned chess four years ago when she stumbled across a board her father was planning to discard, knows exactly what she wants to accomplish next. “I’m trying to become the youngest grandmaster in the world,” she said, “and also one of the greatest players of all time.”

She has some three years to achieve her first goal. The youngest person to become grandmaster, the highest distinction other than world champion, is Abhimanyu Mishra, an American player who was 12 years, 4 months and 25 days when he became grandmaster in 2021.

International masters have called Bodhana a phenomenon since she burst onto the competitive chess scene that same year. She beat adults for the first women’s prize in the European Blitz Chess Championship last December, and is currently ranked as a woman FIDE master, a title given by the International Chess Federation.

That helped her land one of five spots on the English Olympiad team “purely on merit,” said Malcolm Pein, the director of international chess at the English Chess Federation. She is by far the youngest player on the squad; Lan Yao, the second youngest player on the team, is 23.

It was pure chance that led Bodhana to discover her talent. When she was 5, she found a board with a missing pawn that her father, Sivanandan Velayutham, had been planning to give away.

“I was interested in the pieces, and I wanted to use to them as toys,” she said. “I was mainly interested in the knight and the queen.”

Mr. Velayutham explained that it was a game. “I said, it’s not easy, but if you learn, you can play,” he recalled. It was the first year of the coronavirus pandemic, and Bodhana spent her time watching YouTube videos and playing chess online. Soon, she was beating her father and entering online tournaments.

While Bodhana’s talent may be extraordinary, her recent discovery of the game isn’t, Mr. Pein said. Chess in recent years has seen a boom, in particular among young people and women, driven by pandemic boredom and the Netflix hit “The Queen’s Gambit,” which follows a troubled prodigy navigating the male-dominated chess world.

After that, “a whole new generation of incredibly talented children appeared,” said Mr. Pein. The game offers extra appeal for younger children: “It’s very empowering to be able to beat an adult at something,” he said.

And Bodhana started doing exactly that, after Mr. Velayutham brought her to a London chess festival and her local chess club.

“It was supposed to end up in a charity shop,” said Mr. Velayutham in an interview, speaking of the chess board. “But it ended up in a good cause for our country!”

These days, Bodhana trains about an hour each day after school, with the help of international-master level mentors and coaches from the England Chess Federation. (She particularly enjoys studying the games of the late Cuban chess player José Raúl Capablanca, she said: “I like the endgame.”)

Mr. Pein called her a “positional player,” with a keen awareness of the pieces’ position relative to each other on the board. “She doesn’t rush headlong into sacrificial attacks,” he said, praising her “very cool, focused demeanor at the board and an incredible determination to win.”

One international master chess player, Lawrence Trent of England, expects the 9-year-old to be the country’s greatest player. “The maturity of her play, her sublime touch, it’s truly breathtaking,” he said on social media last year.

Bodhana likes how chess “activates your brain,” she said. Her advice on playing well? “You should just keep going, and you should stay calm.”

The excitement around Bodhana drew players to ChessFest in Trafalgar Square on a recent Sunday, where she played exhibition games, some simultaneously. She was one of several English chess prodigies present, among them Supratit Banerjee, 10; Shreyas Royal, 15; and Ethan Pang, 9.

It is not unusual to see children competing at high levels of chess. The youngest player to compete in the 2022 Chess Olympiad, according to the International Chess Federation, was Randa Seder of Hebron in the West Bank, who was 8 at the time. That year, Rameshbabu Praggnanandhaa, a 16-year-old prodigy from India, defeated Magnus Carlsen, the world champion, in an online tournament.

For now, Bodhana’s father wants her to enjoy the game without imposing expectations.

“At end of the day, she is a kid, right?” Mr. Velayutham said. “I don’t want to set the milestones. Let her set her own milestones.”
