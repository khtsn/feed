Andy Reid, Coach of Kansas City Chiefs, Makes Football Fun

Skip to contentSkip to site index

Andy Reid’s diligence and sense of mischief have made him one of the game’s best-ever coaches. Can he get his struggling Chiefs back to the Super Bowl?

Michael Sokolove

  • Jan. 11, 2024

Andy Reid, the coach of the Kansas City Chiefs, has won more than 250 games in his career, fourth all-time, which puts him high on any list of the N.F.L.’s greatest coaches. Most of the others in that pantheon are men who personify the sport’s militaristic soul — Vince Lombardi, for example, the fabled coach of the 1960s-era Green Bay Packers, or Reid’s contemporary, the grim Bill Belichick of the New England Patriots. But Reid is no Lombardi or Belichick; he’s Steve Jobs. He’s a designer, a tinkerer, a product engineer who imbues his football with creativity and even an occasional touch of whimsy.

To take his measure is like looking into a kaleidoscope. Adjust the view, and you get different images. He’s the jolliest fat man in America — renowned for his appetite and for being the wryly comic figure in TV ads for State Farm and Snickers — or the saddest. He’s a father figure to his players, and he’s the father of two sons who have faced serious criminal charges, one now dead from a heroin overdose and the other in prison after grievously injuring a little girl while driving drunk. He controls everything. And he controls nothing.

The constant is Reid’s football genius. Football is, of course, one of America’s great distractions, and Reid’s version — fast, inventive, wide-open, surprising — is the most distracting of all. It simultaneously separates you from your day-to-day concerns while making you forget about the sport’s pathologies. At one point in the Chiefs’ final game of the 2022-23 regular season, for example, all 11 players on offense gathered in a huddle, then locked arms and began circling rapidly before scurrying to the line of scrimmage and running a trick play. (Reid has said they are not trick plays if you practice them.) The center snapped the ball to a running back, who pitched it to the Chiefs’ magician of a quarterback, Patrick Mahomes, who then threw it across the field to a wide-open receiver. The next day’s sports coverage likened the presnap dance to a game of Ring Around the Rosie.

The N.F.L., at that moment, was in a state of shock. Days earlier, a player for the Buffalo Bills, Damar Hamlin, came within moments of dying after a tackle caused him to go into cardiac arrest. He was defibrillated back to life, and an ambulance sped him from the middle of the field to a hospital. After an uncomfortable hour of TV, the game was finally canceled. Whether Reid intended it or not, his crazy-looking play seemed to be a message: Football is still fun!

The following month, the Chiefs won their second Super Bowl under Reid for one big reason: He found ways to keep scoring even after Mahomes, who came into the game with an ankle injury, reinjured it in the second quarter. “Patrick was limited,” Tom Melvin, the Chiefs’ tight-ends coach, told me. “The conversation at halftime was: Let’s get the ball out of his hands quickly, because he’s not going to be able to move around normally.” Reid’s offenses feature a seemingly endless array of formations, presnap motions, plays and options off those plays. He comes into every game with a dozen or more new plays the opposing team has not seen. Robert Smith, a retired running back and now an analyst for Fox Sports, put it to me this way: “In every N.F.L. game, the offense is asking questions before the ball gets snapped, and the defense has to come up with answers. What are they about to do here based on what we’ve seen on film? What Andy does is put the defense in a position where they think they know the answer. And then he changes the damn question.”

Early in the fourth quarter of last year’s Super Bowl, the Chiefs trailed 27-21 and faced a crucial third down on the Philadelphia Eagles’ five-yard line. Before the snap, the receiver Kadarius Toney started jogging from the right side of the formation to the left. The Eagles would have seen this countless times before on film, with Toney either continuing out to the left as a receiver or, sometimes, taking the ball in a handoff from Mahomes in what’s called a jet sweep.

The cornerback who was originally lined up opposite Toney signaled for a teammate to cover the receiver. But just as the ball was snapped, Toney reversed direction, got his defender’s hips turned the wrong way and wound up cradling an easy touchdown pass from Mahomes. No defender was within 10 yards of him. Three minutes later, the Chiefs scored another touchdown on a mirror image of the same play.

Mahomes, still just 28, is on track to end up as one of the all-time-great N.F.L. quarterbacks, but Reid’s schemes ensured that his hobbled star didn’t need to do much on those plays. An average high-school quarterback could have made the throws. The Chiefs went on to win, 38-35.

“Andy has an unparalleled ability to scheme guys open,” says Ray Didinger, who as a sports columnist in Philadelphia and then a producer for N.F.L. Films has followed Reid for a quarter century. “He can do it with great receivers, like he’s had in the past, or he can ride with pretty good receivers, which is all he had last year. I look back on that second half, and I think it was the greatest 30 minutes of his career.”

The N.F.L. playoffs begin this weekend, and the Chiefs, having won their division for a remarkable eighth straight time, are again among the favorites. Going into the final regular-season game, Reid’s team had the fourth-best odds at one major sportsbook to win the Super Bowl — 14 of the 32 teams make the playoffs — and it didn’t look as if the Chiefs would have to face a dominant opponent before the A.F.C. title game. But by their high standards, they have had an up-and-down year, primarily because their receivers drop too many passes (they lead the league in that dubious category) and too often run the wrong routes. If Reid can get this group of not-so-great pass catchers to the Super Bowl, it would be his fourth trip in five years and might be an even greater achievement than that second half last year.

Every Super Bowl-winning season kicks off during the preceding summer. One Sunday last July, Reid rose before dawn on the college campus where his team was gathered to prepare for the season, a dorm at Missouri Western State University: Scanlon Hall, to be exact, a freshman residence with tiny rooms, shared bathrooms and walls made of cinder block. His players — the nearly-$60-million-a-year quarterback Patrick Mahomes, the star tight end (and Taylor Swift boyfriend) Travis Kelce and all the rest of their teammates — were bunked in the same dormitory, though surely still snoozing in their own 11-by-14-foot quarters. In a modest concession to comfort, the team had trucked in king-size beds from a local Rent-a-Center. Jawaan Taylor, a 330-pound offensive lineman, told me that he found the setup tolerable enough. “But you’ve got to make sure you put that mattress pad on.” Reid, whose quarters were on the first floor, does not care much about sleep and gets only about three hours a night, if that. “It’s not something I like to brag on,” he told me. The index cards he always keeps with him spent the nights within easy reach because he never knows when he’ll think of a new play and want to draw it up. I wondered if plays ever come to him in his dreams. “I don’t sleep enough to dream,” he said.

Practice that July morning began at 9:15. After the players stretched and went through some preliminary drills, Reid, who is 65, took his place about 10 yards behind where his offense was running plays against the team’s defense. Hands on hips, he was tilted forward in intense concentration. He recorded the result of every play on a sheet of paper. Whenever the offense advanced, he lumbered forward with the players, taking short steps while his arms and upper body seemed not to move at all. Reid is a large man. The stories about him often tend to be about his prodigious eating. While trying to land his first N.F.L. head-coaching job, in 1999 with the Eagles, the team’s owner, Jeffrey Lurie, took him to a steakhouse for dinner. When the server asked him if he preferred the rib-eye, the New York strip or the filet mignon, Reid ordered all three.

As Reid walked off the practice field that day — the session had been fast-paced and choreographed to the minute — his red Chiefs pullover was soaked in perspiration. But he seemed content and energized, though there were still hours of meetings, film sessions and planning for the next day’s practice that would stretch into the evening. Reid does not have hobbies — he doesn’t hunt or golf — and while he and his wife, Tammy, have a beach house in Southern California, he rarely goes to the beach. Most N.F.L. teams no longer travel for training, but the all-football atmosphere of the preseason camp suits him. “I do enjoy it,” he told me. “I say this a lot: I’ve been lucky.”

Reid grew up in Los Angeles, where his father worked as a scenic artist on film and TV sets. “He wasn’t really a football guy,” Reid says. “He was formally trained as an artist. He’d take me to work with him on weekends. If people want to say I have a creative side, that comes from him. I’m sure there’s something to that.”

He played on the offensive line at John Marshall High School and then at Brigham Young University in Utah, where he met his future wife. (He converted to her faith, the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, before their marriage; her father baptized him.) From there, he began a long climb up the coaching ranks, starting as an assistant coach at San Francisco State in the early 1980s. Until the Eagles hired him in 1999, Reid had never been a head coach at any level — or even an offensive or defensive coordinator in the N.F.L.

But Jeffrey Lurie and his general manager and childhood friend, Joe Banner, had determined that experience was overrated. What the best N.F.L. coaches have in common, Banner told me, is that they’re “so detail-oriented they even annoy everyone around them.” Reid aced the interview the moment he showed up with a thick notebook he had been keeping for years. It was filled with his meticulously logged plans for all the things he would do if he ever became an N.F.L. head coach.

The Eagles record the previous season was 3-13. Over the next 14 years under Reid, the team qualified for the playoffs nine times, won six division titles and advanced five times to the N.F.C. championship game. But there was trouble off the field. Eight years into his tenure, in 2007, two of his sons were arrested on the same day in separate incidents. Garrett Reid, then 23, ran a red light in suburban Philadelphia and smashed into another vehicle. The police found syringes with testosterone and heroin in his S.U.V. His brother Britt, two years younger, was arrested earlier that day after an altercation with another driver, at whom he pointed a handgun.

Both sons were living at home with their parents. (The Reids have three other children.) The judge at Britt’s sentencing described the home as a “drug emporium.” “There isn’t any structure there that this court can depend upon,” he said. “This is a family in crisis.”

Reid stepped away from his coaching duties for five weeks (it was the offseason). Five years later, in 2012, Garrett, out of prison and assisting the Eagles’ strength-and-conditioning coaches on his father’s staff, was found dead in his room from a heroin overdose at preseason camp. During a livestream for Latter-day Saints members in the Kansas City area in 2020, Reid and his wife talked about their grief and how their faith helped them through it. “Faith to me is the ultimate game plan,” he said. Tammy Reid said the timing of the funeral was set around the team’s schedule. “Hey, can we have the funeral in two days?” she recalled her husband asking her. “That was the day all the team had off, and he knew it wouldn’t be a distraction, which we didn’t want it to be.”

The season after his son’s death, the chaos in Reid’s family life seemed to find a reflection in his team’s fortunes. The 2012 Eagles won only four games, and at season’s end, Reid was fired. People close to him suggested that he take a year away from coaching. “I was one of them,” Banner told me. “I thought it would be good for him to step back, recharge and be available to his family.”

Reid landed at the Chiefs four days after the Eagles fired him. The coaching staff he hired included his son Britt, who after his time in prison had coached at a Philadelphia high school. Reid immediately led a Chiefs team that had won just two games the previous season into the playoffs. Over the next decade, they made it to three Super Bowls, winning two of them. The one they lost, in 2021, took place three days after the accident in which Britt, driving a pickup truck, slammed into a stopped vehicle by the side of a highway. Ariel Young, who was 5 years old, was in a coma for 11 days and suffered life-altering injuries, according to Tom Porto, the lawyer who represents her family.

A judge sentenced Britt, who had been drinking at the Chiefs’ training site and was legally intoxicated, to three years in prison, which Young’s family objected to as too lenient. “He was already a convicted felon — to my knowledge, the only convicted felon coaching in the N.F.L.,” Porto says. A third Reid son, Spencer, has since joined the team’s staff as a strength-and-conditioning coach. “That absolutely blew my mind,” Porto says.

Now in his third decade as an N.F.L. head coach, Reid remains focused on controlling his team and everything around it. At a practice, I was standing with about a dozen other journalists beyond the end zone. Reid was more than 100 yards away. A fence behind us offered a little shade, so I took a few steps back and leaned against it. “You have to move away from there,” someone from the team’s media staff came up to tell me. “Coach has a thing about leaning.” I laughed, thinking she was joking. “I’m serious,” she said. “He’ll see you.”

Part of Reid’s success is attributable to his bottomless capacity for football and his ability to shut out everything else. His assistant coaches talk about arriving for work as early as 5 a.m. and finding Reid already there, with the whiteboards in his office filled with diagrams transferred from his index cards.

“There are two types of philosophy on offense,” says Marty Mornhinweg, a close friend who began coaching alongside Reid when both were college assistants in their 20s. “One is that you do a very limited set of plays, but you run them better than anyone else. The other is that you do an awful lot, and you keep the defense off balance with different formations and tempos. Most coaches are somewhere in between. Andy is way over on the creative side.”

The downside of Reid’s approach, Mornhinweg told me, is that with so many plays, “you have less repetitions of each one in practice. You have to live with some mistakes.” In fact, on the Chiefs’ final touchdown in last year’s Super Bowl, Kelce lined up on the wrong side of the formation. Mahomes saw it, made a quick decision that it wouldn’t matter and ran the play as called. (Reid saw it, too, and unsuccessfully tried to call a timeout before the snap.)

Reid has had success with a great quarterback in Mahomes, a very good one in Donovan McNabb in Philadelphia and some others (A.J. Feeley, Jeff Garcia) whom many casual football fans might struggle to remember. It’s another contrast with Belichick, whose Patriots teams have nose-dived since Tom Brady departed four seasons ago.

Reid’s success with Michael Vick demonstrates something else: his gift for nurturing football relationships. In 2009, he signed Vick, who had been out of the N.F.L. for two years — a period that included 21 months in federal prison — after his arrest for his involvement in a dogfighting ring, the details of which were horrific. Playing quarterback for Reid’s Eagles, Vick resurrected his career and his life; he is now a football analyst for Fox Sports. “He knew I was a work in progress,” Vick told me of Reid, whom he referred to as Big Papa. “He gave me unconditional love.”

Sustained success is difficult in the N.F.L., because a salary cap prevents teams from keeping all their best players. The Chiefs traded the high-priced receiver Tyreek Hill after the 2021-22 season, and Reid is again this year trying to get to the Super Bowl without a top wide receiver. The closest approximation is Rashee Rice, a rookie who has been subject to a crash course in the intricacies of Reid’s system.

Typically, Reid talks about elevating his team’s offense in terms of relationships. After Rice caught a touchdown pass in a late-season victory over the Las Vegas Raiders, Reid noted that Mahomes had been working individually with the rookie between games and that they were spending time together off the field. N.F.L. quarterbacks have to make split-second decisions, with defenders menacing them, and Rice has had to learn to hit the precise markers where Mahomes wants to get him the ball. “He is working to be more Patrick-friendly,” Reid said after the game. As the playoffs begin this weekend, the Chiefs will need their receivers to start catching the balls that Mahomes delivers right to their hands. Reid’s genius and unyielding focus are factors that can be counted on.

I’m a Philadelphian originally and was pulling for the Eagles in last year’s Super Bowl, but I wasn’t rooting against Reid. I have always enjoyed the way his teams play and have admired his apparent decency and compassion. When he and I talked after a practice, I told him I was sorry my team lost but was happy for his success. “I appreciate that,” he said. That’s how he talks — in snippets. “He makes his point, and then he stops,” Mornhinweg says. “It could be 15 seconds. If he goes on for more than two minutes, something’s wrong.”

Discussing the Reid family troubles is off limits, around the team and beyond. In 2019, a Kansas City radio host criticized Reid as “not good at fixing people” after he signed a player with an arrest record. The comments were perceived as a reference to Reid’s troubled sons, and he was suspended from a show he had hosted for 22 years, before he then stepped away for good.

I knew I wanted to ask Reid how he manages to go on in the wake of family tragedies — and I knew he probably wouldn’t tell me much. How does he continue to concentrate on dreaming up his new plays? “It had to be difficult for you at times,” I began, “with some of the things your family has experienced.”

He seemed to anticipate my question and gave a sort of stock answer meant to move on from that line of inquiry. “I’ve got a good family,” he said. “If something happens to it, we’ve got to rally around that. If it’s a football matter, we’ve got to rally around that, too.” That was it. The team’s media director, who had been hovering, led him away. Reid’s answer didn’t go on for more than two minutes, so nothing was wrong.

Michael Sokolove, a contributing writer for the magazine, is the author of five books, including “Drama High: The Incredible True Story of a Brilliant Teacher, a Struggling Town and the Magic of Theater.”
