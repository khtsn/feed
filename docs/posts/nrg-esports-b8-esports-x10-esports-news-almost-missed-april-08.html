The Manhattan district attorney finds himself in the eye of the storm after an investigation by his office led to the indictment of former President Donald J. Trump.

April 13, 2023, 5:00 a.m. ET

The Manhattan district attorney, Alvin L. Bragg, last month became the first prosecutor to bring a criminal case against a current or former American president when a grand jury indicted Donald J. Trump.

Mr. Bragg, 49, a Democrat who had never held public office before last year, now finds himself on the national stage as Mr. Trump and his Republican allies lob attacks at the district attorney.

Congressional Republicans have directly scrutinized Mr. Bragg’s Trump investigation, which resulted in 34 felony charges connected to Mr. Trump’s role in paying hush money to a porn star. On Tuesday, Mr. Bragg sued Representative Jim Jordan of Ohio, the chairman of the House Judiciary Committee, in an effort to prevent congressional interference into the prosecution of Mr. Trump.

Mr. Bragg, who was elected in November 2021, is a former federal prosecutor and deputy New York attorney general. He was born and raised in Harlem and attended Harvard as an undergraduate and then Harvard Law School. He is the first Black person to lead the district attorney’s office.

Here’s what to know about Mr. Bragg’s background and career:

The district attorney has lived in Harlem for most of his life.

Mr. Bragg was born on Oct. 21, 1973, to Alvin Bragg Sr., a social services worker, and Sadie Bragg, a teacher. He attended Trinity, an elite private school in Manhattan, before going to Harvard.

At college, he became a leader among his fellow students, heading up the Black Students Association. A lengthy 1995 profile in The Harvard Crimson reported that though Mr. Bragg said then he was unlikely to run for political office, “there is a definite sense of the anointed about him.”

After returning to Harvard for law school, Mr. Bragg began considering a career as a prosecutor while working for the federal judge Robert Patterson Jr. and seeing how powerful the role could be in shaping the outcome of defendants’ lives. He worked for several years as a criminal defense and civil rights lawyer before being hired by the New York attorney general’s office, where he investigated public corruption and white-collar crime.

He then worked as a federal prosecutor in Manhattan under Preet Bharara, then U.S. attorney for the Southern District of New York, before returning to the state attorney general’s office, where he focused on police misconduct and rose to become one of the leaders of the office.

Mr. Bragg beat out eight other candidates to become the district attorney.

In June 2019, Mr. Bragg declared that he was running for Manhattan district attorney. “We’re all supposed to be equal under the law,” he said in his campaign announcement, lamenting that in Manhattan there were “too often two standards of justice, one for the rich and powerful and connected and another for everyone else.”

Many of Mr. Bragg’s priorities and proposed policies were aligned with those of progressive prosecutors who have remade district attorneys’ offices around the United States in recent years, pledging to stop prosecuting petty crime and improve public safety by investing in the health of local communities. But Mr. Bragg defied easy classification during the race, using his experience growing up in Harlem to explain the nuances of his policy positions.

The district attorney’s office, then led by Cyrus R. Vance Jr., had opened an investigation into Mr. Trump the previous summer after the then-president’s former fixer, Michael D. Cohen, told a court that Mr. Trump had directed him to arrange hush-money payments to two women. But the office soon temporarily halted the inquiry at the request of federal prosecutors, who were still looking into the same conduct. When Mr. Bragg entered the race, the inquiry was not active.

But as the Democratic primary for Manhattan district attorney — a more important contest than the general election in the deep-blue borough — heated up, so did Mr. Vance’s investigation, which had come to focus on Mr. Trump’s business practices. Shortly after Mr. Bragg prevailed in the primary over seven other candidates, the district attorney’s office indicted Mr. Trump’s company and its longtime chief financial officer, Allen H. Weisselberg, accusing both parties of having engaged in a yearslong scheme to evade taxes.

In November 2021, Mr. Bragg beat his Republican opponent in the general election handily, with more than 80 percent of the vote. Mr. Vance, who had not run for re-election, left office at the end of December.

The district attorney took office in January and had a difficult introduction to the national stage.

After being sworn in on Jan. 1, 2022, Mr. Bragg found himself in a political maelstrom almost immediately, when he released a memo in his first week that told prosecutors to ask judges for jail or prison time for only the most serious offenses — including murder, sexual assault and serious financial crimes — unless the law required otherwise. That policy, instituted as New York City was experiencing a sharp increase in murders and shootings, drew immediate and fierce backlash from conservative critics.

Mr. Bragg clarified it the following month, saying prosecutors had the discretion to make decisions about their own cases.

Weeks later, Mr. Bragg found himself in another controversy. Two prosecutors who had led the Trump investigation under Mr. Vance had been presenting evidence to a grand jury about the former president’s business practices. But Mr. Bragg was not confident in the case and halted the presentation. When the prosecutors resigned, Mr. Bragg was attacked by many on the left as well.

He continued the Trump investigation, and a grand jury voted to indict Mr. Trump in March.

Mr. Bragg vowed that the investigation into Mr. Trump was ongoing, and by summer, his prosecutors had returned to the hush-money payments, which Mr. Vance’s prosecutors had scrutinized but decided against building a case around. At the same time, Mr. Bragg’s team was pushing forward with the tax fraud prosecution of Mr. Trump’s company and Mr. Weisselberg, who pleaded guilty in August 2022 and agreed to testify at the company’s trial.

In the meantime, the district attorney was able to focus on some of his other priorities. He reorganized the ranks of his prosecutors to ensure that alternatives to incarceration were used where possible and began an initiative to assist defendants with housing and mental health. He also cracked down on gun violence — indicting 24 people said to be involved with the Own Every Dollar gang.

Mr. Bragg’s prosecutors won a conviction of the Trump Organization in December 2022, and the following month, they impaneled a new grand jury to hear evidence about the hush money. Witnesses included some of Mr. Trump’s employees and campaign aides, and the grand jury presentation culminated in Mr. Cohen’s testifying for more than two days in March.

The grand jury voted to indict Mr. Trump on March 30. The charges against the former president — 34 counts of falsifying business records in the first degree — were unveiled on April 4. Mr. Trump pleaded not guilty and has continued to denounce Mr. Bragg as being politically motivated.

After the arraignment, Mr. Bragg framed the prosecution of Mr. Trump using language that echoed his campaign announcement.

“Everyone stands equal under the law,” Mr. Bragg said. “No amount of money and no amount of power” changes that, he added.
