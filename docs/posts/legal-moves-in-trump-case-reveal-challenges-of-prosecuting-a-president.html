May 4, 2023Updated 12:44 p.m. ET

Donald J. Trump is seeking to move his criminal case from New York State Supreme Court to federal court, one of his lawyers said on Thursday.

One month after appearing in a Manhattan courtroom, where state prosecutors accused him of committing 34 felonies by falsifying documents to cover up a potential sex scandal, Mr. Trump will soon seek the legal change of scenery in a court filing. The former president’s legal team will submit the so-called notice of removal in federal court in Manhattan, where a judge will then consider the request.

The lawyer, Todd Blanche, disclosed the plan during an unrelated hearing on the criminal case in state court. The hearing concerned an effort by the Manhattan district attorney’s office, which is prosecuting Mr. Trump, to restrict the ways that the former president can access and speak about evidence from his case.

The effort to move the case to federal court, which is likely to be a long shot, will not have any immediate effect on the state case. The federal judge who considers the request could reject it on its face. But even if the judge finds the request worthy of consideration and orders a hearing, the state case will continue unhindered for now.

At the hearing on Thursday, the state court judge in the case, Juan M. Merchan, asked prosecutors and Mr. Trump’s lawyers to agree on a specific date for Mr. Trump’s trial in either February or March of next year. If that schedule becomes final, the trial will unfold in the thick of the presidential campaign, in which Mr. Trump is a leading contender.

The judge also signaled that he would restrict Mr. Trump from disseminating a narrow portion of the evidence in the criminal case, as proposed by the district attorney, Alvin L. Bragg — a significant victory for prosecutors.

Mr. Bragg will undoubtedly oppose the former president’s effort to remove the criminal case to federal court. And such requests for a change of venue are rarely granted in criminal cases.

A spokeswoman for the district attorney’s office did not immediately respond to a request for comment.

Mr. Trump’s effort hinges on a little-utilized provision in federal law that allows for defendants, within 30 days of their arraignment in state court, to submit “a short and plain statement of the grounds” for having their case moved to federal court.

The provision requires a federal judge to “examine the notice promptly,” and deny it “if it clearly appears on the face” that the change in venue is not warranted. If the judge does not promptly deny it, there will be a hearing on the evidence before the judge makes a final decision.

Mr. Trump might have little to lose in seeking a fresh start in federal court. His case is currently before Justice Merchan, who last year presided over the Trump Organization’s unrelated tax fraud trial, in which the company was found guilty by a jury. And the judge, a former prosecutor, is known to impose tough sentences in white- collar cases.

The case against Mr. Trump involves the payment of $130,000 in hush money to a porn star during the 2016 campaign. His former fixer, Michael D. Cohen, made the payment in the weeks leading up to Election Day. Mr. Trump reimbursed him after winning the presidency.

The reimbursements came in the form of 11 checks, nine of which were personally signed by Mr. Trump. The checks were then processed by Mr. Trump’s company, the Trump Organization, and the payments, prosecutors say, were falsely recorded in the company’s records as having been made for legal services.
