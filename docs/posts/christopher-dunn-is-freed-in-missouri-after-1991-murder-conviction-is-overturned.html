July 31, 2024Updated 3:07 p.m. ET

A Missouri man was freed in St. Louis on Tuesday after spending 34 years behind bars for a murder conviction that has now been overturned.

A judge ordered last week that the man, Christopher Dunn, 52, be released immediately, but the Missouri attorney general appealed the decision, delaying Mr. Dunn’s exit as a series of legal maneuvers played out.

Speaking on the steps of Carnahan Courthouse in St. Louis on Tuesday after being released, Mr. Dunn told reporters that it was “time to move forward.”

“For those who participated in my prosecution, I forgive you,” said Mr. Dunn, who had been serving a sentence of life without parole. “I’m not going to forget, but I do forgive you. But at the same time, I’ve got to move on with my life too.”

Mr. Dunn was convicted in 1991 in the murder of Ricco Rogers, 15, who was fatally shot in May 1990 while running away from a shooter who had fired at a group he was in as they sat on the porch of a St. Louis home around midnight. Mr. Dunn, who was 19 when he was convicted, was found guilty of first-degree murder, first-degree assault and armed criminal action.

The conviction was based on the testimony of two eyewitnesses, boys who were 12 years old and 14 years old at the time. Both eyewitnesses have since recanted their statements.

In recent years, attempts to clear Mr. Dunn’s name have ramped up.

Judge William Hickle of the 25th Judicial Circuit Court of Missouri said in 2020 that he did not believe a jury would convict Mr. Dunn based on information presented to the court after the trial. But the judge would not release Mr. Dunn because under Missouri law, defendants can’t make a claim of innocence to get out of prison unless they have been sentenced to death.

In 2021, a law took effect in Missouri that allowed prosecutors to ask a court to set aside a conviction. Kimberly Gardner, the St. Louis circuit attorney, began the process to vacate Mr. Dunn’s conviction in May 2023.

Ms. Gardner left office weeks later and her successor, Gabriel E. Gore, took up Mr. Dunn’s case.

In February, Mr. Gore filed a new motion to vacate Mr. Dunn’s conviction, which was granted by Judge Jason M. Sengheiser of St. Louis Circuit Court on July 22. The judge ordered that the state of Missouri immediately release Mr. Dunn.

Mr. Dunn, his wife, Kira Dunn, and his lawyers and supporters prepared for his imminent freedom, but Missouri’s attorney general, Andrew Bailey, fought the judge’s order.

On July 24, hours before Mr. Dunn’s expected release, Mr. Bailey filed an emergency stay on the judge’s order, which the Missouri Supreme Court granted, keeping Mr. Dunn imprisoned.

On Tuesday, the state Supreme Court lifted the emergency stay. Mr. Gore then dismissed the charges against Mr. Dunn, a legal formality that allowed Mr. Dunn to walk free.

On the steps of Carnahan Courthouse, Mr. Dunn said that the last week had been “testing.”

“To hear the decision of the judge and then get prepared to leave on Wednesday only to be brought back into the prison, it was torture,” Mr. Dunn said.

Mr. Dunn was represented by the Midwest Innocence Project, a nonprofit group that works to reverse wrongful convictions, and lawyers at the law firm Bryan Cave Leighton Paisner.

Tricia Rojo Bushnell, a lawyer with the Midwest Innocence Project, said that the team was happy and “working on all the next steps to support Chris as he’s rebuilding his life.”

Ms. Bushnell said that the last week had been intense.

“There were definitely a lot of emotions,” she said. “But a lot of just focusing on what we need to do to get him home because it was impossible to imagine that we have a system of justice that would leave him there.”
