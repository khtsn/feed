blameF: "I played many playoffs on stages before, and nothing felt like this"

We caught up with BIG's in-game leader, Benjamin "⁠blameF⁠" Bremer, after the HLC Belgrade PRO final, in which he and his teammates came out champions and denied FaZe a spot at the Major after securing their own by advancing through the tournament's semi-finals.

"We didn't do it perfectly, but we could look each other in the eyes afterwards and be happy, and that's the most important thing," the Dane says about the team's run in Belgrade, while also sharing how happy he is with the organization and the team.

blameF, congratulations on making the Major — it’s not something BIG have been taking for granted in the past few years. How do you reflect on this success after just two months with the team?

It's been an amazing time with BIG. I'm really happy here; it's a place where I feel very comfortable, and they really back my vision of Counter-Strike, my philosophy, and how I want to do things both on and off the server. I just feel like I have complete support from both the organization and the players, and it feels amazing.

I'm really happy it also paid off, because in the end it was also a close call — we could have lost earlier and not qualified. But in the end, we not only qualified, but we also ended up beating FaZe in the final. Just an amazing feeling. I don't want to get ahead of ourselves, but I think this team has a bright future. But we know our future isn't determined by our results right now; hopefully, it's something long-term.

It was a long day, with a lot of pressure. How did you guys handle it?

We just focused on the things we talk about every day. We talk a lot about how to handle pressure, what we can rely on when the pressure is high. We talk about how we treat each other unconditionally, no matter if it's a high-stakes game or a low-stakes game. So I think we just tried to follow these things, and in the end, it turned out great for us.

But I'm also not delusional; I also understand many of those games were close, and we could have lost. Even the first one versus ex-Zero Tenacity, I think, was a very close one.

The pressure was very high. We knew we were one or two games away from qualifying for the Major, so the pressure was super high. I played many playoffs on stages before, and nothing felt like this, when you know you're one or two games away from qualifying for the Major. Insane pressure. But as a team, we went through it. We didn't do it perfectly, but we could look each other in the eyes afterwards and be happy. And that's the most important thing.

it was so important to give the organization this win because I feel like they really deserve it

In the grand final, you were already qualified for the Major, but for FaZe it was a do-or-die. A game with vastly different stakes — how did you reflect on it? "No mercy"?

No, I told the team when we went outside before the game that it's very important for me that we try to win this for us, not because we want to not have FaZe at the Major. Because for us, FaZe are legends of the game, all these players are great players, insanely good players, we have a lot of respect for them. So we wanted to win because we wanted to put ourselves in a better position. We didn't want to scream at them in between rounds about, "Oh, you're not coming to the Major," or something like this. This was very important that this is how we're going to behave in this game. We play for our own sake. If we win, we win; if we lose, we lose — and that's it.

But of course, we also realized that it was a less-pressure game for us, and I think it showed a little on Dust. We came in very sloppily into this BO3, maybe because we were already qualified. But then, after map one, we said, 'We still need to try to win, we still need to do our best". And it went better on the next two.

This Major is taking place in Cologne — did the team feel it was extra important to get there, with Germany being BIG's home soil?

For sure. For me, it was so important to give the organization this win because I feel like they really deserve it. They take good care of the players, and they listen to the players, and it's a very good place to be. I'm so happy I could help them get here, because it means a lot to them. All the German fans will be there. I'm so happy for them, and also, of course, I'm happy for myself.

But I also understand that if we hadn't qualified for this Major, we'd probably have had a better shot at qualifying for the next one, because it takes time to climb Valve's ranking. You need to win multiple LANs and stuff. So we're very fortunate we made it so early; we weren't expecting this at the beginning of the year. But of course, now it's amazing.

After your stint with fnatic, how did you come to BIG, and specifically into the IGL role?

Pretty simple. My contract with fnatic was running out in less than half a year. A couple of different teams talked to me, and I tried to figure out where I could be happiest and have the most success in my next step. And in the end, I chose BIG because they said a lot of things I really valued, they had a lot of trust in me, and they gave me a lot of — I'm not sure it's the right word — free range to implement my ideas. And I think I learned a lot from my past time at different teams about how I want to do stuff, and I feel like I could actually do it in this team.

My calling was always very structured, and I feel like fear taught me the counterpart to that: sometimes having more of an X-factor

Was having a bigger role in deciding how the team plays something you were specifically looking for at this stage of your career?

Yeah, I just want to play with like-minded people. And I feel like the people in BIG are very like-minded to me. The way they play Counter-Strike is very similar to how I want to play, and the way they treat each other is exactly how I want to be treated and how I want to treat other people. It's just a perfect fit, honestly. And also, the organization is very nice, I can't say anything bad. They take good care of me, ask me 200 questions just to make sure I'm 100% comfortable. They're just very nice.

You said you liked working with BIG, but is your firsthand experience as an IGL different now versus before?

Yeah, for sure. I learned a lot in fnatic from fear, actually. In the sense that my calling was always very structured, and I feel like fear taught me the counterpart to that: sometimes having more of an X-factor and stuff. I really appreciated having fear as my IGL, because I think I needed that counterpart to my IGLing, and I think I learned a lot from that.

And then, having been on so many different teams, I've now seen what I think works and what doesn't. Also, outside the server too, I've been very harsh about implementing certain things into BIG, because some teams probably aren't doing it 100%, in my opinion.

What's your reflection on playing with gr1ks? Does it add confidence to this project that BIG committed to another non-German player specifically to address the need for a strong AWPer?

I think he's really good. For me, BIG right now is the perfect lineup. We have an anchor, a support player, an entry fragger, and a star AWPer who has a lot of space. We have me, who can be in the middle and also have a lot of space sometimes, but also sometimes take a step back and focus on calling because we have a lot of firepower. For me, we have such a good balance of who does what — who's aggressive, who's passive, who's supporting, who's entering. For me, it's perfect.

We never have any discussions in the team about who should be doing what. I've been on so many teams before where it's a constant discussion about roles, and then you bring in players, and then you have to switch roles — and that's not healthy in my opinion. It's way better now. For me, it's perfect roles, and we don't have those problems that I think some other teams have about who should be playing this role or whatever.
