Javier C. Hernández

Javier C. Hernández joined scores of journalists from around the world for opening night at Teatro alla Scala in Milan.

  • Dec. 13, 2024Updated 6:40 a.m. ET

Television reporters stood shoulder to shoulder delivering breathless, minute-by-minute commentary, part of a pack of more than 120 journalists from 10 countries.

Celebrities, politicians and titans of industry walked the red carpet past paparazzi and officers standing sentry with capes, sashes, swords and plumed hats.

Outside, protesters used firecrackers, smoke bombs and even manure as they sought to seize on the occasion to draw attention to a variety of causes.

It was not a global summit, a Hollywood premiere or a royal procession. It was the start of the new opera season at Teatro alla Scala in Milan.

Opera may be starved for attention in much of the world. But at La Scala, the storied theater that gave world premieres of works by Donizetti, Puccini, Rossini and Verdi, opera can still feel like the center of the cultural universe. It remains a matter of national pride and patrimony, a political football and an obsession for devoted fans.

“This is sacred for us,” said the critic Alberto Mattioli, who writes for La Stampa, an Italian newspaper. “Opera is our religion.”

The opening — a glittering affair known as Prima della Scala — is held each year on Dec. 7, the feast of St. Ambrose, Milan’s patron saint. This year’s new production of Verdi’s “La Forza del Destino,” starring the soprano Anna Netrebko and conducted by Riccardo Chailly, drew its share of pageantry and pomp.

Here’s a look at the spectacle, the politics and the superstition.

‘The Oscars of Milan’

Standing to one side of the red carpet, the writer Mattia Boffi, dressed in a bejeweled white shirt and a matching cape, assessed the looks of arriving celebrities: politicians, leaders of finance and design, media moguls, television personalities and actors, dressed in Giorgio Armani suits and Alexander McQueen dresses.

“This is a very special night,” said Boffi, who writes for Vogue Italia. “This is the Oscars of Milan.”

Canio Romaniello, who has been photographing La Scala’s opening since 1988, moved acrobatically around the small, packed lobby with his Canon camera.

“Ragazzi!” he shouted, working to get the attention of celebrities and instructing them to move closer. He said that the secret of getting a good photograph at La Scala was “knowing who you have in front of you” — being able to quickly identify people on the red carpet. “There’s no time to waste,” he said.

From the Royal Box to the Upper Balconies

Some of Italy’s most prominent officials — including Ignazio La Russa, president of the Senate, and Liliana Segre, a member of the Senate who is a Holocaust survivor — sat in a royal box decorated with an arrangement of hydrangeas and carnations inspired by the neoclassical painter Andrea Appiani’s “The Parnassus.”

But some of the most feared audience members were a few levels up in the less expensive upper balconies: the loggionisti, the demanding die-hard opera fans whose jeers, whistles and boos have terrorized singers over the years.

They greeted “Forza,” being performed on opening night for the first time in nearly six decades, as a special occasion. Many said there was nothing quite like Verdi, who has deep ties to the opera house: his first opera, “Oberto,” had its premiere there in 1839, and his last opera, “Falstaff,” premiered there in 1893.

“Verdi is the best we could expect,” said Davide Cafiero, a pianist who lives in Milan. “He is one of us.”

Rodolfo Rocchi, who watched “Forza” from the uppermost balcony, has been a regular at La Scala since 1954. The highlight of his opera-going life came the following year, when he heard Maria Callas perform in Bellini’s “La Sonnambula.” He was so moved, he claimed, that a fellow audience member had to hold onto the tails of his jacket so he would not fall over.

Rocchi lamented that opera had become less a part of mainstream culture in Italy in recent years. But he said there was nothing like La Scala.

“It’s my house,” he said. “This is where I feel alive.”

War, Politics and Protests

It was a rainy day in Milan. But that did not stop the traditional opening night protesters.

A few blocks from La Scala, pro-Palestinian demonstrators denounced the war in Gaza and criticized Italian politicians who supported Israel. Some threw firecrackers and smoke bombs. Others shoveled manure onto a piece of red carpet they had brought along, placing photographs of Italian officials and Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu of Israel on top.

At a separate protest, demonstrators criticized La Scala for inviting Netrebko, a citizen of Russia and Austria, to sing in “Forza.” In the years before Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, she had expressed support for Putin; she has since been banned by some opera companies, including the Metropolitan Opera in New York.

The protesters held Ukrainian flags and signs reading, “La Scala! Sing for justice, not for Netrebko’s complicity.”

Maria Mikaelyan, who helped organize the protest, said she hoped to be heard by the Italian authorities and public. She praised Netrebko’s singing: “She is the best soprano in the world — nobody can doubt that.” But she said Netrebko should acknowledge “in front of the world that is watching from the La Scala stage” that she made a mistake by supporting Putin.

“I hope she’d find some courage to do that as soon as possible,” Mikaelyan said.

Dominique Meyer, the chief executive and artistic director of La Scala, later defended Netrebko after there were scattered boos during the curtain call.

“I don’t appreciate a show being held hostage like this,” he said. “There isn’t a Netrebko in every generation. If we are lucky enough to have her here in the theater, we must be warm and applaud her.”

Meyer, a respected French impresario who previously led the Vienna State Opera, will depart La Scala in February; Italy’s current right-wing national government has favored Italians for major cultural posts.

Global politics were not the only matters protested. Before the start of “Forza,” a shout erupted from somewhere in the auditorium: “Save Sant’Agata,” a reference to the stalled efforts to reopen the museum in the villa where Verdi lived, farmed and wrote some of his greatest works, which has been closed to the public.

A Cursed Opera?

“Forza” is considered by some to be unlucky — inspiring the kind of superstitions in parts of the opera world that Shakespeare’s “Macbeth” does in the theater world. The baritone Leonard Warren died at a performance of the opera at the Met in 1960, and misfortunes have accompanied other stagings over the years.

At La Scala, audience members were abuzz about the opera’s cursed history. Some said they avoided even uttering its name, fearing bad luck.

Dvora Ancona, a cosmetic surgeon in Milan, was not taking any chances. She showed up in a black outfit covered in small mirrors, which she described as an “anti-negativity dress.” (“The pure soul,” she explained, “must be protected by a special shield, represented by mirrors, in case of curses.”)

Ancona said she was already noticing the opera’s bad vibes, pointing to the downpour outside and the fact that her son had a problem in London and had to cancel his trip to La Scala.

“Destiny is a disaster,” she said.

Carnations and Wall-to-Wall Coverage

The night ended with carnations falling from the upper balconies, where the loggionisti sit.

And it drew reams of coverage in the days that followed.

Commentators examined fashion on the red carpet (“Velvet dresses triumphed,” declared the magazine IO Donna). They analyzed the seating arrangement in the royal box (“For the first time, women were at the center,” reported the broadcaster Rai News). And they asked what lessons could be learned from “Forza,” which is set in war-torn Spain and Italy (“Inside the theater, a hymn to peace rises loud and clear,” wrote the news agency Adnkronos).

The opening made front page-news the next day, and major Italian newspapers devoted several pages each to coverage of La Scala. One assigned scores to each performer (an 8.5 out of 10 for Netrebko; an 8 for Chailly). There were stories about high-profile guests, including the Italian high jumper Gianmarco Tamberi, the dancer Roberto Bolle and the actor Pierfrancesco Favino.

And, as always, the media timed the ovations. “The Colossal ‘Forza’ Triumphs,” said a headline in the newspaper Corriere della Sera. “12 Minutes of Applause.”

In the end, the curse of “Forza” did not materialize. Mattioli, the critic for La Stampa, wrote that the evening “flew by without a hitch and with much applause.”

“It was such a clear success,” he added, “that we can even dare to write the title: ‘La Forza del Destino.’”
