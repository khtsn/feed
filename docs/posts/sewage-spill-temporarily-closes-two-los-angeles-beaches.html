Sewage Spill Temporarily Closes Two Los Angeles Beaches

U.S.|Sewage Spill Temporarily Closes Two Los Angeles Beaches

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/07/23/us/los-angeles-sewage-spill-beaches-venice.html

An estimated 15,000 gallons of sewage spilled into a creek this weekend, closing Venice and Dockweiler State Beaches, officials said. But on Tuesday morning, locals stuck to their beach routines.

July 23, 2024Updated 3:49 p.m. ET

Two Los Angeles-area beaches were shut down for 48 hours after thousands of gallons of sewage spilled into a creek — but that did not seem to bother the crowd of dog walkers, runners, yogis and surfers enjoying the beach early on Tuesday before the day’s heat set in.

An estimated 15,000 gallons of sewage — about the volume a backyard swimming pool holds — were discharged on Saturday afternoon in a neighborhood roughly seven miles east of the Santa Monica Pier, entering Ballona Creek, the Los Angeles County Department of Public Health said in a news release.

“The cause of the sewage discharge was a broken water main that pushed sand into the sewer, causing the blockage,” the agency said.

Parts of Dockweiler State Beach and Venice Beach remained closed, halting any swimming, surfing, and playing in ocean waters, according to red signs dotted across the beach.

But for locals, it was mostly business as usual, even if the water — at a balmy 70 or so degrees Fahrenheit — was a bit less appealing, and parts of the beach seemed to be quieter than usual.

“Personally when I see signs, I don’t go in. It’s common sense,” said Bernard Lizon, a resident of Venice Beach who was walking his dog Poppy in the neighborhood on Tuesday morning. But he said he had seen people in the water over the past few days. “No one can enforce it,” he said about the rules.

The Department of Public Health did not respond to a request for comment on Tuesday morning, including details on testing and cleanup practices, or when the signs might be removed.

In its news release, the Department of Public Health said that beaches would remain closed until two water quality tests indicated that bacterial levels met health standards.

“Water contact may cause someone to become ill,” according to water quality advisories posted online. “When a beach is closed, the Department of Public Health advises beach users to avoid all contact with ocean water in the closure area and where closure signs are posted,” the advisories said.

“We didn’t notice,” said Matt McCarthy, who used to live in the area but now spends most of his time in Yucca Valley, Calif. But Mr. McCarthy said he was skeptical that people would really listen to closure signs anyway if the right waves showed up.

“You can have nuclear waste — that’s not going to stop the die-hard surfers,” he said. Mr. McCarthy suggested that the absence of surfers on Tuesday morning could probably be explained by the calm waters more than anything else.

Mr. McCarthy’s partner, Lulu Stewart, was not deterred by the signs and took a dip in the water.

“I kept my mouth closed the entire time,” she said. “I’m trying to not think about it. I just love the ocean. I’ll take a shower when I get home.”

Others had not heard about the sewage spill at all. While walking along the ocean, as is his morning ritual, Scott Nisbet said he hadn’t been aware nor seen the signs. But, he said, “it seems to be happening more and more.”

Sewage spills that upend beach plans in Los Angeles are not uncommon. More than 14,000 gallons of sewage spilled into Ballona Creek in May, also forcing officials to temporarily close beaches.

There have been other, much larger, sewage spills in Los Angeles, including one in late 2021 when about 8.5 million gallons of untreated sewage spilled into a flood-control waterway. That spill, which was caused by a collapsed concrete pipe, forced at least five beaches to temporarily close.

Adi Machness, who lives in nearby Marina del Rey and comes to Venice Beach several days a week to read by the ocean, said he hoped the water would be clean again soon.

Mr. Machness said he used to swim in the water often, even though people had long told him the ocean at Venice Beach was dirty, even without a sewage spill. But he wasn’t going to break the rules.

“I’m just not going to risk anything,” he said. “It’s gross. That’s all it is.”
