Why a Black-and-White Feminist Film Was Bigger Than ‘Barbie’ in Italy

Guest Essay

Aug. 4, 2024, 1:00 a.m. ET

Veronica Raimo

Ms. Raimo is the author, most recently, of “Lost on Me,” a novel. She wrote from Rome.

Last summer the hot-pink feminism of “Barbie” took more than $1 billion at box offices worldwide. In Italy, however, “Barbie” was beaten by another film, a feminist dramedy with a clear nod to Italian neorealism set in postwar Rome.

“C’è ancora domani,” or “There’s Still Tomorrow,” was a hit at the box office and with Italian critics, who almost unanimously hailed it as a revelation. In May, at the David di Donatello Awards, which are akin to the Italian Oscars, it won six prizes from 19 nominations.

The film presents a monolithic patriarchy which the women bear stoically and symbolically counter, at the end — spoiler alert — by voting. So how did a 1940s black-and-white feminist parable become such a phenomenon? At least part of the answer lies in the fact that “There’s Still Tomorrow” is a safe film set in a very binary world.

The plot follows Delia, a woman devoted to her family and dominated by her violent, sexist husband, Ivano, himself the son of a violent, sexist father. Delia and Ivano have three children, two little boys and an older daughter, Marcella, whom the parents hope will “marry well” — that is, marry a rich guy, a woman’s only chance to escape poverty.

Delia is relentlessly abused by her husband, verbally and physically, but bears it stoically for the sake of her children and because she seems to have no choice. In the end, a choice does emerge, and Delia goes behind Ivano’s back to vote. Delia and her daughter exchange a knowing glance across the street, signaling their hope for a better future, at least for Marcella.

Part of the film’s success is because of the popularity of Paola Cortellesi, who co-wrote, directed and stars, and who is a beloved public figure in Italy (much as Roberto Benigni was when he co-wrote, directed and starred in “Life Is Beautiful”). But another major reason the film was so successful is that it’s the story of a feminism that everyone can agree on and enjoy. Ms. Cortellesi has described the film as “a contemporary movie set in the past,” but shifting the struggle of feminism into the past strips it of its political essence and allows it to be viewed in terms of battles won without having to confront those still to be fought, or those within feminism itself.

In recent years, perhaps partly thanks to the success of Elena Ferrante’s quartet of Neapolitan novels, Italy has leaned into stories that center parables of female emancipation set in the past. Best-selling novels, such as Francesca Giannone’s “The Letter Carrier,” Beatrice Salvioni’s “The Wicked One” and Viola Ardone’s “Oliva Denaro,” feature women fighting against male-dominated structures in periods ranging from the 1930s to the 1960s.

It’s comforting and comfortable to shift the conversation about feminism to a past era and idealize characters like Delia. Even the men watching “There’s Still Tomorrow” can favorably compare themselves to Ivano and his father, who are parodic, one-dimensional incarnations of the patriarchy.

And the film’s conclusion presents an easy solution to the whole problem: the vote. But what does it mean to vote against the patriarchy if the state itself can embody it?

While Italy has been avidly consuming tales of the early-20th-century successes of feminism, it has also witnessed the rise of the far right, and has been governed since 2022 by a right-wing coalition led by Giorgia Meloni, a female prime minister who proudly uses the masculine term “il presidente” and is famous for her motto “I’m Giorgia, I’m a woman, I’m a mother, I’m a Christian.”

Ms. Meloni, together with her minister for family, birthrate and equal opportunities, Eugenia Roccella, have promoted policies that support so-called traditional families and have tabled legislation that allows anti-abortion activists to be present in family planning clinics. (But “There’s Still Tomorrow” was so uncontroversial in Italy that Ms. Meloni has praised it.)

On Nov. 25, only about a month after the release of the film, rallies marking the International Day for Elimination of Violence Against Women were unusually large throughout Italy. The violent murder of the 22-year-old student Giulia Cecchettin by her ex-boyfriend about two weeks earlier was gripping the nation. That was not the year’s first femicide in Italy (more than 100 women were killed last year, around half by partners or exes), but the killing of Ms. Cecchettin led to a shift in the victim narrative thanks to Elena Cecchettin, Giulia’s sister.

In a letter in the newspaper Corriere della Sera, Elena Cecchettin blamed her sister’s murder on the state, “because the state does not protect us.” She urged her readers, rather than observing “a minute of silence,” to “burn everything,” a reference to a poem by the Peruvian feminist and activist Cristina Torres-Cáceres. She urged men to be partners in dismantling the status quo: “Say something to that friend who controls his girlfriend, say something to that colleague who catcalls passers-by,” she wrote. “These behaviors are accepted by society, and can be the prelude to femicide.”

In her speeches and interviews in front of the cameras we saw not a teary relative of a victim talking about her personal pain but a young activist making a political speech and pointing the finger at the state. Here was a woman expressing anger and calling for a revolt. Later that month the Italian senate unanimously approved new legislation that mandated stricter restraining orders and increased surveillance on men found guilty of gender-based violence.

“There’s Still Tomorrow” touched a nerve in Italy, and the protests after the murder of Giulia Cecchettin took place in a climate of renewed awareness. But Delia does not express anger or call for revolt; she’s always there to help with a smile despite her abuse. This is her strength, and yet I’m convinced it’s also her limitation. When we understand female heroism as resilience — the capacity to endure — rather than the willingness to get angry, we serve a narrative that ultimately makes change harder.

Veronica Raimo is the author, most recently, of “Lost on Me,” a novel. This essay was translated from the Italian by Geoffrey Brock for The New York Times.
