Glenn Thrush

Reporting from Washington

July 31, 2024Updated 1:34 p.m. ET

A Justice Department watchdog criticized Attorney General William P. Barr for a “chaotic and disorganized” response to the demonstrations spurred by George Floyd’s murder in 2020 — but found that he did not order the forced removal of protesters from a park near the White House.

The four-year investigation covers one of the most polarizing moments of Donald J. Trump’s presidency: his decision on June 1, 2020, to walk from the Rose Garden through Lafayette Park, which federal law enforcement officers had violently cleared of protesters, for a Bible-brandishing photo opportunity at a nearby church.

Video taken that day showed Mr. Barr, known for his aggressive, hands-on management style, conferring with officials in the park just before Mr. Trump strode through with a small entourage, including Mr. Barr, to pose in front of damage inflicted on St. John’s Church by demonstrators.

But Michael E. Horowitz, the Justice Department’s inspector general, found that Mr. Barr was, at that moment, not in the chain of command at the scene, according to a report released on Wednesday.

The clearing of the park by baton-wielding officers, some on horseback — with a handful of Justice Department employees providing support — was directed by the Park Police and the Secret Service, not the Justice Department, the report added.

“We found that the decision to clear the area of protesters on the evening of June 1 was made by the U.S.P.P. and U.S.S.S. unified command at Lafayette Park and not by Barr, and that Barr did not impact the timing of the clearing operation,” Mr. Horowitz wrote in an exhaustive 168-page report that included more than 100 interviews.

Mr. Barr and other top department officials declined to be interviewed for the report, the third to be released over the last three weeks concerning the Justice Department under Mr. Barr’s tenure.

“We determined that the unified command had already decided to initiate the clearing operation and had begun preparations to do so prior to Barr’s arrival,” Mr. Horowitz added.

Still, the inspector general found that the attorney general, at Mr. Trump’s urging, seemed eager to deploy the department’s uniformed work force to prove he was willing to stand up to left-wing protesters he considered a threat to civil order.

But Mr. Barr’s most important actions were arguably taking place out of public view. He was working behind the scenes to prevent Mr. Trump from deploying active-duty soldiers to the streets of the city, and the report suggests that Mr. Barr’s aggressive posture might have been motivated by his effort to keep Mr. Trump from taking more drastic steps.

Mr. Barr was delegated by Mr. Trump to oversee the federal response to the demonstrations, which were mostly peaceful, just before Lafayette Park was cleared out. But he would take over that role after the events of June 1, mostly to order the deployment of tactical teams and to oversee the eventual expansion of the security cordon around the White House by a block.

During the investigation, Mr. Horowitz and his team reviewed Mr. Barr’s overall response to the unrest in the District of Columbia from the end of May, when Mr. Floyd was killed by a police officer in Minneapolis, to the beginning of June, when protests peaked.

Mr. Horowitz said he shared “the serious concerns” relayed by law enforcement officials about the department’s tendency to deploy officers from its divisions, including the Bureau of Prisons and the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives, without proper planning or procedural guardrails.

“We found the department’s efforts at times to be chaotic and disorganized,” he wrote. “And that Barr, in an attempt to demonstrate that law enforcement could handle the civil unrest without active-duty military intervention, pressed D.O.J. law enforcement components to deploy personnel without sufficient attention to whether those personnel were properly trained or equipped for their mission.”

Late on June 1, Mr. Barr’s chief of staff deployed prison bureau officers with no guidance on their mission or clear rules of engagement. That deployment, and the subsequent use of F.B.I. personnel to patrol the area around the park, was also made without an assessment of their legal authorities — and posed “safety and security risks for the agents and the public,” the investigation concluded.

Investigators also faulted a top aide to Mr. Barr, his press secretary, Kerri Kupec, for giving the impression that he had played a more important role than he actually had.

Ms. Kupec, who now works as an editor at Fox News, told reporters on June 2 that Mr. Barr had ordered law enforcement officials in the park to “get it done” the day before.

“Witnesses told us they did not hear Barr make such a statement, and we therefore concluded that Kupec’s statement gave the inaccurate impression Barr had personally ordered the operation and led to significant public confusion regarding D.O.J.’s role in it,” Mr. Horowitz wrote.

Mr. Barr and Ms. Kupec did not respond to requests for comment.

Top Justice Department aides interviewed by investigators recalled the uncertainty, bordering at times on panic, that engulfed law enforcement officials as they watched intensifying protests around the White House, and a president running on a law-and-order platform.

Advisers to Mr. Barr described him as under intense personal stress, as he tried to balance his need to keep Mr. Trump from going too far with his personal revulsion at the civil unrest. In particular, he appeared dismayed by the vandalism of St. John’s, which turned out to have only superficially damaged the small yellow building that has played host to presidents since the birth of the Republic.

“I distinctly remember being in the Justice Command Center when the church was on fire and the news is showing that and receiving a call from the attorney general who is very exercised, very upset,” Zachary Terwilliger, then the U.S. attorney for the Eastern District of Virginia, told investigators.

“The Church of Presidents was burning, it seemed like the city was out of control and that, you know, just steps from the White House,” he added.
