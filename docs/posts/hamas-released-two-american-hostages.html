Hamas Released Two American Hostages

Hamas released an American mother and daughter who were kidnapped by the Palestinian group during its deadly Oct. 7 attack and held hostage for almost two weeks in Gaza. The Israeli military received them at the border and took them to an army base in central Israel to be reunited with family members.

The Israeli Embassy in Washington, D.C., identified the released hostages as Judith Raanan, 59, and her daughter Natalie Raanan, 17. They had traveled from their home in suburban Chicago to celebrate a Jewish holiday and the 85th birthday of Judith’s mother at a kibbutz in southern Israel. (Read more about the Raanans here.)

Hamas said that it released the women for “humanitarian reasons.” The group is believed to have taken more than 200 more hostages. Most were captured from small Israeli towns near the border with Gaza; others were abducted from military bases or from an all-night music festival. They include civilians, soldiers, peace activists, grandparents and a 9-month-old baby.

President Biden, who earlier today formally asked Congress for billions of dollars in emergency funding for Israel and Gaza, said that the U.S. had worked with Qatar to secure the release of the Raanans. He asked the public to respect their privacy “as they recover and heal.”

U.S. Secretary of State Antony Blinken said there were still 10 American hostages being held in Gaza.

Hopes that humanitarian aid would begin to trickle into Gaza from Egypt faded today as negotiators were still trying to work through thorny issues, including who would inspect the shipments for weapons, officials said.

Jim Jordan is no longer the G.O.P. nominee for speaker

Representative Jim Jordan failed for a third time to become speaker today. Hours later, he lost an internal vote to continue as his party’s nominee, plunging the House into further uncertainty and sending Republicans searching for a new leader.

There is no consensus among Republicans about an alternative candidate to Jordan. Many have expressed resistance to a temporary solution that would give Representative Patrick McHenry, who is acting as speaker pro tempore, the authority to run the House in a more expanded capacity for a few months. McHenry said Republicans would gather again on Monday, with the goal of bringing a nominee to a floor vote on Tuesday.

One reason Jordan lost: Mainstream Republicans — long derided as “squishes” — refused to back down.

America’s federal budget deficit doubled

The U.S. economy has remained relatively strong throughout 2023, yet the deficit — which typically balloons during tough times — increased to $1.7 trillion, up from $1.37 trillion in 2022, according to government data released today. However, when factoring in an accounting mirage related to a student-loan plan that never took effect, the deficit jumped from about $1 trillion in 2022 to $2 trillion in 2023, administration officials said.

The increase comes at an uncomfortable moment, as the president looks for more aid for Israel and Ukraine. Republicans, who have helped run up big deficits, have also begun insisting on deep budget cuts.

Philadelphia debates a center for supervised drug use

In an effort to blunt the opioid epidemic, public health experts have long endorsed a controversial strategy of opening supervised drug-consumption sites, where people are allowed to take illicit drugs under professional supervision to prevent overdoses. But the sites still have many critics. In Philadelphia, the issue has captivated the city, pitting elected officials, residents and public health advocates against one another.

Scorsese’s ‘heartbreaking masterpiece’

Martin Scorsese’s “Killers of the Flower Moon,” one of the biggest film releases of the season, arrived in theaters today. The film traces the envy, greed and racism that resulted in the murders of dozens of Osage people in Oklahoma in the 1920s. Our critic, Manohla Dargis, called it a “heartbreaking masterpiece” that Scorsese “has turned into a requiem for the country.”

The success of Scorsese’s three-and-a-half-hour epic will also be a big test for Apple. It’s the first time that the tech giant has financed a major theatrical release.

Cher is ready for Christmas

The musical superstar Cher, now 77, has been performing for six decades. But through all of her successes, she never took a stab at a Christmas album, considered among the most ubiquitous and profitable pop endeavors. That changed today.

Cher’s new LP, “Christmas,” which dropped this morning, is a 13-track romp through holiday music, with guest appearances from Stevie Wonder, Cyndi Lauper, Michael Bublé and Darlene Love. We talked to her about the album, her legacy and an unexpected romance with a music producer 40 years her junior.

Toys are for kids. ‘Kidults,’ too.

Every year, costume retailers try to prepare for the Halloween rush by predicting the year’s hottest outfits. This year they expect Barbie to be a big hit. Not only with trick-or-treating children, but grown-ups as well.

In the retail industry, the audience of adults who find joy in buying toys, seeing movies about toys or dressing up as them is referred to as “kidults.” And it’s more common than you might think: A recent survey found that about 40 percent of parents bought toys for themselves in the past year.
