No Trial for Biden Over Documents, but a Political Mess

The special counsel investigating President Biden’s handling of classified material after leaving the vice presidency in 2017 concluded in a report released today that “no criminal charges are warranted.”

Robert Hur, the special counsel, found that Biden had “willfully” retained and disclosed some sensitive material, including notebooks with entries “implicating sensitive intelligence sources and methods.” Hur criticized the president for sharing some of the classified information with a ghostwriter, but added that the evidence “does not establish Mr. Biden’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.”

For Biden, Hur’s decision not to prosecute was a long-awaited legal exoneration, but the special counsel’s report still turned out to be a political nightmare. In passages explaining his reasons not to pursue a trial, Hur said that Biden had presented himself as a “well-meaning, elderly man with a poor memory” and described interviews in which Biden could not recall when he served as vice president or what year his son Beau Biden died.

A lawyer for Biden criticized parts of the report as “inaccurate and inappropriate.” In a statement, Biden appeared to suggest that his memory lapses were hiccups during five hours of interviews held in the days after Israel was attacked. But Hur’s comments resurfaced widespread worries about the president’s age.

“Privately, several Democratic strategists expressed deep concern about how the report depicted the president and how that will be used in a campaign,” our political correspondent Maggie Haberman said.

Justices looked poised to reject efforts to kick Trump off ballots

The Supreme Court appeared prepared to rule today that states do not have the power to remove Donald Trump from election ballots under the 14th Amendment. Over two hours of oral arguments, justices on both sides of the ideological spectrum skeptically questioned a Colorado ruling that found Trump ineligible to hold office for engaging in an insurrection.

There was very little discussion of the Jan. 6, 2021, assault on the Capitol or of the former president’s role in it. Instead, a majority of the justices indicated that individual states may not disqualify candidates unless Congress first enacts legislation.

The Senate voted to advance aid for Ukraine and Israel

A bipartisan group of senators voted 67 to 32 today to advance a bill that would provide $60.1 billion for Ukraine, $14.1 billion for Israel and $10 billion in humanitarian aid. But it could still be days before final passage, as Republican senators draw out the process and debate internally over whether to kill it.

Some Republicans are insisting on adding border restrictions to the package, a day after voting to block a similar bill with border crackdowns included.

In Ukraine, President Volodymyr Zelensky removed his top general in the biggest leadership shake-up since Russia’s invasion two years ago.

Pakistan’s election highlights the military’s sway

Pakistanis went to the polls today for an election that analysts say will be among the least credible in the country’s 76-year history. The party of former Prime Minister Nawaz Sharif — the military’s preferred party of the moment — is widely expected to come out on top but may not pull off the easy victory that is expected. Preliminary results are coming soon.

“Many people feel demoralized by the military’s intimidation campaign,” our Afghanistan and Pakistan bureau chief Christina Goldbaum said. “A common refrain is that this is a selection, not an election, as in, The military has predetermined the winner. So why bother to vote?”

On the eve of the Super Bowl, Usher proves his mastery

Usher is three decades into a recording career in which he has been an R&B prodigy, a crossover pop star and a consummate showman. Now, just days before he is set to perform at the Super Bowl halftime show, he is releasing a 20-track album that reprises many of his most familiar personas.

In a review of the new album, “Coming Home,” my colleague Jon Pareles writes that Usher cruises through every musical challenge, proving yet again that his sound stands the test of time.

It’s time to dress up again

Fashion Month, the four-week extravaganza of shows, celebrities and clothes from many of the world’s best designers, officially begins tomorrow in New York before heading to London, Milan and Paris later in February. Our fashion critic Vanessa Friedman offered a preview of what to expect, including three major debuts and front-row gossip.

Unofficially, the shows already began, thanks to Marc Jacobs’s rejection of the formal schedule. If his amuse-bouche of a collection is any sign of what is to come, Vanessa wrote, we will be in for a banger of a season.

Gold, silver, bronze and … iron

Athletes who win medals at this year’s Olympics and Paralympics in Paris this summer won’t just win gold, silver or bronze. Their medals will also include a piece of iron — wrought iron, to be exact, from the Eiffel Tower.

All 5,084 medals that are set to be handed out will feature a hexagon-shaped iron fragment from the Eiffel Tower’s original 1889 construction that was sitting unused in a warehouse after renovation work. The iron will be surrounded by ridges of radiating lines that are designed to reflect light, a nod to Paris’s nickname as the City of Light.
