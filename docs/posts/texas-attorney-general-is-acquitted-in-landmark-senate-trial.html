Texas Attorney General Is Acquitted in Landmark Senate Trial

The Texas Senate voted on Saturday to acquit the state’s attorney general, Ken Paxton, after a nine-day impeachment trial that focused on allegations of corruption and divided the Republican Party.

Mr. Paxton, a three-term incumbent who had been suspended from the post since his impeachment in May, was immediately reinstated.

Despite a process overseen by Republicans, with Republicans in the defense and in the prosecution, the result ended in near party-line votes, with members of his party lining up behind Paxton. Only two Republican senators voted in favor of conviction on any article. With a two-thirds vote required for conviction, no article received even a majority vote.

Mr. Paxton responded quickly to what he called a “sham impeachment” that he said had been coordinated partly by a “kangaroo court” in the Texas House.

“The weaponization of the impeachment process to settle political differences is not only wrong, it is immoral and corrupt,” he said in a statement.

He added a warning to the Biden administration: “Buckle up, because your lawless policies will not go unchallenged.”

The impeachment case has split the party in Texas, with those who lined up behind Mr. Paxton attacking Republicans in the Texas House who backed impeachment by a wide margin.

After the vote on Saturday, the lieutenant governor, Dan Patrick, who had been serving as judge during the trial and maintained a studious impartiality, gave a speech excoriating his fellow Republicans in the House for even sending the articles of impeachment to the Senate for trial.

“Millions of taxpayer dollars have been wasted on this impeachment,” Mr. Patrick said. He said the Senate had held a thorough trial, which included hundreds of subpoenas for information and testimony. As he spoke, one Democratic senator walked out of the Senate chamber.

Mr. Paxton, who appeared at the trial only on its first and last days, was not in the chamber for the votes on Saturday. After his acquittal, his wife, Senator Angela Paxton, hugged or shook hands with the lawyers who represented him, including Tony Buzbee, a Houston trial lawyer, one of the many high-powered lawyers involved in the case on both sides.

Mr. Patrick, in his fiery speech, said he would call for an official audit of the spending by the Texas House on its investigation and impeachment.

Before the trial began, Mr. Patrick received a $1 million contribution and a $2 million loan to his campaign from Defend Texas Liberty, a group strongly backing Mr. Paxton. After the acquittal, the group’s leader promised “to lead the charge to fire” those Republicans who supported the impeachment.

The lieutenant governor’s criticisms prompted immediate pushback from the speaker of the Texas House, Dade Phelan. “I find it deeply concerning that after weeks of claiming he would preside over this trial in an impartial and honest manner, Lt. Gov. Patrick would conclude by confessing his bias and placing his contempt for the people’s House on full display,” Mr. Phelan said in a statement.

Some of the senators, who had been prevented from speaking during the trial by a gag order imposed by Mr. Patrick, emerged from the vote eager to discuss the case.

“There was no evidence,” said Senator Bob Hall, a conservative Republican who had voted to dismiss all the articles at the start of the trial.

Senator Nathan Johnson, a Democrat, disagreed. “He abused his powers, not in a subtle way,” he said of Mr. Paxton, adding that the case had been proven by the evidence. He said he looked outside during the vote, “and I saw the United States flag and the Texas flag blowing strong in the wind and the rain, and I felt sick, because what are we doing to what those flags represent inside this building?”

Only two Republicans out of the body’s 19 voted in favor of conviction: Kelly Hancock, who represents an urban and suburban district that includes parts of Fort Worth, and Robert Nichols, an occasional iconoclast whose East Texas district includes the city of Beaumont. (Ms. Paxton was ineligible to vote under the rules.)

The 12 Democratic senators voted to convict on almost every one of the 16 articles considered during the trial, and they voted to hear argument on four others that had been set aside for later consideration. The Republican senators voted unanimously to dismiss those four, which dealt with securities fraud allegations related to Mr. Paxton’s 2015 indictment in state court.

After the vote, Gov. Greg Abbott, who had not commented on the impeachment trial, addressed the result, saying, “the jury has spoken.” He said Mr. Paxton had received a fair trial and, as attorney general, “has done an outstanding job representing Texas, especially pushing back against the Biden administration.”

Despite his success avoiding impeachment, Mr. Paxton still faces legal troubles. The case against him on charges of securities fraud, delayed for years, has begun moving forward again in a state court in Houston.

And a federal investigation into Mr. Paxton — prompted by many of the same charges explored during the impeachment — is still continuing.

The impeachment trial focused on 16 articles of impeachment related to accusations, leveled primarily by former top deputies who became whistle-blowers, that Mr. Paxton had abused his office to help an Austin real estate investor, Nate Paul, who was said to have given Mr. Paxton help with home renovations and an extramarital affair in return.

Both Mr. Paxton and Mr. Paul have denied any wrongdoing.

The vote for impeachment in the Texas House in May was 121 to 23, including a majority of Republicans.

Mr. Patrick praised one of the House Republicans, John Smithee from the deep red Texas Panhandle, who voted against sending the articles to the Senate. Mr. Smithee’s speech to the House against impeachment was, Mr. Patrick said, among the most “honest and courageous” that he had ever heard.

It would have taken 21 of the 31 senators to vote in favor of conviction on any of the 16 articles to remove him from office. But none of the articles of impeachment received more than 14 votes.

“This was a herculean task,” said Mr. Buzbee, the lead defense lawyer. “We were proud of this case. We just should not have had to prove our innocence, but that’s what we did.”

The senators cast their votes for or against impeachment, writing on a slip of paper on their desks and handing them up to be read aloud, one by one, for each of the articles of impeachment.

As they voted, the Senate chamber was almost completely silent. A storm passed over the Capitol, breaking the quiet with rumbles of thunder. Occasionally, the sound of crickets, which have swarmed over Austin in recent days, could be heard in the spectator gallery, and, in some cases, hopped between the chairs.

In the end, the vote resembled many that take place in the staunchly conservative Texas Senate, where Mr. Patrick rules with a strong hand and legislation is adopted almost exclusively along party-line votes.

Paxton still faces an ongoing criminal case for securities fraud.

When senators voted on Saturday to acquit Ken Paxton, the Trump-backed attorney general, of all charges of bribery and abuse of power, Texas power players welcomed him back to the political fray.

“The jury has spoken,” said Gov. Greg Abbott. “I look forward to continuing to work with him.”

Other officials in the state capitol were less diplomatic. Roland Gutierrez, a Democratic state senator who voted yes on all 16 articles of impeachment, said after the acquittal that he expected Mr. Paxton to answer still to several pending criminal charges of securities fraud. “People elected to office are sworn to an oath to protect all the citizens under their charge,” he said.

Pretrial disagreements from both prosecutors and defense have led to years of delays.

Eight years ago, Mr. Paxton was indicted and arrested on criminal securities-fraud charges. A trial date in that case is still pending. In August, Mr. Paxton’s attorneys argued before a criminal court in Houston that they wanted to deal with the impeachment trial first. With that trial now over, it is likely that Mr. Paxton, who is up for re-election in 2026, will face a civilian jury in Houston.

In 2015 a grand jury in the city of McKinney, a suburb of Dallas, returned a three-count indictment against Mr. Paxton. He is charged with soliciting clients and investors for two companies while he was a member of the Texas House of Representatives, before he became attorney general. In one of the most serious charges, Mr. Paxton is accused of misleading investors in a technology company, Servergy Inc., which is based in McKinney, his hometown.

Prosecutors said that he encouraged investors in 2011 to put more than $600,000 into Servergy Inc. while failing to tell them he was making a commission on their investment, and misrepresenting himself as an investor in the company.

The trial became a contest over Republican politics.

The case against Ken Paxton, a nationally prominent conservative politician, was overseen by a board of managers from the state’s House, led by Republicans, and it played out at times as a contest over how much Republican politics should matter in the proceedings.

The House lawmakers approached the case like a criminal trial, appointing a prominent Houston lawyer, Rusty Hardin, who methodically elicited testimony over seven days from 15 witnesses who were mostly senior aides to Mr. Paxton.

In a nod to the politics looming in the background, the prosecutors also frequently sought to highlight the staunchly conservative backgrounds of the witnesses against Mr. Paxton.

Part of the reason for doing so is the nature of the jury pool: The voting senators included 19 Republicans and 12 Democrats. But it also reflected efforts by the conservative wing of the party to attack the impeachment as a political plot put forward by lobbyists, RINOs — Republicans in name only — and Democrats in Austin.

“Are you a RINO?” Mr. Hardin asked the first witness in the case, a recurring question.

“No” was the universal answer.

“Have you ever voted for a Democrat in your life?” Mr. Hardin asked another witness, Mark Penley, a former deputy attorney general for criminal justice.

“No,” Mr. Penley replied.

Mr. Paxton’s lawyers tried to create the impression that politicians like him, a supporter of former President Donald J. Trump, were under attack by moderate Republicans. They named well-known lobbyists, donors and consultants as well as Texans for Lawsuit Reform, a conservative policy organization that has been powerful in Austin, as instigators.

They separately suggested that the former top aides who became whistle-blowers against Mr. Paxton were attempting to take over the attorney general’s office. As evidence, the lawyers pointed to an agency letterhead without Mr. Paxton’s name used by the aides to report their concerns.

“You were staging a coup, weren’t you?” said one of the defense lawyers, Tony Buzbee, in cross-examining Mr. Paxton’s former top aide, Jeff Mateer.

“Absolutely not,” Mr. Mateer said.

Paxton’s trial became a litmus test for loyalty to the Republican Party.

With television ads and text messages, direct mail and billboards, supporters of the embattled Texas attorney general, Ken Paxton, embarked before the vote on an escalating campaign of political pressure, backed by hard-right billionaires, aimed at trying to sway the outcome of Mr. Paxton’s upcoming impeachment trial.

The targets of their efforts were narrow: the 19 Republican members of the State Senate who are acting as jurors in the trial and decide whether allegations of corruption and abuse of power are serious enough to warrant permanently removing and barring Mr. Paxton from office.

But the effort to save Mr. Paxton, who is seen by many hard-core conservatives as their legal standard-bearer, is also the latest proxy in the broader fight over the future direction of the party, both in Texas and nationally.

It has drawn in a range of conservative figures on both sides, with Rick Perry, the former Texas governor, and Karl Rove, the political consultant to former President George W. Bush, arguing in support of the impeachment process, and Steve Bannon, the former Trump political adviser, lampooning it as a Democrat-inspired witch hunt.

“We want the entire MAGA movement to understand that what’s going on in Texas is not just about Texas,” Mr. Bannon told his podcast audience this month.

Mr. Paxton has so far managed to survive politically under both a criminal indictment and the looming impeachment, in part because he has become a key player on the right flank of the conservative legal movement.

He secured Mr. Trump’s endorsement in a hard-fought primary last year, after demonstrating his willingness to contest the results of the 2020 election in court. An outspoken partisan fighter, he addressed the crowd at a rally for Mr. Trump on Jan. 6, 2021, that preceded an insurrection at the Capitol.

In May, a majority of Republicans in the House joined with Democrats to impeach Mr. Paxton, disturbed by his conduct in office — including longstanding allegations of corruption and a criminal indictment for securities fraud — and his effort to obtain $3.3 million in state funding to settle a lawsuit brought against him by some of his senior aides, who became whistle-blowers.

“It’s unconscionable to defend and protect someone who advocates our policies if they’ve abused their office,” said David Simpson, a former Republican member of the Texas House. “I would hope that instead of defending these people, we would say we support their policies but we don’t support their behavior, and ask them to step down.”

A well-funded political action committee, Defend Texas Liberty, has begun targeting some Republican members of the Texas House who voted to impeach Mr. Paxton.

“People know this is a political witch hunt. They’re doing it to Trump, they’re trying to do it to Paxton,” Jonathan Stickland, president of Defend Texas Liberty, said this month on conservative talk radio.

Mr. Stickland, a former member of the Texas House, promised that the group would spend “an epic ton of money” in advance of the impeachment. “And if they stand against that, then every one of these bums will be kicked out,” he said.

Reporting from Austin, Texas

The lieutenant governor, a partisan Republican, shelved political confrontation as the trial’s judge.

As a former talk radio host who never went to law school, Lt. Gov. Dan Patrick was under intense scrutiny when it emerged that he would oversee the State Senate’s impeachment trial of the attorney general, Ken Paxton.

After eight years as lieutenant governor, a role in which he serves as the Senate’s presiding officer, Mr. Patrick, 73, is known as one of the state’s most aggressive political combatants.

There were two previous impeachments in Texas history. Both ended in conviction.

There have been only two other impeachment cases in Texas in the past 106 years, and the last one was nearly half a century ago.

The most famous case involved Gov. James E. Ferguson, who was impeached by a 74-to-45 vote of the Texas House in 1917 and convicted in a lengthy State Senate trial. Mr. Ferguson tried an end-run around the process by tendering his resignation, but the Senate refused to recognize the action and enforced the conviction.

Undaunted by his removal from office, the governor, popularly known as “Farmer Jim” or “Pa” Ferguson, remained an irrepressible force in Texas politics for another two decades by boosting his wife, Miriam Amanda Ferguson, or “Ma,” into the governor’s office. She served nonconsecutive two-year terms in the 1920s and ’30s.

An anti-prohibitionist, Mr. Ferguson won the governor’s race in 1914 but angered the powerful University of Texas leadership during his bid for re-election in 1916. Specifically, he vetoed university appropriations after school regents refused to fire faculty members he found objectionable, according to the Texas State Historical Association.

Investigations into purported malfeasance in the Ferguson administration resulted in nine criminal indictments against the governor, including embezzlement and misapplication of public funds. The criminal allegations led to a special session of the Legislature and 21 articles of impeachment by the House. The Senate convened a three-week-long trial and convicted Ferguson on 10 of them.

The last impeachment trial, nearly 50 years ago, involved allegations right out of a local corruption playbook. The target, Judge O.P. Carrillo, was part of a multigenerational political machine in South Texas run by George Parr, known as the “Duke of Duval.” Mr. Parr, whose area of control covered Duval and Jim Wells Counties, died in 1975 of a gunshot wound while facing prison for income tax evasion. It was ruled a suicide.

Mr. Carrillo, a powerful politician in Duval County, was accused of using county employees to work on his ranch, siphoning off county welfare money, avoiding taxes and using public funds to buy groceries. He was impeached by the House in 1975 and convicted and removed from office in January 1976.

Like the current trial of Ken Paxton, Judge Carrillo’s impeachment battle put plenty of legal star power on display. The Watergate prosecutor, Leon Jaworski, was general counsel for the Senate, and Richard “Racehorse” Haynes, a nationally known defense lawyer, represented Mr. Carrillo. John Hill, the state attorney general who had been a prominent plaintiffs’ lawyer before entering politics, led the prosecution.

The Legislature’s investigation of Judge Carrillo was initiated with a resolution introduced by Representative Terry A. Canales, a Democrat from Jim Wells County, which called for Mr. Carrillo’s impeachment.

His son, Terry Canales, is now a member of the State House and one of the 12 House managers overseeing Mr. Paxton’s impeachment trial.

A conviction requires 21 senators to be convinced beyond a reasonable doubt.

After the arguments ended on Friday, the state senators went into their deliberations. Only 30 of the 31 senators were taking part: Under the rules adopted by the Senate for the trial, Senator Angela Paxton, Mr. Paxton’s wife, is not allowed to deliberate or vote.

As in a courtroom, the burden of proof is on the House impeachment managers, acting as the prosecution, to prove each article of impeachment beyond a reasonable doubt.
