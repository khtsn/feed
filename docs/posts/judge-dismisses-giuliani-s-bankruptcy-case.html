Judge Dismisses Giuliani’s Bankruptcy Case

Politics|Judge Dismisses Giuliani’s Bankruptcy Case

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/07/12/us/politics/giuliani-bankruptcy-case-dismissed.html

The decision means that the former New York City mayor and lawyer for Donald Trump will face his creditors without protection from federal bankruptcy laws.

July 12, 2024Updated 4:21 p.m. ET

A federal judge dismissed Rudolph W. Giuliani’s bankruptcy case on Friday, leaving the former New York City mayor without legal protection from his creditors.

The ruling was expected. Judge Sean H. Lane of the U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of New York said earlier this week that he was leaning in the direction of a dismissal. He said that Mr. Giuliani, who served as former President Donald J. Trump’s personal lawyer, had ignored court filing deadlines and evaded questions about his finances.

It is also an outcome sought by Mr. Giuliani, who this week asked the court to dismiss his case to avoid the appointment of a trustee who would take over his finances, a request from his creditors that the judge was considering.

“Mr. Giuliani has failed to provide an accurate and complete picture of his financial affairs in the six months that this case has been pending,” Judge Lane wrote in the dismissal order. “Transparency into Mr. Giuliani’s finances has proven to be an elusive goal.”

Mr. Giuliani owes 20 people and businesses nearly $153 million. Without bankruptcy protections, his creditors can pursue foreclosures, and lawsuits against him that have been on hold can move forward.

Most of what Mr. Giuliani owes is to Ruby Freeman and Shaye Moss, two Georgia election workers he was found to have defamed in the aftermath of the 2020 election.

In December, a federal jury in the District of Columbia determined he should pay $148 million to Ms. Freeman and Ms. Moss, who are mother and daughter. Mr. Giuliani spread lies about the women cheating in their vote-counting roles on Election Day 2020. It was part of an effort to overturn the election results, a charge he helped lead for Mr. Trump.

A lawyer for Ms. Freeman and Ms. Moss said they will move quickly to have liens placed on Mr. Giuliani’s homes in New York and Palm Beach, Fla.

The women have waited too long for justice, the lawyer, Rachel Strickland, said.

“We are pleased the court saw through Mr. Giuliani’s games and put a stop to his abuse of the bankruptcy process,” she said after Judge Lane issued the order.

In the dismissal order, Judge Lane banned Mr. Giuliani from filing for bankruptcy again for at least a year. This will give Ms. Freeman and Ms. Moss, as well as his other creditors, a year to pursue claims against him in state courts before he can use the bankruptcy system to put proceedings against him on hold again.

Just a few hours before the ruling, Mr. Giuliani sent an email soliciting contributions to his legal defense fund.

“There was a time when I read the paper in the morning and saw headlines about me taking down the mob in New York,” Mr. Giuliani, a former U.S. attorney in the Southern District of New York, wrote in his email bid for donations, lamenting the more recent headlines about his financial demise.

Invoking Mr. Trump, he added, “I always knew the price I could one day pay for standing by the truth and the side of my good friend, the 45th and our future 47th president.”

Mr. Giuliani, 80, faces multiple lawsuits, including three connected to his efforts to help Mr. Trump overturn the election. He faces criminal charges in Georgia and Arizona of plotting to subvert the election results. And Dominion Voting Systems has sued him over accusations that he spread lies about its voting machines after the 2020 election.

He is also being sued by Noelle Dunphy, a former employee of Mr. Giuliani’s who claims he harassed and assaulted her. Ms. Dunphy’s lawyer said her case will pick up where it left off. She is waiting for the judge overseeing the suit in a Manhattan state court to set a date to argue that Mr. Giuliani should be penalized for knowingly lying to the court.

In filing for protection from his creditors under Chapter 11 of the bankruptcy code, Mr. Giuliani was expected to disclose his finances and come up with a plan to pay off some of his debts and potentially get others discharged.

But in the nearly seven months he enjoyed protection from debt collectors, Mr. Giuliani often flew between New York and Palm Beach, Fla., where he has multimillion-dollar homes. He dined out and spent time at his private clubs. He spent thousands of dollars on Amazon purchases without ever fully disclosing what he had bought, and he appeared to enjoy indiscriminate purchases of entertainment on streaming services.

During this time, Mr. Giuliani’s financial disclosures to the bankruptcy court were often late and incomplete. In the five months that he did disclose details about his spending, he said his living expenses averaged nearly $46,000 a month.

He refused to provide any information about his multiple businesses and blamed his filing delinquencies on his inability to retain an accountant, a problem Judge Lane said was incredibly rare.

Typically, when a debtor is told that he or she is not meeting the requirements of the bankruptcy law, Judge Lane said, the debtor will fix at least some of the problems.

“By contrast, Mr. Giuliani has done nothing,” he wrote.

The dismissal of the case ended a monthslong forensic investigation into Mr. Giuliani’s finances that had ensnared many of his business partners and other people close to him. This leaves many unanswered questions that his creditors can pursue individually in state courts, including getting a full accounting of his net worth.

The decision also avoids forcing Mr. Giuliani into filing a lawsuit against Mr. Trump to collect about $2 million in unpaid legal fees the former president owes him for his work after the 2020 election. A bankruptcy trustee would most likely have forced such a lawsuit.
