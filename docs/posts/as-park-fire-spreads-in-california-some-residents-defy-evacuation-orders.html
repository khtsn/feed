As Park Fire Spreads in California, Some Residents Defy Evacuation Orders

In Butte County, Calif., some defied evacuation orders so they could protect their property from the fire that has quickly become the state’s largest this year.

By Thomas Fuller

Reporting from the edges of the Park fire in Butte County, Calif.

  • July 27, 2024

As California’s biggest fire of the year raced toward his neighborhood and the police ordered residents to evacuate, Justin Freese decided he was going nowhere.

Instead, he calmly unfurled his 300 feet of fire hose, readying his generators and surveyed the fire’s progress. Mr. Freese, who owns a shop specializing in car exhaust systems, calculated that he had enough fuel and food to last him about a year — and enough water, nearly 10,000 gallons, to defend his two-story, wood-shingled home in Forest Ranch from the onslaught of fire that leveled a neighbor’s home on Thursday.

“I live up here — I’ve gotta be prepared,” Mr. Freese, 40, said Friday, as he paced his property set amid the conifer forests a 20-minute drive outside the city of Chico.

Through the trees, the sky looked angry. A tower of wildfire smoke — streaked orange, red and black — soared into the sky as the Park fire tore through the valley below. By Saturday morning, it had consumed more than 300,000 acres of land, making it the largest active fire in the country, and it was still zero percent contained.

Eight years ago, when the Camp fire killed 85 people in and around the town of Paradise, it awakened many Americans to the dangers of living on the edge of wilderness in fire-prone states like California.

But for people like Mr. Freese and many of his neighbors, the decimation of Paradise — which is just 10 miles away and is under an evacuation warning as of Friday night — had the opposite effect. They doubled down on the choice to live in thick forests. And some chose to stick it out this week, even as the tendrils of the Park fire flared in the hills and valleys surrounding their homes.

As in many parts of California where wildfires are common, residents find it increasingly difficult to obtain homeowner’s insurance, partly because so many homes have been leveled by wildfires in recent years. The fast-spreading Park fire has destroyed more than 130 structures since igniting on Wednesday.

Yet living in forested hamlets remains popular, and the price of real estate in the towns around Paradise rose sharply in recent years, as they did in many other parts of the country.

Part of the allure of living in the woods for Mr. Freese and his neighbors is a rugged, chop-your-own-firewood, dig-your-own-well lifestyle. Residents of Forest Ranch tend to be less trusting of authorities and embrace the notion that going it alone is a prized American value.

As a group, the two dozen or so neighbors defying evacuation orders joke about being misanthropes — one wore a shirt on Friday that said “I hate people.” At the same time, they say they cherish a sense of community and mutual assistance, especially during times of crisis.

Others who remained in the evacuation zone said they had no choice. Jesse McCutcheon, 40, who owns a family logging business, is desperate to save his hulking equipment, each machine worth hundreds of thousands of dollars. Mr. McCutcheon spent all of Friday razing enough forest to create a clearing the size of a football field and gathered his equipment in the middle, as far away as possible from the towering Douglas fir, cedar and pine trees on the perimeter.

“This is our livelihood,” he said as he peered down into the valley where the Park fire relentlessly chewed through forests.

Late in the afternoon on Friday, the heat from the inferno was palpable even hundreds of yards from the flames. Tiny embers swirled through the air and pricked skin like a mosquito.

A pile of timber Mr. McCutcheon had recently harvested went up in flames. He squinted across the canyon, through the apocalyptic smoky haze, and shook his head at the site of his log pile glowing with fire.

“We put a lot of work into that, and now it’s just gone,” he said of the timber.

Mr. McCutcheon is among a list of contractors and utility company employees who have special dispensations to operate in the evacuation zone.

That is in contrast to Mr. Freese and his neighbors who defied the orders to leave. Kory Honea, the sheriff of Butte County, said Friday that 4,000 people were currently under evacuation orders.

“We don’t order evacuations unless we believe it is absolutely necessary and people’s lives are in danger,” he said at a news conference on Friday. The sheriff has repeatedly said that those who did not follow orders to leave their homes do so at their own risk.

Mr. Freese says he is both careful and practical about making his stand against the wildfire. He has been helping firefighters navigate the hills and gullies of his neighborhood in the foothills of the Sierra Nevada. Many of the some 2,400 firefighters and other personnel deployed to battle the Park fire arrived from faraway counties and are not familiar with the terrain.

And like his neighbors, Mr. Freese says he is realistic about his ability to stay if the fire bears down on his house.

“I’m prepared, but I’m not stupid,” he said. “If there’s a 100-foot wall of flames coming, I’m not going to stay put and melt my skin.”
