What to Know About the Deadly D.C. Shooting of Israeli Embassy Aides

Two employees of the Israeli Embassy in Washington were killed. A suspect was charged with murder.

Published May 22, 2025Updated May 25, 2025, 11:13 p.m. ET

Two young employees of the Israeli Embassy in Washington were fatally shot on Wednesday outside an event at the Capital Jewish Museum, an attack that U.S. officials were investigating as a hate crime and as terrorism.

A suspect, identified as Elias Rodriguez, was arrested shortly after the killings and charged with first-degree murder and other crimes on Thursday, according to an F.B.I. affidavit filed in federal court.

The suspect said: “I did it for Palestine. I did it for Gaza,” after he was detained, according to officials.

The F.B.I. said it was also examining pro-Palestinian writings circulating online that might be connected to the gunman. A post from a social media account, which The New York Times verified as belonging to the suspect, condemned the Israeli and American governments and what it called atrocities committed by the Israeli military against Palestinians.

What happened?

The shooting happened just after 9 p.m. on a street outside the Capital Jewish Museum, where the American Jewish Committee was hosting a reception for young diplomats. The museum is in the heart of official Washington, among federal buildings, embassies and other museums. The U.S. Capitol, the F.B.I.’s Washington field office and the headquarters of the Justice Department are all nearby.

A man was seen pacing in front of the museum before the shooting, the police said. He approached four people who were leaving the event, including Yaron Lischinsky and Sarah Milgrim, and shot them at close range, the police said.

The man continued to fire even after Mr. Lischinsky had fallen and as Ms. Milgrim tried to crawl away, federal officials said. The man then entered the museum, where he was detained by security officers, the authorities said.

The night before, Mr. Rodriguez had checked a gun with his baggage when he flew from Chicago to the Washington area for a work conference, officials said. The gun used in the killings had been purchased legally in Illinois, according to two officials briefed on the initial trace of the weapon by the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives.

Who is the suspect?

Mr. Rodriguez, a 31-year-old from Chicago, was named as the only suspect in the killings.

He chanted, “Free, free Palestine” after the police took him into custody, said Pamela A. Smith, the chief of the Metropolitan Police Department. Mr. Rodriguez told the police where he had discarded the gun, she added.

Mr. Rodriguez worked for a health care nonprofit organization and previously held jobs in production, research and writing, according to his online profile. An online job biography said he was born and raised in Chicago and had graduated from the University of Illinois, Chicago, with a degree in English.

He posted video from a pro-Palestinian march in Chicago on X in 2023.

F.B.I. agents arrived on Thursday morning at the Chicago apartment that records indicated was Mr. Rodriguez’s home. Agents blocked the sidewalk in front of the brick apartment building, and some were seen going in and out of the apartment, including two F.B.I. agents in full tactical gear. Bomb technicians were also present.

In the window of the unit that appeared to be Mr. Rodriguez’s were two signs related to Palestine, including one that read, “Justice for Wadea,” a reference to the 6-year-old Palestinian American boy killed in Chicago in 2023. Another sign read, “Tikkun Olam means free Palestine.” (Tikkun Olam is a Hebrew phrase that means “repairing the world.”)

At his arraignment on Thursday in federal court in Washington, Mr. Rodriguez was represented by a public defender. He calmly told Magistrate Judge Matthew J. Sharbaugh his background and listened solemnly as the judge told him he faced the possibility of the death penalty in connection with a list of crimes.

Mr. Rodriguez entered no plea.

Who were the victims?

Mr. Lischinsky, 30, and Ms. Milgrim, 26, were two employees of the Israeli Embassy. She was an American from Prairie Village, Kan. He was born in Israel, moved with his family to Germany for several years and returned to Israel when he was 16, said his 32-year-old brother, Hanan.

Mr. Lischinsky was about to ask Ms. Milgrim to marry him, according to Yechiel Leiter, the Israeli ambassador to Washington. “The young man purchased a ring this week with the intention of proposing to his girlfriend next week in Jerusalem,” he told reporters.

Mr. Lischinsky was a research assistant in the embassy’s political department, and Ms. Milgrim organized diplomatic missions and visits to Israel, according to Israel’s Foreign Ministry.

The couple met about a year and a half ago, after Ms. Milgrim joined the embassy. They had been scheduled to travel this weekend to Jerusalem, where Ms. Milgrim was planning to meet Mr. Lischinsky’s parents for the first time, Mr. Leiter said.

Mr. Lischinsky was buried on Sunday near his family home west of Jerusalem. Ms. Milgrim will be buried on Tuesday in Overland Park, Kan., near Prairie Village.

Has there been a rise in antisemitic attacks?

Experts and law enforcement officials say there has been a global surge in antisemitic incidents after terrorists led by Hamas killed more than 1,200 people and seized 250 hostages on Oct. 7, 2023. Since the attacks, Israel has waged a 19-month bombing campaign and aid blockade that the Gazan health ministry says has killed more than 50,000 Palestinians. The tolls from Gazan officials do not distinguish between civilians and combatants.

Across the world, offenses against Jewish people and property have increased since the 2023 attack on Israel and subsequent war. Groups that monitor hate crimes have logged tens of thousands of episodes, including cases of verbal abuse, a torrent of online attacks on social media, Nazi-themed vandalism, personal threats and violent attacks resulting in injury and death.

What is the reaction in Israel?

Israeli officials expressed shock and described the shooting as an antisemitic act of terrorism.

“I’ve been worried for the past few months that something like this would happen, and it did,” said Gideon Saar, Israel’s foreign minister.

At a news conference on Thursday, Mr. Saar drew a connection between the killings on Wednesday and pro-Palestinian protests that have taken place in the United States and other countries since Israel went to war against Hamas.

“This is the direct result of toxic antisemitism against Israel and Jews around the world that has been going on since the Oct. 7 massacre,” he said.

Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu of Israel ordered stronger security for Israeli diplomatic missions around the world, his office said.

Many Israelis have become more wary about traveling overseas, fearing that their nationality could put them in danger. Israeli officials have at times warned members of the public to avoid showing Israeli and Jewish symbols, lest they become potential targets.

Yair Golan, who leads the left-wing Democrats party in Israel, blamed Mr. Netanyahu’s right-wing government, which has vowed to take control of all of Gaza, for “fueling antisemitism and hatred of Israel.”

“The result is unprecedented political isolation and danger to every Jew in every corner of the globe,” Mr. Golan said in a statement.
