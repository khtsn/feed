How Washington May Approach the Capital One-Discover Deal

DealBook Newsletter

Regulators have been tough on big financial mergers, though there are nuances in Capital One’s $35.3 billion takeover bid for Discover.

Feb. 20, 2024, 8:05 a.m. ET

Challenges, and opportunities, for a financial megadeal

Capital One’s $35.3 billion takeover to buy Discover Financial Services will create a colossus in the fast-growing credit card industry and a more powerful force in the payment networks that underpin the consumer economy.

That will almost surely invite tough scrutiny from a Washington that is increasingly skeptical of big financial mergers. But continuing scrutiny of the two biggest payment networks in the U.S., Visa and Mastercard, may complicate the regulatory math.

The deal: Capital One agreed to pay 1.0192 of its shares for each share of Discover, a roughly 26 percent premium to Friday’s trading prices. Discover’s shares were up more than 13 percent in premarket trading on Tuesday.

If completed, the transaction would become a giant among credit card lenders, with Bloomberg estimating that the combined company would outstrip JPMorgan Chase and Citigroup in U.S. card loan volume. (That could ratchet up examinations over shrinking competition, and what that means for consumers.)

Perhaps more important is the potential supercharging of Discover’s payment network, which has long lagged Visa, Mastercard and American Express. The Wall Street Journal reported that Capital One plans to switch some of its credit cards to the Discover network.

The contrarian argument: This is good for Visa and Mastercard. The longtime giants of the payment network business have long been criticized for their fees, with Visa being investigated by the Justice Department. Monday’s deal could give them the opportunity to argue that they would face a newer, bigger competitor.

Capital One may try to position itself as a savior for Discover, helping pull the smaller financial company out of a morass of financial and regulatory troubles, including a steep drop in fourth-quarter profit and the resignation of a C.E.O. amid a review of incorrectly classified credit accounts.

The question for regulators is whether they will “view this as a white knight coming in to help fix a troubled player in the market or whether they view this as a limitation of competition — and therefore something to avoid,” David Schiff, a senior partner at West Monroe, a digital services firm, told DealBook.

While the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency said last month that it planned to slow down approvals for bank takeovers, Washington has also encouraged deals for challenged institutions. (Whether Discover meets that threshold is another question.)

Worth noting: Monday’s transaction involved two of the most seasoned financial deal makers around, Ed Herlihy of Wachtell, Lipton, Rosen & Katz and Rodge Cohen of Sullivan & Cromwell. Other advisers included Centerview Partners for Capital One and PJT Partners and Morgan Stanley for Discover.

HERE’S WHAT’S HAPPENING

Calls to penalize Russia, including over Aleksei Navalny’s death, grow. President Biden said he was weighing new sanctions on Moscow after the death of the Russian opposition leader, while the E.U. has been discussing its own measures. Meanwhile, Donald Trump broke his silence on the matter on Monday — but only to compare Navalny’s death to his own legal struggles, and he didn’t condemn, or mention, President Vladimir Putin of Russia.

The European Union investigates TikTok’s “addictive design.” E.U. regulators are examining whether the short-video app puts young people at risk of being exposed to harmful content, potentially violating the political bloc’s Digital Services Act. The Chinese-owned platform, which is already being scrutinized on both sides of the Atlantic, could face a penalty of up to 6 percent of global revenues.

Barclays plans a major overhaul and returns billions to investors. The embattled British bank said it would pay £10 billion ($12.6 billion) to shareholders via dividends and buybacks and revamp its structure in an effort to lift its stock price. The news came after Barclays reported a roughly $140 million fourth-quarter loss.

Global authorities bust the “world’s most harmful” ransomware group. Britain’s National Crime Agency announced on Tuesday that international authorities had seized the digital operations of LockBit, a hacker syndicate accused of extorting billions from companies and governments around the world. As part of the multicountry operation, the U.S. Justice Department said two individuals had been detained and unsealed indictments against two others who are Russian nationals.

The growing economic costs of the war in Gaza

Piece by piece, the toll from the four-month war between Israel and Hamas is crystallizing. Regional trade has plummeted; global shipping has been upended; and Israel’s closest allies, even the U.S., are stepping up calls for a cease-fire in Gaza as the death toll in the territory mounts.

Israel’s economy shrank nearly 20 percent in the fourth quarter, after the government mobilized reservists following the Hamas-led Oct. 7 attacks. That sent workers and entrepreneurs to the front lines, bringing business in some areas of the economy to a near standstill. (By comparison, the Israeli economy shrank almost 30 percent during the onset of the coronavirus pandemic.)

It’s the latest blow to the government led by Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu. This month, Moody’s downgraded the country’s sovereign credit rating over concerns that the war could weaken the economy and political institutions. The war has hurt the economies of Israel’s neighbors — Egypt, Lebanon and Jordan — too.

Political support is fraying. The U.S. is trying to negotiate a cease-fire “as soon as practicable” via a United Nations Security Council resolution, The Times reports.

Until now, Washington had largely been unwavering in its support of Israel, repeatedly rejecting demands for an outright cease-fire to the conflict in U.N. resolutions. But the war has weighed heavily on President Biden’s overall approval ratings.

Meanwhile, turmoil in the Red Sea continues. The Iran-backed Houthi militia on Monday launched what appeared to be one of its most damaging attacks yet on a cargo ship in the waterway. The attack forced the crew to abandon the vessel and raised fears that the pressure campaign, which the Houthis say is in solidarity with Palestinians under attack in Gaza by Israel, could grow more disruptive.

Shipping rates have soared — more than doubling for some logistics companies — since the Red Sea crisis began late last year, but there were signs last week that the costs may have peaked.

The attacks are again putting pressure on oil prices. Brent crude, the global benchmark, traded near a three-month high this morning as concerns grow that regional tensions could affect supplies.

Anthropic and the new rules of A.I. deal making

Coming up with innovations in artificial intelligence is hard work. So, too, is raising the seemingly endless amounts of capital needed to finance that, as start-ups like Anthropic illustrate.

To fund the surging levels of computing power needed for A.I. operations, Anthropic has collected more than $7 billion from tech giants like Amazon and Google, as well as from investors led by the venture capital firm Menlo Ventures.

But amassing that money has forced Anthropic — along with rivals like OpenAI and Cohere — be more creative in its fund-raising, The Times’s Erin Griffith and Cade Metz report:

In one of those deals, Anthropic agreed to use technology such as chips and cloud computing services from the companies that invested in it. That meant, in effect, that some of the money it raised would be pumped back into its investors. And to consolidate smaller investors who were interested in Anthropic, Menlo created a legal entity known as a “special purpose vehicle.”

“These deals are so complicated,” said Dave Brown, an Amazon Web Services vice president who was involved in Amazon’s deal with Anthropic.

For all of A.I.’s promise of transforming every aspect of society, it has started by upending Silicon Valley’s start-up deal-making. Young companies typically raise money every 15 months or so, after showing that their businesses have grown. But since generative A.I. — which can generate text, images, sounds and video — burst onto the scene in late 2022, the rule book has been thrown out as investors have fought for a piece of the hottest developers.

That deal making has come under the focus of the F.T.C., which is reviewing the Anthropic investments for potential antitrust violations. Anthropic told The Times that it planned to cooperate with the regulator, while Amazon and Google said that the investments and cloud credit arrangements were made at arm’s length.

The week ahead

The Fed, earnings and artificial intelligence will be in focus this week. Here’s what to watch for.

Wednesday: The Fed will release the minutes from its January rate-setting meeting, giving investors new clues on its timeline for lowering interest rates. Hotter-than-expected inflation data last week has forced traders to scale back their bets on imminent cuts.

The week’s big event: Nvidia, the darling of A.I. stocks, reports fourth-quarter profits. Investors will be tuning into what the chip giant has to say about demand for its high-end products as they question whether its $1.8 trillion market capitalization is justified.

Thursday: It’s the European Central Bank’s turn to publish minutes from its most recent meeting. On the earnings front, Nestlé, Intuit, Booking Holdings and Mercedes-Benz report.

Friday: Closing out earnings this week will be reports from Allianz, BASF and Warner Bros. Discovery.

Saturday: Warren Buffett is set to release his annual letter to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders, along with the company’s annual report. It’s also the day of the South Carolina Republican primary, which could be a last stand for Nikki Haley’s campaign.
