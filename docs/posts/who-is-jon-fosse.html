Who is Jon Fosse?

Pinned

European culture reporter

Here’s what to know.

The Nobel Prize in Literature was awarded on Thursday to the Norwegian novelist and playwright Jon Fosse “for his innovative plays and prose which give voice to the unsayable.”

Fosse’s work has long been lauded throughout continental Europe, but he has recently found a growing audience in the English-speaking world. By receiving what is widely seen as the most prestigious prize in literature, the author (whose name is pronounced Yune FOSS-eh, according to his translator) joins a list of laureates including Toni Morrison, Kazuo Ishiguro and Annie Ernaux.

Critics have long compared Fosse’s sparse plays to the work of two previous Nobel laureates: Harold Pinter and Samuel Beckett. And he had long been tipped to win. In 2013, British bookmakers temporarily suspended betting on the prize after a flurry of bets on Fosse’s winning. In the end, the action proved unnecessary, as Alice Munro, the Canadian short story writer, took the award.

Along the prestige and a huge boost in book sales, Fosse receives 11 million Swedish krona, about $991,000.

In recent years, the Swedish Academy, which organizes the prize, has tried to increase the diversity of considered authors after facing criticism that only 17 Nobel laureates had been women, and that the vast majority were from Europe or North America. The choice of Fosse is likely to be interpreted as step back from those efforts.

Before Thursday’s announcement, at a news conference in Stockholm, Fosse was among the favorites, although Can Xue, a Chinese writer of often surreal and experimental short stories was the also tipped, as were Haruki Murakami; Gerald Murnane, a reclusive Australian author; and Laszlo Krasznahorkai, a Hungarian author who Susan Sontag once called a “master of the apocalypse.”

Ben Brantley

The former chief theater critic for The Times.

Fosse as a playwright: ‘A feeling unlike anything else I was seeing in the theater.’

About 10 or 15 minutes into the first play I saw by Jon Fosse, this year’s Nobel Prize winner for literature, I realized I had succumbed to a strangely paradoxical feeling of calm, continuous dread. Staged Off Broadway in 2012 in a production starring Karen Allen, the work bore the baldly generic title of “A Summer Day,” which seemed to suggest an idyllic memory play and a summoning of a specific moment in time.

I suppose those descriptions are accurate, as far as they go. But they are also woefully incomplete. Yes, that day — on which a young woman’s husband set out, as he often did, in his boat from their rural home but never returned — technically happened in the past.

But Fosse’s point, rendered through scenes that flowed as elementally as water between then and now, seemed to be that the day never really ends. It belongs instead to a looping, unresolvable present in which the woman continues to exist decades later.

That sense of dread I mentioned didn’t come from worrying about what might happen. We more or less knew what had happened from the play’s opening moments. Yet as it moved forward and backward and nowhere at all, with characters exchanging simple, elliptical, contradictory dialogue, the suspense and anxiety never let up.

It was a feeling unlike anything else I was seeing in the theater. I could understand why Fosse’s work had never caught on with English-speaking audiences — it lacked the release of humor that Beckett provided — but there was no denying its stealthy power.

This is your life, Fosse seemed to be saying to both his characters and his audience. It goes on — and on — without solutions and without catharsis. I remember thinking when I left the theater that Fosse’s summer’s day was what the critic Richard Gilman had said that Chekhov’s plays provided, “an opening into eternity.”

Two years later, I caught another play by Fosse in New York. This one, “I Am the Wind,” occurred not by the sea but on it — in a small boat, inhabited by two men, on a voyage to …. where? The allegory seemed starker and tidier than in “A Summer’s Day.”

But there was no denying the propulsive circularity of Fosse’s writing. Or his distinctive gift for finding the momentum in staying endlessly in place.

Henrik Pryser Libell

Reporting from Oslo

Fosse writes in the less common of Norwegian’s two written forms.

The language in which Fosse writes, Nynorsk, is one of the two written forms of the Norwegian. The other is Bokmål. While Nynorsk has a strong standing in Norwegian literature, and is used by many authors, Fosse is probably the most prominent.

Nynorsk, or “New Norwegian,” was constructed in the 19th century by the philologist and poet Ivar Aasen, after studying Norwegian dialects around the country. Bokmål evolved from Danish. (Norway was in a union with Denmark from 1380 to 1814.)

Bokmål is used by around 85 percent of the population, while Nynorsk is used by 10 to 15 percent, according to the Norwegian Directorate for Education and Training. Unlike most other major languages in Europe, Norwegian has no official standard for the spoken language, and most Norwegians speak a dialect, even in official settings. Most of the dialects are somewhat closer to Nynorsk than Bokmål.

All Norwegians learn both written languages in school. The primary language taught in each school district is determined by local referendum, and Nynorsk is most common in western Norway, where Fosse grew up.

Nynorsk differs from Bokmål in vocabulary and grammar. The Nynorsk style tends to be more direct, compact and have a more active use of verbs.The Scandinavian languages (Norwegian, Swedish and Danish) are mutually intelligible, and most Scandinavians can read the other languages.

In Norway, Fosse’s win means a lot.

Norwegian writers have received the Nobel Prize in Literature four times, but Jon Fosse is the first in 95 years. Before him, the last Norwegian recipients were Sigrid Undset, a writer of historical fiction who took the prize in 1928, and Knut Hamsun in 1920.

Cecilie Asker, the culture editor of Aftenposten, a Norwegian newspaper, said in a telephone interview that since Norway was unlikely to win the soccer World Cup, Fosse’s prize was something the country “should really celebrate.”

Fosse’s writing meant a lot to many in the country, Asker said, particularly because it is in the Nynorsk language, linked to the country’s heritages. “It makes everything feel close and more real,” Asker said. “For us, reading a Fosse book is like reading music.”

Joumana Khatib and Tina Jordan

Where to start? A guide to Fosse’s work.

Throughout a decades-long career, the playwright and author Jon Fosse has inspired comparisons to Henrik Ibsen, Samuel Beckett and even George Harrison from the Beatles.

One of his English translators, Damion Searls, writing in The Paris Review in 2015, described Fosse’s work this way: “Think of the four elder statesmen of Norwegian letters as a bit like the Beatles,” he wrote. “Per Petterson is the solid, always dependable Ringo; Dag Solstad is John, the experimentalist, the ideas man; Karl Ove Knausgaard is Paul, the cute one; and Fosse is George, the quiet one, mystical, spiritual, probably the best craftsman of them all.”

His work is spare and existential, often focusing on the interior lives of rather solitary characters. Winding, run-on sentences are common; so are fishermen. “You don’t read my books for the plots,” he told The Financial Times in 2018.

Here is a guide to his major works.

Novels

Septology I-VII

Written in the wake of Fosse’s conversion to Catholicism, the seven novels in the extraordinary “Septology” series track an aging artist’s reckoning with the divine, and represent his most significant novelistic work. “Each novel begins, midthought, the same way, with Asle reflecting on how to finish his painting of the St. Andrew cross; each one ends the same way, mid-Latin prayer, at least until something else happens in the final book,” Randy Boyagarda wrote in his review.

Morning and Evening

This short, powerful novella opens with the birth of Johannes, whose parents hope he will become a fisherman like his father. Years later, as an old man, Johannes reflects on his family and close friendships. (Yes, he did end up becoming a fisherman.)

Melancholy I-II

These novels fictionalize the life of Lars Hertervig, a 19th-century Norwegian painter, as he careens into madness. While studying in Dusseldorf, Hertervig is paralyzed by anxiety about his talent and is left essentially homeless after his attraction to his landlady’s daughter leads him to outrageous sexual delusions.

Aliss at the Fire

A woman named Signe thinks back to more than 20 years earlier, when her husband set out by boat and never came back. Soon, her thoughts take on a metaphysical quality, and even include the memories of family members from generations prior. The fjord where Signe lives is a constant across all of the memories of loss and grief.

A Shining

Late at night, as an unnamed narrator drives aimlessly through the remote Norwegian woods, his car becomes mired in the rutted road. Hopelessly lost, he finally gets out of his car, only to see a strange creature, “a shining whiteness,” approaching him.

Fosse’s literary agency calls the work, which will be released by Transit Books in the United States on Oct. 31, “a brilliant novel about the border between life and death.”

Boathouse

After a man — more or less a hermit — encounters an old friend and his wife, the three become enmeshed in a sinister love triangle.

Plays

“I Am the Wind”

Fosse has been said to be the most performed of living European dramatists, though English adaptations are less common. “I Am the Wind” is an existential play centered on two men in a fishing boat. “Fosse’s terse, rhythmic script captures a gut-level anxiety about elemental questions of identity,” a critic for The Times wrote in 2014.

“A Summer Day”

This may remind you of “Aliss at the Fire” — the emotional center of this play is a woman waiting plaintively for her husband to return from a fishing trip. Even with a clear, unabiding sense of dread, the play “exerts a strong but stealthy undertow, a distinctive dramatic momentum,” the Times critic wrote.

“Someone Is Going to Come”

In this play of jealousy, sexual tension and paranoia, a couple move to a remote, tattered old house by the sea where neither can shake the thought that “someone is going to come.”

“The Name”

A young pregnant girl moves to her parents’ home, along with the child’s father. Her parents don’t know that she’s expecting, adding to the play’s sense of claustrophobia and the tension of the unsaid.

Alexandra Alter

The writer attracts global renown for his plays.

Damion Searls, Fosse’s English-language translator, said in an interview that the prize could help Fosse reach a larger audience, particularly in the United States and Britain, but that he was already globally renowned for his plays.

Searls said that while Fosse wrote in a range of mediums, including plays, novels, poetry, children’s books and essays, a unifying thread in his work was a feeling of serenity, which is why his work is often described as hypnotic or evocative of a spiritual experience.

“It is hard to describe. It’s a very unique and special experience when you’re reading him,” said Searls, who translates from German, Norwegian, French and Dutch. “It’s like you’re communing with other people and the larger forces of life.”

“One of the key words he uses to talk about his fiction is peace,” added Searls, a writer in residence at Wesleyan University in Connecticut. “There’s a real peacefulness in it, even though stuff happens, people die, people get divorced, but it radiates this serenity.”

Fosse’s plays, minimal in style and in elevated language, are ‘strong’ on emotion.

For Norway’s theater world, Jon Fosse’s Nobel accolade is major news.

Erik Ulsfby, the director of the Norwegian Theater, in Oslo, said in a telephone interview that many of Fosse’s plays, going back to the 1990s, were part of theater repertoires there because of “their quality and importance.”

Written in the Nynorsk language, they are minimal in style, and in an “elevated language” that is distant from daily life, Ulsfby said. But Fosse is “very strong” on discussing relationships, and his work carries deep emotion.

Ulfsby, who this year organized a festival of Fosse’s work attended by several thousand fans, said his favorite play was “Dream of Autumn,” in which a couple meet in a cemetery, apparently for the first time — until it becomes clear that they’ve spent their lives together.

The play gave him “a feeling of being part of something bigger,” Ulfsby said, both religiously and in terms of a connection to nature.

Alexandra Alter

Karl Ove Knausgaard on Fosse, his teacher.

The writer Karl Ove Knausgaard, who studied creative writing with Jon Fosse, credits Fosse with steering him away from poetry, toward prose.

In a 2019 essay about Fosse’s work, Knausgaard described the difference between Fosse’s essays and fiction: “Whereas the essays stand outside art and peer in on it, probing and investigating, wondering as to its nature,” he wrote, “the opposite is true of Fosse’s fiction, which instead of peering in from the outside, peers out from the inside, at the world and at the reader.”

He continued, “Jon Fosse’s voice is unmistakable in whatever he writes, and is never anything if not present, but whereas the voice of the essays is a presence in their contemporary age, the voice of Fosse’s fiction is a presence unconnected with Fosse’s own time, but connected rather with something else, which the essays endeavor to isolate in various ways according to their time of writing.”

Alexandra Alter

‘When I manage to write well, there is a second, silent language.’

In an interview with the Los Angeles Review of Books in 2022, Jon Fosse spoke about his evolution as a writer, his relationship to God and mysticism, and how he tries in his writing to push the boundaries of what language can evoke.

“When I manage to write well, there is a second, silent language,” he said. “This silent language says what it is all about. It’s not the story, but you can hear something behind it — a silent voice speaking.”

He also described how writing in Nynorsk rather than in Bokmål, the more widely used Norwegian language for literature, was not a political act but simply a creative choice.

“It’s simply my language,” he said. “It’s what I learned from my first day in school until I left, so for some 12 or 13 years. It’s a minority language, and that’s only an advantage for me as a writer. It’s almost never used in commercials or business the way it is in academia, literature, and in church.”

Who is Jon Fosse?

Jon Fosse made his name as a novelist, starting in 1983 with “Red, Black,” and in recent years there has been surge of interest in his fiction.

His “A New Name: Septology VI-VII,” the last in a seven-novel sequence, explores an older man’s reckoning with God, and was a finalist for the 2022 National Book Awards. Several of his works have recently been published to acclaim by Fitzcarraldo Editions, a trendy British publisher that is home to several Nobel winners including Annie Ernaux.

But Fosse, 64, is perhaps better known in much of Europe for his plays. He turned to theater in his mid-30s and has since produced some 40 works that have been translated into dozens of languages. Several have been performed in New York, including “I Am the Wind” and “Deathvariations” about a couple coming to terms with the death of their daughter.

Anita Gates, in a 2006 review of “Deathvariations” for The New York Times, said it was easy to see Fosse’s work “as Ibsen stripped down to its emotional essentials. But it is much more,” adding that his play had “a fierce poetic simplicity.”

Although widely staged on the continent, Fosse’s plays rarely make a dent in London or New York, and Anglo-Saxon audiences can find them obtuse. Ben Brantley, the former chief theater critic for The New York Times, highlighted that problem in a 2014 review: “Red lights are sure to flash in the minds of certain theatergoers when they hear that the characters in ‘I Am the Wind’ are identified as the One and the Other,” he said.

In 2018, Fosse told The Financial Times that he knew his work was difficult for some. “You don’t read my books for the plots,” he said. “But it’s not because I want to be a difficult writer,” he added. “I’ve never tried to write in a complicated way. I always try to write as simply and, I hope, as deeply as I possibly can.”

When asked what his work was about, he said, “I try to write about the mystery of life,” adding, “I’m not looking for answers in a simple way; I want to celebrate the enigma.”

Fosse is also known as a teacher of creative writing. His former students include Karl Ove Knausgaard, the Norwegian writer, who has also been tipped for the Nobel Prize.

Annie Ernaux, last year’s recipient, was lauded as a ‘thrilling’ choice.

Annie Ernaux, from France, received last year’s Nobel Prize in Literature. The choice was welcomed by many critics who were glad to see the honor go to a writer who had for decades been publishing books that delved into the deepest corners of her experience.

Dwight Garner, writing in The New York Times, said that the Swedish Academy’s decision to pick a writer who blurs the boundary between fiction and memoir was “thrilling.” Ernaux, 83, was a “brave and interesting” choice, he said, whose books excavate difficult aspects of her past, including a back alley abortion, a failing marriage and an extramarital affair. “Hers is a voice of rough compassion,” Garner said.

Readers quickly responded: Jacques Testard of Fitzcarraldo Editions, Ernaux’s British publisher, said that he had sold nearly 120,000 copies of her books in the year after the prize, compared with about 41,000 in the previous 12 months.

Dan Simon, the founder of Seven Stories Press, which has published Ernaux in the United States for over 30 years, said that interest in her work had been building before the Nobel. Many American readers, he said, were especially drawn to “Happening,” Ernaux’s account of an illegal abortion, in light of the Supreme Court overturning Roe v. Wade. (The book was also made into a movie). Still, Simon said, the Nobel “supercharged” such interest and was a just reward for an author who had “stayed true to a vision for decades.”

Receiving the Nobel puts heavy demands on any writer, and Testard said the notoriety had affected Ernaux. At a sold-out talk in England in May, Testard recalled, Ernaux said that the Nobel had been “like a bomb” in her life: so disruptive that it left her unable to write for months.

Yet, Testard added, neither the acclaim nor the demands had changed Ernaux’s “humble, modest, and very down-to-Earth” nature.

At the end of her talk, the 400-strong audience gave Ernaux a standing ovation, but the author simply hushed the crowd so that she could thank the moderator, Sally Rooney. Ernaux then said that Rooney’s own writing had given her a window into a younger generation.

“Annie’s always been so supportive of generations of women writers,” Testard said, “and that generosity came through.”

How to Choose the Nobel Laureate: Tap a Literature Professor

About five years ago, Ankhi Mukherjee, a professor of world literature at Oxford University, received a letter asking whether she wanted to nominate someone for the Nobel Prize in Literature. She assumed it was a hoax.

“I couldn’t believe the process was so informal,” Mukherjee recalled in an interview.

Only after talking with colleagues, many of whom had received the same surprise in the mail, did Mukherjee realize the request was genuine. The sudden responsibility “was exciting,” she recalled.

The Nobel Prize is arguably the world’s most secretive literary award. A casual observer could be forgiven for thinking that each October a handful of Swedish literary experts simply meet in a grand hall in Stockholm and then argue over which author, poet, playwright or musician to give that year’s award to.

In reality, the process is far more byzantine.

Each November, the Swedish Academy sends out thousands of letters seeking nominations. A request goes to every living laureate, to the chairs of writers’ organizations, to “professors of literature and language subjects” at prominent universities, as well as to members of the Swedish Academy and similar institutions around the world.

Once a nominator submits their choice, the academy sends them a thank-you note (until recently, it arrived in the mail on embossed paper), but otherwise breaks off contact. Nominators are barred from speaking about their choices — Mukherjee declined to discuss whether she had ever taken up the academy’s offer to nominate somebody.

Nominators’ suggestions are also kept secret in the Nobel Prize archives for 50 years, although the online archive now makes public all of the nominations from 1901 to 1971.

In January, the academy collates all of the suggestions to draw up a longlist of about 300 names. Then the members of the its special Nobel Committee whittle the list down, sometimes adding their own picks. In May, the committee presents a shortlist of five candidates to the wider academy, and its members are invited to catch up on each writer’s work before choosing a laureate in the fall.

What makes a good Nobel choice? For Mukherjee, a writer must have produced a “critical mass of excellent writing which also innovates continually.”

For other Nobel observers, more prosaic considerations are also important. Jacob Blakesley, an associate professor in comparative literature at the University of Rome, said that an author was more likely to be given the Nobel if their work has been translated into Swedish, since that is most Academy members’ native tongue.

Being the recipient of other major awards, such as the Neustadt International Prize for Literature, also helps, Blakesley said, “as they’re seen as a more objective measure that ‘This is an amazing writer.’”

Blakesley, who said he had never been asked to nominate someone for the prize, said a typical Nobel laureate also had political relevance and spoke to wider events. Ludmila Ulitskaya, a Russian author who moved to Berlin after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, would be a good pick this year, because she spoke to events in Eastern Europe, he said.

The final stage in the decision-making process is kept as secret as possible.

The academics who take part don’t find out the recipient until the official announcement, along with everyone else. Mukherjee said she had no problem with the secrecy. Every year, she said, she waits for the announcement, “excited to be surprised.”

Who’s tipped for this year’s literature prize?

The Nobel Prize in Literature, organized by the Swedish Academy, is the capstone to any writer’s career. Past recipients have included a who’s who of literature: Toni Morrison in 1993, Kazuo Ishiguro in 2017 and Ernest Hemingway back in 1954.

It’s also such a high-profile award that it is the subject of avid interest from bookmakers.

Here are five writers favored by betting firms this year.

Can Xue

This year’s favorite, the Chinese author Can Xue, is a writer of surreal and experimental short stories that sometimes include weird characters such as a man who wakes up with a stomach full of needles.

Is that too strange for the Nobel?

Lisa Michaels, reviewing her collection of short stories “The Embroidered Shoes” for The New York Times, said that “reading Can Xue’s fiction is like running downhill in the dark,” adding, “You’ve got momentum, but you don’t know where you’re headed.”

Ludmila Ulitskaya

Ulitskaya has long been tipped for the prize.

Several of her books have been best sellers in her native Russia, including “The Big Green Tent,” about dissidents and collaborators in the Soviet Union. Outside Russia, she’s often championed as much for her opposition to authoritarianism as for her writing.

Awarding Ulitskaya the prize would be widely seen as a statement by the Swedish Academy against Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, not least because Ulitskaya moved to Berlin shortly after the invasion began.

Thomas Pynchon

Pynchon, 86, is considered one of the great American writers for his labyrinthine novels including “Gravity’s Rainbow,” “Mason & Dixon” and “Inherent Vice.” Would the reclusive author make for a good Nobel laureate?

Bookmakers think so — he’s fourth on the list of favorites — although the Swedish Academy may want to steer clear. Pynchon has for decades avoided publicity, including being photographed (the image above is one of the few of him available). So he might decline to travel to Sweden to collect the prize.

Homero Aridjis

Is it time for a Latin American writer to receive the Nobel?

The last was Mario Vargas Llosa in 2010. Bookmakers suggest several as potential winners this year, including Homero Aridjis, a Mexican poet, novelist and environmental activist.

Ed Vulliamy, in a 2018 profile of Aridjis for The Guardian, said that his verse was “at once mystical, personally intimate and romantic.”

Ngugi wa Thiong’o

A Kenyan novelist and playwright whose work often focuses on Africa’s struggle with the legacies of colonialism, Ngugi wa Thiong’o would be a popular choice for the award. He also has a biography that many readers might find empowering.

In the late 1970s, Ngugi abandoned English as his main creative language, and instead chose to write in Gikuyu, his mother tongue.
