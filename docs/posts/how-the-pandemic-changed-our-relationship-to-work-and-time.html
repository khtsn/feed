How the Pandemic Changed Our Relationship to Work and Time

I have sometimes liked to imagine the hours in a day, or a week or a month, like swaths of fabric hanging in a clothing shop. Some are coarse and practical, others stretchy and deliciously frivolous. Some are filmy, made to be worn with weightless ease.

The most elastic that hours have ever felt to me was on Saturday afternoons when I was a child. My family observed the Sabbath, which meant that from sundown on Friday until sundown on Saturday we abstained: from driving and typing, from music and phone calls, from handling computers and spending cash. All those rules meant that time could seem like a problem in need of solving. There was too much of it and too little to do, the minutes seeming to multiply like pasta in Strega Nona’s pot. I disappeared into the worlds of Fudge, Matilda and James with his giant peach. I put on productions of “Peter Pan,” in which I cast myself as Peter, Wendy and Captain Hook, and my little brother as the crocodile.

I stopped keeping the Sabbath when I left home for college. There was no ceremony that accompanied the shift; it was a choice as swift as a pajama-themed party the first Friday night of college and a Monday exam that meant hitting the library on Saturday. There was so much to do that time no longer felt stretchy. It was denim, or leather, or like those American Apparel sweetheart dresses we wore out on weekends, the hours reminding me of their constrictive seams.

I was transported back to those childhood Sabbath afternoons recently, as I read two new books that ask us to question what we do with our time. One was “The Good Enough Job,” by Simone Stolzoff, a journalist and designer; the other was “All the Gold Stars,” by Rainesford Stauffer, also a journalist. Stolzoff’s book considers what our days and weeks might feel like if we didn’t place careers at their center. Stauffer’s probes how we might trade professional ambitions for a different kind of striving — for friendships, communities, even leisure.

It’s tough to describe our relationship with time. We have few words for it; it yields no visible effects. Colliding unexpectedly with a deadline or new year doesn’t result in a bruise. Marks of mishandled time might show up years later — as regret over a severed relationship, as a parent’s guilt about bedtimes missed — but not in the moment. The next day arrives, unfailingly, like clockwork.

But over the past three years it seems that most of us have been rethinking our time, and how to spend it. Workers in every corner of the economy are examining the conditions of their jobs and demanding better wages, flexible hours, expansive benefits or union representation.

It’s no wonder so many feel emboldened to reject shoddy work. The pandemic turned our economy inside out. The thing about earth-shattering events is that they have a way of shattering professional rules too, making old assumptions about labor seem less relevant, or at least less rigid. With World War II came an economy that welcomed women workers; with the Great Recession came an uptick in gigging and precarious work.

And with the pandemic came a collective reassessment of what we do when we’re off the clock. There was the psychedelic feel of hours in lockdown, when a single afternoon seemed to last three seasons. Jenny Odell watched moss spores arrive in her kitchen, an event she chronicled in her new book, “Saving Time”; Oliver Burkeman, in “Four Thousand Weeks,” imagined how people could live if they stopped trying to manage their inboxes. Then, after Covid vaccines, there was the rush of economic reopening, which prompted so many people to ask themselves if they were satisfied with how they spent their days. More than 40 million people quit their jobs.

Stolzoff and Stauffer look at the current labor force flux from new angles, and the results are more philosophical than self-help. There’s something about “rethinking work” that sounds like a career counseling session or a PowerPoint from H.R. Rethinking time feels more intimate.

In THE GOOD ENOUGH JOB: Reclaiming Life From Work (Portfolio, 239 pp., $28) — the title is an allusion to the British psychoanalyst D.W. Winnicott’s theory of “good enough” parenting — Stolzoff profiles nine people who have scaled back their commitment to a career in search of new kinds of meaning. There’s a chef who leaves a Michelin-star restaurant, a librarian repulsed by the idea that delight in her work should substitute for good pay, a software engineer who realizes how ludicrous it was for him to live in a box truck in Google’s parking lot. Stolzoff follows the subjects through hours they’re spending in looser ways, as they cook dinner at a co-op, surf in the Pacific Ocean or roll a joint on a rock in the middle of a river. (He has less to say about how his subjects are rethinking their finances.)

In ALL THE GOLD STARS: Reimagining Ambition and the Ways We Strive (Hachette Go, 282 pp., $28), Stauffer traces the history of the term “ambition,” how it shifted from being seen as a vice (synonymous with soliciting votes for office in ancient Rome) to a virtue (associated with serving God and country, through work). Then she asks whether we can be ambitious about life outside our careers, including in how we parent, take care of friends, get to know neighbors or even just play. She captures, too, the moments when people realize there’s a cost to spending time only on work: when a young woman learns that the company to which she’s devoted 40 to 50 hours every week won’t give her adequate leave to be with her sick mother; when another woman grasps that her self-worth is so bound up with her job performance that she has allowed her relationship to become a casualty of her professional stress.

Both books are part memoir, unsurprisingly. The boundaries between our jobs and our lives are porous. Stolzoff recalls hopping between careers, finally quitting his job and learning how to enjoy being idle. Stauffer describes how juggling freelance jobs made her physically sick.

When I was younger, it was the personal aspect of keeping the Sabbath that made me most uneasy. During restless Saturdays, sitting in synagogue watching men with their prayer books while I sneaked glances at my copy of Junie B. Jones, I wondered about the point of policing my time. In the Bible, there are few injunctions to work. Over and over there are commandments to rest. But it seemed hard to believe that anyone, let alone God, could care how I spent a Saturday afternoon.

Yet in the book “The Sabbath World,” the literary critic Judith Shulevitz teaches that keeping Sabbath is actually a social practice. She describes it as a noncompete clause of sorts. The minute one person starts working on the seventh day, everyone else feels compelled to work as well. Far from being just about abstention, then, the Sabbath becomes about communal action, or what Shulevitz refers to as the “social morality” of time. When one person keeps the Sabbath, others feel permission to do so too. In the same way, Stolzoff and Stauffer suggest how one worker’s choice to reject bad hours or bad pay encourages colleagues to follow suit. In each book, the choice to rethink work is both personal and collective.

Now that I don’t keep the Sabbath, the stretchiest my time feels is in an airport. I’m obsessively early for flights — so early that I sometimes get seated on the plane before mine — and I love the languid quality that an hour has when I’m wandering near my gate, choosing a Gatorade flavor to buy. Always, in Hudson News, there’s a shelf of books about turbocharging work: how to win the day, how to win the hour, how to influence God and your boss. I’ve wondered, lately, whether airports might consider a shelf for books about rest. There’s Stolzoff, Stauffer, Burkeman and Odell. These are inversions of beach reads, invitations not to escape but to luxuriate — books in search of a new texture for time.
