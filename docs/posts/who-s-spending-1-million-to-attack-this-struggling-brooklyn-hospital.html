Who’s Spending $1 Million to Attack This Struggling Brooklyn Hospital?

The three protesters stood outside Maimonides Medical Center in Brooklyn on an autumn Wednesday, holding signs denouncing the hospital’s rock-bottom ranking for cleanliness and calling for the ouster of its chief executive, Kenneth Gibbs. They had been there nearly every day since the middle of summer, their presence a continual irritant to hospital leadership.

One of the protesters, Derrick Taylor, explained that he had found the job on Craigslist and that he was earning $600 per week to protest and collect petitions. But he added that he also believed in the cause. “We are here to restore, not just to be a black eye,” he said. “And we have high-profile people behind us.”

The campaign they represent, Save Maimonides, may seem quixotic, fronted by paid canvassers and dwarfed by the whir and hum of Brooklyn’s largest independent hospital. But it has money behind it — at least $1 million so far — as well as a mystery: Who is spending all this money to disparage a hospital, and why?

Since last June, Save Maimonides has run a barrage of commercials in Yiddish and English blasting the hospital for being ranked last in the state for patient satisfaction. It has wrapped local buses in ads asking for people to share their stories of suffering there. In December, it submitted petitions with 30,000 signatures calling for leadership change and independent oversight of the hospital by the state’s Department of Health.

The campaign, officially founded by Mendy Reiner, an Orthodox Jewish businessman and philanthropist, says that it is a grass roots effort that simply seeks to improve the quality of care at the hospital, for both the Orthodox Jewish population that lives nearby and every other community that relies on it.

But hospital leaders suggest there are more nefarious reasons behind Save Maimonides. Mr. Gibbs, the hospital’s chief executive, considers it an aggressive disinformation campaign that seeks to weaken the hospital. The actual person behind it, he and other hospital executives argue, is not Mr. Reiner. Instead, they claim, it a wealthy nursing home operator based just north of New York City named Eliezer Scheiner, who had recently been in discussions about management with the hospital and, they suggest, would gain personally from a takeover.

Save Maimonides denies that Mr. Scheiner is even involved. Mr. Scheiner himself denies he is behind the campaign. But the group also declined to provide the names of any of its donors, saying people feared that going public would endanger their ability to get good care at the facility. The campaign maintains that it is only being aggressive in pointing out the hospital’s flaws because otherwise they won’t be fixed.

But can you save a hospital by attacking it?

A Community Institution

The Orthodox Jewish community has long had a special relationship with Maimonides, a sprawling institution in Borough Park, one of the nation’s most populous Jewish enclaves. The hospital was founded by Jewish philanthropists more than a century ago. It takes its name from one of the great Jewish sages, a 12th-century philosopher and doctor.

Though most of its patients are not Jewish — its emergency room echoes with Mandarin, Urdu, Albanian, Spanish, Russian and many other languages — Maimonides still has an exclusively kosher kitchen. And though Mr. Gibbs wants everyone to feel welcome, the hospital’s mission statement maintains that Maimonides “remains uniquely committed to serving the special health care needs of the Orthodox Jewish community.”

Day to day, Jewish community members volunteer, donate money and make no secret that a well-placed call can help ensure faster and smoother treatment, according to more than a dozen interviews. Even so, many people in the local Jewish community go to Manhattan hospitals for care when they can.

It’s a trend highlighted by the Save Maimonides campaign, which says the community deserves a truly top-notch hospital in its backyard. If care were better, it argues, more people with commercial insurance would go to the hospital, improving its bottom line. But hospital executives say the campaign is, in fact, accelerating the exodus by publicizing and overstating the hospital’s problems.

While Maimonides has an excellent track record of keeping people alive after heart attacks and does well in surgical care, for example, it fares poorly in other ways. Out of 152 hospitals in the state, patients scored Maimonides the worst in multiple categories, including “patient’s room and bathroom always kept clean,” “patients always got help as soon as they wanted,” “nurses always communicated well” and “staff always explained about medicines.” Also, patients are more likely to have complications like bloodstream and antibiotic-resistant staph infections than at an average hospital, according to the federal Centers for Medicaid and Medicare Services, which gave the facility two out of five stars.

Eli, a 38-year-old patient with a chronic disease, described from his hospital bed how his roommate’s bedpan had overflowed on the Sunday after Thanksgiving, sending bloody urine all over the floor. The nurse cleaned up what she could, he said, but he was told the remaining blood would have to wait, because the cleaning staff was not available on Sundays.

On past visits, Eli said, he had trouble getting his bedding changed when he really needed it; sometimes he went without an IV pole when there weren’t enough to go around. He said he believed that if he’d had connections, he might have been better served. He did not want to be identified by his last name, he said, because his wife feared the hospital would retaliate the next time he needed care.

Outside Maimonides Medical Center, Sara Schwartz, 52, signed a Save Maimonides petition calling for new leadership after visiting her mother-in-law upstairs. “They woke her up in the middle of the night to get an M.R.I., and they didn’t let her go to the bathroom first,” Ms. Schwartz explained. “They weren’t kind. When a person doesn’t feel good, you need that extra love and care.”

Some patients say they have had good experiences at Maimonides. Yasmine Ibrahim, 20, who lives in Flatbush, spent four hours in the emergency room on a recent Wednesday, checking to see whether her ketones were too high (a potentially life-threatening situation for Ms. Ibrahim, who is diabetic). She comes often. “They are very busy, but when they get to me, the care is really good,” she said. “They are always apologetic about the wait.”

But the Save Maimonides campaign, which is officially incorporated as the Brooklyn Health Initiative, has seized on the negative experiences and statistics. “Have you suffered from the shortage of staff? Have you waited hours for care? It’s time for change at Maimonides hospital,” one ad that wrapped a bus read.

Nurses at Maimonides have protested working and patient conditions at the hospital, but they are also upset by the Save Maimonides campaign, which has criticized the demeanor of hospital staff. (One campaign video likened treatment there to being in a prison.) Nancy Hagans, the president of the New York State Nurses Association and a registered nurse at Maimonides for 37 years, said the criticism was particularly galling because she believed the people running the campaign “own the worst nursing homes in the country,” though she wasn’t completely sure who they were.

“How do you talk to a faceless person?” she said.

Behind the Curtain

Despite an absence of proof and denials from Save Maimonides, hospital executives are convinced that they know exactly who is behind the campaign: Mr. Scheiner, a Borough Park native who has made a fortune investing in nursing homes in Texas, Florida, New York and beyond.

His mother was a longtime volunteer at Maimonides, and his father was the respected head of a Brooklyn yeshiva who was treated at the hospital and, ultimately, died there. After Mr. Scheiner, 53, went into the nursing home business, he became a stakeholder in at least 63 for-profit nursing homes, according to federal and state data.

Homes connected to Mr. Scheiner and his company, TL Management, have been the subject of several exposés recounting terrible lapses, including one resident who died after being left for hours in the Florida sun. Of the 43 homes that list him personally as a stakeholder in a federal Medicare ownership database, 17 have earned overall ratings of one or two stars, out of five, from the Center for Medicaid and Medicare Services, and 28 of them have earned a single star specifically for staffing, a review by The New York Times found.

John Gallagher, Mr. Scheiner’s spokesman, said that, in most cases, Mr. Scheiner was an investor in the nursing homes, not an operator, and that he “encourages management to make improvements.” He mentioned two homes that are operated by Mr. Scheiner that have three stars from regulators, including Beacon Rehab in Far Rockaway, Queens.

Regardless of issues at the nursing centers, within the Orthodox Jewish community, Mr. Scheiner’s philanthropy is legendary. Mr. Scheiner founded Bais Medrash Ohr Chaim, a bustling synagogue near his home in Monsey, N.Y., that is open for prayers all night and offers free coffee and soup to everyone who enters. In Lakewood, N.J., where the nation’s largest yeshiva is, he donated millions to raise stipends for postgraduate rabbinical students. He is known to travel by helicopter, and has met the pope.

As the Covid-19 pandemic raged in 2020, killing more than 600 patients by that September at Maimonides alone, he donated money to help set up a convalescent plasma donation program at the hospital. Later that year, Maimonides executives approached Mr. Scheiner for additional help.

At one meeting between Mr. Gibbs and Mr. Scheiner, the hospital says, Mr. Scheiner made an offer. He said he could bring in 16 new board members from the community, forming a de facto majority on what the hospital said was a 30-member board. Each would donate $2 million in charity to the facility.

To the executives, it sounded like a takeover attempt, which shocked them. But Mr. Scheiner did not want to “control” those people, his spokesman, Mr. Gallagher, said. “He only wanted to bring in people with the skills and resources to help Maimonides.”

Ultimately, Mr. Gibbs and Mr. Scheiner did work together to elect four new Orthodox Jewish members to the board, both sides said. A meeting was planned for 2022 to discuss progress. Then, in June 2022, things changed abruptly. “We woke up under attack,” Mr. Gibbs said.

Officials at Maimonides say they would actually like to relinquish control of the hospital, just not to Mr. Scheiner.

For nearly a decade, Maimonides has courted a merger with Northwell Health, the state’s largest health care system. The idea behind the plan, agreed to in 2015, was that the two systems would develop an affiliation and that, ultimately, Maimonides would become Northwell’s Brooklyn hub.

Mr. Gibbs, a former investment banker, helped design the plan, and he rose from board president to chief executive shortly after it was inked. Eugene Keilin, also an investment banker, has been the board president since.

But while the two systems remain affiliated, the plan to turn Maimonides into Northwell Health Brooklyn has been held back by Maimonides’s weak finances, Mr. Gibbs said. Though the hospital has sold off real estate and cut costs, the pandemic set back the effort again, leaving Maimonides with a $145 million operational deficit in 2021.

For now, any merger is on hold. “There are no plans to bring Maimonides in as a full member of Northwell,” said Michael Dowling, Northwell’s chief executive, citing Maimonides’s financial losses. “It would jeopardize the integrity and the well-being of Northwell, and we are just not going to do that.”

The core problem, Mr. Gibbs said, is that Maimonides is a safety-net hospital that mostly treats people with Medicare or Medicaid, which pays about 60 cents on the dollar compared with commercial insurance. Given the patients it treats, hospital executives argue, Maimonides is doing well with patient outcomes, even if its aging buildings lack the polish of the Manhattan medical centers, its packed emergency room lacks privacy and its air conditioning sometimes fails. But the low reimbursement rates create a perennial shortfall.

Save Maimonides actually says it wants the same thing as hospital management: for Northwell to take over. But the campaign argues that Mr. Gibbs, who earned $3.2 million in 2020, and Mr. Keilin are making poor decisions and dragging their heels on purpose, to retain the institution’s independence. (Mr. Gibbs’ 2020 earnings included a one-time payout; he earned $1.8 million in 2021 and 2022, the hospital said.)

Apart from Northwell’s involvement, the hospital leaders are dubious that the two sides ultimately agree on the future of the hospital. They suggest a different motivation behind the Save Maimonides campaign.

Mr. Scheiner’s business interests include companies that provide services and products to nursing homes. Hospital officials say they believe the Save Maimonides campaign is actually a play for influence over the enormous purchasing power of Maimonides’s $1.5 billion annual budget, in order to benefit those businesses. And they have put their allegations in writing.

“Gaining control of a hospital could be extremely beneficial for Scheiner’s extensive businesses in New York,” the hospital wrote on a flier that it distributed to reporters at a Save Maimonides event.

“It’s not done for altruistic reasons but because somebody wants to financially benefit,” said Dr. Jacob Shani, the chairman of the hospital’s heart and vascular center and its highest paid employee, with an annual salary of $3.5 million in 2020. “It’s a $1.4 billion entity,” he said, referring to Maimonides. “You can supply a lot of stuff to it.”

Despite the hospital’s assertions, however, Maimonides could offer no proof that Mr. Scheiner was the force behind the Save Maimonides effort beyond a single bus shelter ad that listed his name among two dozen rabbis and “askanim,” a Yiddish term for community activists, demanding that the hospital improve conditions.

For Mr. Scheiner, who declined requests to be interviewed for this article, the hospital’s motivations for focusing on him are clear. “Mr. Gibbs and the executive management are desperate to blame anyone but themselves for their failures,” his spokesman said. “The hospital’s leaders only care about their own power and will do anything to stay in power.”

Mr. Scheiner “walked away from trying to fix the hospital a year and a half ago” and “is not involved in or contributing to the Save Maimonides effort,” the spokesman added.

Saving Maimonides

Since last summer, the relationship between the campaign and the hospital has hardened into a kind of war. Each side circulates opposition research and contacts journalists in an effort to tarnish the other’s reputation.

The effort is hurting the hospital, Mr. Gibbs said. There were 25 percent fewer Jewish patients admitted in the third quarter of 2022 than in the same period in 2019, for example, though stays among non-Jews had recovered to prepandemic levels.

“Anybody who claims that they’re trying to help our institution by driving patients away and hurting our cash flow — it is just indefensible,” Mr. Gibbs said.

Mr. Reiner, who founded Renewal, a kidney donation nonprofit, shares the job of chairman of Save Maimonides with Sol Mayer, a co-president of Chai Lifeline, a children’s health charity. In January, Jimmy Li, an Asian community activist from Sunset Park who ran unsuccessfully in a Democratic congressional primary last year, was added as a third chairman.

On its website, Save Maimonides recounts in general terms how an effort from within the community to improve the hospital broke down, leading to its campaign to restore the beloved institution. The website is also translated into Chinese, as part of Save Maimonides’ effort to reach beyond the Orthodox community.

“Nobody’s looking to take over the hospital,” Mr. Reiner said in December. “We want a decent hospital to come in and take over Maimonides and service the communities around it. If it’s Northwell, great. Bring Northwell into the picture. Our goal is one goal, very simple: Fix the broken hospital.”

In recent months, Maimonides has spent tens of thousands of dollars on media consultants and positive advertising. But it has also been working to improve the hospital and the experience of its patients, including by hiring hundreds of new nurses, though Mr. Gibbs insists that Save Maimonides is not the reason. He did acknowledge, however, that the last-in-the-state showing for patient experience was “embarrassing.”

Mr. Scheiner, whether involved or not, has become a symbol of the battle at Maimonides. For people who dream that an Orthodox philanthropist would run a better hospital, he is a touchstone. For those who deny that Save Maimonides reflects real anger against hospital leadership, he has become a stalking horse.

“He has zero interest in becoming a board member at Maimonides hospital, but it’s a shame,” said Baruch Rosinger, who was the master of ceremonies at a Save Maimonides banquet in August, echoing the heroic terms with which some supporters refer to him. If he were, he said, “instead of 15 patients per nurse, we would have had 15 nurses per patient.”
