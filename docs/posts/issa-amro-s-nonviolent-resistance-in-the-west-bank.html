Issa Amro’s Nonviolent Resistance in the West Bank

On Oct. 7, as the Israeli military deployed across the country to combat Hamas, a group of soldiers waited on an ancient hill called Tel Rumeida in Hebron, a city in the West Bank. Their target was a burly Palestinian man, then 43, named Issa Amro. As Amro approached the soldiers through an olive grove, they moved in and struck him with their rifles, he later recalled. Amro put up little defense; he was handcuffed and taken to a military base. There he was tied to the back of a chair with zip ties. He was first blindfolded, then gagged. Then a beating began, Amro says. The ordeal lasted more than 10 hours.

Amro is no Hamas terrorist, however. He is a Palestinian activist — a man who for decades has urged his followers, whether in viral online videos or in speeches before leaders at the United Nations, to take the path of nonviolent resistance forged by Mohandas K. Gandhi and Martin Luther King Jr. That path, difficult in any environment, is a notably challenging one in the West Bank. Since Israel conquered the region in 1967, its military has forbidden nearly every form of protest, even things as small as gathering more than 10 people for a political conversation or simply waving the Palestinian flag.

The simplest acts of defiance can be seen as a threat: Amro once organized and videotaped an effort by a Palestinian man to pass through an Israeli checkpoint while balancing on his shoulder a large watermelon — a longtime symbol of Palestinian nationalism because its colors are those of the Palestinian flag. In the video, soldiers confiscate the watermelon and, as the camera zooms in, eye it warily as though it might explode. But Amro’s protests often offer a more direct challenge to Israeli authority. He has repeatedly filmed Israeli soldiers at close range and been beaten and grabbed by the throat when he refused to stop. When armed settlers eyed new sites in the city on which to raise the Israeli flag, Amro organized local Palestinians to “occupy” the land first, often in the dead of night. Using the same tactics, Amro helped start a kindergarten, tried to open a cinema in an abandoned factory and persuaded multiple Palestinians to move into homes after residents had fled. Amro himself lives in one such home, surrounded by some of the West Bank’s most violent settlers. “He’s trying to do to settlers what the settlers are doing to Palestinians,” Yehuda Shaul, a left-wing Israeli activist who has known Amro for nearly 20 years, told me. “He is an extremely stubborn person.”

The Israeli authorities, and even sometimes the West Bank’s Palestinian ones, have rarely viewed his actions as legitimate political activity. He has been arrested more times than he can count, evicted from his home for weekslong stretches, shot in both legs, and arraigned by both Israeli military courts and the Palestinian Authority on charges ranging from participating in an assembly without a permit to “causing strife” and “insulting the higher authorities.” Yet the cause of nonviolent Palestinian resistance has advanced little in recent years — and support for Hamas in the West Bank has grown following the Oct. 7 attack. Many of Amro’s neighbors praise his efforts as a human rights defender, but others question what his nonviolent approach has yielded after so long. Some have wondered aloud about his various stunts in Hebron. Why does he seem so determined to martyr himself?

Gagged the night of Oct. 7, Amro could say nothing to the soldier before him. Amro says he felt a rifle at his head, then flinched as he heard the trigger click on an empty chamber. He recalls that a group of Hebron settlers were let into the room, Amro’s own neighbors whose voices he recognized. He could hear them posing for pictures, snapping selfies as he remained tied and blindfolded. When the room was quiet again, Amro says, a soldier began to punch him. The soldier then told him Amro was his “bitch” and pulled out his penis, ordering him to perform oral sex, Amro recalls. He refused and was beaten again. Hours later, Amro was released. (In a response to written questions an Israeli military spokesman said: “With respect to Mr. Amro’s claims of violence on the part of the army, in the past or present, he has the option to file a complaint through the established channels.” Amro says that he has, but no action has been taken.)

Amro was asked no questions, he says, and as he walked home, he knew that the political landscape had changed, perhaps forever. The Oct. 7 attack, in one day of deadly violence, had done what years of nonviolent resistance had failed to: The entire world would be debating the fate of the Palestinians again. But not under the conditions Amro had imagined.

He felt his wrists, swollen from hours in the handcuffs. What place was there for someone like him when a war was about to begin?

Amro’s awakening to the possibilities of nonviolent action came at the beginning of 2003, at a notably violent moment in the West Bank. The Oslo Peace Accords, intended to open a path to Palestinian self-rule, had fallen apart a decade earlier when Yitzhak Rabin, the prime minister who pursued the deal, was murdered by a far-right Jewish law student. By 2000, the Second Intifada, or uprising, had begun; during the four years it lasted, Palestinians mounted suicide attacks inside Israel and the Israelis retaliated with armored sieges and airstrikes of the West Bank and Gaza.

In 2003, Amro was completing his final year of studies in electrical engineering at the Palestine Polytechnic University in Hebron. “I was a good student, and I wanted to become a professor in engineering,” he told me. “That was my dream.” But that January, Israeli soldiers descended on the campus, sealing its entrances. The teachers had “endorsed terror and acts of vengeance as part of their curriculum,” the Israeli military said, and the army had “a duty to prevent education that incites murder of Israelis.”

Until then, Amro had little interest in activism. “But at that point, I was ready to do anything, even to be violent,” he said. He opened an internet browser and typed in a search string: “How to make a revolution.” “Luckily,” Amro said, “what first came up was Martin Luther King.”

Amro was soon reading books by King and speeches by Nelson Mandela and diving into Gandhi’s concept of satyagraha, or “truth force.” He became especially interested in an American political theorist named Gene Sharp, whose writings would become influential in both the Arab Spring protests and the Occupy Wall Street movement.

Sharp warned that when authoritarians were overthrown through violence, those who next rose to power tended to be violent too. In his 1990 book, “Waging Nonviolent Struggle,” he described a technique that is instead “based on the ability to be stubborn, to refuse to cooperate, to disobey and to resist powerful opponents powerfully.” Corporations, Sharp argued, have been brought down when workers refuse to work, or consumers to consume. Military powers dissolve when orders are disobeyed by civilians. “Massive stubbornness can have powerful political consequences,” Sharp wrote. Amro, stubborn by nature, found this vision exhilarating.

He began organizing, starting a student committee to discuss what actions members might take. One of their first protests shut down a main thoroughfare in Hebron — one that was used by Palestinians, not Israelis. “Everyone asked me, ‘Why are you shutting this street down to our cars?’” Amro recalled. His answer: Palestinians had to be shaken out of their complacency first.

By that June, five months after the Israeli takeover of the university, Amro had managed to organize an occupation of campus buildings. Some 3,000 students, according to Amro, crossed the military barrier, and over the following weeks, they held classes, with the older students teaching the younger ones, as soldiers looked on. For the first time, Amro found himself in the cross hairs of both Israeli and Palestinian authorities: When Israeli soldiers threatened to demolish university buildings if the students didn’t leave, its Palestinian administrators urged Amro to stand down. Amro refused, and instead, the military relented. After seven months, Palestine Polytechnic reopened, a rare victory for Palestinians in the West Bank.

As Amro read more about South African history, he concluded that there was no way to talk about his own struggle without addressing the parallels to Mandela’s campaign against apartheid. His entire life in Hebron had been lived under a similar system of segregation, he told me: Israelis were given different license plates from West Bank Palestinians, which allowed them to drive on different roads; they had different colored identification cards, which entitled them to different rights from those, like Amro, who held West Bank Palestinian cards. If Amro was beaten by a settler on the streets of Hebron, the settler would be tried in a court in Israel under civilian law. If Amro carried out the beating, however, he would be tried in a military court, where the punishments were more severe. As a Palestinian, Amro was no longer even allowed to walk the street where he grew up.

Yet in Amro’s mind, Palestinian politics offered few paths out of the status quo. Armed factions like Hamas sought wars over peace deals; the Palestinian Authority had become a stateless bureaucracy known more for its corruption than for taking on Israel. In the rural villages of the West Bank, however, a small but growing movement had begun to challenge Israeli soldiers with marches, usually held on Friday, the Muslim holy day, often highlighting issues like grazing rights for livestock or water access. Amro saw potential for similar actions in Hebron, the West Bank’s largest city: a steady series of sit-ins, marches and boycotts, directed at challenging the Israeli occupation and settlements.

But even nonviolent resistance can be risky. In 2003, as Amro was leading his first protests, an American activist in the Gaza Strip named Rachel Corrie was crushed to death by an Israeli military bulldozer after she tried to block it from destroying a Palestinian home under demolition orders. In 2010, the year Amro received a Human Rights Defender of the Year award from the United Nations, a civilian flotilla carrying humanitarian aid approached a beach on the Gaza Strip and was met by Israeli commandos who boarded its flagship and killed nine of its crew.

In this conflict, nonviolence would be no shield from violence. Amos Gilad, an Israeli defense ministry official, put it more bluntly in 2010 in a leaked U.S. diplomatic cable about a wave of crackdowns on West Bank villagers. “We don’t do Gandhi very well,” Gilad said.

In January, I went to visit Amro at his home in the West Bank. The hill where he lives, Tel Rumeida, is surrounded by an enormous fence threaded with barbed wire and punctuated by Israeli military checkpoints. The one that I, my Palestinian colleague Rami Nazzal, who was translating, and a security adviser approached that day was topped by an assault rifle attached to a camera that could be operated from afar, a remote-controlled sniper. It took us about 25 minutes to pass through T.S.A.-style security during which our bags were searched and our IDs checked, and we were told to take off our belts, shoes, jackets and keys before passing through a metal detector.

Amro’s best-known act of protest is probably the place where he lives. He had taken to heart King’s counsel to African Americans not to abandon the South, despite the presence of the Ku Klux Klan. As the settlers’ influence grew in Hebron, many Palestinians in the Israeli-controlled zone moved out of the city altogether. Soon afterward, many of those homes were declared “abandoned” by the Israeli military and turned into an army base; eventually, if the soldiers decamped, settlers sometimes arrived with their families.

“Welcome to the daily life of all my neighbors,” said Amro, who was waiting on the other side of the barrier for us. No sooner had we started to walk toward Amro’s home than we ran into another checkpoint, an impromptu roadblock manned by a pair of soldiers. One asked Amro where he was going.

“But we know each other,” Amro began. “Don’t you know where I’m going?” (Amro often challenges authority by responding to a question with a question.)

He asked another: “What’s that?” Amro was now pointing to a patch on the soldier’s uniform, and we all looked to read what it said.

“HAMAS HUNTING CLUB” was embroidered over a skull and bones and a pair of rifles, an accessory that has become popular since Oct. 7.

“You can’t wear this, it’s not allowed,” Amro continued, noting that the military banned adding personal accessories to uniforms.

“So you support Hamas then?” the soldier asked.

Amro offered no reply, but the soldier seemed to have had enough of him and let us continue to his house. Amro and his Palestinian neighbors are no longer allowed to walk on the street that leads to their homes, which the military has set aside for the exclusive use of Israeli settlers. We followed Amro down a muddy path that the Palestinians had cut through one another’s backyards, under clotheslines with crisp socks hanging out to dry and past shutters closed to guard what little privacy remained there. An escarpment rose above the neighborhood, and we could see Israeli flags fluttering on the balconies of the Jewish settlers who lived there. This was cohabitation without coexistence: The backyards of the Palestinian neighbors had been turned into a trash heap where settlers cast down all manner of refuse, including empty beer bottles, rotten food and dirty diapers. An old refrigerator rusted on its side, vines growing over the door.

While Hebron’s occupation goes back for generations, the misery that makes this city unique can be traced to a more recent date: Feb. 25, 1994. Early that morning, Baruch Goldstein, an Israeli-American settler armed with an automatic rifle, set out to the Cave of the Patriarchs, where tradition holds that the biblical Abraham was buried. The site is known to Muslims as the Ibrahimi Mosque. Goldstein then opened fire on Muslims at prayer there, killing 29 people. Rather than removing the settlers, who were there illegally under international law, Israel expanded its military presence in Hebron, taking control of a large swath of its city center, which it said was necessary to prevent future unrest. Hebron’s main commercial thoroughfare, Al-Shuhada Street, was shut down, and much of the perimeter was barricaded. The scenes today — the barbed wire, the checkpoints, the remote-controlled sniper — all flow from Goldstein’s attack.

We made it to the gate of Amro’s home, and in the valley below we could see the white walls that surround the Cave of the Patriarchs. At the top of the hill, it felt almost as if we were floating above the anger of the city below: There were old olive trees and a carpet of white irises that had just started to bloom. And there was the sound of people approaching. As they drew closer, we saw that they weren’t Israeli soldiers but, rather, Europeans. A small delegation, led by Alexandre Stutzmann, a kind of European ambassador to the West Bank and the Gaza Strip, had come to see how Amro was faring.

Alone, Amro has the air of an introvert, but when he is in front of even a small crowd, his sentences grow longer, and he gestures animatedly, like a professor in a lecture hall. But before long, his talk to the diplomats was interrupted by the buzzing sound of a small drone.

“Is this yours?” Stutzmann asked Amro.

It wasn’t. The drone drew closer, its buzzing getting louder. We moved to the other side of the olive grove. So did the drone. It seemed to pause for a moment to snap a picture of each person in the group. An Israeli soldier, standing nearby in a crow’s nest built on top of a confiscated Palestinian home, stared at the drone, too.

“Is this your drone?” shouted Amro, trying to be heard above the din.

“I don’t speak English,” the soldier shouted back in English.

Amro repeated the question in Hebrew.

“Mind your own business!” the soldier said in Hebrew.

Amro guessed the drone might belong to one of his settler neighbors. The diplomats suggested we go inside for some privacy. Amro said that if the settlers wanted to spy on him, they could easily do so from their porches.

“It would be convenient to get rid of you,” Stutzmann said, craning toward one of the porches to see if he could make out any of the neighbors. “Your house would be theirs.”

In fact, the house, which originally belonged to a Palestinian family that left for East Jerusalem in the 1990s, had been claimed by others before. Around 2001, the army took over the house as a lookout; in 2007, when the army left, settlers began to arrive. Amro says he spent months trying to persuade the original Palestinian owners to return, but they refused, saying they feared the armed settlers. So Amro asked the family if he could move into the house, signing a lease. With the settlers still claiming the house as their own, Amro moved in, too, bringing his mattress and other basic items. When the military arrived, Amro showed them the lease, but they declared the house a temporary “closed military zone,” a designation used to block civilians from an area. Amro returned the next day, after the order expired. This time, Amro was arrested and interrogated. Amro kept coming back, kept being arrested. Sometimes when he was physically blocked from entering, he slept on the ground outside the house.

Israel has long banned Palestinians from bringing in construction materials, which Israeli officials claim might be used for violent purposes. Palestinians say that the real reason is to keep Palestinians from repairing and building homes. Over several months, Amro smuggled in concrete for repairs in flour bags. Using his engineering training, he rewired the house. Soon dozens of activists began to join him for sit-ins. Amro filed a lawsuit, and officials eventually relented, letting him stay. He lives there today, and the home has also become the headquarters of his organization, Youth Against Settlements.

Stutzmann and the Europeans finished their coffee on Amro’s patio, and we all headed out. As we reached the street — one that Palestinians were allowed to use — Amro ran into a neighbor, an Orthodox Jewish woman, who was loading her child’s stroller into a station wagon. Amro told me that it was her husband who arrested him before his beating on Oct. 7. Amro tried to move us along without making eye contact, but she stopped him.

“Issa,” she said.

“Hello,” Amro said.

“We’re very sorry that you lost your brother — your dear brother,” she said in English. Amro’s brother, Ahmad, died of a heart attack in December.

But she was grinning. The woman was not sorry.

“Oh, this is so sad,” she said sarcastically.

The settler continued to taunt Amro. She told him he was a pedophile. She called him a terrorist. I scanned his face, waiting for a reaction. This was a man who had lost his brother, and a Palestinian who had been forced out of his home time and again. But all that was visible was the man who had spent years telling people to turn the other cheek. He asked us to keep moving.

“Thank you,” he said to the woman as we left.

In March 2013, during a visit by Barack Obama to the West Bank, Amro led a protest of Palestinians wearing masks of Obama and Martin Luther King Jr. along with shirts that read: “I have a dream.” (“The idea was to get ‘Obama’ arrested by the Israelis, and that is what happened,” Amro told me.) Unknown to Amro, the military was building a criminal case against him, and in 2016, a military court charged him with 18 counts for what it deemed illegal protests over the last six years, including insulting a soldier, resisting arrest and participating in an assembly without a permit.

Amro had been arrested before, but he had never faced the possibility of prison time for his activism. What’s more, the prosecutor would be an active-duty soldier and the judge, Lt. Col. Menachem Lieberman, was a settler. Over the years, Amro had watched many Palestinian activists accept reduced sentences under plea deals rather than risk a trial under such circumstances. But Amro wasn’t interested in a deal: He would rather go to prison, he told me, than tell a court his protests had been crimes.

For Amro, the trial was about more than winning his freedom; it was a rare chance to present his case to the Israeli public because the Israeli press would cover the trial. Amro told me, “I’m not allowed to do any kind of nonviolent resistance, and I wanted people to see that.”

The trial was held at Ofer military base near Ramallah. The prosecution’s case focused on the physical allegations against Amro, including that he had resisted arrest by soldiers. At one point, the court had to determine whether Amro said “you are stupid” to a soldier. Amro says his words were “I’m not stupid.” The soldier, when questioned, said he spoke only limited English. (Whatever it was Amro actually said, he told me he was subsequently beaten by the soldier.)

Amro’s defense was built on a more philosophical issue: Did Palestinians have the right to peacefully defy the Israeli occupation? “According to international law, I am allowed as a Palestinian to express my opinion, to practice my right to general assembly and to participate in peaceful activities,” Amro told the court, according to an account of the trial published in The New York Review of Books.

“Which international law?” the judge asked.

“The Geneva Convention,” Amro said.

“You have a right to gather at a protest of any amount of people?” the judge asked.

“We can gather without any violence,” Amro said.

“They said you were inciting,” the judge said.

“I don’t incite,” Amro said. “And they misuse that term. I’m a person who believes in nonviolence.”

After the court adjourned to decide Amro’s fate, the military’s approach to Palestinian protests grew more severe. In March 2018, activists in Gaza organized what they called the Great Return March, a set of weekly protests at Gaza’s border fence. It was the kind of mass mobilization Amro had dreamed of in Hebron. But Israeli soldiers repeatedly opened fire on the crowds as some people rushed the fence, killing an estimated 189 Palestinians and injuring more than 6,000 over a nine-month period, according to the United Nations. The dead included children, journalists and first responders.

In 2021, after five years of deliberation, the military found Amro guilty of six of the charges, including participating in an unauthorized assembly, and acquitted him of the other 12. He was given a three-month suspended prison sentence and ordered to pay a fine; the prison time was eventually reduced to two years’ probation. Two charges were overturned on appeal.

But Amro wanted the last word on the matter. Courtroom cases always have an aspect of theater to them, and during Amro’s appeal, an Israeli playwright named Einat Weizman was in attendance researching a possible play based on his case. In the months after the trial concluded, she and Amro met in Hebron to outline a script. They titled the play “How to Make a Revolution.”

In this version of the trial, Amro breaks the fourth wall, testifying directly to the audience while also questioning the judge and prosecutor as he looks over their shoulders to inspect the evidence. In one performance of the final monologue, the character of Amro offers a bleak assessment of the Palestinian struggle, asking whether revolution is even possible. “If we participate in armed resistance, then this is terrorism, right?” he says, improvising from the script. “But if we ask to boycott — then this is antisemitism, right? If we fully cooperate with the Palestinian Authority, then our lands will be stolen, we’ll still be killed and no one will be blamed. So, I’m asking. What the [expletive] do you want us to do?”

Amro’s final act of protest came on the night of the play’s premiere in 2022, Jaffa, the Israeli port town, with Tamer Nafar, a well-known Palestinian rapper born in Israel, in the role of Amro. The real Amro said he asked Israeli officials for permission to attend the premiere but was denied. Instead, he traveled to the nearby West Bank city Yatta, where, before Oct. 7, taxis with Israeli license plates shuttled Palestinian laborers without work permits into Israel. Amro and the workers were all waved through the checkpoint. Amro told me he wanted to see his play — but he also wanted to prove that Israel’s separation wall with the West Bank, a series of barriers that the country has been erecting as a security measure for the last two decades, did little to stop Palestinians from entering. “Where there is a wall, there is a hole,” he said.

On another day, Amro and the rest of our group drove to the eastern edge of Hebron. We sat in the car overlooking the Kiryat Arba settlement, which is home to roughly 7,000 people, including Israel’s security minister and, at one time, Baruch Goldstein. Its identical white buildings looked like something from a science-fiction movie, underscoring the parallel universes that Jews and Arabs inhabit. They were separate worlds except for this spot where Amro had taken us: A Palestinian road that faced the settlement where the signs of the storefronts were all in Hebrew. It was a strip mall of sorts, a place where Palestinians had long offered their services to settlers looking for low prices.

But it now looked like a ghost town. Amro pulled out his cellphone and began to record a video to post to Instagram, where he has more than 20,000 followers: “All the shops here are closed by military orders since Oct. 7,” he began. “Here the Israeli military is creating facts on the ground to prevent people to live properly here.”

Moments later, we were surrounded by military vehicles. Soldiers, carrying rifles, emerged in battle gear. Amro raised his hands. A commander barked out orders in Hebrew.

My colleague, Nazzal, and I showed the soldiers our press cards and passports. The soldiers separated us: Nazzal and Amro, both Arab, were taken to one side of the street, while the non-Arabs, myself and the security adviser, were told to stand on the other side. One soldier took my phone and began swiping through its contents. On the other side of the street, Amro and Nazzal were being ordered to sit on the ground. Amro refused. “Be aware that I am recording your voices now,” Amro said, pointing to his phone.

After about half an hour, the Israeli soldiers who detained us let us go. They ordered us to walk in separate directions, saying Amro couldn’t come with us to the car. Amro shot me a concerned look; he insisted that we not be separated. After a few minutes of arguing, the soldiers let us leave together. Once we were out of earshot, Amro told me why he was so worried about being split up. He feared if there were no witnesses, the soldiers might beat him again, as they had on Oct. 7.

Two days later, Amro wanted to take me to a place that was shuttered in his youth, and so we returned to Al-Shuhada Street, which was closed in the years after Goldstein’s 1994 massacre. The wind blew quietly on the empty street. “This used to be our Times Square,” he began. “My father used to hold my arm because of the number of Palestinian customers.” He showed me an abandoned corner where several years ago he brought a bushel of cabbages with other activists and set up a mock “market” to sell them on the street. They were soon surrounded by what he described as a “platoon of soldiers” and ordered to stop. We continued to walk down Al-Shuhada Street until we encountered the military men stationed there, who raised their arms for us to go no further.

“This area is for the Jewish people,” said one of them, a young soldier in his 20s who was toting a rifle and said his name was Joseph. “A lot of terrorists come down this street,” he continued, gesturing toward Amro.

Amro shook his head as we wandered off. “I’ve known Joseph since he was a child,” he said. “He used to attack me, throw stones. He was never a good boy or a smart boy.”

Even if Amro couldn’t walk down the street, there were plenty of things he said he could do that day. He needed to check in on some neighbors whose gas heater had broken down and couldn’t be fixed because the military had stopped repairmen from entering the neighborhood. There were 11 kids in the house who had suffered without heat for two weeks. There were more bags of concrete he needed to sneak into the neighborhood: He was trying to help someone reinforce a door that was damaged during a search by Israeli soldiers.

“I’m constantly asked: Where is the Palestinian Gandhi? Where is the Palestinian Mandela?” Miko Peled, an Israeli-American activist who has long been Amro’s friend, said about Amro at a talk in Jordan this winter. Amro had spent his adult life preaching nonviolence and practicing it himself. During that time, he was arrested, convicted, spat on by settlers and evicted from his home on countless occasions. Last year, a soldier grabbed him by the throat and shoved him, an attack that was filmed and widely viewed online.

I could not help recalling what Shaul, the activist, had said about how Oct. 7 radically changed the world for Amro: There were now limits to how far he could take his nonviolent actions, given the Israeli anger over the unprecedented terrorist attack. Gene Sharp, whose activist handbooks Amro read as a student, had warned his methods weren’t foolproof. Many mass movements achieved their goals, Sharp said. Some even toppled governments. But others, when faced with a well-armed state, failed — or, in the face of failure, were subsumed by violence themselves.

“What Issa Amro is doing, turning the other cheek, it might be on the verge of extinction now,” Shaul told me. “But that’s not what matters. Issa is keeping this flame alive and waiting for better opportunities and conditions, and one day, just maybe, the fire will catch and maybe the flames will go very high.”
