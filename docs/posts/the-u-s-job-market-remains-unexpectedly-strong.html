The U.S. Job Market Remains Unexpectedly Strong

Briefing|The U.S. Job Market Remains Unexpectedly Strong

Surprising job numbers

The American labor market is proving to be unexpectedly resilient despite some big headwinds, including the Federal Reserve’s attempts to slow down the economy by hiking interest rates.

Data released today showed that employers added 253,000 jobs last month, far more than expected. Wages rose and the unemployment rate fell to 3.4 percent, matching January’s level, which was the lowest since 1969.

“I don’t make predictions,” my colleague Ben Casselman told me. “But honestly, I was bracing for this morning’s report to show a sharp downturn, and instead it showed the opposite.”

The job market’s continued strength is a problem for Fed officials, who are hoping that the broader economy will cool off in order to tame inflation.

But it’s not all bad news: The tight labor conditions have been a boon for many workers, especially those at the bottom of the pay scale. The unemployment rate for Black Americans is at its lowest point on record and a growing number of women ages 25 to 54 are employed.

“Even as we worry about the risks of a recession — and those risks are very real — there’s also a lot to celebrate here,” Ben said.

Russia’s mercenary leader threatens to walk

Yevgeny Prigozhin, the head of Russia’s Wagner private military company, said he would pull his fighters out of Bakhmut next week if the defense ministry doesn’t give him more ammunition. He delivered the threat in a scorching video in which he railed against Russia’s military leaders while walking among the corpses of his fighters killed in Bakhmut, one of the longest and bloodiest battles in the war in Ukraine.

Prigozhin, who previously accused Russian military leaders of treason, has made similar threats in the past, but he has never before given a date.

Covid is no longer a global pandemic

The World Health Organization announced today that it was ending the emergency it declared over Covid more than three years ago. The milestone marks the end of an era in which the coronavirus killed millions and reshaped lives. But W.H.O. officials cautioned countries against dismantling their Covid response systems.

The C.D.C. said it would scale back its coronavirus tracking efforts and no longer report the percentage of tests that come back positive. Rochelle Walensky, who took over as the agency’s director in the middle of the pandemic, also announced that she is stepping down.

The monarchy persists. But why?

London will be replete with extravagance and pageantry tomorrow for the coronation of King Charles III, Britain’s first new monarch in 70 years. The event will draw the world’s attention to a royal family that elicits both fascination and skepticism.

Questions about the monarchy’s legitimacy have been around for more than 1,000 years. But for many people, my colleague Sarah Lyall writes, it would be difficult to disentangle the royal family from British identity.

The most exciting two minutes in sports

Competing with the coronation tomorrow (at least on this side of the Atlantic) is the 149th running of the Kentucky Derby. There will be flamboyant fashion and hundreds of millions of dollars wagered.

Searching for sacred relics and ancient secrets

Next week’s release of Tears of the Kingdom, the latest Legend of Zelda game from Nintendo, comes after years of anticipation. Die-hard fans have spent months combing clips and previews, looking for clues.

Such obsessive attention is only fitting for a game like Zelda, which is centered around discovery. For the past four decades, players have been maneuvering Link, the game’s protagonist, around the mysterious kingdom of Hyrule. Now they can’t wait to get started on the next journey.
