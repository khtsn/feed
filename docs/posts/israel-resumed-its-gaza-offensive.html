Israel resumed its Gaza offensive

A weeklong cease-fire in the Gaza Strip collapsed this morning, with both Israel and Hamas blaming the other for the breakdown of the fragile truce.

Shortly before the cease-fire expired at 7 a.m. local time, Israel said it had intercepted a projectile fired from Gaza. Moments after the deadline passed, Israel announced that it was restarting military operations. Since the resumption of fighting, the Israeli military said it had launched 200 strikes. Here’s the latest.

The cease-fire had allowed for the exchange of scores of hostages and prisoners. Ultimately the two sides failed to overcome disagreements, including about how to define soldiers versus civilians and how many Palestinian prisoners Israel would release for its hostages, officials from Israel and Hamas said.

International mediators said talks were continuing in the hopes of reviving the truce. But Israeli officials expressed determination to carry on with their campaign to eradicate Hamas.

The next phase of Israel’s offensive is expected to target southern Gaza, where Israel believes Hamas’s top leadership is in hiding. U.S. officials have made it clear to Israel that it cannot pursue a campaign in the south that would have the same devastating consequences as in the north. During the next phase, the Israeli military may be more precise in its combat operations, U.S. officials say.

Ignored warning: Israel obtained Hamas’s battle plan for the Oct. 7 attack more than a year before it happened, documents show. Israeli military and intelligence officials dismissed the plan, considering it too difficult for Hamas to carry out.

The House expelled George Santos

Representative George Santos, the New York Republican whose tapestry of lies and schemes made him a figure of national ridicule and the subject of a federal indictment, was expelled from Congress today. The House of Representatives voted 311 to 114 in favor of expulsion, with 105 Republicans supporting the measure.

Santos is the first person to be expelled from the House without first being convicted of a federal crime or supporting the Confederacy. His forced departure leaves Republicans with an even thinner majority in Congress and sets the stage for a high-stakes special election early next year to fill his swing seat.

Remembering Sandra Day O’Connor

Sandra Day O’Connor, the first woman to sit on the Supreme Court, died today at 93.

President Ronald Reagan named her to the court in 1981, and she served for 24 years, retiring in January 2006 to care for her ailing husband. During a crucial period in American law — when abortion, affirmative action, sex discrimination and voting rights were on the docket — she was the most powerful woman in the country.

What not to say to your partner

Having a fight with your partner? There are some phrases people commonly use that therapists say only make the situation worse.

Generalizations like “you always … ” and “you never … ” are often exaggerations, and they don’t acknowledge any efforts your partner is trying to make. Comparisons like “you should be more like … ” can lead to jealousy. We spoke to therapists about other phrases they wish couples would stop saying during conflicts.
