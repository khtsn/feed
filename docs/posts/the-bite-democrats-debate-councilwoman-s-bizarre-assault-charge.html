‘The Bite’: Democrats Debate Councilwoman’s Bizarre Assault Charge

Susan Zhuang, a first-year City Council member from Brooklyn, was charged with assault for biting a police officer. Democrats are split over how harshly to reprimand her.

July 18, 2024Updated 4:54 p.m. ET

A midsummer City Council meeting in New York can often be a sleepy event, but the gathering on Thursday was a clear exception.

Everyone, after all, was still talking about the Bite.

A day earlier, Susan Zhuang, a first-year Democratic councilwoman from Brooklyn who ran on a law-and-order message, was charged with assault after the police said she had bitten an officer at a protest over a proposed homeless shelter.

So, as the Council convened its so-called stated meeting, the formal gathering during which bills are introduced or passed, Ms. Zhuang drew much of the attention — and she wasn’t even there.

She held her own news conference near the scene of her alleged crime in Brooklyn, although a police photo of the bloody circular gash on the officer’s arm put Ms. Zhuang more in the position of explaining the confrontation than denying it.

Speaking in front of an enthusiastic crowd of well-wishers, Ms. Zhuang said that she was trying to protect an older woman who had tumbled to the ground by police barricades erected at the protest. She said that she and other protesters had asked in vain for a barricade to be removed to give the woman safe passage, and the situation quickly deteriorated.

“I just couldn’t understand what happened,” she said, adding: “I was protecting 81-year-old grandma,” pointing to a woman seated toward the front.

Ms. Zhuang, 38, represents a growing constituency in New York City politics: Asian Americans whose concerns about crime and education have fueled a shift toward the right. She was elected last year in a new district created to amplify the voices of Asian voters.

In a city where Democrats outnumber Republicans by more than six to one, there is plenty of ideological diversity among Democrats, and Ms. Zhuang is on the conservative edge of her party. She is part of the Council’s nine-member Common-Sense Caucus, which is comprised mostly of Republicans, including Inna Vernikov, who was arrested in October for bringing a gun to a pro-Palestinian rally.

The political reaction to the biting also seemed to underscore the differences among Democrats.

The powerful Brooklyn Democratic machine immediately defended Ms. Zhuang. Assemblywoman Rodneyse Bichotte Hermelyn, the borough’s party chair who is close with Mayor Eric Adams, called her arrest “unlawful.” She said that Ms. Zhuang was “suddenly attacked by a mob of unknown forces” and appeared to have been choked and that she had responded in self-defense.

Justin Brannan, a council member from Brooklyn who has feuded with Ms. Bichotte Hermelyn, responded on social media: “Y’all non compos mentis” — using a Latin phrase to suggest that the assemblywoman was “not of sound mind.”

Mr. Brannan said in a statement that Ms. Zhuang’s actions were “deeply disturbing and unbecoming of an elected official.”

Mr. Adams, who counts Ms. Zhuang as an ally in his coalition of moderate Democrats, said that he was disappointed in her.

“She’s a new young councilperson that wants to advocate on behalf of her community, and these are extremely emotional issues that we’re talking about, but it was a pretty nasty bite,” the mayor said in an interview on 1010 WINS radio on Thursday.

At a news conference on Thursday, Adrienne Adams, the speaker of the City Council, said she had not yet spoken with Ms. Zhuang, but planned to do so later in the day. She referred to videos she had seen of the altercation as “disturbing,” but said that the legal process would have to play out before any decisions were made regarding Ms. Zhuang’s status on the Council.

“Violence is completely unacceptable and respect for our city workers as they do their job, it’s just not negotiable,” Ms. Adams said.

The protest was in response to a city plan to turn a 6,000-square-foot building in Gravesend into a new men’s homeless shelter that could house up to 150 people.

Ms. Zhuang was part of a group of demonstrators pushing barricades into law enforcement officers, according to police charging documents. As officers tried to control the scene, Ms. Zhuang turned her head toward Deputy Chief Frank DiGiacomo and bit him on the forearm, the complaint said.

Outside Brooklyn Criminal Court on Wednesday evening, after Ms. Zhuang’s arraignment, her lawyer, Sarah Krissoff, was asked if her client had bitten an officer. “A lot of times during the course of arrests, things happen,” she said.

Chief DiGiacomo was left with “broken skin and teeth marks” and “substantial pain,” according to the complaint. He was taken to a hospital where he was given a tetanus shot and antiviral medication.

Mr. Adams, who has appeared at events with Ms. Zhuang and included quotes from her in official news releases, said in an interview on NY1 that she was wrong to oppose the shelter. The mayor said that there were no homeless shelters in Ms. Zhuang’s district while other neighborhoods had more than a dozen.

“Everyone must share in this homeless crisis and the migrant asylum-seeker crisis,” the mayor said.

Before Ms. Zhuang began her news conference, two men who said they were arrested in Wednesday’s melee displayed their charging documents, torn clothing and bruises, prompting a round of applause.

The reception was far more enthusiastic when the councilwoman arrived shortly after 2 p.m., with the room erupting in clapping, raised fists and chants of “Susan.”

A video of the police interaction, which showed the older woman lying under a barricade, led to a chorus of boos.

The news conference at City Hall held by Ms. Adams, the Council speaker, was more orderly. She sought to turn attention back to the Council’s battle with the mayor over a bill to wrest more control over his appointments.

The Council has pushed to vet more of the mayor’s city commissioners, with the issue potentially being placed on the ballot in November. Mr. Adams, however, could prevent that from happening by offering a ballot measure from his own charter commission.

As for Ms. Zhuang, Ms. Adams voiced doubt that other council members would be treated as gently by the mayor as she had been.

“I honestly don’t think that some of my other members would be receiving the same level of grace right now from the mayor,” she said.
