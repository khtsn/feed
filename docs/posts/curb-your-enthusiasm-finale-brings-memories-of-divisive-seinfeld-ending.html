As “Curb Your Enthusiasm” draws to a close, the “Seinfeld” co-creator gets another shot at ending a TV show.

April 6, 2024, 5:04 a.m. ET

Larry David has long defended the “Seinfeld” finale. He’s often been its lone champion as critics, fans and the cast, including Jerry Seinfeld, have continued to lament the conclusion of one of television’s most successful, enduring sitcoms.

On Sunday, David — the “Seinfeld” co-creator who left the show after its seventh season but returned to write the two-part finale, which aired on May 14, 1998 — will wrap up his other popular show: “Curb Your Enthusiasm,” the on-again, off-again 12-season HBO comedy that started in 2000. And, if the signs are to be believed, the final episode may pay homage to the much-maligned “Seinfeld” send-off.

If you didn’t experience it, it’s a tall order to convey the hype that surrounded the end of “Seinfeld,” which took the gang out of New York City and landed them in a Massachusetts jail for violating a Good Samaritan Law. A trial included a parade of character witnesses, many of them wronged by the defendants over nine seasons, attesting to their unethical behavior. Jerry, George, Elaine and Kramer were found guilty of, as the prosecutor put it, “selfishness, self-absorption, immaturity and greed.”

“Seinfeld” was at the peak of its popularity, a cultural juggernaut and still making record profits for NBC at the end of its run — about $200 million a year, according to advertising industry estimates at that time. Nonetheless, Seinfeld was ready to close shop, turning down an offer from the network that would have been the most lucrative deal ever extended to a television star.

“We’ve all seen a million athletes where you say, ‘I wish they didn’t do those last two years,’” Seinfeld said at the time. “I wanted the end to be from a point of strength. I wanted the end to be graceful.”

But, by most accounts, graceful is not the first word that comes to mind for the finale. The lead-up to it alone was “the longest, most carefully orchestrated goodbye in television history,” as the critic Caryn James put it in a Times article two days before the broadcast. “The hype has become excruciating. The tone of this farewell suggests manufactured hysteria, not an outpouring of affection, despite the show’s immense popularity.”

A staggering 76 million viewers watched it, but the hangover quickly set in. “The show’s swan song was off-key and bloated,” Ken Tucker wrote in his Entertainment Weekly review at the time, adding that it was like “taking your doctoral exam in Seinology — and about as funny.” It was a sentiment shared by many viewers. (One of the most favorable reviews came from The Times; James called it “hilarious.”) And it hasn’t aged into a better reputation in the decades since. A 2010 article in Time magazine recalled that the reception of the episode was “downright awful.” In 2015, Rolling Stone ranked it as one of the worst TV finales.

Seinfeld himself has perhaps been the most vocal about the episode in the years since. In a 2014 Q. and A. on Reddit, he said that he was “happy” with it and that he didn’t “believe in trying to change the past.” But in 2017, at a New Yorker Festival, he called it an “impossible” episode. “I sometimes think we really shouldn’t have even done it,” he said. “There was a lot of pressure on us at that time to do one big last show, but big is always bad in comedy.”

In a 2013 interview with the Television Academy, Jason Alexander, who played George, called it “a good episode, not a great episode.” But he said he loved the idea and appreciated that David found an organic way to bring back characters who had shaped the show: the Soup Nazi, for example, or the low-talker. “We never really got to say thank you,” Alexander said.

And in probably the funniest sendup of the episode, Julia Louis-Dreyfus, who played Elaine, joked on the final “Late Show With David Letterman” Top 10 List: “Thanks for letting me take part in another hugely disappointing series finale.”

In its defense, David has repeatedly said that, given the buildup and popularity, there was no way to live up to expectations.

“Everybody has it in their head what they think this show should be,” he told Charlie Rose in a 1998 interview, ahead of the finale. “Everybody has their own scenario going, so you have to top their scenario. Not only do you have to top their scenario, you have to come up with something that really brings some closure to the entire series. So it was a daunting prospect.”

If “Curb” goes for a redo of sorts on Sunday, it wouldn’t be the first time David has reopened this can of worms. In Season 7, he reunited Seinfeld, Alexander, Louis-Dreyfus and Michael Richards, who played Kramer, for an extended “Seinfeld” reunion story line. At one point, Seinfeld jokes to David, “We already screwed up one finale.” To which David responds, “We didn’t screw up a finale, that was a good finale!”

The episode title of the final “Curb” — “No Lessons Learned” — certainly nods at the core ethos of “Seinfeld,” minted by David: “no hugging, no learning,” meaning the characters don’t evolve as people or become sentimental. (Not to mention that Larry is, in fact, facing a trial this season.) And the “Curb” finale trailer shows a cavalcade of fed-up characters ready to shove, punch and kick David to the, well, curb.

Despite the collective disappointment around the “Seinfeld” finale, it will always have its place as one of the most watched televised events of all time, placed firmly among the likes of the Apollo 11 moon landing, most Super Bowls, a couple of presidential debates and two other highly anticipated final episodes: “Cheers” and “M.A.S.H.”

“Curb,” which drew around one million viewers for its Season 12 premiere (up about 25 percent from its previous season’s premiere and finale), isn’t under the same kind of intense scrutiny, which may work in its favor. In fact, no one might be as casual about the show ending as David himself. As he recently said, with his signature intonation, on “The Rich Eisen Show”: “I don’t really get sentimental. I’m not sentimental about it.”
