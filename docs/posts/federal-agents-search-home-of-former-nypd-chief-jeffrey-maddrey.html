Federal Agents Search Home of Former NYPD Chief Jeffrey Maddrey

New York|Federal Agents Search the Home of Former Top N.Y.P.D. Official

https://www.nytimes.com/2025/01/02/nyregion/nypd-jeffrey-maddrey-home-search.html

Jeffrey Maddrey, who had been chief of department, resigned on Dec. 20 after a lieutenant accused him of coercing her into sex.

Jan. 2, 2025Updated 12:51 p.m. ET

Federal agents on Thursday morning searched the home of Jeffrey Maddrey, the former chief of department for the New York Police Department, Commissioner Jessica Tisch said.

Mr. Maddrey, who resigned on Dec. 20 after a lieutenant accused him of coercing her into sex, is also being investigated by the department’s Internal Affairs Bureau, which is working with law enforcement authorities in its inquiry, Commissioner Tisch said in a statement on social media.

Mr. Maddrey, who left the department but whose resignation is not yet effective, was suspended without pay Thursday morning as the search warrant was executed, the commissioner said. He was replaced on Sunday by John Chell, the former chief of patrol.

Agents also searched other locations, Commissioner Tisch said, though she did not identify them. She referred questions to the U.S. attorney for the Southern District of New York.

Mr. Maddrey owns a home in Jamaica, Queens, according to city property records. Nicholas Biase, a spokesman for the Southern District, declined to comment on whether federal agents searched that house or any other properties.

Federal investigators have joined an inquiry by the city’s Department of Investigation into the provision of overtime and the allegations of sexual misconduct against Mr. Maddrey, according to two people with knowledge of the matter. Mr. Biase declined to comment on the nature of the probe and the search warrants.

Commissioner Tisch has transferred hundreds of officers since she was selected to lead the Police Department in November in a shake-up that has rocked an agency mired in scandal.

Among the transfers were top officials, including Tarik Sheppard, the combative head of communications who has clashed with reporters and been criticized for his public spat with the former interim police commissioner, Thomas Donlon. Commissioner Tisch also appointed Chief Edward A. Thompson to lead the Internal Affairs Bureau, replacing Miguel Iglesias, who she said had been “relieved of his command.”

The restructuring follows months of turmoil for Mayor Eric Adams, who faces federal charges for bribery and fraud. Edward A. Caban, a former police commissioner, was forced to resign after federal officials seized his phone as part of a criminal investigation. A wave of City Hall resignations and local, state and federal investigations followed.

Mr. Maddrey’s downfall was the latest blow to the Adams administration. The lieutenant, Quathisha Epps, last month filed a complaint against Mr. Maddrey with the U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission that was described in a blog post by her lawyer, Eric Sanders.

He wrote that Mr. Maddrey had taken advantage of her financial problems and vulnerable emotional state to force her into “unwanted sexual acts in exchange for professional benefits, including overtime opportunities.”

The Manhattan district attorney’s office is also conducting an investigation into the matter.

“These are extremely serious and disturbing claims that allegedly occurred at N.Y.P.D. headquarters in Manhattan,” said Danielle Filson, an office spokeswoman, after the allegations surfaced.

Mr. Maddrey has denied the accusations.

Last week, the former police chief stood silently next to his lawyer, Lambros Lambrou, as Mr. Lambrou addressed reporters about the accusations. Mr. Lambrou said that Mr. Maddrey had been accused by a subordinate who had engaged in overtime abuse and been investigated. Mr. Lambrou said that she had decided to blame Mr. Maddrey to “deflect her wrongdoing.”

Mr. Lambrou called the allegations against his client a lie. He did not immediately respond to a message for comment on Thursday.

Mr. Adams, a former police captain, this week addressed the accusations against Mr. Maddrey. In an interview on Monday on a YouTube channel, Corey’s Corner, Mr. Adams defended Mr. Maddrey’s police record and downplayed the tumult at the department, using a vulgarity for emphasis.

There have “always been scandals,” Mr. Adams said in the interview with Corey Pegues, a retired New York City police inspector. Mr. Adams also blamed the response to the department’s troubles on race, saying a “scandal under the Black mayor” should not be considered “unique.”

When asked whether he had overlooked problems involving Mr. Maddrey, including when Mr. Maddrey voided the arrest of a retired officer accused of waving a gun at three boys, Mr. Adams said: “I’m a firm believer that the worst day of your life should not define your life.”

Jeffery C. Mays and William K. Rashbaum contributed reporting.
