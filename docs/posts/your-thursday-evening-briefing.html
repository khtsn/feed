Good evening. Here’s the latest at the end of Thursday.

1. Secretary of State Antony Blinken demanded an end to the war in Ukraine during a meeting with his Russian counterpart.

In their first face-to-face meeting since Russia’s invasion last year, Blinken told Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov that Russia should halt its “war of aggression” against Ukraine and free Paul Whelan, an American detained on what Blinken called “sham espionage charges.”

Blinken also asked that Moscow return to the New START, the nuclear arms control treaty that Russia withdrew from last month. The diplomatic encounter occurred at a meeting of the Group of 20 countries in New Delhi, where leaders failed to produce a joint communiqué because of disagreements over the war.

The Biden administration is also facing disagreements at home about support for Ukraine: Polls show that American public support for arming Ukraine is softening.

In other news from the war, a Ukrainian mayor who kept working under Russian occupation was hailed by residents as a hero. But others have questions about his loyalty.

Also, hundreds of Western businesses have decided to continue operating in Russia.

2. A lawsuit against Donald Trump over the Jan. 6 riot should proceed, the Justice Department said.

The department told a federal court that it should allow a lawsuit brought by members of Congress and the Capitol Police to proceed against Trump.

The government lawyers — who have traditionally taken a broad view of executive power and defended the prerogatives of the presidency — said that if Trump’s speech falsely claiming a stolen election had incited the Capitol riot, then he was not shielded by immunity. They added that inciting violence was not within a president’s constitutional duties.

In other news from Washington, the House Ethics Committee unanimously voted to authorize an investigation into George Santos, the Republican congressman under scrutiny for lies about his background.

3. A Texas judge is poised to rule in an abortion case that could have consequences in every state.

The case, filed by anti-abortion groups, seeks to overturn the F.D.A.’s approval of the most common, safe and effective abortion pill regimen — and have it taken off the market.

A decision to remove the drug’s approval would likely be the first time a court ordered the F.D.A. to revoke a drug against its will. The agency would immediately appeal such a ruling, but, if allowed to stand, it could have significant ramifications even in states where abortion is protected. We looked at where restrictions on abortion pills would matter most.

4. Thousands of migrants are illegally crossing from the U.S. into Canada.

The record inflows of asylum seekers — nearly 40,000 last year and about 5,000 in January alone — are forcing Canada to deal with an issue that has long bedeviled other wealthy Western nations.

While the country has recently committed itself to significantly raising the number of legal immigrants and accepting 1.5 million newcomers by 2025, many officials are fuming about the spike in illegal migration. Using the kind of anti-migrant language rarely heard in Canada, some opposition politicians have called on the government to deploy the police to shut down border crossings.

In other immigration news, Tunisia’s president is openly vilifying migrants from sub-Saharan Africa, and hundreds of Black foreigners have been swept up in arrests, rights groups say.

5. Iran is investigating the suspected poisoning of hundreds of schoolgirls.

More than 800 schoolgirls in at least 10 cities in Iran have come down with respiratory, cardiac and neurological symptoms over the past three months. This week, after toxicologists said toxins found in the schools were nitrogen gas, President Ibrahim Raisi ordered an investigation into the possible poisoning.

Until recently, the government had dismissed the cases, but the rise in the number of sick schoolgirls meant the illnesses could no longer be ignored. Videos circulating widely show girls collapsing to the ground, gasping for air and lying on emergency room beds.

Several government officials who said they suspected the poisoning was deliberate suggested that the motive was an attempt to prevent girls from attending schools — raising alarm about the possible infiltration of Islamic extremist groups.

6. C.E.O.s see hybrid work as the future. But making it work is harder.

In the nearly three years since the pandemic began, many workers have grown accustomed to working at home. But, citing the benefits of collaboration, many employers are asking their staff to go back to the office for two or three days a week.

Employees say that their primary motivation to commute to the office is the guarantee of seeing teammates. That demands coordination, but policies that are too rigid can cause greater backlash. Some companies are turning to office perks like pickleball and beer to attract workers back.

Also emerging from the pandemic, revenge spenders, who ramped up purchases to make up for time lost during isolation, are now finding it hard to stop.

7. Satellites are threatening our view of the cosmos.

Over the last 30 years, the Hubble Space Telescope has helped clarify countless mysteries, including about the age of the universe, the makeup of our galaxy and the pervasiveness of black holes. But, increasingly, a proliferation of private satellites in space is spoiling the Hubble’s view.

With thousands of satellites already orbiting our planet, and many more expected to launch in the coming years, scientists warn that our ability to study space could be noticeably reduced.

For more space news: Something seems to have gone wrong with China’s Mars rover.

8. America’s next great speedskater is coming for the Dutch.

At just 18 years old, Jordan Stolz is already considered among the best in the sport. In just about every race he competes in — especially the 1,000- and 1,500-meter — he is a favorite to win, regardless of the opponent.

This weekend at the world championships in the Netherlands, Stolz will test his talent against the Dutch, speedskating’s biggest powerhouse. The races offer a preview of America’s best hope at medaling in the sport at the next Winter Olympics.

In other sports news, an unheralded player may break the college basketball scoring record tonight.

10. And finally, the “it” coat this winter is … a pasta puffer?

It’s not much more complicated than it sounds — a short, black jacket printed with golden images of bow-tie pasta. Yet suddenly the coat, designed by Rachel Antonoff, has attracted a serious cult following.

It’s become a seasonal phenomenon on the streets of New York, Chicago and San Francisco, and social media is littered with images of people showing off their farfalle fashion.
