Sen. Robert Menendez Awaits Verdict as Jury Is Sent to Deliberate

Senator Robert Menendez is charged with 16 separate crimes, including bribery, obstructing justice and acting as an agent of a foreign government.

July 12, 2024, 2:12 p.m. ET

The Manhattan jury in Senator Robert Menendez’s corruption trial began its deliberations midday Friday on a raft of federal charges in what prosecutors describe as a complex and yearslong bribery conspiracy.

A third-term Democrat who represents New Jersey, Mr. Menendez is accused of steering aid to Egypt, applying political pressure to preserve a friend’s business monopoly and meddling in criminal investigations in exchange for bribes of gold, cash and a Mercedes-Benz.

On Thursday, Mr. Menendez, 70, seated at the defense table with his lawyers, leaned back in a chair, hands clasped on his lap, as a prosecutor offered an hourslong rebuttal to frequently impassioned closing arguments by lawyers for the senator and two businessmen, Wael Hana and Fred Daibes, who are being tried with him.

In his rebuttal argument, the prosecutor, Daniel C. Richenthal, took direct aim at a pillar of the senator’s defense strategy — an effort to shift blame to his wife, Nadine Menendez, 57.

Mr. Menendez’s lawyers had told the jury that Ms. Menendez was in dire financial straits early in their relationship and kept her husband “in the dark” about what she was asking others, like Mr. Hana and Mr. Daibes, to give her.

“Are you going to accept that there was a secret plan to dupe Senator Menendez,” Mr. Richenthal asked the jury, “that his wife cooked up a scheme to secretly collect money and gold by invoking his name with two men, one of whom was his close friend, and he never learned about it?”

“Because let’s be clear about what that means,” Mr. Richenthal said. “It means that she duped her husband — her boyfriend and now husband — an experienced public official, one of the most powerful people in the entire U.S. Congress for five years.”

“He knows all about the payments,” Mr. Richenthal said. “He’s taking actions, he’s part of it.”

Ms. Menendez, who last year was indicted with her husband in the bribery case, is not being tried with him. Ms. Menendez, who has pleaded not guilty, had her trial postponed because she is being treated for breast cancer.

Mr. Menendez, Mr. Hana and Mr. Daibes are charged in a 66-page federal indictment that includes 18 counts, not all of which apply to each defendant. Mr. Menendez faces 16 counts, including bribery, conspiracy, obstructing justice, extortion and acting as an agent of a foreign government — a charge never before leveled against a U.S. senator.

Throughout the trial, the judge, Sidney H. Stein of Federal District Court, has complimented the jurors on their punctuality and attentiveness during weeks of sometimes tedious testimony about evidence charts, the life span of a dollar bill, Egypt’s import market, and the process for allocating foreign military aid.

In sometimes soaring rhetoric, the lawyers and prosecutors, who had begun delivering closing arguments on Monday, echoed the judge’s words, reminding the jurors of the import of the decisions they were being asked to render in the coming days.

“What makes this system that you now have spent quite some time existing in so special,” Adam Fee, one of Mr. Menendez’s lawyers, said, “is that everyone — the powerful, the powerless — have one thing standing between them and a lifetime of shame, a conviction: a jury.”

The jury is racially diverse and varies in age. The two youngest jurors are 27 and 28; the two oldest are 65 and 68.

Many hold advanced degrees, according to responses given to questions Judge Stein asked during the first days of the trial. The panel includes a doctor who works at a New York City hospital, a mother of a 2-year-old and a 5-year-old, and an investment banker.

On Friday, shortly before 1 p.m., Judge Stein finished instructing the six-woman, six-man jury on the law. He then said he had been informed that the 12 jurors and five alternate jurors, who had sat with them throughout the two-month trial as potential backups, had asked to eat one last lunch together as a group, before the 12 went in to deliberate.

“That’s perfectly fine,” the judge said, but he cautioned that the 12-member jury could not be begin deliberations until after lunch, because all 17 of them would be present.

Then, shortly after 2 p.m., the jurors returned to the courtroom, and the 12-member jury was sent to deliberate.
