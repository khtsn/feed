Sean ‘Diddy’ Combs Threatened to Kill Kid Cudi, Former Employee Testifies at Trial

Capricorn Clark, who worked for the music mogul for years, said he kidnapped her while seeking revenge on the rapper, a romantic rival. The defense denies she was kidnapped.

May 27, 2025Updated 3:55 p.m. ET

A former employee of Sean Combs testified at his federal trial on Tuesday that he repeatedly threatened her while she was working for him, and that the music mogul once kidnapped her during a jealous rage over a romantic rival.

The woman, Capricorn Clark, testified that Mr. Combs came to her apartment early one morning in December 2011, after he learned that Casandra Ventura, his longtime on-and-off girlfriend, had been dating the rapper Scott Mescudi, known as Kid Cudi.

Mr. Combs had a gun, Ms. Clark testified, and told her “get dressed, we’re going to go kill” Mr. Mescudi. “I had never seen anything like this before,” Ms. Clark testified.

She said she waited in a Cadillac Escalade as Mr. Combs and a bodyguard entered Mr. Mescudi’s Los Angeles home while the rapper wasn’t there, part of a chaotic series of events at the center of the government’s racketeering case.

Mr. Combs is facing sex-trafficking and racketeering conspiracy charges, which include accusations that he or his associates kidnapped Ms. Clark twice while she worked for him. Lawyers for Mr. Combs, who has pleaded not guilty to all of the charges, have denied that Ms. Clark was ever kidnapped, and they have said there was never any criminal conspiracy.

A longtime Combs associate who rose from personal assistant to a fashion marketing director, Ms. Clark is the most prominent former employee to testify at the trial so far. She did not say during her testimony why Mr. Combs was so adamant about her joining him on the trip to Mr. Mescudi’s house.

Ms. Clark grew distraught at times on the stand, removing her glasses to wipe tears from her eyes as she described the terrifying fallout after Mr. Combs discovered the relationship between Ms. Ventura, known as Cassie, and Mr. Mescudi.

She testified that after Mr. Combs kidnapped her, he threatened to kill her if she did not help him evade a police investigation into a break-in report that Mr. Mescudi had made. Later that day, she said, she watched Mr. Combs beat Ms. Ventura. “He repeatedly kicked her, and with each kick she would crouch more and more into a fetal position,” Ms. Clark said.

On Thursday, Mr. Mescudi testified that Mr. Combs’s meltdown escalated to his Porsche being destroyed by a Molotov cocktail in early 2012. Mr. Combs’s lawyers denied the mogul’s involvement.

During cross-examination, one of Mr. Combs’s lawyers, Marc Agnifilo, walked methodically through Ms. Clark’s account of a kidnapping in 2011, zeroing in on inconsistencies in her recollections. Before trial, prosecutors said Ms. Clark was kidnapped “at gunpoint.” Ms. Clark clarified that the gun was not pointed at her, but Mr. Combs was “waving it around.”

Questioning whether Ms. Clark had really been forced out of her apartment against her will, Mr. Agnifilo referenced a meeting that Ms. Clark and her lawyer had with Mr. Combs’s attorneys in April 2024, after raids on Mr. Combs’s homes revealed that he was under investigation.

During that meeting, Mr. Agnifilo said, Ms. Clark said she went with Mr. Combs to Mr. Mescudi’s home that day so that her boss “wouldn’t do something stupid.” On the stand, Ms. Clark denied having said that.

“If he did ‘something stupid,’ you would be out of a job,” Mr. Agnifilo said to Ms. Clark.

“A myriad of things were happening, but I did not want to go, sir,” she replied.

The defense also sought to underscore how Ms. Clark repeatedly sought to return to work for Mr. Combs, despite the incidents she said she endured. Ms. Clark acknowledged that during that April 2024 meeting, she expressed interest in working for the mogul again — this time as his chief of staff.

On Tuesday, Ms. Clark described her time working for Mr. Combs as a fast-paced “form of business school,” and an experience that had been characterized by inspiration — but also by fear.

On her first day as his personal assistant in 2004, she said, Mr. Combs alluded to her previous experience working for a rival record producer, Suge Knight. Appearing to underscore his need for loyalty, he told her that “if anything happened, he would have to kill me,” she said.

Prosecutors have alleged that Mr. Combs’s associates first kidnapped Ms. Clark that year. Ahead of that incident, Ms. Clark said, she realized that three pieces of diamond jewelry that were on loan to Mr. Combs had disappeared. After she reported the missing jewelry, she testified, she was subjected to five days of lie-detector tests to prove that she was not involved in the theft.

Each day, she was taken by a bodyguard for Mr. Combs to a “dilapidated” floor of a Manhattan office building that later became Mr. Combs’s corporate offices. She said that an unnamed man who conducted the testing told her that if she failed, “they’re going to throw you in the East River.”

“I was petrified,” Ms. Clark said.

After Ms. Clark repeated those words, Mr. Combs, sitting at the defense table beside his lawyers, shook his head.

The kidnapping accusations are meant to buttress the racketeering conspiracy charge against Mr. Combs, which accuses him and members of his inner circle of a series of crimes dating back to 2004. The crimes cited in the indictment that relate to this charge include sex trafficking, arson, drug violations, bribery, obstruction of justice and the two acts of kidnapping.

Ms. Clark worked for Mr. Combs in periods between 2004 and 2018, though she left multiple times over disputes with her boss.

In 2006, Ms. Clark said, she left her job after Mr. Combs “charged” at her in response to another employee reporting to him that Ms. Clark had expressed dissatisfaction about her job.

She later returned, but she said she was fired in 2012, after reporting the 2011 kidnapping to executives at Bad Boy, Mr. Combs’s record label. She said the reason she was given was a disagreement over a vacation she took to Europe — with the pop singer Rihanna, she added. At that time, Mr. Combs told her that she would “never work again,” she said, and she later received a settlement from Mr. Combs over allegations of wrongful termination.

But after she was unable to obtain work elsewhere in the entertainment industry, Ms. Clark returned again in 2016 as creative director to Ms. Ventura, who was a singer on Mr. Combs’s label. She described Mr. Combs directing the minutiae of Ms. Ventura’s music career, including her schedule and what she wore on the red carpet.

“Soup to nuts, we needed to get approval,” she said.

Two weeks into the trial, the witnesses have largely been aimed at corroborating Ms. Ventura’s account of living under intense control, enduring rampant physical abuse and participating in sexual encounters known as “freak-offs,” which she gave during four days of testimony during the first week of testimony.

The witnesses that followed have included people who say they witnessed Mr. Combs’s physical abuse of Ms. Ventura or its aftermath, and two men who were paid to have sex with her while Mr. Combs watched. The defense asserts that Ms. Ventura and another woman that Mr. Combs is accused of sex trafficking are not victims, but rather former girlfriends who agreed to participate in sex that, while “kinky,” was entirely consensual and legal.
