Stock Prices and Oil Fall in Sharpest Decline Since Early August

The S&P 500 fell more than 2 percent on Tuesday, its biggest single-day drop since an early August meltdown.

Sept. 3, 2024Updated 4:33 p.m. ET

A sharp decline in major stock indexes on Tuesday ruptured a weekslong rebound on Wall Street, with the S&P 500 falling by the most in nearly a month.

The index fell 2.1 percent, its worst day since Aug. 5, when data pointing to slowing economic growth fueled a global sell-off. The technology-heavy Nasdaq dropped 3.3 percent as shares of the chipmaker Nvidia skidded.

Oil prices also fell sharply, a move that often reflects unease about the global economy.

The tumble is a setback for the rally that had lifted the S&P 500 within reach of a record by the end of last week.

Here’s what to know about the trading.

  • Oil prices fell sharply on Tuesday, with Brent crude, the international benchmark, down nearly 4 percent to just over $73 a barrel, its lowest level of the year.

  • A plunge in Nvidia’s stock drove a sell-off among chipmakers and a broader drop in tech stocks. Shares of Nvidia fell 9.5 percent, dragging the Nasdaq Composite down 3.3 percent.

  • All eyes are on the Labor Department’s jobs report for August, set to be released Friday. It will indicate how quickly the labor market is cooling — and provide clues about how aggressive the Federal Reserve will be in its expected interest rate cut later this month. Anticipation of that payroll report, coupled with data released on Tuesday showing weakness in the manufacturing sector, has brought concerns about a slowdown in the U.S. economy back to the forefront.

  • Last month’s jobs report, which came in weaker than expected, briefly catalyzed fears of a U.S. recession. While a series of data points since then have eased those concerns and reassured investors of economic resilience, the drop on Tuesday suggests labor market data this week remains critical.

  • “September seasonality and Friday’s jobs report have investors playing defense,” said Steve Sosnick, the chief strategist at Interactive Brokers. A market rally last Friday also meant that trading on Tuesday started at “a higher and more precarious perch” than usual, contributing to the steep drop, Mr. Sosnick added. “We came in too high,” he said.
