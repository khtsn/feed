President Biden remembered the Supreme Court’s first female justice as “gracious and wise, civil and principled” in a ceremony at Washington National Cathedral.

Dec. 19, 2023Updated 4:13 p.m. ET

Justice Sandra Day O’Connor was remembered on Tuesday for her trailblazing role on the Supreme Court, trading the sweeping skies of the Arizona desert as a cattle rancher’s daughter for the marble halls of the court to become the first female justice and one of the most powerful women in the country.

“Gracious and wise, civil and principled, Sandra Day O’Connor, daughter of the American West, was a pioneer in her own right, breaking down the barriers in legal and political worlds and in the nation’s consciousness,” President Biden said in remarks during a funeral service at Washington National Cathedral.

“She knew no person is an island,” he added. “In the fabric of our nation, we are all inextricably linked, and for the America to thrive, America must see themselves not as enemies but as partners in the great work of deciding our collective destiny.”

As the justice’s coffin, draped in white, entered the cathedral on Tuesday morning, bells tolled. Hundreds of people, including all nine current Supreme Court justices and retired Justice Anthony M. Kennedy, gathered for the service. The ceremony was filled with traditional music and admiring tributes to the determined woman who carved a path for the generations of female lawyers and judges to follow and who did so with grace, wit and, as one of her three sons told those gathered, “unearthly energy.”

On the court, Justice O’Connor was known for her place at its ideological center and for her practical approach to legal decision-making. Her vote became the key to decisions on some of the most contentious issues that came before the court, including affirmative action, voting rights and abortion.

Although Justice O’Connor did not serve as chief justice, she held such power during a crucial period on the court that it was often referred to as the O’Connor court.

In his eulogy, Chief Justice John G. Roberts Jr. described Justice O’Connor as a pioneering force on the bench — and for generations of women.

“It has been said that the Supreme Court is like a family, a family composed entirely of in-laws,” Chief Justice Roberts said, to laughter. He added that “she had to ignore slights and work to bring people together in social, professional and political life.”

Justice O’Connor built collegiality among the justices, he said, all while forging a new path.

The justice “had to find her own style to cajole, persuade and unite colleagues when there was no example to follow,” he said.

Her determination and skill left no doubt that women could perform at the highest levels of the law.

“Her leadership shaped the legal profession, making it obvious that judges are both women and men,” Chief Justice Roberts said. “She was so successful that the barriers she broke down are almost unthinkable today but not so in her lifetime.”

On the bench and in interactions with colleagues, he said, Justice O’Connor’s approach was “simple and direct — get it done.”

That approach has extended to her personal life, said Jay H. O’Connor, one of her sons.

Mr. O’Connor said his mother’s direct, confident style was rooted in her childhood on the Lazy B, her family’s cattle ranch. Her father instilled in her toughness; her mother, grace.

Life could be lonely out in the high desert, but books “transported” her, he said. Her education would take her to Stanford Law School, where she excelled academically and fell in love with a classmate, John Jay O’Connor III. The pair married shortly after graduation.

The couple took great joy in each other’s company, he said. They took disco dance lessons together and were known to clear the dance floor at Washington parties.

His mother’s tireless energy extended to their home, he said, where she raised her three sons to believe that their “turbocharged” family life was normal. She was fiercely committed to her children, driving one son to swim practices before dawn and typing up another’s high school homework assignments.

Despite her demanding job, “she was still a mom in every sense of the word,” Mr. O’Connor said. “My brothers and I all had a front-row seat, and we still wonder how she did it all.”

Justice O’Connor, who died this month of complications from dementia at 93, even planned many of the details of her memorial service, he said.

In 1987, she wrote a letter to her family and sealed it, he said. It included her hopes for her funeral.

“The unmistakable theme of her selections was justice on earth,” Mr. O’Connor said, adding, “We are following her instructions here today to a tee.”

She also included a message to her sons, “Our purpose in life is to help others along the way.”

Her son paused, before adding, “What a beautiful, powerful and totally Sandra Day O’Connor sentiment.”

The justice’s biographer, Evan W. Thomas III, described the justice’s faith as an important part of her life. Justice O’Connor was baptized in the Episcopal Church, regularly worshiped at the National Cathedral and had sat on its governing board.

It may be hard to envision now, Mr. Thomas said, but when she started out, “the law, like much else in our society had long been dominated by men.” When she graduated from law school, she struggled to find work, he said, finally persuading a local district attorney to give her a job. When the lawyer told her he could not pay her, she worked for free. When he said there was not enough office space, she countered that she could share a secretary’s office.

That same determination served her well at the court, he said.

When she joined the Supreme Court, he said, “she found the place to be cold and not just because she missed the Arizona sun.”

When Justice Clarence Thomas started at the court, Mr. Thomas recalled, the new justice did not feel like going to lunch. But Justice O’Connor prodded him.

“She was the glue, the reason this place was civil,” Mr. Thomas remembered Justice Thomas telling him.

The service was the second day of ceremonies in Washington, where the justice had served for 24 years before she retired in 2006.

On Monday, she lay in repose in the Great Hall of the Supreme Court, as her former law clerks took turns watching over her coffin.

Justice Sonia Sotomayor, the third female justice, spoke of Justice O’Connor’s commitment to creating an atmosphere of collegiality at the court and added that she believed Justice O’Connor would be “smiling, knowing that four sisters serve” on the nine-member court.

The Rev. Jane E. Fahey, one of Justice O’Connor’s first clerks in the 1980s, remembered her for “her cowgirl grit, energy and no-nonsense sense of duty.”

During her retirement, the justice focused on two causes: judicial independence and civics education. She also traveled with her grandchildren and wrote two children’s books that drew from her childhood on a ranch.

In October 2018, she announced that she had been diagnosed with the beginning stages of dementia and would withdraw from public life.

During the service, the justice’s son announced that her family would take her remains back to the Arizona desert she so loved, back to the Lazy B, “where she learned how to see forever across the high open desert.”
