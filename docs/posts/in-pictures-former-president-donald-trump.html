After months of speculation about how three major new rules would change the game, and a spring training filled with fast games and plenty of flubs, opening day of the 2023 Major League Baseball season finally arrived. And for the first time since 1968, every M.L.B. team started its season on the same day.

It was a busy day, with the action starting at 1:10 p.m. Eastern and finishing up at 12:45 a.m.

Here is what we learned:

  • Try not to blink. Of the 15 games, 10 were finished in fewer than three hours. The shortest game was in Tampa Bay, where the Rays beat the lowly Detroit Tigers in 2 hours 14 minutes. But the improvement in pace was perhaps the most shocking in the Bronx, where the Yankees, who have long been known for their deliberate pace, handily dispatched the San Francisco Giants in 2 hours 33 minutes. That game featured 32 combined strikeouts and 276 combined pitches but still came in 40 minutes faster than an average Yankees game in 2022.

  • Marcus Stroman is headed to Cooperstown. Chicago’s ace had a lovely day, outdueling Corbin Burnes of the Milwaukee Brewers by tossing six scoreless innings in a 4-0 win. But Stroman’s start also included the first pitch clock violation in major league history, which means the Baseball Hall of Fame will be asking for some game relics to add to their collection.

  • Some of the violations will surprise you. There were 13 violations called on Thursday, to varying degrees of player annoyance. A few of the violations were more interesting than others. Alejandro Kirk, the catcher of the Toronto Blue Jays, for instance, got called for a violation in his team’s 10-9 win over St. Louis because he forgot his PitchCom device in the dugout.

  • Stolen base totals could soar. Anthony Volpe made his major league debut and the Yankees’ 21-year-old shortstop walked in his first plate appearance and immediately stole second base. That set the stage for a day that had as many stolen bases (21) as it had home runs (21). Perhaps more interesting: Runners were 21 of 23 on stolen base attempts, a success rate so high that even the most analytically-minded front office executives would nod in approval.

There was plenty to learn even outside the scope of the new rules.

  • Shohei Ohtani is the ultimate one-man show. In the first game of his walk year — an audition that could lead to the largest contract in major league history — Ohtani not only started at pitcher and designated hitter for the Los Angeles Angels, he also wore a PitchCom device and called his own game. He didn’t rake the infield or sell beer in the stands, but it’s reasonable to assume he would have done a great job on either task if he had tried.

  • Pitchers haven’t figured out Aaron Judge. A slump at the worst possible time in October may have had some fans wondering if baseball had figured out how to pitch around Judge, the Yankees’ superstar outfielder who set an A.L. record with 62 home runs in 2022. Put that idea to rest, as Judge, the new Yankees captain, homered to center field on the second pitch he saw in 2023.

  • Adley Rutschman may never record an out. Baltimore became an entirely different team once Rutschman, a catcher, was called up last season. In his first opening day start, he made the most of things going 5 for 5 with a home run, a walk and four runs batted in. The visiting Orioles beat the Red Sox, 10-9.
