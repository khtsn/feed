Armed With Saran Wrap, She Sinks in the Muck to Save the Planet

A dogged paleoecologist named Dorothy Peteet is digging through the salt marshes of New York in the hope of preserving the city’s best natural defense against climate change.

One moment, Dorothy Peteet stood on a mat of squishy grass in Idlewild marsh, on the edge of Jamaica Bay in southern Queens. The next moment she sank to her hips in the cool, sulfuric muck. She was neither grossed out nor surprised.

“Yes, this happens,” Dr. Peteet, one of the world’s foremost experts on marshes, said. “I don’t think I remembered to bring any towels.”

A saltwater marsh is an in-between place. Twice a day, it’s flooded by the tide. When the tide is low, a marsh may appear to be solid grassy ground, but under the cordgrass it is riven with creeks and rivulets, mud snails, diamondback terrapins and fiddler crabs. For centuries, New Yorkers viewed Jamaica Bay’s swamps as smelly barriers to progress, fit to become garbage dumps only when they couldn’t be drained and paved into highways and airports.

In recent decades, scientists like Dr. Peteet have recast the marshes as environmental marvels, providing a rare habitat for thousands of species, filtering the water and preventing contamination by locking tons of industrial pollutants away in their soggy underground vaults.

Most important, Jamaica Bay’s marshes serve as New York City’s best defense against climate change, said Dr. Peteet, a paleoecology researcher at NASA and a professor at Columbia University. As the oceans warm and storms grow more violent, the marshes act like a cushion, absorbing energy from big waves before they crash into people’s homes and threaten to suck whole neighborhoods into the sea.

Today, the marshes of Jamaica Bay are the site of a slow-moving race. Every year, a collection of government agencies, nonprofits and private companies inches a little closer toward construction projects that together promise to restore hundreds of acres of degraded marshes. And every year, a few more acres of marshland erode into the water.

The rate of loss has slowed in recent years, said Terri Carta, executive director of the Jamaica Bay-Rockaway Parks Conservancy, a nonprofit group. But the marshes continue to disappear faster than government can buttress and rebuild them.

“We’re losing,” Ms. Carta said.

Wielding tape measures, something called a Dachnowski peat corer and several boxes of Saran Wrap, Dr. Peteet has spent 25 years documenting the steady decline of Jamaica Bay’s marshes. The point of her work now is to catalog their value — all the quiet work the marshes do to filter water, support a complex ecosystem and sequester millions of tons of natural carbon and industrial pollution.

“Marshes are the best natural storm protection we have,” Dr. Peteet said. “They’re good as habitat. They’re good as archives for climate change. And when you destroy them, you lose all those things.”

Dr. Peteet has the soft-consonant lilt of a childhood in Georgia, the creased tan of someone who has lived a life of outdoor work and the distracted intelligence of someone who runs more than a dozen research projects at once. As the summer day in the marsh grew warmer, a section of Dr. Peteet’s peat corer went missing. Manufactured in Russia of heavy steel for the purpose of extracting core samples from the marsh, it is more than five feet long, but it’s olive brown, the same color as marsh soil.

“Well, it’s got to be here someplace,” Dr. Peteet said, nosing around the marsh floor with the toe of her brown rubber boot.

Dr. Peteet was accompanied in the marsh by half a dozen students. Eventually, one of them located the device. They righted it, with its pointy end aiming down. Two students grabbed the handlebar at the top, and used their body weight to plunge the corer deep into the mud.

“I’m glad you’re here,” Dr. Peteet said. “You guys are strong!”

When it was pulled back to the surface, the cylinder contained a tube of peat two inches thick and a meter long. It contained traces of all the animals and plants that had died on this spot since about 1624, the year Dutch colonists first settled in Manhattan.

Dr. Peteet knelt on the ground. She placed the core in a bed of tinfoil and Saran Wrap, then rolled it all up like a three-foot long burrito.

“Can you smell it?” she said. A wide-body passenger jet roared overhead, descending the last few feet to the nearby runway at Kennedy Airport, but she was talking about the loamy scent of the salt marsh. “That’s the sulfur.”

The marsh around her was a small, stranded remnant of a landscape that once was vast. In the early 20th century, the marshes of Jamaica Bay spread for more than 16,000 acres, an area larger than the island of Manhattan. Today only around 700 acres remain, according to the National Park Service, which owns many of the remaining marsh islands as part of Gateway National Recreation Area. At least 18 streams originally fed nutrients and sediment to the marshes. The eight streams that remain have been straightened and forced into pipes or culverts, depriving them of sediment the marshes need to survive, Dr. Peteet found.

“It’s very sad when you look at the maps and see what’s been lost,” she said. “These are precious places.”

The losses continued. Between 1994 and 1999, Jamaica Bay lost another 220 acres of salt marsh, according to the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers. In addition, New York City’s sewage treatment plants and sewer pipes routinely released 250 million gallons of wastewater into Jamaica Bay every day, flooding the marshes with excess nitrogen. This dumping went on for decades and slowed only in recent years, as the city upgraded wastewater treatment plants around the bay.

In a research paper, Dr. Peteet documented that with so much food available at the surface, plants stopped sinking their roots deep into the soil, weakening the marshes and making them vulnerable to erosion.

“The marshes are starving, and the roots are falling apart,” she said.

But there are plans to save them. The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, in partnership with New York City’s Department of Environmental Protection and the Department of Parks and Recreation, plans to spend $32.7 million to restore eight marshes and an oyster reef in Jamaica Bay, according to the corps.

“We’re on the cusp of the biggest rebuilding effort yet,” said Dan Mundy Jr., president of Jamaica Bay Ecowatchers. “We’ve never had so many projects lined up.”

Such projects move slowly. The army corps identified the shrinking marshes as a problem in 2013, finding that without intervention, “the marshes could vanish by the year 2025, destroying wildlife habitat and threatening the bay’s shorelines.” Seven years later, the corps finalized its plan to save some of the marshes, including Stony Creek, at the center of the bay.

Yet construction at Stony Creek, according to James D’Ambrosio, a spokesman for the army corps, has been delayed until at least 2026.

Rebuilding a marsh is hard manual work. First, sand is pumped by the ton onto the remains of an existing marsh, enough to raise the land’s elevation by several inches. Workers, many of them volunteers, then plant sprigs of marsh grass by hand, Mr. Mundy said.

Left untended, however, rebuilt marshes tend to disappear quickly.

“This isn’t a natural habitat that’s going to take care of itself,” said Ms. Carta, of the Jamaica Bay-Rockaway Parks Conservancy. “It’s in an urban environment, and the marsh islands need to be actively managed and maintained.”

By herself, there’s little that Dr. Peteet can do to contribute to such efforts. Instead she focuses her research on establishing all the benefits that Jamaica Bay’s marshes provide, and emphasizing what’s at stake if they’re lost. Trees are famous reservoirs for carbon. But the decaying plants held within an acre of marsh trap 50 times more carbon than an acre of forest, she said. Dr. Peteet’s core sample research suggests that since 1885, efforts to clear and fill the marshes have cost Jamaica Bay 95 percent of its stored carbon.

“We’ve lost a treasure for New York City,” she said.

Traditionally, marsh researchers explored just the top few centimeters of soil, Dr. Peteet said. By taking samples with her peat corer, she established that Jamaica Bay’s marshes are at least 13 feet deep, enough to sequester 55 million metric tons of underground carbon, plus many additional tons of industrial wastes like lead and nickel that otherwise would flow into the oceans. Some of the carbon would be exposed to air, kick-starting the process of decomposition and releasing greenhouse gases into the atmosphere.

“A marsh is like a refrigerator,” she said. “It’s cold and dark and wet, so you have preservation. If the marshes erode, all that carbon and pollution goes into the water.”

Dr. Peteet’s core samples also function like snapshots, recording the region’s atmosphere as it changed over millenniums, reflecting forest fires, invasive species, the rise of New York City and industrial pollution. In one of her long burritos of muck, a great concentration of lead appears 58 centimeters down, she said, with lead isotopes matching America’s smelting boom of the 1830s. The leaded section persists for the next 40 centimeters, and then stops around 1975, just as the federal government banned leaded gasoline.

The most important story the marshes contain is the one of their own disappearance. In healthy high marsh, around half a sample should be just the remains of dead plants, Dr. Peteet said. The other half is sand, slit and clay from streams, mineral matter that helps to hold the living plants in place.

In her latest research, Dr. Peteet found the peat of Jamaica Bay to be wildly out of whack. Only 20 percent of the gunk laid down in recent years is mineral, making the marshes weak just as the waves grow higher and stronger.

“The marshes are starving,” she said. “So unless you add sediment, these marshes are going to go under.”

The opposite is also true. We know why the marshes are disappearing, Dr. Peteet said, and we know how to reverse that. Much of the sediment needed to rebuild the marshes didn’t travel far, having eroded into the waterways right beside them, she said. All that’s needed, she said, are dredges to scoop it up, pumps to spray it back onto the marsh, people to replant the grasses and money to pay for it.

“We have a chance,” Dr. Peteet said. “We know what to do. It’s up to us, I think.”
