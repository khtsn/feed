G2 arrived in Rio without their captain, Nemanja "⁠huNter-⁠" Kovač, after he was injured shortly after the team's victory at Stake Ranked Episode 1.

Vilius "⁠tAk⁠" Keserauskas, a 21-year-old rifler for the organization's academy team G2 Ares who has averaged a 1.15 rating over 34 maps in 2026, was called up on short notice to take the Bosnian's place.

The tier-one newcomer admitted nerves got the better of him at the start of the series, where he struggled on Inferno, but those were shed as the game wore on and he ended the Overpass decider atop the board for G2.

"Today was a very important day, that I just feel the atmosphere, how things were here," tAk told HLTV after the match. "The first map was tough, but afterwards I managed to get back, and I think this will bring me confidence for tomorrow's match."

The Lithuanian is playing outside of his usual roles and with less of a voice on the team than he's used to, but he is trying to make the most of the opportunity at his first Big Event.

"First of all, and the most valuable, is experience," tAk said of what he hopes to get out of his stand-in appearance. "Also, there are a lot of eyes in this tournament, so it's also important for me to show off what I'm capable of. Even though the first game was shaky, but I think I got it. Yeah, as I said, experience, meeting all the people not only from G2, but the tier-one scene overall."

Listen to the interview here.

Congratulations, you started Rio with a win. You're coming in on pretty short notice, so how did it feel to come into your biggest tournament yet and get that victory?

A lot of emotions running, of course. A mixture of everything: excitement, anxiety also maybe. But happy to bounce back after a hard first map, which was very tough for me individually, but it was to feel the atmosphere in the arena and everything. Yeah, happy that we closed the second and third maps pretty cleanly. I'm happy with the win overall.

It's a big step up for you, both in terms of the competition and the tier of event. How did you prepare for that?

Team-wise, we had only two days of practice with the guys, so it was pretty hectic. We had a very long two days as we prepared for the tournament. Individually, it was just the same as I would do in my own academy team, maybe it was more extra mental work before games just to calm down and play my own game. Nothing special, just do my work and what I'm here for.

You said you felt some anxiety coming in. What was your approach to handling that?

Probably it was just letting it go. Like, I know I did what I could do before the games, I put in the practice, so what's done is already done and now I just need to get into the game and it's already written. What will happen will happen, and I just know in the back of my mind that i did everything I could and I just need to play the game.

We have a lot of data from the main team that we use, so I was familiar with some things. Not everything of course, but some details helped me infiltrate in the team better

How much has being part of G2's academy program helped with coming into this event and being ready for it?

We have a lot of data from the main team that we use, so I was familiar with some things. Not everything of course, but some details helped me infiltrate in the team better. And also just knowing the guys from before helped me, so I was familiar with the voices, the faces, and their playstyle overall. Combined, I was pretty prepared.

When you say you have data from the main team, can you explain more about that? How does it actually work for you as an academy player and how that sharing of data works?

We have recordings of the main team, how they play practices and stuff. There are a lot of things, maybe I will not go into details, but just overall we have some stuff from the main team that we can use in the academy team and it's very valuable.

This was really short notice, as you said you had two days of practice. Coming into this event, what are you hoping to get out of it?

First of all, and the most valuable, is experience. Also, there are a lot of eyes in this tournament, so it's also important for me to show off what I'm capable of. Even though the first game was shaky, but I think I got it. Yeah, as I said, experience, meeting all the people not only from G2, but the tier-one scene overall. And yeah, getting used to it.

It also feels like you're doing it the hard way a little bit because you're replacing huNter-, who was the in-game leader. SunPayus is stepping up in that role instead, but not having the structure that G2 normally has, has it been a bit more difficult than you expected?

For me individually, it was of course very hard because I overtook all of Nemanja [huNter-]'s roles, so there were a lot of maps where I was playing different positions, doing different things, and I had to overtake his roles and learn a lot of new stuff. And there's a lot (laughs). It's not easy, but game by game it will get easier, I will remember things better, and I just need to work more before tomorrow's game.

It's tough to sometimes see your teammates go to a better team, but you're also happy and you know that it's possible because you see it [happen]

The wider Counter-Strike world doesn't really know you, so tell me about you as a player, your background, and how you came into this spot.

I started playing in my native teams from Lithuania, then at some point I got picked up by AGO, the Polish organization, but it was a very short-term that we played. Then it was an unlucky period of time with Sprout Academy, like a one-month duration, and from that point I got invited to G2 Academy and I've been here for almost two years already.

And... yeah, it's tough to sometimes see your teammates go to a better team, like getting picked up since it's an academy team and individuals get bought out, loaned and stuff. It's tough to see, but you're also happy and you know that it's possible because you see it [happen] around yourself, people getting picked up, and you just need to work hard, put in enough hours, and if you work it will pay off. It's a cliche, but it is how it is.

How about you as a player, what is your mentality and your general approach to the game like?

It differs a lot from how I play here in the main team and the academy team. I have different roles that I need to take care of, and in the academy team I'm more like the second voice after the IGL, doing plays, calling plays, and taking initiative a lot. Here with the guys, I'm just following their instructions and I need to listen well, try to support as best as I can, and whatever else is extra for me, like getting some kills or whatever.

Have you found it difficult to take a step back with your voice?

There are some situations where I would call something and the guys are, of course, good with it. Like they say, 'If you have some play, call it, do it, don't hesitate,' but of course I believe in the main team, it's obvious, so I let them call more than myself. But if I see some opportunity, I will for sure call it out, bring support with me, and just execute what i want. But mainly it is the other guys doing it, and I'm following it.

This is your first official with the team, and NertZ was being very loud, very local. What was that first experience playing with them like?

NertZ is a great guy, he's very funny (smiles). As soon as we have a great atmosphere and great energy, I think we can do a lot of damage in this tournament. Of course, we're not as structured as they would play with Nemanja, so our attitude and atmosphere is very important during this type of a playstyle. If we keep it up, yeah, we can do great things here.

It's another level tomorrow than today, but I'm just excited to play. It's one hell of an opportunity, so if not tomorrow, then when? (laughs)

You say you have a chance to do a lot of damage, and your next match is going to be against Vitality, so you're being thrown into the deep end a little bit with the hardest matchup you could have in your second official. Talk to me about that.

Today was a very important day, that I just feel the atmosphere, how things were here. The first map was tough, but afterwards I managed to get back, and I think this will bring me confidence for tomorrow's match. Of course, it's another level tomorrow than today, but I'm just excited to play. It's one hell of an opportunity, so if not tomorrow, then when? (laughs)

You mentioned anxiety earlier, and when you come into this kind of environment, playing against these players that you've watched from how for the longest time, do you feel any nerves or sense of fear?

I had a lot of nerves during the first map, but after I started the second one well, everything just disappeared and I was playing like I was at home. It's very important for me usually to start well so I can keep it up. It's a bit harder for me to get back from a slow start, but if it happens sometimes this tournament, I will try my best to do it well. But yeah, I mean it's natural that we feel pressured and nervous, that means you care about the game. And you will not run away from it, you can just use it to your advantage, like this excitement, to hype up the boys and play a good game.
