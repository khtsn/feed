Overshadowed by a Senator, Two Businessmen Were Convicted With Menendez

The businessmen, Fred Daibes and Wael Hana, did not deny giving Senator Robert Menendez and his wife valuables.

  • July 16, 2024, 1:25 p.m. ET

Gold and cash discovered by F.B.I. agents in Senator Robert Menendez’s home were bribes given by two New Jersey businessmen in exchange for the senator’s willingness to use his political clout on their behalf.

The businessmen, Wael Hana and Fred Daibes, were convicted Tuesday with Mr. Menendez. They did not deny giving Mr. Menendez and his wife, Nadine Menendez, valuables, but they had argued at trial that the items were either loans, “good-will gifts” intended to nurture a relationship, or presents from longtime friends.

Mr. Hana, an Egyptian American businessman, had been friendly with Ms. Menendez for years before she began dating the senator.

In early 2018, Mr. Hana and Ms. Menendez began arranging meetings between Mr. Menendez and Egyptian officials. The next year, the government of Egypt awarded a halal meat certification company that Mr. Hana founded, IS EG Halal, a lucrative monopoly that prosecutors say was used to funnel bribes to the senator and his wife.

Mr. Hana was convicted of giving Ms. Menendez a low-show job and paying off her home mortgage in exchange for the senator’s help safeguarding his business monopoly. Eleven one-ounce bars of gold, bought by Mr. Hana and worth roughly $12,000, were also discovered during a search of the senator’s home.

Mr. Daibes, a wealthy New Jersey real estate developer, had been a close friend of Mr. Menendez’s for years. Two of the one-kilo bars of gold that F.B.I. agents found in a closet in the senator’s home were stamped with serial numbers linked to Mr. Daibes, who, testimony showed, bought gold as an investment.

Envelopes filled with $10,000 in cash wrapped in bank bands also held Mr. Daibes’s fingerprints and other identifying features, according to trial evidence.

Prosecutors said Mr. Daibes gave Mr. Menendez and his wife the gold bars and cash in exchange for the senator’s help on several matters: trying to quash an unrelated federal bank fraud case in New Jersey; propping up the halal business; and connecting Mr. Daibes with a Qatari investment fund that provided $95 million in financing to jump-start a stalled real estate venture in Edgewater, N.J.

In a closing statement, Larry S. Lustberg, Mr. Hana’s lawyer, said prosecutors had failed to prove a case built largely on speculation. “They can’t say when, to whom, or for what,” the bribes were given, Mr. Lustberg told jurors.

César de Castro, Mr. Daibes’s lawyer, did not call a single witness after the judge precluded testimony from associates of Mr. Daibes who Mr. de Castro said were prepared to describe him as a habitual gift giver.

“This case is about assumptions, guesses, hunches, presumptions, suspicions, suppositions and unfair inferences,” Mr. de Castro told jurors.

The jury convicted the men on all counts against them.
