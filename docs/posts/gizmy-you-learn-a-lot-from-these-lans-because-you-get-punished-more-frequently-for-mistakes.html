Gizmy: "You learn a lot from these LANs because you get punished more frequently for mistakes"

Monte got revenge over HEROIC to lift the trophy at CCT Global Finals 2026, earning their second LAN title this year and continuing their steady progression despite losing Gytis "⁠ryu⁠" Glušauskas to Astralis in January.

Speaking to HLTV after the win, Jack "⁠Gizmy⁠" von Spreckelsen praised his team for learning from their mistakes after losing to HEROIC in the upper bracket final, and gave credit to Aurélien "⁠afro⁠" Drapier for his performance and how he is impacting the team.

"He's an extremely smart player, like obviously he's very experienced, he's seen a lot," Gizmy said. "Not too often does he make mistakes, and he is usually also a great calming presence for the team which is really useful, especially as we are a bit of a younger team."

The British in-game leader also spoke about balancing his studies while competing in Counter-Strike, saying he was thankful for his university's understanding and assistance in rescheduling some of his exams due to the team's upcoming appearance at PGL Astana. The event in Kazakhstan will be the first big international LAN Gizmy has attended since ESL Pro League Season 19 in April 2024, and he says he's looking forward to facing top teams.

"To speak realistically, our main goal would be to make playoffs and get out of the Swiss stage, but I'm gunning for the win," he said. "I have a lot of strategies I want to run, especially versus these top teams, because I've watched them a lot and I understand how a lot of them play. So it would be great to test some gameplay versus them."

Congratulations on the win! Two very strong CT sides to start the series, including a 13-0, and you got revenge over HEROIC to lift the trophy. Just give me your reaction that series and taking home the title.

Yeah, obviously it feels great to win a tournament. We had a second place at DraculaN vs M80 recently, which didn't feel the best, so very happy to close it out today in the finals. We had pretty good prep for the first two maps because they were maps we expected in the best-of-three veto we played versus them.

We learned from our mistakes from the last time; we played more as a unit, really stressed doing our things correctly, having strong protocols, making sure our afterplants were A-1, and the way we're speaking to each other as well, like we can never be tilted and stuff. Basically, we had some parts of the tournament where it was not so good, but I'm happy that as time went on, it progressed really well and we managed to end on a pretty good note.

It's your second title with Rainwaker, and another strong result after you finished second at DraculaN. You mentioned at Roman Cup V that he fit into the team pretty seamlessly, but now that he's had more time to integrate, how has the progress been coming along?

Rainwaker is great, yeah. He's obviously a very good fit for the team, he's a really good individual. I think the way I call is a way that stresses and is basically maximizing player output, and when you have a lurk that is very capable on his own timings and knows when to play and stuff like this, it makes your life so much easier.

In a lot of rounds I can leave him be, some rounds I set him up for specific lurks that I want him to do to maybe punish rotations, but beyond that, he is doing a great job all the time of really not dying on his lurks and creating a lot of pressure.

You had said your dream was to make the Major, and you did that. Meanwhile, you're studying for finals in college, so how can you combine these things and what does it mean to you in the end?

Yeah, it's pretty hard to combine it so far. I'm already starting to come into a few complications with some of my exams; I've had to have a few of them moved because they were overlapping with PGL Astana, which we're going to. Thankfully, the university understood the profession is real and they were happy to move the exams, but it is tough managing them both.

But at the end of the day, I'm basically so far committed now that I might as well see it through. The original rationale is, because obviously CS is not a guaranteed career and you have to perform, that in case I didn't, I would have university to fall back on and actually go into a finance job.

afro is usually also a great calming presence which is really useful, especially as we are a bit of a younger team

afro has really stepped up this year, with two stellar showings between DraculaN and this tournament. He was on fire in the final too, so what do you make of his performances? Is there anything in particular that is helping him step up?

Yeah, as you said, afro is doing really good. A lot of it is just on the individual, and the only thing I can speak on, other than saying he is just really good at the game, is that basically we are getting him in good moments to really show his talents.

Maybe some IGLs don't really activate their AWPers enough, maybe they're putting them in too supportive roles where they are flashing and stuff, and I think we have a very strong balance of allowing afro to get entry kills or take aggressive lines at the start of rounds to allow our rifles to have more freedom and movement. And then of course, in every stage of the round — the same way as it is with a lurk, like with Rainwaker for example, or with the entries — we always consider where to position afro so he can get the maximum clutch potential.

Beyond that, he's just doing a really great job. He's an extremely smart player, like obviously he's very experienced, he's seen a lot. Not too often does he make mistakes, and he is usually also a great calming presence for the team which is really useful, especially as we are a bit of a younger team.

You've attended many tier-two LANs this year and done quite well at them. How has it been having this circuit to play in and get these kind of reps? Anything that you'd like changed?

Not so much. I think a lot of LANs have been doing a pretty good job, like for example, even this CCT LAN, the production was really good, and obviously the PCs were top-notch here, so I don't really have many complaints.

The only thing that, in most of these LANs, needs to be looked into is the schedule. But again, I'm not going to sit here and criticize it, like I understand it's not a tier-one tournament so you can't have one game a day, for example.

Obviously going through a lower bracket and playing a best-of-three and then a best-of-five is definitely hard and what we had to do, but I understand that if it was the other way, they'd have to have a whole extra day for one game and it's not like there is a massive crowd watching where there is a bunch of money coming in, so I don't really have many complaints.

It's a very good learning experience to have before the tier-one LANs, where you would need to have a top-notch game plan anyway to actually beat these teams

Alongside that, you haven't attended any top LANs despite a few solid results online like beating NAVI in Bounty and PARIVISION and HEROIC in EPL. That will change as you're going to Astana soon, so what are your goals for that event?

At the end of the day, I'm trying to win every event. Even if we're not the favorites, like obviously at this event I would say we were one of the favorites to win, and PGL Astana is a much bigger difference. So to speak realistically, our main goal would be to make playoffs and get out of the Swiss stage, but I'm gunning for the win. I have a lot of strategies I want to run, especially versus these top teams, because I've watched them a lot and I understand how a lot of them play. So it would be great to test some gameplay versus them.

You haven't played at a big LAN since EPL S19 in April 2024, so what kind of impact does getting these kinds of results at these smaller LANs have for you and the team when it comes to taking this next step up?

You learn a lot from these smaller LANs because you get punished more frequently for mistakes. For example, because of the frequency of the games, you can't prep as much as you would at a tier-one tournament. So for example, in the first game versus HEROIC, we lost an anti-eco round on every single map we played, which really disrupted our game.

Having these smaller LANs means you need to always make sure that your prep and how you're coming into the game, your game plan, is top-notch. It's a very good learning experience to have before the tier-one LANs, where you would need to have a top-notch game plan anyway to actually beat these teams and show a good performance.
