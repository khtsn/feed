The maritime package of more than 200 tons of food is a welcome milestone, but not nearly enough to prevent famine, said relief officials, who called on Israel to allow more aid delivery by land.

A ship hauling more than 200 tons of food for the Gaza Strip left Cyprus on Tuesday morning, in the first test of a maritime corridor designed to bring aid to hundreds of thousands of Palestinians who the United Nations says are on the brink of starvation.

The ship, named Open Arms, for the Spanish aid group that provided it, was the first vessel authorized to deliver aid to Gaza since 2005, according to Ursula von der Leyen, the president of the European Union’s executive arm, which has supported the effort and describes it as a “pilot project” that could clear the way for more sea shipments.

The rice, flour, lentils, beans, and canned tuna, beef and chicken that it was hauling on a barge were supplied by World Central Kitchen, a charity founded by José Andrés, the renowned Spanish American chef. The United Arab Emirates was providing financing and logistical support for the operation, he said.

“We may fail, but the biggest failure will be not trying!” Mr. Andrés said on Tuesday on social media.

Still, the food was only a tiny fraction of what it would take to alleviate the widespread hunger in Gaza, and aid officials emphasized that it was no substitute for the volume of goods that could be delivered by truck, if Israel opened more land crossings into Gaza. The enclave has been under a near-total blockade since the Oct. 7 Hamas-led attack on Israel.

With no end in sight to the war in Gaza, clashes flared anew along another front, Israel’s northern border, between Israeli forces and the militant group Hezbollah in Lebanon. Hezbollah and Hamas are allies, both backed by Iran, and the fighting along the Israel-Lebanon border has raised fears of a wider regional conflict.

Hezbollah fired more than 100 rockets into northern Israel on Tuesday morning, in one of the heaviest barrages in months of near-daily cross-border strikes, the Israeli military said, and Israeli fighter jets retaliated by striking a number of sites linked to Hezbollah in Lebanon.

In a statement, Hezbollah said it had launched the volley in response to Israeli military strikes on Monday in Lebanon’s Bekaa Valley and as a show of support for Palestinians in Gaza.

The United States, Qatar and Egypt are still trying to negotiate a weekslong cease-fire between Israel and Hamas that would allow for more aid to enter Gaza. Proposals on the table include the release of at least some of the more than 100 hostages still held by Hamas and its allies, in exchange for the release of Palestinians held in Israeli jails.

But negotiators were not able to broker an agreement by the start of Ramadan, the Muslim holy month that began this week, as they had hoped. At a news conference in Doha on Tuesday, Majed al-Ansari, a spokesman for the Qatari foreign ministry, which has been a key mediator in the talks, said he could not offer any timeline for an agreement.

“The situation is very complicated on the ground,” he said. “We are not near a deal, meaning that we are not seeing both sides converging on a language that can resolve the current disagreements over the implementation of the deal.”

Each side has blamed the other for the lack of progress. Hamas has said that it will agree only to a long-term cease-fire that requires Israel to withdraw its troops from Gaza. Israel has said it will not stop fighting in Gaza until Hamas is eliminated as a military threat, and has indicated that even then it would maintain a military presence in the territory.

One person who was thought to be among the hostages still being held in Gaza, Itay Chen, a 19-year-old American and Israeli citizen, was actually killed on Oct. 7, his parents announced on Tuesday. The Hostages and Missing Families forum, a group representing the families of Israeli captives, said his body was being held by Hamas.

The parents, Ruby and Hagit Chen, said that the Israeli military had told them that it had intelligence indicating that their son, an Israeli service member, had been killed while defending civilians on the Israel-Gaza border. Israel’s government says the Hamas-led assault killed about 1,200 people.

“Our hearts are broken,” the Chens said in a statement. “We loved him so much, and we would have done anything to bring him home alive.”

Ruby Chen was part of a group of family members of the hostages who met frequently with U.S. officials to push the White House to negotiate the release of their relatives. Five other Americans are thought to still be held captive in Gaza.

In a statement, Mr. Biden pledged to continue working to bring the hostages home and recalled meeting with Itay Chen’s father and brother at the White House in December.

“No one should have to endure even one day of what they have gone through,” he said. “At the end of our meeting, they gave me a menorah — a solemn reminder that light will always dispel the darkness, and evil will not win.”

According to Israeli intelligence, more than 100 hostages held in Gaza since Oct. 7 are believed to be alive, but at least 30 others are dead. Some were killed during the attack and their bodies taken to Gaza, while others are said to have died in captivity.

Israel’s response to the attack has been a bombing campaign, ground invasion and blockade that have killed more than 30,000 people, according to Gazan health officials, displaced most of the population from their homes, left much of the territory in ruins, and produced severe shortages of food and medicine.

Hunger is especially dire in the north, where U.N. agencies have mostly suspended aid operations, citing Israeli restrictions on convoys, security issues and poor road conditions.

The vessel carrying food for Gaza set sail on Tuesday after the United States, Britain, the European Union and other governments said last week that they would establish a maritime corridor to transport aid from Cyprus to Gaza. It was not clear how the food would be unloaded and distributed once the ship reaches Gaza after a journey of about 240 miles that usually takes 15 to 17 hours, but was expected to take longer because of the heavy load. Mr. Andrés said that the construction of a jetty on the Gaza coast was “well underway.”

Mr. Biden has announced plans to build a floating pier to facilitate aid deliveries because Gaza does not have a functioning port. But U.S. officials have said it could take 30 to 60 days to set it up. American and allied military planes have also parachuted food into Gaza.

Gazan officials have said that sea shipments and airdrops are both cumbersome and cannot come close to supplying as much as trucks. Each truck carrying food into Gaza has been loaded with roughly 16 to 33 tons, according the United Nations and other agencies, meaning that the amount coming by sea is far less than what enters Gaza by land in a single day.

In a statement on Tuesday, two U.N. aid officials, Sigrid Kaag and Jorge Moreira da Silva, said they welcomed the opening of a marine corridor to Gaza. But they added, “For aid delivery at scale there is no meaningful substitute to the many land routes and entry points from Israel into Gaza.”

Israel has insisted on inspecting shipments into Gaza and has argued that aid intended for civilians could be diverted by Hamas. The Israeli government has said it supports the maritime corridor as long as shipments are inspected in Cyprus “in accordance with Israeli standards.”
