Isabel Kershner and Ismaeel Naar

Isabel Kershner reported from Jerusalem, and Ismaeel Naar from Dubai, the United Arab Emirates.

Dec. 26, 2024Updated 4:43 p.m. ET

The Israeli military on Thursday unleashed a significant air assault on parts of Yemen controlled by the Iranian-backed Houthi militia in retaliation for the group’s missile attacks against Israel, striking back at an adversary more than a thousand miles away.

The Israeli assault comes after a week of the Houthis’ near-nightly launches of ballistic missiles and drones against Israel.

Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu previously telegraphed Israel’s aim against its foes in the region, saying on Wednesday of Iran’s proxies and the recently toppled government of Bashar al-Assad in Syria: “The Houthis, too, will learn what Hamas, Hezbollah, the Assad regime and others have learned. And even if it takes time, this lesson will be understood across the Middle East.”

On Thursday after the Israeli strikes, Mr. Netanyahu said in an interview with an Israeli TV station of the Houthis: “We are just getting started with them.”

At least four people were killed and 21 others injured in the attack on Thursday after Israel struck the international airport in Sana and the city of Al Hodeidah, the Saba state news agency said, citing Yemen’s Health Ministry. The report could not be independently verified.

Since Hamas led the Oct. 7, 2023, attack on Israel, the Israeli military has not only decimated the Palestinian group in Gaza, but it has also bombed other Iran-backed proxies in the region, wiping out most of the leadership of the Hezbollah militia in Lebanon after the group began rocket launches into Israel in solidarity with Hamas. Israel has also hit sites in Syria that it said hosted Hezbollah infrastructure. And Israel has struck Iran itself in retaliation for the huge barrages of missiles and drones that Iran launched against Israel.

Military analysts have widely deemed the Houthis less of a threat than other Iran-backed militias like Hezbollah or Hamas. But Israel is apparently turning more attention to the Houthis now as the last major Iranian proxy threatening its national security, after reaching a cease-fire agreement with Hezbollah last month and largely undermining Hamas’s capabilities in Gaza over more than a year of war.

As it intensified its focus on the Houthis, Israel has also kept up what Mr. Netanyahu has called a deliberately “relentless” campaign against Hamas. On Thursday, Gaza’s health ministry said that an Israeli airstrike near Kamal Adwan Hospital had killed 50 people, including five hospital staff members, some of whom died while trying to rescue the injured.

Dr. Hussam Abu Safiya, the hospital’s director, said people were still buried under the rubble. The Israeli military did not immediately respond to a request for comment.

The Israeli military’s attack in Yemen on Thursday targeted not just the airport in Sana, a Houthi stronghold, but also the Hezyaz and Ras Kanatib power stations. Israel described the targets as “military infrastructure used by the Houthi terrorist regime for its military activities.” The jets also struck military infrastructure in the Al-Hudaydah, Salif and Ras Kanatib ports on Yemen’s western coast, the military said.

The director general of the World Health Organization, Dr. Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus, said in a social media post that “the airport came under aerial bombardment” as he and other United Nations officials were about to board a plane there, and that a crew member was injured.

“The air traffic control tower, the departure lounge — just a few meters from where we were — and the runway were damaged,” he wrote, but added that he and his W.H.O. colleagues were safe. They had been on a mission to negotiate the release of detained U.N. staff and to assess the health and humanitarian situation in Yemen, he wrote.

A spokesman for the Israeli military, Lt. Col. Nadav Shoshani, said that Israel had no previous knowledge that the W.H.O. director general would be at the airport at the time of the attack. “We didn’t know,” he said. “We wish him well.”

Where Israel Attacked Yemen on Thursday

In a video statement on Thursday from the Israel Air Force command and control center in Tel Aviv, Mr. Netanyahu said: “We are determined to cut off this terrorist arm of Iran’s axis of evil. We will persist until we get the job done.”

Israel’s defense minister, Israel Katz, who sat in the command center with the prime minister and the military’s chief of staff, Lt. Gen. Herzi Halevi, threatened on Thursday to “hunt all the Houthi leaders.”

General Halevi said the attack demonstrated Israel’s “ability to reach and strike any threat to the citizens of the State of Israel.” He added, “Over the years, we have developed capabilities to strike very far from Israel’s territory — precisely, powerfully and repetitively.”

In response to Israel’s attack, Mohammed Abdulsalam, a Houthi spokesman, said in a statement: “The targeting of Sana International Airport and other civilian infrastructure is a Zionist crime against the entire Yemeni people.” He added, “If the Zionist enemy thinks that its crimes will deter Yemen from supporting Gaza, it is delusional.”

Israel and the Houthis, who act as the de facto government in much of northern Yemen, have traded fire since last October, when the Yemeni militia began firing rockets and drones at Israel in solidarity with Hamas.

The air assault on Thursday was Israel’s fourth in Yemen over the past year and came a week after Israeli fighter jets bombed sites in the country, including in Sana and on the port city of Salif. The Israeli military said it had struck infrastructure like power plants, as well as fuel and oil tanks.

Those strikes killed nine people, according to Al-Masirah, a TV channel affiliated with the Houthis, and came hours after Israel’s military said it had intercepted a missile launched from Yemen.

The Houthis have since stepped up their efforts to strike Israel. A missile launched by the militia landed in a playground in Tel Aviv before dawn on Saturday, shattering glass and injuring multiple residents in nearby buildings. The group then launched another missile early Tuesday, hours after Israel’s defense minister suggested that his government would seek to kill the Houthis’ leaders. The Israeli military said that air defenses had successfully intercepted the missile.

Mohamed Albasha, an analyst on Yemen and founder of the Basha Report, a U.S.-based risk advisory focused on the Middle East, said the intensifying attacks between Israel and the Houthis could portend an extended conflict.

“Projections for 2025 indicate a dire year ahead for Yemen’s population as the outlook grows increasingly grim,” he said. “Israel seems intent on targeting critical infrastructure — including seaports, airports, telecommunications, banks and power stations — echoing strategies employed in its campaign against Lebanon.”

Israel previously struck several of the sites hit on Thursday, but the blows have not appeared to have deterred the Houthis so far. Some Israeli analysts, noting that the conflict with the Houthis may turn into a long-distance war of attrition, have made the case that a direct Israeli strike against Iran, the Houthis’ backer, would be more effective than repeated attacks on Yemen.

Assaf Orion, a retired Israeli brigadier general and an international fellow at the Washington Institute for Near East Policy, said Israel was capable of keeping up with the exchange of blows. But he added, “The question is, what is the strategy?”

In its attacks, the Houthis have launched missiles and drones at cargo vessels crossing the Red Sea, one of the world’s busiest shipping lanes, significantly disrupting international trade, and U.S. forces struck Houthi targets in Yemen on Saturday.

On Wednesday evening, the Israeli military said a drone launched from Yemen had crossed into Israeli territory and had fallen in an open area.

Johnatan Reiss and Ephrat Livni contributed reporting.
