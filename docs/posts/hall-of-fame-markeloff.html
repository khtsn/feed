Hall of Fame: markeloff

The Ukrainian former superstar is part of the Hall's class of 2025 on account of his incredible peak during the late 1.6 years while playing for NAVI.

Yegor "⁠markeloff⁠" Markelov's case for a Hall of Fame induction might seem puzzling to those who reward longevity over peak, but a dominant stretch as impressive as his 2010-2011 run is impossible to dismiss.

The Ukrainian left an indelible mark on the game as part of the vibrant Natus Vincere squad that dominated the scene in 2010, inspiring a new generation of talent, including one Oleksandr "⁠s1mple⁠" Kostyliev. In the end, he may have had a more modest career than some of his fellow Hall of Famers, but his historical significance and high peak make him worthy of induction.

markeloff's life changed at the end of 2009, when he was offered a spot in a new Ukrainian squad featuring players from HellRaisers and KerchNET, the two strongest teams in the country at the time. The lineup was put together by Sergey "⁠starix⁠" Ischuk at the request of Murat "Arbalet" Zhumashevich, a Kazakh multimillionaire who had been sponsoring international tournaments that year, offering the scene a much-needed lifeline in the wake of the 2008 financial crisis.

According to Arseny "⁠ceh9⁠" Trynozhenko, the players were offered a monthly salary of around $1,000 — a monumental step up from what they had been earning until then.

"It was a huge amount of money," markeloff told HLTV in a remote video interview. It was awesome, but because of that, we felt a lot of pressure on ourselves. And maybe that's why it was one of the reasons we were so motivated to improve ourselves and we were trying to win every tournament we participated in because we didn't want to upset Arbalet, [or] he would stop supporting us.

"It was all verbal, and we knew that if something went wrong, we could lose that opportunity, and we didn't want to. Of course, everybody wanted to win, no matter what, but it was an extra motivation for us."

markeloff at this first international LAN, the KODE5 Global Finals in 2008

The team raised a few eyebrows in the scene, but with Ukraine having little international pedigree to speak of — Amazing Gaming's bronze medal at WCG 2007 in Seattle being the notable exception —, expectations were kept in check.

Even markeloff, an AWPer who had grown up watching frag movies "with cool music," was regarded as a bit of a gamble. "His effectiveness wasn't that high," Duncan "Thorin" Shields recalled in a 2015 video. "He would take lots of crazy shots and he would try very difficult shots. He would just go for it, and he wouldn't set it up. But he would whiff a lot of them.

"You saw some potential to be better, but it wasn't quite realized."

It took markeloff only a couple of months with his new team to establish himself as one of the best players in the world. His reign of terror began at the IEM IV World Championship in Hannover, where he put up a 1.26 rating, a 0.38 AWP KPR, and eight clutches as Natus Vincere pocketed the lion's share of a $100,000 prize pool, the highest on offer in 2010.

NAVI rose to the top of the food chain when they won the IEM 4 World Championship

That was one of the big three events of the year, along with ESWC and WCG, both of which Natus Vincere also won, beating SK to the title in Paris and mTw in the final in Los Angeles. markeloff was the (unofficial) MVP each time.

NAVI also won Arbalet Cup Asia, Arbalet Cup Dallas, Arbalet Cup Best of Four, and DreamHack Winter, and had top-four finishes at four other events — crafting one of the best seasons in Counter-Strike history.

"It was an insane year," markeloff reflected. "I don't know the reason. Maybe people weren't ready for us or expecting us to be this good. Maybe we got lucky and were just winning the crucial rounds. I have no idea. It is what it is.

"But we played really hard, we tried our best, we played 24/7 in the bootcamp and whatnot. It just happened."

markeloff posted some incredible numbers that season: he finished 80 percent of his matches with at least a 1.00 rating and averaged a 1.22 rating, 0.78 KPR, 0.53 DPR and 0.30 AWP KPR.

"In conclusion, Markelov was the most consistent player and the most successful awper along with many other impressive stats; he was the best at the most important tournaments and played excellent in many important matches," HLTV wrote as it awarded markeloff the No.1 spot in the inaugural Top 20 Player of the Year ranking.

Questioned about his massive glow-up between 2009 and 2010, markeloff said:

"I think that the main reason was my teammates and how we played together," he explained. "They helped me a lot, and without them I wouldn't be here at all. It was a team effort. You couldn't play and win tournaments by yourself back in the days; you needed to play all together.

"Zeus usually supported me in eco rounds, dropping Deagles, trying to boost me somewhere, trying to go first so I could refrag. So it was not all me; it was my team and teammates as a whole."

Natus Vincere began 2011 with a fourth-place finish at the IEM V European Championship before successfully defending its IEM World Championship title in Hannover, beating compLexity, mTw and Frag eXecutors in the playoffs.

It looked like NAVI were on course for another historical year, but that turned out to be the only major title they secured in 2011. markeloff remained as dominant as he had been the year before — even though starix was taking up AWPing duties more often at times — but the rest of the team struggled to keep up.

He was in the MVP discussion at five of the eight big tournaments he attended and was a solid performer in the other three. "What impressed the most about him was that he didn't play badly in any tournament, while he had five amazing performances where he earned MVP nominations," HLTV wrote as it gave markeloff the No.3 spot in the Top 20, behind Filip "⁠NEO⁠" Kubski and Christopher "⁠GeT_RiGhT⁠" Alesund.

The team got off to a strong start to the 2012 season, winning the IEM Global Challenge in Kyiv and finishing second at the IEM VI World Championship. But by then, the 1.6 scene was already on its last legs as development of CS:GO continued to advance. The new game was officially released in August, but while most of the scene immediately made the switch, Natus Vincere and a few other teams continued to play 1.6 a while longer.

NAVI's final 1.6 tournament took place in October 2012, when they finished second to a fnatic team featuring Finn "⁠karrigan⁠" Andersen at PGS 2012 in Portugal. By then, NIP were already on their way to building their 87-0 map win streak on LAN.

NAVI's last 1.6 event in October 2012; by then, many teams had been playing CS:GO for several months

"It was a big mistake to get into CS:GO so late, when people had already been playing this game for a year," markeloff reflected.

"Nobody liked the game in the state it was. It was awful. I think that most players who played at the time were Source players. I assume so, I'm not sure. If I could change something, it would be a faster transition into CS:GO. Maybe things would have been different. Who knows?"

That was, markeloff admits, one of the biggest regrets of his career. Another was his departure from NAVI in the summer of 2013, which stemmed from a place of frustration after the team's rocky start to life in CS:GO.

After a close grand final against Ninjas in Pyjamas at the StarSeries VI Finals, they lost the DISTRICT Prague Challenge title to Nostalgie, an unlikely mix team put together by Aleksei "⁠OverDrive⁠" Birukov that also featured Dan "⁠apEX⁠" Madesclaire, Kenny "⁠kennyS⁠" Schrub, Wiktor "⁠TaZ⁠" Wojtas and NEO. He later called that defeat "a disaster, the most epic throw ever."

"It was really hard not to be on top all of the time after all of our success, and I saw that our potential was drained," he explains.

"We couldn't play together anymore; nothing worked out. I wasn't thinking about leaving or something. I was just frustrated about the game, and after a tournament in Prague, I came back home, and ANGE1 showed up at the train station, and we had a conversation. He asked me if I was interested in something new, and I said, 'Fuck it, why not? Because this is not going to work.'

"I knew that some players wanted to leave so that I would stay in NAVI, but I didn't want that. I didn't want somebody to leave the team just because I don't want to play on it anymore, so I decided that I would leave."

markeloff went on to join Astana Dragons, with whom he enjoyed a successful second half of the season, winning the TECHLABS Cup 2013 Finals — which earned him his sole MVP medal —, and placing second at StarSeries VII and third at ESWC and DreamHack Bucharest. There was also DreamHack Winter, the first-ever Counter-Strike Major, where Astana Dragons reached the quarter-finals before losing to Complexity.

Even though markeloff was far removed from the form that had defined his peak years, his floor was high enough to land him the 15th spot in the HLTV Top 20 — his last-ever appearance in the ranking.

2014 marked the beginning of a shift in his career, as he started to use the AWP less frequently. He took a back seat and became more of a supportive element, first on HellRaisers and then on FlipSid3, his final team.

"I was satisfied playing the game, that was all that mattered for me," he said. "It didn't matter if I was AWPing, rifling, or supporting. It's all part of the game; you're part of the team, and it's a team effort to win games and tournaments."

FlipSid3 are mostly remembered for finding ways to pull off upsets in big tournaments, including Majors. At ESWC 2015, they reached the semi-finals with Spencer "⁠Hiko⁠" Martin as a stand-in. At the 2016 Cologne Major, they beat OpTic and Ninjas in Pyjamas to make it to the playoffs.

With markeloff on the roster, FlipSid3 made eight Majors in a row, but the cracks became hard to ignore at Boston 2018, where they were eliminated in the Challenger Stage with a 0-3 record following losses to Misfits, Liquid and AVANGAR.

A last-place finish in Boston was the beginning of the end for FlipSid3

The magic was gone. The team limped through the following months, failing to qualify for any events and even finishing ESEA MDL in last place with a 3-13 record. The death blow was a 12th-14th place finish in the closed qualifier for the CIS Minor leading to the FACEIT Major — markeloff's final tournament as a professional player.

"It was really fun," markeloff reflected on his final years as a player. "I enjoyed playing those days." It was really cool until the moment it wasn't, when we started losing a lot, much more than we had before, and started not even qualifying for regional Major qualifiers.

"We switched players like every month and had stand-ins for this and that tournament. There were a lot of problems at that time, in real life and in the game, on the team, and in the organization. It was a lot of stuff. All combined, I burnt myself out."

FlipSid3 disbanded shortly after the Minor qualifier, with Igor "⁠lollipop21k⁠" Solodkov and Jan "⁠wayLander⁠" Rahkonen being placed on the transfer list, and Andrey "⁠B1ad3⁠" Gorodenskiy transitioning into coaching. Initially, markeloff thought he would step away from the game for a bit and then come back, but he didn't touch CS "for like three or four years." At some point, he felt it was just too late to return to activity.

Being the first Ukrainian to be inducted into the Hall of Fame is a huge source of pride for markeloff, but he knows he won't be the last. Eventually, he will be joined by GOAT contender s1mple, who grew up idolizing him. The two eventually crossed paths in 2014 and 2015 on HellRaisers and FlipSid3.

"It's no secret that he's very hard to play with," markeloff said. "He was very harsh sometimes, but outside of the game, he's a really cool guy. But in the game, he was much better than all of us at the time. It was no secret. And of course, he wanted more, more and more, and he saw that we couldn't keep up with his performance. He was frustrated all of the time, and that continued in all other teams because nobody could come even close to him.

markeloff playing alongside B1ad3 and s1mple at ESWC 2015

"I talked to him a lot about this, that he needed to be calm, that he needed to respect his teammates... I knew he was young; he was 17 or something, and I thought that it was just a phase, that he would get better and be smarter."

markeloff was never one for the spotlight, even during his heyday — which helps explains why he never felt the need to formally announce his retirement, or why the absence of a proper farewell doesn't seem to trouble him.

When asked about how he views his legacy in the game, he preferred to focus on how far NAVI have come since he first donned their colours.

"I'm very proud of what we did with NAVI back in the day," he said. "I'm very happy to see this organization growing from year to year.

"I still consider it like my second home in my mind, no matter the lineup or the game they play, I always try to watch NAVI games in Dota2, in CS, in PUBG, wherever, and I'm still cheering for them like they're my own. It's a part of me, and it always will be."
