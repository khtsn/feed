Donald Trump, Prince of Self-Pity

The size of the crowd at a rally for Vice President Kamala Harris in Atlanta last month rivaled the turnout for Donald Trump days later only “because she had entertainers,” Trump told the audience at his event, referring to the rappers Quavo and Megan Thee Stallion. “I don’t need entertainers.”

Translation: Harris cheated. Even so, she didn’t get the better of him.

She isn’t really Black but “happened to turn Black” over the course of her political career. That’s what Trump said at a meeting of the National Association of Black Journalists, insinuating that Harris had performed a melanin metamorphosis and was falsely improvising identities to contrive some perk unavailable to him.

Poor Trump. Always forced to compete on an uneven playing field.

Of all his feats of projection, which is psychology’s term for seeing your own methods and motivations in someone else, none fascinates me more than his incessant insistence that every one of his adversaries — that everyone, period — is the beneficiary of some scheme or scam that puts him at a disadvantage. If he triumphs nonetheless? It’s a testament to his peerless might. If he doesn’t? It was never a fair fight.

He’s the prince of self-pity, the bard of bellyaching, reportedly worked up over the imagined injustice or trickery of Harris’s late replacement of President Biden on the Democratic ticket. According to an article in The Washington Post this week, he told an ally: “It’s unfair that I beat him and now I have to beat her, too.”

The more assertively Trump presses a complaint, the more you know it’s bunk. He operates on the theory that if you’re selling falsehoods, peddle like the wind; your audience might well assume that you’d never speak that extravagantly and be that audacious if there weren’t some legitimacy to your claim.

So it is with his underdog yap — his pantomime of Cinderella when he’s really her wickedest stepsister.

He was born to a rich family. His father’s money and connections greased the start of his career. Legions of lawyers helped him skate away from bankruptcies and bad behavior. The moral cratering of the Republican Party gave him license to do and coup as he pleased. Friendly judges — including on the Supreme Court — afforded him impunity.

And, of course, he’s the schemer. I direct you to the “perfect phone call” that prompted his first impeachment. To the Big Lie that prompted his second. To lesser shadiness galore. Cutting corners is his cardio.

But woe is Trump! The Justice Department targets him as it does no one else. (That would be news to Hunter Biden.) The media simply blows kisses at Democrats. (News to Joe Biden, now cheering Harris from the sidelines.)

According to Trump’s public statements or emails sent by his campaign, top Republicans in Georgia are conniving to ensure his defeat there, Democrats from coast to coast are rigging the vote, plutocrats are showering Harris with such a deluge of campaign contributions that she’s basically buying the election, and on and on.

The forces arrayed against him are infinite and omnipotent. And Harris, as a Black woman, has a first-class ticket to 1600 Pennsylvania Avenue. She can bask in tokenism and entertainment-industry love while he struggles for a mere sliver of the spotlight.

When children exhibit persecution complexes this severe, they’re sent to their rooms or to a therapist. Trump may be sent to the White House — for a second time. Woe is us.

Can you ride a one-syllable adjective all the way to political glory? Many accounts of Gov. Tim Walz’s selection as Harris’s running mate suggest as much, claiming that his ascent was a function of not only his political record, yes, and his biography, absolutely, but also his vocabulary.

And that’s just … weird.

Walz and “weird.” “Weird” and Walz. They’re joined at more than the W. They’re twin pillars of what has already taken on the gleam of myth: The Democratic Party was long stymied in its efforts to define Donald Trump in a manner that truly resonated, snugly fit and genuinely wounded, and then this jocular, avuncular Minnesotan turned his gaze toward the orange overlord and pronounced him “weird.” Cable news commentators swooned. Social media rejoiced. A thousand memes bloomed.

Oh, and Harris found her perfect partner, someone deemed singularly effective at making the case against Trump.

I just don’t know about that.

I’m not questioning Walz’s suitability and utility as a vice-presidential pick. He has a whole lot going for him. I’m just not sold on the wizardry of “weird.” I share my Times Opinion colleague Tom Friedman’s concern that it’s undignified, blurring an important contrast between the Democratic and Republican parties today. I also think it trivializes and minimizes the problems with — and the threats posed by — Trump.

He’s contemptuous, cruel, bigoted, unscrupulous, dishonest, egomaniacal and more. In the context of all the descriptions rightly and readily applied to him, “weird” comes close to a compliment.

For the Love of Sentences

On NPR’s website, Linda Holmes had a strong reaction to recent ads for Gemini, Google’s A.I. assistant, that promoted its use for writing: “The marketing of generative A.I. is a broadside against singularity in favor of digestibility, against creativity in favor of drudgery. It’s perfect for anyone who watched the video for Pink Floyd’s ‘The Wall’ and rooted for the meat grinder.” (Thanks to Chris Curtis of Midland, Mich., for nominating this.)

In The Atlantic, a recent roundup of excellent movies that are only about 90 minutes long included a mini-review by Damon Beres of “Notes on a Scandal,” which stars Judi Dench as a boundlessly bitter teacher: “Dench is almost unrecognizable in the role; she’s done up like a raisin you’d find lodged between your couch cushions, and about half as pleasant to behold.” (Karin Lagerson, Philadelphia, and Jean Lindsay, Prescott, Ariz., among others)

Also in The Atlantic, David A. Graham marveled at Trump’s continuing to question Harris’s Black identity even after the grotesqueness and political lunacy of that was made clear: “Finding himself in a hole, Trump tossed aside his shovel and reached for a jackhammer.” (Debbie Landis, Garrison, N.Y., and Elaine Satlak, Boston, among others)

In The Stamford Advocate, Susan Campbell explored how a “choir of male keyboard warriors” took issue with every aspect of Harris’s background, including the time she dated Willie Brown, a prominent California Democrat: “The choir decries Harris’s sexual history, as if a long-ago relationship in a faraway state had the power to propel her all the way to the White House. I mean, the male sexual organ simply does not possess that kind of magical power, guys, and I’m sorry to be the one to tell you.” (Ken Horowitz, Stamford, Conn.)

In The Times, Rebecca Davis O’Brien observed that if Harris’s campaign “started out as ‘Veep,’ it has now taken a detour to ‘Ted Lasso,’ by way of ‘Friday Night Lights.’” (Marjorie Gadue, Colchester, Vt., and Patricia Hewitt, Manhattan)

In The Star Tribune of Minneapolis, in advance of Harris’s veep pick, Sheletta Brundidge reflected on an unusual challenge and dynamic confronting whichever of the finalists would end up in the winner’s circle: “Being strategically invisible or staying in the background will require a humility that many white men have never had to demonstrate. She’s going to be Gladys Knight; he will be a Pip.” (John Jensen, Vadnais Heights, Minn.)

In The Washington Post, Daniel Pink reviewed the plans floated for a second Trump administration: “Trump allies have laid out two equally disturbing plans of governance. One is Project 2025, 900 pages of authoritarian revenge porn that rolls back rights, rewinds regulations and rewards the president with the powers of a king. The other is the platform the party unveiled at its convention this month — a shaggy collection of randomly capitalized catchphrases and outbursts that read as if Bart Simpson scratched them on a chalkboard.” (Mary Alice Schiller, Randolph, N.J., and Douglas Ochs Adler, White Stone, Va.)

In The New Yorker, Anthony Lane studied Trump’s bearing and mien as he listened to tributes at the Republican National Convention: “Occasionally, he stood to applaud, but most of the time he was pleased to wear an expression of froggy beatitude — a soft wide grin, ascending far above smugness to achieve a kind of gratified peace. Thus would a medieval liege lord have accepted obeisance from his vassals; all that was missing was the flicker of torchlight and the haunch of venison turning on its spit.” (Elliot Brown, Manhattan)

Also in The New Yorker, Katherine Rundell defended finned ocean predators against a senseless slur of a phrase: “There is no such thing as shark-infested waters, in the same way that there is no such thing as a child-infested school.” In contrast, she added: “Beware an ExxonMobil-infested State Department; beware a fossil-fuel-infested politics. These are dark times, and there are many things to fear. But none of them are found swimming under a vast sky as the waters around us warm and empty.” (David P. Barash, Goleta, Calif.)

And Adam Gopnick flashed back to British treatment of convicted criminals in the past: “Public hangings were simply part of street life. Pickpockets attended the hangings of other pickpockets in order to pick pockets.” (Max Sinclair, DeKalb, Ill.)

What I’m Reading and Writing

  • Was Harris’s decision to have Walz join her on the Democratic ticket bold or safe, wise or worrying? Here are a few analyses, presenting different perspectives, that I found especially interesting: Nate Silver’s argument that Gov. Josh Shapiro of Pennsylvania would have been the smarter pick; a succinct summary in Politico of the reasons that Harris went with Walz; in The Times, Howard Wolfson’s examination of Walz’s football coach past as a crucial asset; also in The Times, Nate Cohn’s appraisal of the ideological cast of the Democratic ticket; and in his newsletter, Sanity Clause, Joe Klein’s quibble with descriptions of Walz as left-wing.

  • We’ve had plenty of major-party presidential nominees who went to college in the Ivy League; Harris is the first from one of the country’s historically Black colleges and universities, often referred to as H.B.C.U.s. (She went to the best known of them, Howard University.) How important have those schools been to the cultivation of Black leaders and to the life of our nation? My wise and charismatic Duke University colleague Deondra Rose examines that in her new book, “The Power of Black Excellence: HBCUs and the Fight for American Democracy.”

On a Personal Note

You can’t predict the best sunsets. Or, if you can, I don’t know how. You can examine the pattern of clouds on the horizon, watch the sun as its downward slide seems to accelerate and guess, correctly, that its final bow will be pretty.

But spectacular? That’s serendipity. And that’s much of what makes it so special.

I finished dinner with friends on Long Island recently and walked outside to behold the colors in the image above. To say that they stopped me in my tracks would be a cliché. It would also be accurate. I felt almost absurdly happy. And I later contemplated why.

Maybe because so much in our lives becomes so complicated, especially as the years accrue and our web of acquaintances, obligations and regrets sprawls. We have all these decisions and amends to make, so many needs crowding our thoughts and wants roiling our hearts. It can be unnerving, overwhelming. Then Mother Nature stages a show like the one to which she treated me and, for a moment, nothing matters but the beauty before you. Pleasure is as simple as opening your eyes. Peace is as easy as standing still.

I’m so often so jaded. I can predict how the seemingly happy story will turn dark. I brace for the unveiling of the newest hero’s flaws. On various fronts and in various ways, I gird for disappointment, reasoning that it will be softened by my preparations for it. Then the sky catches fire more brilliantly than I could have imagined, and I’m reacquainted with a feeling from which I should never have estranged myself. It’s the tickle of wonder.
