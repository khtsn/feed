Leaders Back a Climate Fund and Voice Frustrations at COP28

On the first day of the U.N.’s climate summit, known as COP28, diplomats from nearly 200 countries approved a draft plan for a fund to help poor countries hit by climate disasters. The speedy adoption of the fund, which developing nations fought more than 30 years to create, was widely viewed as a positive sign for the two-week summit in Dubai.

But in a year that scientists say is “nearly certain” to be the hottest in human history, and as emission levels reach record highs, many attendees brought to the summit a sense of disillusionment with the U.N. effort. Some climate advocates are frustrated that COP28 is being hosted by the United Arab Emirates, which is ramping up its production of oil and has been accused of using its host position to promote its fossil fuels.

“It’s impossible to ignore the fact that the summit is being held inside gleaming new facilities built with oil money,” said my colleague David Gelles, who is covering the summit.

However, David said that recent developments offered hope for the summit. Two weeks ago, the U.S. and China, the world’s two biggest polluters, agreed to accelerate efforts to sharply increase clean energy, although they didn’t provide a timeline.

The U.S. urged Israel to take steps to protect Gazans

Secretary of State Antony Blinken met with Israeli leaders today in an effort to extend the fragile cease-fire between Israel and Hamas — which is set to end tomorrow morning — and to improve conditions for civilians in Gaza. Blinken said Israel had a right to ensure that Hamas was dismantled. But, he added, “The way Israel defends itself matters.”

Kissinger, who died at 100, was both celebrated and reviled

Henry Kissinger, the scholar turned diplomat who advised 12 presidents from Kennedy to Biden, died yesterday at 100. His life story is without comparison: Kissinger fled Nazi Germany at 15, and later rose to become the most powerful secretary of state of the postwar era. His passing elicited sharply divergent reactions.

Kissinger engineered the country’s opening to China, negotiated its exit from Vietnam and remade U.S. power relationships with the Soviet Union, sometimes trampling on democratic values to do so. Friends have hailed his diplomatic achievements, while critics assailed his policies in Vietnam, Cambodia and elsewhere around the globe as murderous.

A ‘Goon Squad’ of deputies got away with years of brutality

For nearly two decades, a loose band of sheriff’s deputies, who sometimes called themselves the Goon Squad, roamed impoverished neighborhoods across a central Mississippi county, meting out its own version of justice. The deputies barged into homes and held people down while they beat and choked them, witnesses said. Their violence might have continued unchecked if not for one near-fatal raid in January, after which several pleaded guilty to criminal charges.

‘May December’ is sly, humorous and unnerving

Todd Haynes’s eagerly awaited film “May December,” starring Natalie Portman, Julianne Moore and Charles Melton, arrives in theaters tomorrow. Portman’s character is an actress studying to portray Moore’s character — a woman with a dark tabloid back story.

When the film premiered at Cannes this spring, my colleague Kyle Buchanan called it the festival’s “most fun movie.” Our critic Manohla Dargis agrees, writing in her review that Haynes and his stars nail the unnerving tale with destabilizing humor. “The first time I watched the movie, I almost clapped my hand over my mouth during one absurd moment, unsure if I was supposed to be laughing this hard,” she wrote.

Penguins are champion power nappers

A new study found that over the course of a single day, a penguin can fall asleep thousands of times, each bout a few seconds long.

“It’s really unusual,” said Paul-Antoine Libourel, a neuroscientist who helped make the discovery. He speculated that their sleep patterns reflected the extreme, noisy and crowded conditions where they dozed. Humans, too, can experience this sort of microsleep, though typically only after failing to get a good night’s rest.
