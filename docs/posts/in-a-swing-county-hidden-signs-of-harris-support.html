In a Swing County, Hidden Signs of Harris Support

Oct. 18, 2024, 5:31 p.m. ET

I recently spent the night at my aunt Eleanor’s house, in Luzerne County, Pa., in an old coal mining community near the Poconos that has the odd distinction of having voted twice for Barack Obama and then twice for Donald Trump. I knew I’d reached her driveway when I saw a Harris-Walz sign and another that read, “Lock him up,” next to a security camera that she installed to stop her neighbors from tearing them down. So many Trump signs dotted that old country road that you’d think my aunt was the only Democrat in town.

But she invited nine of her friends over to talk politics with me, including three Republicans who planned to vote for Kamala Harris. They felt just as passionately as my aunt did, but many said they didn’t want to put up signs. One said she and her husband had moved there recently and didn’t want to rock the boat with their neighbors. Another expressed fear of “weird militias.”

“People are very nervous what’s going to happen,” Becky Loftus, a Puerto Rican woman who married a coal miner’s son from the area, told the group. If Harris wins, there will be violence, she said, and if Trump wins, there will be violence. “It’s a no-win situation.”

They were not unlike Democrats in other places. They hated Trump — his lies, his policies, his character, his hair. They had all gone through the same emotions as every other Democrat I knew: depression after Trump’s debate with President Biden, skepticism that Harris could lead and then absolute elation when she made it a race again after Biden dropped out. Now they were experiencing a new emotion: political dread of Election Day, when, after all that we as a nation have been through, it will still not be over.

But it felt good anyway, they said, to gather in the same room and be reminded that they weren’t alone. Records show that there are almost the same number of registered Democrats (87,314) as Republicans in the county (87,724) and there are 22,456 unaffiliated with either party.

There is no doubt that Trump has deep support here. But perhaps not as deep as one would think from counting signs along these old country roads. Let’s remember that yard signs don’t vote. People do.

The Texas justice system really wants to kill Robert Roberson.

It’s hard to come to any other conclusion after watching the life-or-death pinball game judges and prosecutors there have been playing with Roberson, who was sentenced to die in connection with the death of his 2-year-old daughter, Nikki, in 2002. In the two decades since, overwhelming evidence of Roberson’s innocence has come to light, and yet those with the power to stop his execution have refused to act or have pushed affirmatively ahead with plans to end his life.

On Thursday night, the pinball took a small bounce in Roberson’s favor, when the Texas Supreme Court halted his execution at the last minute. The pause was temporary and procedural; his death sentence could still be carried out soon.

It would be the nation’s first execution in a case involving shaken-baby syndrome, according to Roberson’s lawyers. That was how Nikki died, according to doctors at the time, but the diagnosis — both as to her and generally — has been thrown into doubt as evidence of its scientific unreliability has mounted.

The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals, the state’s highest criminal court, acknowledged that unreliability just last week, when it ordered a new trial in another shaken-baby case. The court cited Texas’ “junk science” law, which allows prisoners to challenge their convictions in light of changed scientific understandings. But the court has refused to invoke that law in Roberson’s case, perhaps because his prosecutors continue to stand by their expert testimony from the trial.

The court has also refused to consider other evidence in Roberson’s favor, including straightforward medical explanations for Nikki’s death and his own diagnosis of autism, which would explain his apparent lack of emotion on the night Nikki died.

The case for Roberson’s innocence has become powerful enough to compel a bipartisan group of 86 Texas lawmakers to plead for a grant of clemency from Gov. Greg Abbott. Earlier Thursday, Supreme Court Justice Sonia Sotomayor made the same plea in response to the court’s denial of Roberson’s request for a stay of execution. Citing what she said was the “mounting evidence” that Nikki died of pneumonia and improperly prescribed medication, she lamented that the federal courts did not have jurisdiction in the case, but urged the governor to grant a reprieve of 30 days to give the pardon board a chance to review the evidence of innocence.

“Few cases more urgently call for such a remedy than one where the accused has made a serious showing of actual innocence, as Roberson has here,” Sotomayor wrote.

Abbott has yet to act, and he may not be able to at this point, under a Texas law that bars clemency if the state pardons board has recommended against it.

Perhaps the most compelling advocate in Roberson’s corner is Brian Wharton, the chief detective who investigated Nikki’s case, but who has since come to have what he calls “unassailable doubt” about Roberson’s guilt. He is horrified that the state is moving forward to execute him anyway.

“We claim to be a pro-life state and yet we stack ’em up in the death house faster than anybody else,” Wharton told The Times in a video for Times Opinion.

“Humans are too fallible to do this fairly,” he said. “We make mistakes.”

Mistakes can be fixed; executions cannot. That’s why the only sure way to avoid the chaos and miscarriage of justice in cases like Roberson’s is to ban state-sanctioned killing for good.

I will say this as far as Springfield is concerned because I do know that situation, you have a city of 52,000 people and they’ve added almost 30,000 migrants into the city. Oct. 17, 2024, 2:36 p.m. ET

It’s no surprise that the internet would fiercely grieve the first death of a member of One Direction, the band of teenagers that gave birth to online culture, although no one expected this day to come so soon. On Wednesday, the British singer and songwriter Liam Payne, 31, died after falling from a third-floor balcony in Buenos Aires; he will most likely be remembered by audiences for songs that stole the hearts of girls across the globe in the 2010s.

But the band he helped build will surely be remembered for the way its devoted audience became the architects of contemporary fandom. As news of Payne’s death spread online, long-dormant fan accounts sputtered back to life to marshal parasocial grief, earnest social media tributes and teary fan edits of the band’s best days.

But what these tributes do not capture is the way the public that propelled Payne to the heights of fame spent years doing precious little to avert his undoing.

Anyone who has been paying attention to Payne’s career after One Direction fell apart will have noticed that he was relentlessly mocked by pop culture watchers for false starts in his solo career, his comments about the band, his struggles with substance abuse, his body and the degree to which he supported his former bandmates.

In recent weeks, The Daily Mail reported that Maya Henry, Payne’s ex-fiancée, accused Payne of obsessively contacting her. There was a cascade of networked harassment from fans and critics directed at both parties — at Payne for his alleged behavior and at Henry for daring to speak about it. After his death, this online harassment grew more acute.

Successive waves of anonymized internet outrage do not offer sufficient opportunities for change, rehabilitation or justice. Instead, they trap people in the worst-case versions of their past selves in the name of perceived proximity and clever dunking. More crucially, telling a story of someone’s life on the basis of online narratives can easily occlude systemic issues, like, for instance, the grueling conditions that make teenagers competing on a reality competition show into a hyper-commercial boy band that burned so bright it collapsed on itself.

The singer Rebecca Ferguson, who competed on the reality competition show “The X-Factor” with Payne, intimated in a social media statement that Payne was a victim of an industry’s “exploitation and profiteering of young stars” and claimed that he would be alive if he hadn’t auditioned. There’s no way to know whether she is right. But anyone who’s paying attention does know that time and again, former teen stars have spoken about how the entertainment industry prioritizes profit at the expense of their stability, safety and sense of self.

It’s not difficult to see shades of their stories in what the public knows about One Direction’s rise to fame. Consider what one of the band’s major-domos, Simon Cowell, said when he was asked last year what he regretted about One Direction. His answer? Not Zayn Malik’s anxiety or Payne’s alcoholism, which each attributed to his time in the group, or the band’s exhausting schedule. (The band recorded five albums in five years while touring the globe.) Instead, Cowell said: “I should have owned the name. That’s the problem. When you give an artist the name, it’s not yours.”

An artist’s audience need not be as callous or controlling as the industry that brings him or her to our ears. And so it’s worth remembering that One Direction’s greatest legacy lies with the fan culture that made its members stars, for better and for worse.

Oct. 17, 2024, 5:24 a.m. ET

The Nebraska Supreme Court struck a blow for democracy on Wednesday when it ruled that people with felony convictions could register to vote upon completion of their sentences instead of waiting two years, as the state had historically required.

The Nebraska Legislature had eliminated the waiting period in a bipartisan vote this year, but the secretary of state tried to block the new law, asserting that it was unconstitutional. The State Supreme Court ruling could add thousands of voters to the rolls — a momentous development at a time of hotly contested elections at all levels.

The change in Nebraska is part of a welcome trend outlined in a new analysis by the Sentencing Project, a nonprofit group that studies inequality in the criminal justice system. Since Jan. 1, 2020, 11 states — including California, Connecticut, Iowa and Minnesota, — have expanded voting rights for some non-incarcerated people with felony convictions. As states have enacted policies to curtail disenfranchisement, there has also been a decline in the number of people in prison, on probation and out on parole. As a consequence, the disenfranchised population has fallen from a little under six million in 2016 to about four million today.

It is important to bear in mind that fewer than a third of those who are barred from the polls are incarcerated. Approximately 72 percent of those not allowed to vote are living in their communities — on probation, on parole or after completion of their sentences — carrying the shame of being denied a right that is fundamental to democracy.

The denial of this right casts a shadow over the poor communities where those who are locked out of the electoral system tend to live. Young people in these enclaves grow up with the unfortunate example of neighbors and family members who cannot vote and are permanently disengaged from the political process. The all-too-common lack of clarity about voting rules as they apply to former offenders leaves many Americans who are eligible to vote fearful of walking into the polls.

Mass disenfranchisement as practiced in the United States stands in sharp contrast with how other democracies handle this matter. Our peer nations would never think of stripping the vote from their citizens as casually as we do. In fact, many nations keep inmates connected to the societies to which most will inevitably return by setting up polling places behind prison walls. International analyses invariably show that the United States is an outlier in permanently marginalizing people who are convicted of crimes.

One especially troubling thing about state laws that bar people convicted of crimes from voting is where those bans came from. They proliferated during the late 19th and early 20th centuries, as states took extraordinary measures to prevent Black Americans from exercising the right to vote under the 15th Amendment. This racially discriminatory system normalized disenfranchisement, allowing it to spread unchecked. The sooner the United States parts with this toxic legacy, the better off it will be.

Sharon Manitta, a 76-year-old retired textile conservator in Wiltshire, England, has been doing her best to get out the vote for Kamala Harris on the European side of the pond. Manitta, who hails from Auburn, N.Y., moved to Wiltshire for work and stuck around. Now she’s the global press secretary for Democrats Abroad, the overseas arm of the Democratic National Committee, which sets up tables at farmers markets and touristy areas in London with information about how Americans living abroad can cast a ballot from afar.

“People are working every minute they have, seven days a week, around their normal lives to do this work,” she told me in a phone conversation. “We are so concerned.”

An estimated 4.4 million Americans live overseas — from digital nomads to U.S. soldiers to students who are studying abroad. According to the Federal Voting Assistance Program, more than 1.2 million Americans living abroad were registered to vote in 2020. They make up an overlooked constituency that can pack a surprising political punch. In 2020, 18,475 overseas voters cast ballots in Georgia, a state President Biden won by 12,670 votes, while 18,435 cast ballots in Arizona, where Biden won by 10,457 votes.

Last week, in a bid to woo these voters, Donald Trump pledged to address their greatest grievance: double taxation. Americans are virtually alone in the world in having to file taxes, even if they don’t live in the United States. It’s a big bureaucratic headache, especially for “accidental” Americans who simply happen to have been born in the country but haven’t lived there for years. As in his previous tax cut proposals, he didn’t say how much it would add to the deficit, or in this case how he would prevent Americans from moving abroad to low-tax countries just to save on their tax bills.

“We have been working on this problem for over 10 years, with other American organizations,” Manitta told me. (Exceptions for health insurance requirements was another big issue for them.) But she is unmoved by Trump’s campaign promises. “If he didn’t do anything before, when he was in office,” she asked, “what makes people think he’s going to do something this time?”

If you live abroad, it’s not too late to exercise your right to vote: Go to the nonpartisan website Votefromabroad.org to find out how.
