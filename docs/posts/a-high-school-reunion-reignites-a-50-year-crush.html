Elaine Hall and Roland Passaro, both 88, met in junior high school in 1950 but lost touch before acting on their connection. They finally did, five decades later.

Roland John Passaro looks back at his time growing up in the 1950s with a sort of wistful tenderness, especially when it comes to the time he spent with his first crush, Elaine Hall.

The two met in the ninth grade in 1950 at what was then called Harrison-Morton Junior High School in Allentown, Pa. It wasn’t hard for them to get along. He was a star athlete — the pitcher on the baseball team and a shooting guard and forward on the basketball team. She was a cheerleader.

Together, they danced away many Saturday nights at the Y.M.C.A., went swimming in the community pool in the summers and attended sock hops and house parties. They lived only four blocks from each other and walked home together after school.

Mr. Passaro was also Ms. Hall’s first crush, but, as he said, “there was somewhat of an innocence about the period.” They didn’t even go so far as to kiss, and, over time, the attraction ran its course. After graduating from junior high school and switching over to Allentown High School for the 11th grade, they saw each other less and less. They were in a class of about 900 students, and they had very different schedules. He dated other girls, and she met the man she would marry at 18.

After graduating from high school in 1953, they went their separates ways. Mr. Passaro, 88, became a player for the minor-league baseball team Winston-Salem Red Birds from 1957 to 1961, retiring after suffering a spinal injury. He graduated from what was then called Moravian College in Bethlehem, Pa., with a bachelor’s degree in English. There, he met the woman that would become his wife.

Ms. Hall, also 88, worked for the local newspaper, The Morning Call, until she retired in 2001 as the director of advertising and business development. The paper covered Mr. Passaro’s stints in professional baseball, so she was able to keep up with his career for those four years.

From then on, she would hear what he was up to through the grapevine — that he was married, lived in Miami and had a longtime stint at Eastern Airlines, where he was the vice president of labor relations.

Despite living their own full lives, the first crush nostalgia lingered for decades.

“I had a special place in my heart for my first crush that I kept my whole life,” Ms. Hall said. “I often wondered, was he happy? Did he have a good life? But I had no idea.”

They each had three children. He had three sons (one died in April 2002), and she had two daughters and one son (her son died in 2021). Ms. Hall’s first marriage ended in divorce in 1976. A few months after Mr. Passaro’s son died, his wife died in February 2003. “It was a very tough time for me,” he said.

That’s why when their 50th high school reunion was approaching, Mr. Passaro did not plan on attending. But his friends swayed him to make the trip from Miami to Allentown.

At the reunion, in October 2003, Ms. Hall swung by Mr. Passaro’s table and took a seat in an empty chair next to him. She had read his wife’s obituary in The Morning Call and offered her condolences. He told her about his son, which she hadn’t known about. They talked about that, and then they reminisced about old times. It was their first time seeing each other in 50 years.

When it was time for dinner, she returned to her table. They saw each other a few more times that night. One of Ms. Hall’s friends even took a photo of the two.

At the end of the night, as Ms. Hall was heading out for her long drive home to Atlantic City, N.J., where she moved after retiring, Mr. Passaro caught her at the door. He knew he needed to talk with her. He told her how much he enjoyed seeing her again and asked her for her email address so that they could keep in touch. And then, he leaned over and kissed her gently on the lips, the couple blissfully recalled.

“He says I made this little ‘oop’ sound, like I was surprised,” Ms. Hall said. But one thing was for sure: “I got the goose bumps, the butterflies. And I headed out the door.”

“I don’t know if that was to start something or to say goodbye,” Mr. Passaro said. “But it all worked out.”

Three weeks later, Ms. Hall received a letter in the mail from Mr. Passaro. (She still has the letter.) She had been patiently waiting for an email, but in his defense, his computer had broken. After weeks of searching, he was able to get a hold of her mailing address through their high school reunion committee.

In the letter, he said he regretted he hadn’t said more at the reunion, and that if she wanted to continue the conversation, she should give him a call.

So she did, and they talked for six hours. “I mean we had so much to catch up on,” Ms. Hall said. They chatted via email and telephone for three months, reconnecting and filling each other in on their lives.

“I think one of the things that got me so interested was having somebody who actually grew up with me,” Mr. Passaro said. “When I mentioned a street or something, she knew exactly what I was talking about.”

But their phone calls and messages also got a little spicier. “He was very romantic in the emails,” she said. When he had seen her at the reunion, he had thought she was just as “hot” as she was in the ninth grade, after all, he said playfully.

In January 2004, Mr. Passaro flew her out to Miami.

“I saw him waiting at the airport and got the feeling down my spine — the chills,” Ms. Hall recalled. “He was so sweet.”

They hopped in his car, and he drove her around Miami’s Coconut Grove neighborhood and nearby Coral Gables. They made a stop for a cocktail at a restaurant in CocoWalk, an outdoor shopping mall, and held hands. When they got to his home in Kendall, they shared their “beautiful welcoming kiss,” Ms. Hall said.

She stayed with him in Miami for four days, and throughout the year, she made several more trips down to Miami. After each visit, he noticed more of her clothes in his closet. “I knew she was moving in somehow or other,” he said.

That permanent move-in happened during the holiday season that year, in 2004. Her daughters had sat her down a few weeks before the holidays and suggested that she spend them with him. So she did, and she arrived to a home decorated with twinkling lights and a Christmas tree.

“It was so beautiful,” Ms. Hall said. “I was madly in love with him, he was in love with me. I never left then.”

In their next 15 years together in Miami, they often cruised around Coconut Grove and South Beach, enjoying many breakfasts at Greenstreet Cafe and people-watching at News Cafe. They golfed together and explored Miami’s vibrant art scene. They were living the good retired life. In 2019, the couple moved to Palm Coast, a city in northern Florida.

For some reason, the topic of marriage never came up until Oct. 2, 2022. That day, a couple at the Palm Harbor Golf Club asked them how long they had been married.

Ms. Hall and Mr. Passaro are used to this question. People tend to assume they have been married for decades. Usually, Mr. Passaro’s “eyes would glaze over and he’d say nothing,” Ms. Hall said, and she would then vaguely answer the question.

This time was different. When they returned home after golfing, she asked him, over a cocktail, “What do you think about getting married?” He responded, “Oh, I think we should.”

And just like that, the plan was set.

On March 23, the couple were wed at the Grand Haven Village Center, a gathering space in their gated community in Palm Coast. James Passaro, Mr. Passaro’s son who was previously ordained through the Universal Life Church, officiated in front of 22 guests, which included their four children, eight of nine grandchildren and two great-grandchildren.

“Truthfully, I didn’t think this world had built somebody who truly deserved her,” said Jonathan Edward Durham, a writer and Ms. Hall’s grandson. “But that all changed the moment I met Rollie and his family.”

Ms. Hall said she wanted two things for her wedding: “I wanted to have all of our family together. And I wanted a pretty dress because the first time I got married, I didn’t have that.” She found her dress in the Bridal Gallery in Orlando, Fla., during a girls trip with her daughters.

Her daughters also spent the night before the wedding with her in a hotel room at the Hammock Beach Golf Resort and Spa, while Mr. Passaro’s two sons stayed with him at home. They each had an evening to themselves, talking to the wee hours of the morning with their children.

On their wedding night, the couple stayed at an ocean view suite at the same hotel. Their children surprised them with white fluffy robes that read “Bride” and “Groom,” laying them out on the bed for them. They wore the robes, took a selfie and sent it to all of their children that night with a text message: “Good night, thanks for making our wedding so special,” Ms. Hall recalled.

“And we had a wonderful wedding night,” she said.

“People ask me, ‘Well, does it feel any different?’ Because we were together so long,” she added. “Yes it does. It feels different. I don’t exactly know how, because I thought I loved him as much as I could before, but I’m getting the goose bumps as I talk about it. I definitely feel like we’re going to face the future together.”

Mr. Passaro said, “I’m very happy that we started in ninth grade. I’m very happy that we’re still starting.”

“It really is a love story,” he added. “Just like in the movies.”

When March 23, 2024

Where Palm Coast, Fla.

A Weeklong Affair The first family members arrived in town the Tuesday before the wedding, with the last ones leaving the Tuesday after the wedding. Throughout the week, family members hung out in the couple’s patio.

Take Me Out to the Ballgame Mr. Passaro walked down the aisle with his two sons, Ron Passaro and James Passaro, to “Take Me Out to the Ballgame” because they all grew up playing baseball. The couple’s great-granddaughters, ages 1 and 11, were their flower girls. “They couldn’t have been cuter,” Ms. Hall said. And Ms. Hall was escorted by her daughters, Patricia Beals and Elizabeth Zakeosian, to a live saxophonist rendition of “Satin Doll,” a classic jazz song.

First Dance The couple’s first dance song was introduced as a romantic number, but they surprised everyone with a “lively jitterbug” to the 1950s song “Kansas City,” Ms. Hall said. “The family got a kick out of it.”
