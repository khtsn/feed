Cities in the Northeast and Midwest braced for another round of chilly weather and slippery commutes, with Arctic air expected to filter east on Friday and Saturday, along with some snowfall.

A few inches of snow will fall in parts of the Midwest, the Mid-Atlantic States and the Northeast. Snow totals should stay on the mild side, with two to four inches in most places and a few pockets of about half a foot in the mountainous regions of the Central Appalachians.

Officials warned commuters that snow and plunging temperatures could make roads dangerous by Friday evening. In Columbus, Ohio, teams on Thursday night cleared main roads in preparation for snow, and school classes were canceled. Philadelphia declared a snow emergency on Friday, and schools, libraries and health centers were shut. Passengers should build in extra time to fly in case of delays, the city’s airport said on Friday.

Slippery conditions, particularly in the evening, were also expected in New York, which ended a snow drought this week and will again see more than an inch on the ground.

Snow may start light in the morning but will pick up and become more widespread throughout the day. This will be an all-snow event for the area, but where the heaviest snow piles up is still a little uncertain.

The snowy conditions have already caused travel disruptions and other complications. A landing at a Rochester airport did not go to plan, when an American Airlines plane from Philadelphia slid off the runway. No injuries were reported, American Airlines said in a statement, blaming the incident on “snowy airfield conditions.”

In New York, obliging football fans were asked to help shovel snow off the seats of Highmark Stadium ahead of Sunday’s N.F.L. game between the Buffalo Bills and the Kansas City Chiefs.

Warming centers in Chicago will also be open for residents this winter when temperatures drop below 32 degrees, officials said.

High temperatures on Friday across the Plains and down into the South will be 20 to 25 degrees below normal. While not as cold as the Arctic outbreak earlier in the week, it will still be colder than many people in those areas are accustomed to. The cold will persist in the Plains and the South on Saturday and seep east, with temperatures dropping below average up and down the East Coast.

On Sunday, temperatures will begin moderating across the country. By the middle of next week, most of the United States will be near or above average, with most major cities across the country seeing high temperatures back above freezing.
