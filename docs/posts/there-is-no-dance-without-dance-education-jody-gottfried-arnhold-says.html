There Is No Dance Without Dance Education, Jody Gottfried Arnhold Says

Jody Gottfried Arnhold has a mission (and the means) to cultivate dance education. To her, it is a basic human right — and vital to democracy.

Aug. 8, 2023, 5:01 a.m. ET

At the 92nd Street Y, New York, last month, a diverse bunch of dance teachers from around the country played a game called Slow Land/Fast Land. A strip of blue tape on the floor divided the room in half. Those on one side improvised quick motions while those on the other kept to the opposite speed.

These teachers were all students of the Dance Education Laboratory, and the instructor leading the exercise was Jody Gottfried Arnhold. Now 79, she founded the program nearly 30 years ago and has been teaching in it ever since. But longevity isn’t the most remarkable part of her story.

In dance education circles, her first name is enough. Everyone knows Jody. They call her the doyenne of dance, the godmother. They speak of her as the No. 1 cheerleader, the most tireless and tenacious champion, the general.

The name Arnhold is all over dance in New York: atop lists of donors to theaters and companies; affixed to university programs, scholarships, studio spaces and other parts of buildings. Arnhold’s class was in the newly renovated Buttenweiser Hall, now part of the 92nd Street Y’s Arnhold Center, a dance and performance complex to which she and her husband, John, donated $30 million. The woman who led Slow Land/Fast Land with the firm, kind manner of an exemplary children’s teacher is also chairman of the board of 92NY, as the organization is now also known.

In the fields of dance and dance education, Jody Gottfried Arnhold could be considered the most influential philanthropist in the country. But she doesn’t like that last title.

“I’m not a philanthropist,” she said in an interview. “I’m a dance educator.”

That she certainly is. Before her decades training dance teachers, Arnhold taught elementary students in New York City public schools for 25 years, the last 14 of them also spent developing a dance curriculum rooted in dance-making and integrated into the life of the school.

And she didn’t stop being a dance educator when she married John Arnhold — whose father, Henry, was the patriarch of a banking family and a philanthropist in the hundreds-of-millions-of-dollars class. She just became a dance educator with access to much greater means and the attendant persuasive force — a dance educator with power.

Over the past 30 years, she has transformed dance education in the city. When she was a public-school teacher, in the 1970s and ’80s, she was one of very few full-time teachers of dance in the system. Now there are around 450. Many have earned master’s degrees in a program she founded at Hunter College. Others pursue doctorates at the program she funded at Teachers College, Columbia University — the only doctoral program in dance education in the country.

“She’s a force of nature,” said David C. Banks, the New York City schools chancellor. He has been at the job for 20 months — months filled with calls, emails and texts from Arnhold, with meetings and joint visits to schools. “We all know the arts are a good thing,” he said. “She is the voice in your ear that will not let you forget how important they are.” (He also called her “a sweetheart.”)

Even in interviews, Arnhold educates, buttonholes, lobbies for her cause — graciously but inexorably. She repeats her sayings, like “There is no dance without dance education.” She means, in part, that the next great dancers and choreographers might now be in a kindergarten class in Brooklyn, in need of exposure. She also means that the future dance audience is there.

But her vision is broader than that. For her, dance education isn’t fundamentally about dance technique; it’s about creativity, emotional development, cooperation, connection with other disciplines, education in the largest sense. Thus another saying: “Dance must have a seat at the table wherever education is discussed.”

“What would it mean if every child were dance literate?” she asked. “It would be a different citizenry.” This is what she thinks about dance education: That it’s vital to democracy.

Hence her mission slogan, the one on the dance laboratory T-shirts: Dance for Every Child. Or, in a variation her daughter coined: dance for every child whether you like it or not. (“She’s right,” Arnhold said. “Nobody asks you if you want to take math.”) She sees dance education as a basic human right. And, as she likes to remind people, she doesn’t take no for an answer.

ARNHOLD FELL IN LOVE with dance early. Growing up in Washington, D.C., she was exposed to piano lessons and visual art classes, “but dance grabbed me,” she said. “It was my identity.” From 8 to 18, she studied and performed with Erika Thimey, a modern dancer who had emigrated from Germany. “We learned dances, we made dances,” Arnhold said. “Everything was there that would become my life.”

She went to college at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, because it had been (in 1926) the first American university to offer a degree in dance. After she graduated, she moved to New York City — to be a dancer.

“I had to have a job,” she said. So she got one as a caseworker for the welfare department and took dance classes at night. Then she saw an ad for teacher training at New York University, completed the course and was hired as an elementary school teacher by the city.

She wasn’t teaching dance. “I didn’t know anybody who was teaching dance in the public schools,” she said. But after a few years she talked her way into the dance education program at Teachers College and earned a master’s degree.

In 1977, she was hired to teach dance at P.S. 75 on the Upper West Side. Over the next few years, she developed a curriculum. Ballet Hispánico, then a grass-roots local dance company founded by Tina Ramirez, came to the school. “And I saw the importance of having artists as role models, teaching what only they can teach,” Arnhold said.

“I saw dance transform the kids, the school, the neighborhood,” she added. “Dance was the soul of the school. Nothing happened without it. Every holiday we made a dance. A teacher got married, we made a dance. A teacher died, we made a dance.”

In 1982, Arnhold won an award from the Department of Education that helped teachers from all over the city visit her classes and her visit theirs. “What we had at P.S. 75 was fantastic,” she said, “and I was thinking, ‘What about that school?’ and ‘what about every school?’”

By this time, she had met John Arnhold at a party. After they married, in 1983, she gave birth to a son, then a daughter, and kept teaching. “I never changed,” she said. “I’m exactly the same.”

But some parts of her life did change. At a dinner, someone suggested to her that she join the board of Ballet Hispánico. “What’s a board?” she recalled asking. She joined it, eventually serving as chair. “That’s where I learned how to build an organization,” she said.

By 1992, when she had transitioned from teaching to mentoring teachers, she was asked to join the board of the 92nd Street Y, where her children attended nursery school. Joan Finkelstein, then the director of the dance department, asked Arnhold what projects she wanted to support. “I want to train teachers,” she said.

Finkelstein liked the idea, but Arnhold became worried. “I could feel my life’s work going down the drain,” she said. “I didn’t work there. She was going to train teachers without me.”

It was a watershed moment. Arnhold didn’t want to be only the source of money. She wanted to be the mastermind. She didn’t just help fund the Dance Education Laboratory and get it approved. She designed the curriculum and taught it with a mentee, Ann Biddle.

Next Arnhold turned to the city’s Department of Education, where she convinced Finkelstein to apply for a job. When Finkelstein became director of dance in 2004, there wasn’t even a database of dance teachers in the system. “We cobbled together a list of about 135 names,” Finkelstein said. By the time that she left the job in 2014 — succeeded by another Arnhold protégée, Ana Fragoso — the number was over 400.

With Finkelstein and Ramirez, Arnhold led the committee that developed the city’s Blueprint for Teaching and Learning in Dance, with guidelines and benchmarks for different grade levels. (“It’s a bible,” she said. “I organized it. That’s why it makes sense.”) She also created the Arnhold New Dance Teacher Support Program, which provides supplies, free courses, one-on-one mentorship and a $5,000 stipend to partner with a cultural organization. “My dance teachers get more support and training than any other teacher in the city,” Arnhold said.

To publicize this work, Arnhold had a film made: “PS Dance!” To create a pipeline of teachers, she established the Arnhold Graduate Dance Education Program at Hunter College in 2012, with a new studio called the Jody and the chance to earn a master’s degree at public university prices.

“That makes it easy for students of color or anyone who doesn’t have the means,” said Fragoso, who became director of the Hunter program in 2021. “Everybody gets hired,” she added, and whenever a dance teacher leaves the public school system, “we have someone to step in and take that position, so we don’t lose it.”

Then, in 2018, came the doctorate program at Teachers College and the establishment there of the Arnhold Institute for Dance Education Research, Policy & Leadership. “We need the research,” said Barbara Bashaw, the Arnhold Professor of Practice in Dance Education. “Our students are getting jobs before they graduate.”

One of the first graduates is Chell Parkins. The program, she said, gives her the theory and scholarship to promote dance education more effectively. That’s what she is now doing as the first Arnhold Director of Dance Education at the University of Wisconsin-Madison.

THIS CHAIN OF ACCOMPLISHMENTS could not have happened without Arnhold’s strategic vision. Nor could it have happened without Arnhold money. “I had something I wanted to do, and John has supported every one of my ideas as it got bigger and bigger,” Arnhold said. “That’s not a given, and I’m very lucky. I married the right guy.”

She has built a pipeline, a network, an army. The institutions she has established are led almost exclusively by people inspired by her, mentored by her, pushed and supported by her, who take her advice and promote her ideas. That one person can wield so much influence might trouble some. But it cheers the many who have benefited.

“She sees people’s potential and helps them realize it,” said Finkelstein, who just earned her doctorate from Teachers College. “She could spend that money on anything. She’s spending it on dance and dance education.”

“It’s not just me anymore,” Arnhold said. “It’s all these people who got the message.” And she isn’t finished. There are still hundreds of New York City public schools without dance teachers. “Then there’s the rest of the state, and the country, and the world,” she said, laughing.

“I want to tighten the web so nobody can escape a dance education.”
