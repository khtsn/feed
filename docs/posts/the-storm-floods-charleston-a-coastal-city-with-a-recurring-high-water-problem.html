As climate change leads to more intense downpours, millions of Americans are at risk from severe storms that deliver more rain per hour than local pipes and culverts are designed to handle, researchers estimated in a study earlier this year.

Every additional increment of global warming increases the likelihood of strong rain for a simple reason: Hotter air can hold more moisture. This means larger and more frequent floods can occur than storm infrastructure in many places was built to manage.

According to the First Street Foundation, the research group that produced the calculations, present-day flood risks are being especially underestimated in some of the nation’s largest cities, including New York — where intense rains produced flash flooding on Monday morning — as well as Baltimore, Chicago, Dallas, Detroit, Houston, Philadelphia and Washington.

Charleston, S.C., was contending on Monday with the aftermath of the powerful storm that has been lashing the East Coast with strong winds and heavy rainfall. The city was inundated with surging floodwaters that swamped roads and stranded motorists.

The storm caused the fourth-highest tide ever recorded in Charleston, and the highest not driven by a tropical storm, meteorologists said. The conditions prompted forecasters to issue flash flood warnings in and around the city.

Intense rainfall fell across the Carolinas as the storm passed through. Some parts of South Carolina received at least of 10 inches of rain.

The circumstances were a magnified version of a familiar threat in Charleston. Roads in the city become impassable rivers even during more modest storms, and some streets routinely flood at high tide on sunny days, as rising sea levels breach the city.

Climate change has forced Charleston to quickly adapt in recent years. The owners of many of the preserved historic houses, a mainstay of the city’s charm, have paid at least $100,000 to have their homes hoisted higher up as protection from floodwaters.

Flooding forced dozens of road closures on Sunday, and motorists had to be rescued from vehicles that had been swept up in the water.

There were some school closures on Monday in communities near Charleston. But in general, things were starting to dry out.

“Good riddance to the potent surface low that impacted our area yesterday,” the National Weather Service office in Charleston wrote on social media. “A much drier and colder air mass will fill in the void behind the distant low, allowing for considerable drying after the record rainfall yesterday.”

At Floyd Bennett Field in Brooklyn, migrants sheltering in large white tents the length of city blocks recounted a frightful night in the storm.

On Monday morning, puddles filled the former airfield near the Rockaways that New York City has used to house migrants since last month. The tents have capacity for up to 2,000 migrants.

Annabel Lopez, 39, has sheltered in one of the tents with her 11-year-old son for more than two weeks. Speaking in Spanish, Ms. Lopez, who is from Colombia, described the tent violently shaking as the wind whipped around it. She said security workers had turned on the lights in the middle of the night to reassure residents that they were safe, but that their efforts hadn’t comforted her.

Ms. Lopez said the tents were not built well and that the storm made her fearful for the coming winter. She was worried about more severe and colder storms.

Other migrants, who declined to give their names for fear of reprisals, also described intense rattling and clattering of the tents. One man showed a video in which the interior ceiling and walls wrinkled and rippled in the wind.

Edgar Marin, 38, said his two small children, ages 1 and 2, could not sleep all night as the tent rocked back and forth. Mr. Marin, who is from Venezuela, said in Spanish that he thought the roof would rip off and that children around him were very scared.

“It was very hard,” he said.

Cathy Cuero, 29, said her 10-year-old daughter, Ariana, was unable to sleep and was late for school on Monday as a result. Ms. Cuero, who is from Ecuador, added that drops of water had fallen from the ceiling, and that another resident’s bedding got soaked.

Monday’s storm punctuated what has been an above-average year of rain for New York City, especially in the second half of 2023.

It rained on 14 out of the 30 days in September, and on every weekend between early September and mid-October, helping make it the third-wettest autumn ever. On Sept. 29, heavy rain flooded schools, shut down subway lines and inundated basements, causing chaos and storm damage but no deaths.

It also continued a run of major rainstorms in the city the last few years. In September 2022, many New Yorkers received a tornado warning as rain and thunderstorms lashed the city, flooding areas in Queens and Westchester and leading to public transportation delays.

And in September 2021, the remnants of Hurricane Ida lashed New York City, dumping over three inches of rain in Central Park in a single hour. Sixteen people died in New York, and 25 died in New Jersey. In New York City, 13 people died, most of whom had been trapped in basement apartments.

Heavy rain and howling winds associated with a major coastal storm were pounding portions of the Northeast early Monday as officials in Philadelphia, New York and Boston asked residents to brace for flooding and power outages.

Across Rhode Island, New York, New Jersey, Connecticut and elsewhere, tens of thousands of customers were without power early Monday, according to PowerOutage.us, which compiles data from utilities.

Storm conditions were expected to be at their worst during peak commuting time in the region early Monday, threatening to make travel difficult. The Metropolitan Transportation Authority said early Monday that some train service had been disrupted because of flooding and reported felled trees on tracks on multiple lines.

Several bus routes across the city were also running with delays because of heavy rain and flooding, officials said, and the New York City Ferry was suspending multiple services, including operation along the Rockaway route because of “extremely high winds.”

Some Metro North trains were also operating with delays up to 15 minutes because of weather conditions while some Long Island Railroad service was also interrupted, officials said.

The Verrazzano Bridge was temporarily closed early Monday and when it reopened hours later, tractor-trailers, minibuses, step vans, motorcycles and similar vehicles were banned from using it because of high winds.

Areas to the west of the city have already received two to four inches of rainfall, even over four inches in northeast New Jersey. The new influx of precipitation in addition to rain amounts from earlier Monday will raise concerns over flash flooding.

The New York State Department of Transportation urged drivers to avoid crossing flooded roadways and to find alternative routes. Similar messages were issued by transportation officials in Massachusetts and in New Jersey. An official in Nassau County urged residents to stay off the roads altogether.

Downpours were also expected east of Interstate 95 in Washington and Baltimore through the morning hours, forecasters said, with flood watches issued for those areas. The Allegheny Mountains could get as much as 10 inches of snow, forecasters said.

Flooding was also a concern farther north. The Weather Service in New York issued a coastal flood warning through 6 p.m. Monday for southern Westchester County and several communities in southern Connecticut, including New Haven.

Along the New York coast, flooding with up to two and a half feet of inundation and sustained winds of 25 to 40 miles per hour — with gusts of 55 to 60 m.p.h. — could damage power lines and topple trees, the Weather Service said.

New York City officials implored those living in basements to move to higher ground and advised all residents to download the city’s emergency alert app.

They also cautioned residents to brace for powerful winds, with gusts up to 60 m.p.h. sweeping through Brooklyn and Queens early Monday through midmorning. Waves 12 to 16 feet high could pummel the coastline, the Weather Service said.

Up to two to four inches of rain was also expected to fall over the Philadelphia area through Monday morning, elevating concerns about potential urban and coastal flooding.

The service warned that strong winds there could also bring down tree limbs and cause power outages.

A storm warning was also issued for stretches of the Massachusetts and Rhode Island coasts until Monday evening.
