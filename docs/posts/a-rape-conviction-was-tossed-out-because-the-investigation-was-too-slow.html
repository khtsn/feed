A Rape Conviction Was Tossed Out Because the Investigation Was Too Slow

The decision by the New York Court of Appeals could give prosecutors a reason to abandon difficult cases, advocates for victims say.

April 3, 2023, 5:00 a.m. ET

In August 2009, two couples went out drinking after a wedding.

At the end of the night, one of the friends, a 22-year-old woman, invited her boyfriend and the other couple to stay at her home in Norwood, N.Y., just south of the Canadian border.

The woman fell asleep alone, then woke in the night to find the other man, Andrew Regan, crushing her beneath him, according to court documents. She roused her boyfriend and told him she had been sexually assaulted, and they called the police. Mr. Regan was interviewed and released. At a hospital, a nurse collected evidence with a rape kit.

But the investigation dragged on for four years, in part because prosecutors in St. Lawrence County said they did not know how to get a warrant to compel Mr. Regan to provide a DNA sample, which he had repeatedly refused to do. He was finally found guilty of first-degree rape by a jury in 2015 after a genetic match was made and was sentenced to 12 years in prison.

But the yearslong investigation, which dragged on, the New York Court of Appeals said, because of “inexplicable delays caused by lethargy or ignorance of basic prosecutorial procedures,” led the court to overturn the conviction on March 16. The decision freed Mr. Regan and expunged the charge from his record.

New York has had no statute of limitations on first-degree rape since 2006, and Mr. Regan had not been jailed as he awaited the charges.

But because it took 31 months to obtain the warrant, the court found prosecutors violated his right to a prompt prosecution, which is protected by a state law meant to keep district attorneys from delaying prosecutions without a good reason.

The conviction must be vacated even though it creates “a genuine risk that a guilty person will not be punished, or, as in this case, not finish out his full sentence” to protect “vital societal interests,” wrote Judge Rowan D. Wilson in the majority opinion.

The legal system “spurs prosecutors to take crime seriously and give all parties the prompt closure they need to move on with their lives,” he wrote.

The victim, who still lives in the town where the assault happened, said in an interview that she was devastated when she heard about the decision and has had security cameras installed around her house. (The New York Times is not publishing her name because The Times generally does not publish the names of rape victims unless they choose to be identified.)

Prosecutors said that the ruling has added to a growing feeling among crime victims that their rights are being eroded as legislators and courts re-examine the treatment of criminal defendants.

Advocates for sexual assault survivors say they fear that the ruling could discourage some prosecutors from bringing charges of sexual assault, a crime in which the victim and the defendant often know one other and in which there are rarely third-party witnesses to corroborate a survivor’s claims. It could also give defendants a reason to resist investigations at every turn in hope of running out the clock.

In New York City, where the U.S. Department of Justice has said it would investigate the Police Department’s handling of sex crimes, less than half of sexual assault charges led to convictions between 2020 and 2022, a rate far below other serious crimes.

Prosecutors are struggling to fill job openings, and offices across the state are reporting vacancy rates as high as 30 percent, according to the District Attorneys Association of the State of New York.

An overwhelmed prosecutor might use the ruling as an excuse to dismiss a case, said Judy Harris Kluger, a former judge and prosecutor in Brooklyn who now runs Sanctuary for Families, a nonprofit that helps victims of domestic violence, trafficking and sexual abuse or assault.

Mr. Regan, a former U.S. Army sergeant, declined to comment through his lawyer, Matthew Hug. Mr. Hug said that Mr. Regan has “maintained his innocence” for the past 14 years and that the decision reaffirmed that every defendant has a right to due process.

“The district attorney and the police sat and did nothing with this case for years and left someone in the community to sit for years with no action being taken,” Mr. Hug said. “The Constitution and our basic rights are more important than anything.”

In the 4-to-2 decision, Judge Wilson said the police and prosecutors failed to take the rights of both defendant and the victim seriously.

Judge Madeline Singas wrote in a dissenting opinion that the court’s decision would “be weaponized against victims.”

The court, she wrote, “fails to appreciate the practice implications of the precedent that they are creating: If law enforcement negligently delays rape investigations, women’s voices will continue to be stifled, rapists held unaccountable and jury verdicts discarded.”

In trying to strike a balance, the court moved “in a way that was to the detriment of a woman who promptly reported a rape and testified at trial,” Ms. Harris Kluger said.

After the woman reported the assault, the police took a DNA sample from her boyfriend and found that it did not match the semen on the victim’s underwear. The police interviewed Mr. Regan, who told them he did not have sex with the woman.

For at least a year, prosecutors struggled over how to procure his DNA, a straightforward procedure that involves submitting an affidavit to a judge.

The woman who accused him said in an interview that in the years that followed, she called the district attorney’s office or the police at least once a month to find out what was happening with her case.

New investigators were assigned to the case at least three to four times in the four-year ordeal, she said. No one ever offered a clear explanation for the delays, she said. As she waited for charges, she often saw Mr. Regan in town at the grocery store or marching in Fourth of July parades.

In the meantime, the district attorney’s office was upended by political upheaval.

In October 2011, a 12-year-old boy was strangled, and the murder case brought national attention and led, in part, to the ousting of the incumbent district attorney two years later. The new prosecutor, Mary Rain, later became the subject of investigations after she failed to disclose evidence to the defense in the murder case.

But during a hearing for the rape case, the assistant district attorney representing St. Lawrence County did not cite those factors as impediments.

Gary M. Pasqua, who became the district attorney in 2018, said he did not believe that the ruling in this case would set a precedent.

“The facts are so specific and inarguable that I don’t know that you’re going to run into a situation like this down the line,” he said. “I am confident that a situation like what happened in this case will never happen again in St. Lawrence as long as I’m district attorney.”

Yung-Mi Lee, president of the New York State Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers, said the ruling sends a “clear warning again to prosecutors and law enforcement that they need to act diligently.”

Tony Jordan, president of the District Attorneys Association of the State of New York, said the case “should serve as a great reminder to all of us that understanding and moving cases matters.”

The victim said she had questioned whether it was worth coming forward. It was, she said, because she had at least had eight years to feel safe.
