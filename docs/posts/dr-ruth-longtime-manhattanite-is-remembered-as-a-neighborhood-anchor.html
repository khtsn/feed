Dr. Ruth, Longtime Manhattanite, Is Remembered as a Neighborhood ‘Anchor’

The Ruth Westheimer that fellow residents of her Manhattan apartment building knew was much the same person known to America as Dr. Ruth: gregarious, funny, peppy and apt to throw out a provocative question in the elevator.

“She was the most well-loved person who lived here,” said Geoffrey Katin, 81, a longtime resident of the building, in Washington Heights.

Dr. Westheimer, who died on Friday at 96, was America’s best-known sex therapist. She first shocked and amused millions in the 1980s with her frank radio counseling sessions and later became a hit on national talk shows, wrote many books, gave lectures and was the subject of a play and a documentary. But her explicit and direct advice, delivered in a casual and jovial manner, was not just for the cameras.

Neighbors enjoyed bumping into her in the building, never certain how she might strike up a conversation. Peter Sparrow, who said he had lived there for 27 years, recalled the time Dr. Westheimer, who was 4-foot-7, asked four burly construction workers in the elevator if they performed a specific sex act on their wives.

“This little woman just quizzing these men about their sex lives,” he said. “It probably helped them.”

Dr. Westheimer’s popularity peaked in the 1980s, but she remained active into the 2020s. She had recently turned her attention to a new challenge: the epidemic of loneliness. Last year she pitched herself to Gov. Kathy Hochul of New York as the state’s first “loneliness ambassador,” having experienced firsthand the isolating effects of lockdowns during the coronavirus pandemic. The governor took her up on the offer.

“I am deeply honored,” Dr. Westheimer said at the time, “and promised the governor that I will work day and night to help New Yorkers feel less lonely!”

That process began for her decades ago in her building with a cohort of neighbors who relished their interactions with her.

Born in Germany to parents who died in the Holocaust, she came to New York in 1956 after living in Israel and Paris. She settled in Washington Heights and never left, staying for 60 years in the apartment she moved into with her third husband, Manfred Westheimer, a telecommunications engineer who died in 1997.

Dr. Westheimer said that the apartment — which was on West 190th Street and had three bedrooms and views of the Hudson River, the George Washington Bridge and New Jersey — was a source of stability after the trauma of her childhood.

“Home is something very sacred,” she told The New York Times in 2013.

She collected novelties and kept two cherished dollhouses, where she could ensure that the doll families were safe.

“I love small things,” she said. “I love them. I walk into my apartment, I smile at them and say hello.”

She was often that way with her neighbors, too. She spoke Hebrew with Mr. Katin and was not shy to ask for help carrying her groceries. If she had one foible as she got older, it was remembering names, even after lengthy conversations.

“She always forgot,” Miriam Katin, Mr. Katin’s wife, said.

Residents in the building remembered Dr. Westheimer for her career, her humor and her lively personality. One woman recalled the tulip garden named for her at nearby Fort Tryon Park, and said she was glad Dr. Westheimer had been able to see the flowers bloom one last time before her death. A couple who lived a few floors down from Dr. Westheimer said they had sometimes joked that she was watching them from her perch above, taking notes on their intimate interactions.

Neal Shultz, 60, a retired reporter and history teacher, said he had lived in the building for 14 years and had spoken with Dr. Westheimer many times. When he first introduced himself, she was intrigued by his profession and immediately sought his opinion on some artifacts she had recently acquired. “I was in her beautiful wraparound apartment and she started showing me these woodcuts that someone had given her,” he said. “Which were erotic woodcuts of courtesans from the Qing dynasty.”

“The acceptance of Ruth was absolute,” Mr. Shultz said. “It’s not like, ‘Oh my god, she was so shocking.’ Nobody was shocked. Nobody cared.”

Mr. Shultz noted that Dr. Westheimer was a kind of anchor for the neighborhood for decades as it evolved from being a refuge for German Jewish Holocaust survivors to weathering the crack epidemic of the 1980s and demographic changes in the 21st century.

Toward the end of her life she used a wheelchair and grew to depend more on the help of her neighbors. Some did her shopping, they said, and some wheeled her to the park. One neighbor, Hortense Noble, said that the last time she saw Dr. Westheimer she was very frail but could still muster a warm smile of recognition.

“I’m very proud we did a New York thing,” Mr. Shultz said, “which is: ‘You’re a celebrity. Big deal. We’re just going to treat you like a person.’ But she was a really great person.”
