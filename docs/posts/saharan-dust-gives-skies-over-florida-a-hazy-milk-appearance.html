Saharan Dust Gives Skies Over Florida a ‘Hazy Milk’ Appearance

Weather|Saharan Dust Gives Skies Over Florida a ‘Hazy Milk’ Appearance

https://www.nytimes.com/2025/06/04/weather/saharan-dust-florida-texas.html

A dust plume that has been traveling across the Atlantic may later spread as far as Texas and Georgia as a weather system breaks it apart.

Published June 4, 2025Updated June 5, 2025, 3:21 p.m. ET

A cloud of Saharan dust was suspended one mile above the ground over the Florida Panhandle on Thursday, lowering air quality and bringing drier weather there before it spreads north along the East Coast and west along the Gulf.

Robert Molleda, a meteorologist with the National Weather Service in Miami, said the dust was giving a “hazy milk appearance to the sky.”

The dry air and dust, known as the Saharan Air Layer, have traveled more than 5,000 miles from North Africa, a brown-ish mass viewable in satellite images as it moved slowly across the Atlantic Ocean.

The air quality in Florida was slightly degraded on Thursday, but was not nearly as bad as the Upper Midwest, where days of wildfire smoke have made the air unhealthy to breathe at times. Unlike the smoke over places such as Minnesota and Wisconsin, the dust moving across Florida was higher in the atmosphere.

“It’s not reaching the ground,” Mr. Molleda said.

The dust has also ushered dry air into Florida, bringing a respite from several days of heavy rain.

“We still have showers and thunderstorms in the forecast, but we’re expecting less rain today because of the dust,” Mr. Molleda said. “Certainly compared to the last few days there’s certainly a lower chance for rain.”

The dust is lifted off the Sahara by winds.

The Saharan Air Layer forms when winds lift sand and minerals from the Sahara in North Africa into the atmosphere. It then gets whisked away by a strong wind current, which sometimes pushes it north into Europe, but more commonly carries it west across the Atlantic Ocean. It usually reaches the Caribbean and the Gulf Coast region several times a year.

The dust events occur year-round but are most common from about mid-May into August, usually peaking mid-June to July.

Earlier forecasts had indicated that a cloud of dust would travel from the Caribbean into the Gulf Coast region, but on Thursday the dust plume began to disperse when it encountered a weather system off the coast of Florida

“It was looking like we were going to get a big slug of dust along the Gulf Coast, now that has trended more toward Florida, Georgia and South Carolina,” said Paul Miller, an assistant professor of coastal meteorology at Louisiana State University. “There’s going to be a lot over the ocean too, but it may meander back over the Florida Peninsula.”

Mr. Miller said a plume of dust was expected to push into Alabama, Mississippi Louisiana and southeast Texas, most likely Friday into Saturday.

Houston is expected to see impacts by Friday afternoon and evening and continuing into the weekend. “Still expecting the main impacts to be hazy skies during the day and a decrease in air quality,” said Jimmy Fowler, a meteorologist with the Weather Service.

Over Puerto Rico, the dust looked like a ‘huge fog.’

On its journey east, the curtain of dust shrouded skies over Puerto Rico Saturday night into Tuesday.

Skies were hazy, and visibility at Luis Muñoz Marín International Airport dropped to five miles. Air quality levels dropped.

Sara Barcelo, who leads walking tours of Old San Juan, said the dust looked like a “huge fog.”

“Puerto Rico is covered in mountains, so something that happens is you can’t see the mountains,” Ms. Barcelo said. “You don’t see the view clearly.”

Ms. Barcelo called the dust typical for this time of year, and said it usually affected air quality and visibility several times in late spring into summer.

By Wednesday, the clear skies had returned over Puerto Rico, but more dust could be on the way with another plume extending off the west coast of North Africa.

“We may have another event during this upcoming weekend, but it’s not going to be as intense as this one,” said Ian Colon-Pagan, a meteorologist at the Weather Service office in San Juan.

Amy Graff is a Times reporter covering weather, wildfires and earthquakes.
