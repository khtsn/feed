Warm-Up Rituals: How Actors, Comedians and Musicians Get Ready

We asked creative professionals how they prepare for their gigs. Jeff Daniels says “budda-gudda” a lot.

Writers, actors, comedians and musicians, including the “Let’s Make a Deal” host Wayne Brady and the standup comedian Rosebud Baker, opened up about how they get ready for work.Credit...

  • May 19, 2025Updated 12:53 p.m. ET

Many of us have a pregame ritual before we go to work. A steaming cup of coffee. Maybe some meditation, or a prayer.

Beyoncé eats a turkey sandwich before she goes onstage, according to “Renaissance: A Film by Beyoncé.” Paul McCartney is said to hum the theme song from “The Monkees” with his band before concerts. Bill Russell, the Hall of Fame center, threw up before games to get rid of jitters.

A warm-up ritual, active or otherwise, can have important psychological benefits, said Fallon Goodman, a psychology professor at George Washington University.

“When the world is more predictable for us, we can more easily get into a flow stage that will improve performance,” she said.

The New York Times reached out to a wide range of creative people, including writers, actors, comedians and musicians, to find out how they warm up for their gigs. Some rituals were simple, others sentimental or a combination of both.

Some answers have been lightly edited for length and clarity.

Jeff Daniels

Daniels, the Emmy Award-winning actor, has a ritual where he — well, we’ll let him tell you.

“I always start with a diaphragm-supported ‘budda-gudda, budda-gudda, budda-guddas’, followed by a parade of vowel sounds (A, E, I, O, U) where I add a consonant (for instance, ‘S’: say say say, see see see, si si si, so so so, sue sue sue). Then I do the same thing using other consonants (T, Z, K, P, V, F, M, N, W, ending with a hard R), climaxing by using my outstretched tongue to spell the alphabet in large capital letters two inches in front of my face. It’s not pretty, but it works.”

Hagel, a comedian and a writer for “Late Night With Seth Meyers,” hides her phone before going onstage.

“I don’t want to be scrolling and then the last thing I see before I go out onstage and try to do comedy is some headline that’s like, ‘Two planes crash into each other and then land on a panda sanctuary.’”

Michael Connelly

Connelly, the best-selling author of crime novels including “The Lincoln Lawyer,” compares his repetitive ritual to a “broken record.” It incidentally involves an actual record.

“I start the writing day by dropping the needle on the late jazz saxophonist Frank Morgan’s song ‘Lullaby.’”

Simmons, a founder of the rock band Kiss who embarked on a solo tour this month, says his warm-up requires a bit more panache when he turns into his “Demon” alter ego.

“The ritual over the course of decades, because we’ve been doing it for 50 years if you can believe it, is we put on music of all kinds — girl groups from the ’60s, the Shirelles or the Ronettes and all that. But later on in the tours, I started playing the full series of the old Friars Club roasts, with Milton Berle and Dean Martin and all those guys that were not televised. So you can say potty words, and I have no idea why, but that laugh-a-minute thing — they make me want to get out there and kick some ass.”

Michael Kosta

As host and correspondent for “The Daily Show,” Kosta says tying his tie is a sentimental ritual.

“I want it to look right. I like the rhythm. I like the structure of how it’s supposed to look. It always reminds me of my father, who passed away a few years ago. This reminds me of tying my tie with my dad.”

Sarah Sherman

The comedian and “Saturday Night Live” star says she sings along to David Bowie’s cover of the Merseys’ “Sorrow” from his 1973 album “Pin Ups” before going live from New York.

“I’m superstitious but I also think it’s medically necessary to have amazing superstar energy before performing.”

Zerna, a timpani player for the New York Philharmonic, uses a practice pad to strengthen his muscles before performances. The pads, made of a type of rubber, vary in their bounce and cushioning, and in the amount of rebound from hitting them.

“You’ve woken up hundreds, thousands of times, but you’re still really tired. You still need to go through your morning ritual of either having coffee or going for a walk or getting sunlight, whatever you have to do. So I say it’s like kind of the same thing with the hands.”

Julia Knitel

“I have a little stone that was given to me by a healer in Lisbon. It’s become a good-luck charm to me, so for the past two years I’ve been putting it in my bra — right next to my heart — before I go onstage. It did almost fall out during a scene recently, so I might have to rethink the placement.”

Andrew Durand

Durand, a Broadway veteran and also a Tony nominee for his role in “Dead Outlaw,” takes a moment of reflection onstage before the doors open.

“Looking out into the empty 1,000-plus-seat house and having a few moments of breath and reverence for the special exchange of energy between actor and audience that is about to fill that space.”

Yang, a former gymnast, is the acrobatics captain in the Broadway production of “Death Becomes Her.” His warm-up ritual, he says, is not so much about stretching and remaining calm as it is about “getting the body firing.”

“Isometric holds like lunges, squats, planks to jumping and ballet-style movements like plies and kicks.”

Gracie Lawrence

Lawrence, a lead singer for the band of the same name, has been nominated for a Tony Award for playing the singer Connie Francis in “Just In Time,” the Broadway musical about Bobby Darin. She sings various versions of the classic “For Once in My Life” to get ready for shows.

“You can play around stylistically. The original version of ‘For Once in My Life’ is sort of a standard. But then there’s also the iconic Stevie Wonder version, which gets a little more pop-soul, which is the genre of my band.”

Marjan Neshat

Neshat, who has been nominated for a Tony Award for her performance as a warm but firm teacher in “English,” has several routines, including meditating and lying down onstage and looking up at the ceiling. And also:

“I try and find a scent for my characters and always wear that perfume when I’m going to perform them.”

Rosebud Baker

Baker, a standup comedian whose new Netflix special, “The Mother Lode,” was released in February, says her ritual while on the road “feels a lot more like study hour.”

“I listen to sets from the city or from my last road gig.”

Mel Robbins

Robbins, a motivational speaker and author whose latest self-help book, “The Let Them Theory,” was released last year, warms up by motivating herself.

“I remind myself that there is one person that will see or hear what I’m about to say, and it is exactly what they needed to hear at this exact moment in time — and that is the only person I am ever speaking to.”

A Tony Award-winning writer of Broadway shows including “Smash” and “Jersey Boys,” Elice recites a speech from a signed copy of the Tom Stoppard play “The Real Thing” before rehearsals. Elice’s late husband, Roger Rees, originated the role of Henry, Stoppard’s alter-ego, in the 1982 London production.

“This particular speech is engraved in my brain the way you scratch your name on a table, retracing it over and over so it can’t fade away. The last line is, ‘If you get the right words in the right order, you can nudge the world a little.’ My goal is to do my damnedest to keep trying to get them in the right order before my final exit stage left.”

Christopher Tramantana

Tramantana, an actor whose television credits include “Ramy” and “Manifest,” sings a song. Well, sort of.

“I gargle ‘The Rainbow Connection’ before I head to set.”

Felipe Esparza

Esparza, a comedian whose latest stand-up special, “Raging Fool,” was released on Netflix in February, always writes down the first line of his act.

“Even if I’m going to say: ‘Hello! How are you doing tonight?’ That helps me remember the whole act.”

Brady’s warm-ups depend on what he is doing, whether it’s his podcast, performing improv or singing. Before hosting the game show “Let’s Make a Deal,” he does a vocal warm-up that includes blowing bubbles.

“And I massage my face because I realized a long time ago that talking is actually worse for you than singing.”
