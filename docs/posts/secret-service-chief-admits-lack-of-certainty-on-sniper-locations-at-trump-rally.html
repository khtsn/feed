Secret Service Chief Admits Lack of Certainty on Sniper Locations at Trump Rally

The location of local snipers when Donald Trump was shot has become a point of deep dispute, with local officials suggesting that the Secret Service is trying to shift blame.

Aug. 2, 2024, 7:30 p.m. ET

The Secret Service’s acting director acknowledged on Friday that when he told congressional investigators that local law enforcement snipers should have been able to see the gunman who shot former President Donald J. Trump at a rally, he did not have details from the snipers themselves on their locations.

Rather, the acting director, Ronald L. Rowe Jr., said his understanding of where the local snipers were positioned was based on a review of the agency’s operational plan and his own agents’ descriptions of what they saw.

Local law enforcement officials have vehemently contested his account, saying the Secret Service appears to be trying to shift blame for the failures that led to the assassination attempt last month in Butler, Pa., even as it claims to take responsibility for them.

Mr. Rowe’s explanation came as he took questions from reporters Friday afternoon during the Secret Service’s first news conference about the assassination attempt.

The sharply diverging accounts about what led to the security lapses have fueled tensions between the Secret Service and local officials just as the presidential campaign enters its final stretches, when the agency must work even more closely with local law enforcement.

In testimony earlier this week, Mr. Rowe told senators that the local snipers would have been able to see the gunman, who was on the roof of the building in front of where they were posted, if they had looked left out the window.

But the local snipers who were in the building say that is not accurate and that they were never told to watch the area with the roofs.

Patrick Young, the commander of the Beaver County Emergency Services Unit, said the local snipers, including one who works for him, were told to post themselves in windows on the other end of the building. Their job, he and other local officials said, was to watch the rally crowd.

“He has had no idea of where our guys were,” Mr. Young said in an interview on Friday, referring to Mr. Rowe’s testimony a few days earlier. “That’s our frustration.”

On Friday, Mr. Rowe sought to make the clear that the Secret Service accepted the blame for all security failures.

“The Secret Service takes full responsibility for the tragic events July 13,” he said, adding that his agency’s “sole” responsibility is to keep the people they are assigned to protect out of danger. “We fell short of that in Butler, and I’m working to make sure that this failure does not happen.”

Preliminary investigations have found multiple lapses in the lead-up to the shooting.

Local law enforcement officials had flagged the gunman, Thomas Crooks of Bethel Park, Pa., as suspicious on the site grounds outside the security perimeter more than an hour before Mr. Trump started speaking. He became more of a concern when he was spotted with a backpack and a range finder, details that were not relayed contemporaneously to the Secret Service because of breakdowns in how the law enforcement agencies communicated with each other.

All told, about 155 law enforcement personnel were at the site on July 13, according to the Secret Service. Mr. Crooks fired eight shots, killing a rally attendee and injuring Mr. Trump and others.

Mr. Rowe said on Friday that among the agency’s failures was that it assumed state and local law enforcement officers understood what duties they should handle for the event.

The Secret Service relies heavily on the state and local police to carry out its protection duties.

Mr. Rowe said that since he took over after the agency’s director resigned in the wake of the shooting, he had spoken to the special agents in charge across the country and emphasized how much the Secret Service needed its local partners.

“But we also have to be very direct, very clear about what our expectations are, about what we need from them,” he said he instructed them.

Mr. Rowe had told senators that the Secret Service had expected the local snipers to be watching the rooftops, but the Secret Service has not publicly said local officials were explicitly told that they were charged with watching the roofs. That has contributed to the finger pointing.

“I can only speak for my guys,” Mr. Young said. “And we are enraged.”

The Secret Service has not released its security plans for July 13, despite requests from Congress and the Pennsylvania State Police, which is leading one of several investigations into the failures.

One of those investigations is an internal “mission assurance” review, Mr. Rowe said.

As part of that review, the agency would interview the local snipers and other local officials present on July 13 after interviews with federal officials are completed, he said.

“We’ll look forward to interviewing them and definitely getting their side of the story,” Mr. Rowe said on Friday.

“This was a Secret Service failure, and so they should not be blamed,” he said. “We’re not trying to shift blame to anybody.”
