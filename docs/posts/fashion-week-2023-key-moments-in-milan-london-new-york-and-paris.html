Fashion Week 2023: Key Moments in Milan, London, New York and Paris

The weirdest, wildest and most wearable moments from the fall 2023 shows.

Hundreds of runway shows will have taken place in New York, London, Milan and Paris by the end of those four cities’ consecutive fashion weeks — or fashion month. Even for the most clothes-obsessed, that’s a lot to keep up with. And what happens inside the fall 2023 shows is only part of the action: For every runway spectacle, there are others just as captivating in the crowd or on the street.

The following collection of articles is a guide to the most memorable shows, clothes and moments witnessed by Styles reporters, photographers and editors during fashion month. (No, that was not Jennifer Coolidge at Diesel — it was a drag queen.) New York and London Fashion Weeks may be over, but Milan is underway and Paris is still to come. We’ll be there reporting, reviewing — and adding to this list as we do.

The Uniform, Reconsidered

Dressing in uniform, it could be argued, is a concept as old as clothes themselves. The theme is constantly revisited by brands, most recently Prada and Fendi, which showed collections that offered two different takes on what contemporary uniforms can be.

On the runway at Prada, there were easy-to-wear separates lavished “with the sort of detailed attention normally bestowed on couture,” The Times’s chief fashion critic, Vanessa Friedman, wrote. She described the clothes at Fendi as a mash-up of the uniforms associated with white-collar professionals and Catholic schools, “taking pinstripes, kilts and suiting, layering them together and twisting them up just a little bit, then adding a touch of kink for interest.”

‘The White Lotus’ Fashion Roadshow Hits Milan

Stars of the HBO series “The White Lotus” have been on the runway circuit since the men’s shows in January. But it was not until Milan Fashion Week that the actress Haley Lu Richardson joined the bandwagon.

At Diesel, Ms. Richardson, who plays Portia, sat in her first-ever front row — right next to a drag queen made up to look like her co-star Jennifer Coolidge. “I don’t really understand how fashion shows work,” Ms. Richardson told Jessica Testa, a Times reporter of fashion news.

Ms. Richardson was not the only spectacle at Diesel. The set included a massive heap of condom boxes, and the clothes displayed a craftsmanship “almost impossible to imagine,” The Times’s chief fashion critic, Vanessa Friedman, wrote.

Myriad Expressions of Punk

The style outside fashion shows in London tends to lean more punk, according to the photographer Simbarashe Cha. Seeing how people buck convention is his favorite part of surveying the crowds, but, he wrote, “even among nonconformists, patterns can emerge.”

Those he noticed during Fashion Week included suits with snazzy prints and leg-baring hemlines (on men and women).

Celebrities Party for … Puffer Jackets?

Though the London shows drew some A-list stars — Florence Pugh opened Harris Reed, and Ian McKellen performed at S.S. Daley — the largest celebrity contingent came not to sit beside a runway, but to attend an event hosted by Moncler, the Italian label known for its expensive outerwear.

Justin and Hailey Bieber, Serena Williams, Pharrell Williams, Lewis Hamilton and Charli XCX were all at the event, which was technically a presentation of the brand’s Moncler Genius line, and featured a foam party, robots and a live performance by Alicia Keys.

Burberry Begins a New Era

The most anticipated show during London Fashion Week, if not all of fashion month, this season was Burberry, which presented its first collection since the British designer Daniel Lee became the brand’s chief creative officer last September.

Following some unsteady years for the label and the British Empire it has come to represent, the stakes were high. With the clothes, which included inside-out trench coats and plenty of plaids, “Mr. Lee got it right,” wrote The Times’s chief fashion critic, Vanessa Friedman. “Ish.”

Remembering a Beloved Rebel

The day before London Fashion Week officially began, a group of supermodels, celebrities and editors assembled to remember the life and legacy of the British designer Vivienne Westwood, who died in December.

While some paid tribute with eulogies, many more honored Ms. Westwood, known as the godmother of punk, with their clothes, which included bold tartans, pinstripe bustiers, devil-horn tiaras, sequined tuxedos and oversize pink top hats.

A Gold Rush on the Streets of New York

While surveying the style outside New York Fashion Week shows, among the trends noticed by the photographer Simbarashe Cha was a surprising amount of gold.

The color, he wrote, “gave dresses, boots and full suits a shine that was hard — impossible, even — to ignore.”

Different Visions of Dressing Rich

On several runways in New York, Vanessa Friedman, The Times’s chief fashion critic, wrote, “The story the clothes told this fashion week was one of redefinition: a change in the hierarchy of aspiration and wealth, the sartorial codes that signpost power and success.”

Labels that offered interpretations on what it means to dress rich included some familiar names — Tory Burch, Michael Kors, Carolina Herrera — and some younger brands worth becoming more familiar with, including Luar and Who Decides War.

Downed Planes and Decadent Dinner Parties

A common refrain in fashion is that clothes speak for themselves. But sometimes elaborately crafted sets can enhance the allure of pieces on a runway.

At Thom Browne, models paraded past a life-size biplane that appeared to have crash landed in a clock-shaped desert made using eight tons of sand. And at Rodarte, a collection featuring what Vanessa Friedman, The Times’s chief fashion critic, described as “Morticia Addams gowns” and “cobwebby” knits was shown inside an old bank that had been filled with shimmering silver dining tables, chairs and candelabras that heightened the drama of the clothes.

Fashion That Embraces Imperfection

Coach, in recent years, “has made a signature out of PG-rated wardrobes,” as Vanessa Friedman, The Times’s chief fashion critic, put it. But its latest show featured what she described as “roughed up” basics — created with recycled, upcycled and salvaged materials that made the clothes look “as if they had been put through the wringer and emerged, victorious, on the other side.”

Elena Velez, who in 2022 was named emerging designer of the year by the Council of Fashion Designers of America, doubled down on showcasing the beauty in imperfection. “Her clothes are ripped, raw, bound and salvaged,” Vanessa wrote, adding that “the bodies on her runway are human-scale messy.”

Ahead of Milan Fashion Week, a Hunger Strike

Stella Jean, the Haitian Italian fashion designer who started showing in Milan in 2013, is the only Black member of the National Chamber of Italian Fashion, the organization that stages the city’s fall and spring fashion weeks.

A few years after calling out what she described as a lack of support for Black designers within the Italian fashion industry, Ms. Jean announced this month that she would go on a hunger strike to draw attention to the issue, which, in her opinion, has not largely improved.

A Pipeline From ‘The White Lotus’ to the Runway

Though many from the cast of “The White Lotus” have attended shows since the series’s second season ended in December, the actor Jon Gries was “the first to be seen on the runway during New York Fashion Week,” Jessica Testa, a Times reporter of fashion news, wrote.

In the series, Mr. Gries plays Greg, the husband of Tanya McQuoid. On the runway at Eckhaus Latta, he modeled a loosefitting yellowish-green sweater and wide-leg linen pants. Walking in a fashion show, he told Jessica, was “something I’d never done and something I wanted to try.” He also suggested that “White Lotus” viewers might not have seen the last of Tanya after her fall from a yacht into the sea.

The Trend of ‘Wearable Clothes’

At the New York shows of labels both established (Proenza Schouler) and emerging (Fforme), Vanessa Friedman, The Times’s chief fashion critic, observed something “radical,” as she put it: clothes designed more to be worn than to be noticed.

The amount of time now spent online “puts a premium on the viral, which itself puts a premium on the extreme and absurd,” she wrote, adding that “just because a garment doesn’t scream, ‘Look at me!,’ it doesn’t mean that it’s boring or corporate or pandering.”

Models Cosplaying as Rabbits and Reptiles

On the first official day of New York Fashion Week, it didn’t take long before things got wild: That evening, Collina Strada presented its collection on models made up to look like a small zoo’s worth of animals.

Some had the scaly faces of reptiles, others the buck teeth of rabbits or the snouts of pigs. Beneath all that makeup, on the models’ bodies, there were some striking clothes, too.

Was That Vivienne Westwood?

New York Fashion Week unofficially started on Feb. 2, when Marc Jacobs held his fall 2023 show more than a week before the official schedule began.

As models started walking down the runway, it became clear that the collection, called “Heroes,” was a tribute to the late British designer Vivienne Westwood, a hero to many (in fashion, at least) for bringing punk from the fringe to the mainstream.
