A battle over the Fed’s future

Donald Trump’s threat to exert more say over the Fed or even fire Jay Powell, the chair of the central bank, has alarmed some on Wall Street. But the president-elect’s effort took on added weight in recent days, after Elon Musk endorsed a push to erode the Fed’s independence.

The fight shows how the future of the Fed could remain high on the agenda, and how far Musk’s influence — and the role of X as place for announcing policy positions — could extend across government.

The Fed has its foes. Senator Mike Lee, Republican of Utah, introduced a bill in June to abolish the central bank, accusing it of being an “economic manipulator that has directly contributed to the financial instability many Americans face today.”

Lee said on X that he wants to see the Fed under the president’s control — a view that Musk backed.

Powell could turn to the courts to challenge any White House attempt to exert more control, according to The Wall Street Journal’s Nick Timiraos. Trump appointed Powell in 2017 but flirted with removing him shortly afterward. Powell held onto his job, but was ready for a fight if Trump made a move, Timiraos writes:

Powell told then-Treasury Secretary Steven Mnuchin that he would fight his removal if sought by the president, according to people familiar with the matter. Trump was upset the Fed was raising interest rates against his wishes.

For Powell, the unsavory prospect of a legal showdown — one he might have to pay for out of his own pocket — was imperative to preserve the ability of future Fed chairs to serve without the threat of being removed over a policy dispute.

Powell has made it clear that the president doesn’t have the authority to remove a Fed chair. Last week, he said he wouldn’t step down if Trump asked him to do so after the central bank lowered borrowing costs by a quarter point. Removing him, he added, was “not permitted under the law.”

A 1977 law gave Congress more oversight of the Fed, but enshrined the institution’s independence on policy.

The central bank’s ability to set monetary policy without political influence is a core tenet for markets and the economy. The Fed also has an outsized influence through its freedom to buy and sell securities, like Treasury notes and bonds, as it looks to bring more liquidity to trading.

It’s one reason many on Wall Street worry about any attempt to erode Fed independence. They fear it would destabilize investor confidence in the stock and bond markets. And the White House Council of Economic Advisers has called the independence doctrine a critical component in the fight to bring down inflation and promote economic growth.

Fed watchers are concerned. “If the president were to succeed at this, that would mean every future chair is subject to removal at the whim of the president,” Scott Alvarez, a former general counsel at the Fed, told The Journal. “I don’t think that’s a precedent Jay would want to set, and that’s why I think he would fight it. This is a humongous precedent.”

The big questions remain: Would a conservative Supreme Court back Trump in his mission to remake the Fed? Or would he ultimately avoid tampering with the Fed, potentially causing market turmoil?

HERE’S WHAT’S HAPPENING

Justice Sonia Sotomayor reportedly won’t retire anytime soon. The Supreme Court’s most senior liberal member will stay on despite pressure from progressive activists to step down before President-elect Donald Trump takes office in January, according to The Wall Street Journal. Some on the left are worried that she would have to retire during Trump’s term and could be replaced by another conservative.

Shigeru Ishiba wins a parliamentary vote to remain Japan’s prime minister. Ishiba defeated the leader of Japan’s main opposition party, even though his Liberal Democratic Party lost its parliamentary majority for the first time in 15 years last month. But analysts said that Ishiba remains in a weak position, and may lack the political capital to effectively deal with inflation, labor shortages and the return of Trump.

FTX sues Binance, a top rival, and its former C.E.O., Changpeng Zhao. The lawsuits by the failed crypto exchange are meant to claw back nearly $1.8 billion it paid to buy back Binance’s and Zhao’s stakes in FTX entities. (The deal was arranged by Sam Bankman-Fried.) FTX also accused Zhao of posting “false, misleading, and fraudulent tweets” before its collapse, which were “maliciously calculated to destroy his rival.”

The latest on the White House transition

Moves to replace Lina Khan at the F.T.C. are picking up steam. Gail Slater, an aide to Vice President-elect JD Vance, will vet candidates to succeed Khan and more broadly have an influential role in shaping competition policy, according to Bloomberg. Slater worked at the F.T.C. under the Obama administration, as a tech policy adviser in the first Trump White House, and for Fox and Roku.

Among those who are up for the F.T.C. role, according to Bloomberg, are Mark Meador, a former Justice Department lawyer who co-founded a boutique law firm; Todd Zywicki, a professor at George Mason University law school; and Alex Okuliar, the global antitrust co-chair at the law firm Morrison Foerster, who as a Justice Department official in the first Trump administration helped lead an antitrust inquiry into Google.

Vetting candidates for the Justice Department, including antitrust positions, are two Republican lawyers, Mark Paoletta and Will Levi.

Questions surround Howard Lutnick, the transition’s co-chair. Lutnick is in charge of identifying some 4,000 new hires to fill roles, The Times reports. Among those he has been consulting, he has said, are Steve Schwarzman of Blackstone and Charles Schwab, the eponymous founder of the brokerage giant.

But watchdogs have raised concerns about Lutnick vetting candidates for roles that would oversee Cantor Fitzgerald, the brokerage he also leads, and clients such as the cryptocurrency firm Tether. It isn’t clear whether Lutnick has signed the transition’s code of ethics or recused himself from weighing in on nominees who would regulate his business interests, according to The Times.

Career government employees worry about an exodus. Officials across an array of departments and agencies told Politico that a wave of departures could soon start, given fears that the Trump administration will cut their budgets and reverse their policy work. That would probably suit Trump and Elon Musk, who has promised to drastically slash the federal budget if he is given a role in the new administration.

But some officials said they would stay to defend their work.

  • In other transition news: Trump named Thomas Homan, a hard-line immigration official in his first administration, as his “border czar.” And Musk and other Trump allies backed Senator Rick Scott of Florida to become the Senate majority leader.

How the election is weighing on Silicon Valley

Prominent tech executives were quick to publicly extend congratulations to President-elect Donald Trump after the election (with some potentially worried about otherwise facing retaliation). Others, including those who backed Vice President Kamala Harris, expressed hope for the next four years.

But that doesn’t mean that they aren’t worried about what the second Trump administration might mean for their businesses, including immigration — and what their own employees are saying about politics.

Many in Silicon Valley are worried about a worker shortage, driven by a potential immigration crackdown, even for the H1-B skilled-worker visa program. One immigration lawyer told Business Insider that the average denial rate for H1-B applications was about 17 percent during the first Trump administration, compared with 3.2 percent under President Biden.

That could matter as tech companies race to hire specialists in artificial intelligence. Some executives expressed hope that a crackdown won’t come to pass. Among them is Aaron Levie of the cloud software company Box, who wrote on X that it is time to modernize the skilled worker system — and got support from Elon Musk.

Tech giants are keeping a lid on employee activism. Companies have maintained policies that restrict dialogue in internal message boards and monitor internal chat channels, The Times reports. (Such limits first emerged in response to workers speaking out on issues including Black Lives Matter and the war in Gaza.)

As a result, progressive employees who loudly protested policies in the first Trump administration have mostly stayed quiet. Moderators of internal Google forums have even taken down posts not explicitly criticizing Trump, including those featuring black squares as a symbol of mourning, The Times reports.

Bitcoin keeps on soaring

Crypto bulls are bidding up Bitcoin again on Monday, pushing the digital token to a new high of about $82,000. The cryptocurrency has surged nearly 16 percent since Election Day on investor hopes that President-elect Donald Trump will usher in a friendlier regulatory environment.

Cryptocurrencies have become a major component of the so-called Trump trade. Bitcoin E.T.F.s, which only got the green light to trade this year, have been booming in the last week. Crypto miners such as Riot Platforms, and Coinbase, the crypto exchange, have also rallied.

The industry poured millions into the election to back Trump and crypto-friendly congressional candidates. The strategy was hugely successful.

Trump has gone further than most by talking up crypto’s economic benefits, and he is promoting a new crypto venture.

The week ahead

The U.N.’s climate change summit, known as COP29, begins today in Azerbaijan, with the specter of Donald Trump looming over the event. The president-elect has promised to pull the U.S., the world’s biggest emitter of greenhouse gases, out of the Paris climate agreement — again. He has also vowed to expand oil and gas production and gut the Biden administration’s efforts to bolster the clean energy transition.

Banking and investment executives who had previously attended COP summits are reportedly skipping this year’s gathering, too, despite the conference’s focus on financial aspects of fighting climate change.

Here’s what else is on the agenda this week.

Tomorrow: It’s a busy earnings day, with AstraZeneca, Home Depot, Occidental Petroleum, Shopify, SoftBank and Spotify reporting.

Wednesday: The Consumer Price Index is set for release, as concerns grow about inflation. Cisco, Tencent and Tower Semiconductor are set to report earnings.

Thursday: Applied Materials and Disney announce earnings, and another update on inflation, the Producer Price Index, is due.
