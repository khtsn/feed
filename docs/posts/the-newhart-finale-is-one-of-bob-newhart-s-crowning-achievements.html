The finale has become so famous in part because it offered a rare moment of real surprise from a taped prime-time television sitcom.

By Noel Murray

July 18, 2024, 7:44 p.m. ET

Open any search engine you like and type in these words: “best TV finales.” Scroll through the dozens — heck, hundreds — of articles written about which shows really “stuck the landing,” delivering the kind finish that fans still talk about.

The “Newhart” finale should be on nearly all of those lists. For its last few minutes alone, “Newhart” deserves emeritus status on every roundup of best TV endings, best TV moments, funniest pranks, you name it. In perpetuity.

What makes it an all-timer? One knockout of a punchline.

For eight seasons — from 1982 to 1990 — Bob Newhart entertained millions on “Newhart,” playing Dick Loudon, a how-to book author and the co-owner of a quaint Vermont inn with his wife Joanna (Mary Frann). The success of “Newhart” was especially remarkable given that Newhart had already had a long run on TV in “The Bob Newhart Show,” which ran for six seasons, also on CBS, from 1972 to 1978.

He had spent those six years playing Bob Hartley, a Chicago psychologist who coped with his kooky patients with the help of his loving wife Emily (Suzanne Pleshette). These two characters, Loudon and Hartley, both drew on Newhart’s stand-up comedy persona: the stammering, muttering everyman, delivering hilariously deadpan reactions to the madness of modern life.

The “Newhart” finale bridged the gap between the two shows, with an ending that had Dick Loudon getting knocked out by a golf ball in Vermont and then waking up in a Chicago bedroom as Bob Hartley, with Emily by his side. The implication was that the entire run of “Newhart” had been Bob’s dream. On the night of the finale’s taping, the “Newhart” studio audience whooped in delight.

There is some important context for this joke. The TV drama “Dallas” had, a few years earlier, negated an entire season by retroactively turning it into one long dream sequence. The medical drama “St. Elsewhere” had also controversially ended its entire six-year run with a finale that suggested the whole show had been an autistic character’s dream. “Newhart” was, in part, spoofing those two shows. (Newhart always said that his wife, Ginny, had the idea for this joke, though the “Newhart” writers have claimed ownership for themselves.)

In the years since, the “Newhart” finale has become so famous that it, too, has been parodied and paid homage to, in several late-night TV sketches. There are many reasons it hasn’t been forgotten, but one big one is that it offered a rare moment of real surprise from a taped prime-time television sitcom.

In an interview for the Television Academy’s Archive of American Television, Newhart described all the machinations that went into preserving the finale’s secret. The writers included a fake ending in the script in which Dick met God after getting knocked out by the golf ball; the showbiz press ran with that news, speculating on who Newhart would get to play God. The show’s production team built the Hartley bedroom set in secret and kept it hidden until they shot the scene, knowing that as soon as the studio audience saw the décor, they would start applauding.

Also for the Archive, Pleshette said she had a code name she had to use whenever she called in to the office to arrange her appearance, so the news wouldn’t leak. On filming day, she had to hide out in a trailer for six hours so she wouldn’t be spotted by audience members. Because Newhart and Pleshette weren’t able to rehearse on the set, she worried she would forget where the light fixtures were in the Hartleys’ bedroom.

But as soon as the scene was done, Pleshette said, “I knew it was a piece of television history.” Still, she left early from the wrap party, because “Newhart” was not her show and she didn’t want to disrespect Frann, who she felt deserved to take her own bows.

In fact, sticklers for narrative continuity in TV shows have complained that the “Newhart” finale is somewhat unfair to Frann — and to the rest of the show’s cast — because it implies their characters are less important than the ones from “The Bob Newhart Show.” In an interview with Jason Zinoman for The New York Times back in 2019, Newhart said of the finale: “Some people felt cheated. They devoted eight years of their life and it turns out none of them existed.”

When asked for his response to that, Newhart said, simply, “O.K.” Newhart was a comedian, first and foremost. He knew what was funny.

Pleshette described her nerves on the day she showed up to shoot the finale, and how while they were covered with bedsheets and holding for their cue, Newhart squeezed her hand and said the words that should be inscribed on his tombstone: “Wait for the laugh.”

As ever with Newhart, timing was everything.
