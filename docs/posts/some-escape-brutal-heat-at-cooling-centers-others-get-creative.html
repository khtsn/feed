Some Escape Brutal Heat at Cooling Centers, Others Get Creative

The Western United States has been sweltering for more than a week with little respite for those without air-conditioning. Cooling centers can help, but not everyone takes advantage of them.

July 12, 2024Updated 3:59 p.m. ET

As millions of people across the country continue to broil under soaring temperatures, many cities are investing in a practical way to help residents beat the heat: cooling centers.

A smattering of studies indicate that cooling centers can help reduce heat-related health emergencies and fatalities. They are a common-sense remedy for residents dealing with an unrelenting heat wave, but one whose efficacy is hard to measure. The facilities are not always accessible to those who need them — particularly at night, which can be a problem when the temperatures stay stifling after the sun goes down.

People who frequent cooling centers tend to be older and lower income than the general population, studies show, and many of them have no shelter at all. That, in turn, has led to a sort of “social stigma,” according to Kathryn C. Conlon, an environmental epidemiologist and associate professor at the University of California, Davis, leading people to see cooling centers as a last resort rather than a reprieve.

With the heat wave that has engulfed much of the Western United States for more than a week lingering on — with triple-digit temperatures expected to stretch into the weekend for millions — the cool communal spaces have been welcome amenities in many cities where the weather is stifling. They offer an escape from the heat and can help hold down use of individual air-conditioners.

Cooling centers can foster a sense of community as well. Some facilities offer board games or spaces to play indoor sports.

At the Ted C. Wills Community Center in Fresno, Calif., a handful of people gathered around a jigsaw puzzle on Thursday, chatting against a soundtrack of squeaking sneakers from the teenagers playing basketball nearby.

Animals shared the space, too. Carlos Barraza, who is homeless, said that he visits the center daily so his aging dog, Rocky, can have access to air-conditioning. “I didn’t know where else to go,” he said.

Even people who have air-conditioners may visit a cooling center as a cost-saving measure.

“I think that as the electricity gets more and more expensive, we have some folks where the bill is a true burden,” said Kate Henry, the administrator for emergency preparedness services in Fresno. “They are thinking, ‘Hey, I’ll go to the cooling center and use their air-conditioning, and not our air-conditioning at home.’”

As long as their air-conditioners work, many public facilities can be turned into cooling centers, often with the addition of bottled water, snacks or extra staffing. But there can be logistical hurdles, Dr. Conlon said.

“A library or a recreation center — often times, these are public, taxpayer-funded facilities,” she said. “How can we fund something like that? How do you staff something like that? What’s the security like for a place that needs to be running after hours?”

If the current forecasts are an indication, municipalities will be grappling with these questions for some time. Another two dozen daily temperature records could be broken to begin the weekend.

“This long-duration heat wave remains extremely dangerous and deadly if not taken seriously,” a forecaster with the Weather Prediction Center warned.

In Las Vegas, where the punishing heat has stunned even forecasters, the weekend was expected to bring some easing, but only by a few degrees.

A heat advisory in the Denver area extends through the weekend, with the National Weather Service predicting record-breaking temperatures for both Saturday and Sunday.

And in southeast Texas, the heat has been made all the worse by power problems left behind by Hurricane Beryl, which knocked out electricity for millions of residents this week. Some 800,000 customers in the Houston area were still without power on Friday afternoon, according to CenterPoint Energy, the main utility there.

The extreme heat will push into the Midwest and the East by early next week, with heat indexes as high as 110 expected in the Philadelphia area and in the low 100s in New York.

Cooling centers are likely to be put to good use then. But they can present a sort of paradox: It takes energy to beat the heat, but electricity-guzzling air-conditioners can drive up the greenhouse-gas emissions that cause global warming. Around the world, each of the past 13 months has been the warmest on record for that month of the year, and last year ranked as the warmest ever recorded.

At a community center run by the Salvation Army in Tucson, Ariz., on Friday, families with young children sat at tables in a small room. The day’s heat hadn’t reached its peak yet, but Ailin Ramos, a social worker, said she and her colleagues were ready to help anyone looking to escape the scorching weather.

“They can sit down to cool off,” said Ms. Ramos, 25. “We give them water or something else to drink, and hygiene items if they need them.”

Some cities and residents have turned to other creative solutions to staying cool.

Mobile, Ala., has seven city-designated indoor cooling centers, but under the city’s summer cool-down initiative, officials have turned some outdoor parks into cooling stations by installing misters and splash pads for children to play with water.

On Friday afternoon at the Tricentennial Park in Mobile, more than a dozen children took advantage of the newly created splash pad under sweltering conditions and a heat index value of 99. Some of them skipped through the pad, while others huddled over neon-colored buckets, waiting for them to fill up with water to drench themselves.

Lataisha Cook, a day care employee, was looking after the children with her co-worker Alicia Barnett. Ms. Cook and Ms. Barnett have resorted to creative measures to keep the children hydrated this summer. “We had water balloons, big pitchers of water, and if it was too hot, we simply kept these kids inside,” Ms. Barnett said.

With people often opting to stay at home during a heat wave, many municipalities emphasize the importance of old-fashioned neighborliness. “A lot of the messaging now around extreme heat events is to check in on your neighbors,” Dr. Conlon said.

In Las Vegas, Darrell Durdin’s friends helped her endure the peak of the heat wave after her air-conditioner died. They donated two swamp coolers, three fans and a mobile air-conditioning unit to cool down her condominium.

Ms. Durdin, an Instacart driver, found refuge in her air-conditioned car while making deliveries, and in shops where she picks up items for customers. “Going into a grocery store is like heaven,” she said.
