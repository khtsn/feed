At Trump Rally, Local Police and Gunman Were in Same Warehouse Complex

The Secret Service left local law enforcement to secure the building, which was within rifle range of the podium where the former president spoke.

  • July 16, 2024, 3:25 p.m. ET

While a gunman was climbing onto the roof of a warehouse less than 500 feet from where former President Donald J. Trump was speaking on Saturday, three law enforcement snipers were positioned inside the same complex of buildings, looking for anything amiss in the crowd.

The director of the Secret Service said the local forces were in the very same building, an account suggesting that the gunman was literally on top of them. A local law enforcement official told The New York Times on Tuesday that was not the case, and that the local forces were in an adjacent building.

The discrepancy in their accounts is just one unsettled element in the effort to determine how security broke down and allowed a 20-year-old with an semi-automatic rifle to open fire in a rapid barrage that left Mr. Trump hurt, one man dead and two other people at the rally gravely wounded.

That this matter is still not easily resolved three days after the shooting shows that the law enforcement agencies have turned to finger pointing after a would-be assassin came close to felling the Republican presidential nominee two days before the Republican Party convention.

At the heart of the investigation is the warren of warehouses adjacent to the rally site, the Butler Farm Show grounds.

What’s clear is that the complex was outside what the Secret Service chose as its most secure perimeter, so the buildings were delegated to local law enforcement to sweep and secure. While local law enforcement officers are used for additional security in an event like a campaign rally, the Secret Service is the agency charged first and foremost with protecting America’s leaders, including former presidents, and it was the agency’s job to determine the security zone and keep the protectee — in this case Mr. Trump — safe.

It’s also clear that the gunman used the roof of a single-story manufacturing warehouse owned by AGR International to fire off shots just after 6 p.m., leaving Mr. Trump’s ear bloodied, before a Secret Service sniper shot and killed him.

The Secret Service and local law enforcement authorities agree that local police forces were in the warehouse complex, using it as a staging area and a perch for the snipers.

Secret Service Director Kimberly A. Cheatle said in an interview with ABC News that local officers were inside the building used by the gunman, Thomas Matthew Crooks, 20, of Bethel Park, Pa., on Saturday evening. That would mean the gunman would have scaled a building while snipers were stationed inside it, watching for trouble.

“There was local police in that building — there was local police in the area that were responsible for the outer perimeter of the building,” Ms. Cheatle said.

That was not the case, a local law enforcement official, who was not authorized to give public statements, said in an interview with The New York Times. The building that the gunman climbed onto was one story and the snipers, the official said, were stationed by the windows on the second floor of an adjacent building. The official added that the local snipers, who were watching the crowd, were not assigned to monitor the buildings outside the security zone.

Regardless, everyone agrees that no one from law enforcement was on the roof of any of the warehouse buildings — in hindsight a tragic call.

Inside their assigned warehouse, a local emergency services team, which can typically include officers from multiple local agencies, had been set up. The team included so-called counter snipers, who were responsible for looking out at the crowd where the rally was to begin at 5 p.m. Saturday, an official familiar with the situation said. Mr. Trump appeared on stage just past 6 p.m.

After the gunman made it to the roof, a local officer was seen scrambling up the building’s wall and using his hands to lift himself over the precipice, only to find the shooter, said Sheriff Michael T. Slupe of Butler County and a federal law enforcement official.

The shooter pointed his gun at the officer, who immediately retreated, the officials said. Shortly after, the gunman began firing at the rally.

The Secret Service, in a post on Tuesday on X, said that it was not criticizing its local law enforcement partners in Butler. “Our agency relies on the support of courageous police officers and local partners. We are deeply grateful for their unwavering commitment and bravery. Any suggestion otherwise is simply not true.”

In her interview with ABC, Ms. Cheatle said that no officers were stationed atop the roof itself because it would not be safe.

“That building in particular has a sloped roof, at its highest point. And so there’s a safety factor that would be considered there, that we wouldn’t want to put somebody up on a sloped roof,” Ms. Cheatle said. “So the decision was made to secure the building from inside.”

Former Secret Service agents said that agents do take up positions on roofs more steeply sloped than this one — indeed, Secret Service snipers were perched on a steeper roof behind Mr. Trump at the same event. But they said the agency also does weigh safety, and sometimes opts to block access to sloped roofs instead of putting someone on top.

Joe Funk, a former agent who protected Presidents George H.W. Bush and Clinton, said he felt that in this case it would have been better to station someone on the roof. “The bottom line is, the roof should have been posted and utilized as an observation post with police officers on it,” he added.
