Short news: Week 31

The Senate overwhelmingly passed the Kids Online Safety Act on Tuesday, but the legislation faces an uphill battle in the House because of censorship concerns.

Cecilia Kang

Reporting on child online safety from Washington

July 30, 2024Updated 1:10 p.m. ET

Last week, the American Civil Liberties Union sent 300 high school students to Capitol Hill to lobby against the Kids Online Safety Act, a bill meant to protect children online.

The teenagers told the staffs of 85 lawmakers that the legislation could censor important conversations, particularly among marginalized groups like L.G.B.T.Q. communities.

“We live on the internet, and we are afraid that important information we’ve accessed all our lives will no longer be available,” said Anjali Verma, a 17-year-old rising high school senior from Bucks County, Pa., who was part of the student lobbying campaign. “Regardless of your political perspective, this looks like a censorship bill.”

The effort was one of many escalations in recent months by those who oppose the bill. In June, a progressive nonprofit, Fight for the Future, organized students to write hundreds of letters to urge lawmakers to scrap it. Conservative groups like Patriot Voices, founded by the former Republican senator Rick Santorum of Pennsylvania, are also protesting with an online petition.

What was supposed to be a simple piece of legislation to protect children online has been dragged into a heated political war. At the heart of the battle are concerns about how the bill could affect free speech on culturally divisive issues, which both sides of the spectrum worry could be weaponized under the guise of child safety. Liberals worry about censorship of transgender care, while conservatives are concerned about the same with anti-abortion efforts. The tech industry has also latched onto the same First Amendment arguments to oppose the bill.

The controversy stems from the specific terms of the Kids Online Safety Act, or KOSA. The legislation would require social media platforms and other sites to limit features that can heighten cyberbullying, harassment and the glorification of self-harm. The bill would also require tech companies to turn on the highest privacy and safety settings for users under 17 and let them opt out of some features that have been shown to lead to compulsive use.

The bill’s opponents worry that these measures could have unintended consequences, pushing social media companies to remove or hide potentially important and lifesaving content.

While KOSA overwhelmingly passed the Senate on Tuesday 91-3, approval in the House could be more difficult as debates over speech continue to flare.

“This bill has flown under the radar as a great way to protect kids online, and everyone wants that,” said Alice Marwick, a professor of communications at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. “But people are also realizing the bill is more like a wolf in sheep’s clothing and could have huge implications for speech.”

If enacted, it will be the first law aimed at protecting children online since the Children’s Online Privacy Protection Act in 1998. Previous efforts were stuck in yearslong debates in Congress.

KOSA has gained momentum as Washington has grown increasingly concerned about the effects of social media on the mental health and safety of children. In January, lawmakers grilled the chief executives of Meta, Snap, X and TikTok about toxic content on their platforms over suicidal ideation, eating disorders and sexual exploitation. This spring, the surgeon general linked social media to a youth mental health crisis.

Dozens of parents have lobbied Congress to pass KOSA, carrying photos of children they say have died from exposure to harmful online activity.

We’re making “the social media platforms accountable for the harms they’ve caused with the product design and business decisions they’ve made — not the third-party content that happens to be on their platforms,” Senator Richard Blumenthal, a Connecticut Democrat and co-author of the Senate’s version of the bill, said in a statement last week.

The bill faced pushback almost from its beginning. In February 2022, Mr. Blumenthal and Senator Marsha Blackburn, Republican of Tennessee, introduced KOSA amid hearings on children’s online safety by the consumer protection subcommittee.

While many child advocacy groups, teachers and parents voiced early support, free speech groups quickly raised concerns. They argued that the bill would give social media sites and regulators too much latitude to define what was harmful to youths.

Transgender-affirming care is under attack and at the center of the political agenda for some Republicans, said Evan Greer, a campaign director for Fight for the Future.

In November 2022, Fight for the Future sent a letter to Senate leaders with the signatures of more than 100 L.G.B.T.Q. and human rights advocates opposing the bill. The group also launched websites where nearly 400,000 people have used auto-email features to try to sway their legislators. The bill wasn’t adopted that year.

In May 2023, after Mr. Blumenthal and Ms. Blackburn reintroduced the bill, the conservative Heritage Foundation posted on X that “keeping trans content away from children keeps them safe.”

Ms. Greer, who is transgender, feared that a conservative administration could pick regulators who defined transgender and reproductive rights issues as harmful to children. That could lead social media companies to block or remove that content, making it unavailable to those in need of help. she said.

“This is not a safety bill, but could put many L.G.B.T.Q.+ youth in danger,” Ms. Greer said in an interview.

Some conservatives have also expressed concern.

Patriot Voices posted a link this year to an online petition protesting KOSA, saying it would “institutionalize federal censorship standards related to medical information,” including vaccinations, masking and transition surgery for minors.

Tech lobbyists have repeated some of the same speech arguments and public concerns by L.G.B.T.Q. groups to stoke worries about the bill, several congressional staff members said.

“KOSA fails to meet basic constitutional principles and fails parents because it won’t make a single child safer online,” Carl Szabo, the chief legal officer for NetChoice, a tech trade group that has sued states over laws similar to KOSA because of speech concerns. Last year, the group won a preliminary injunction against California’s child safety law, which a federal judge described as too broad and a violation of the First Amendment.

KOSA’s authors have revised the bill to try to address First Amendment implications, as well as to regulate the proposed law at the federal level to avoid its political use by conservatives or liberals at the state level.

Still, some teenagers continue to oppose KOSA.

Jax, 13, of New Jersey, has posted online that he is worried about how the bill might affect other transgender youths. When he first questioned his gender identity, he said, he was afraid to tell his friends and family, instead turning to TikTok, where he found scores of videos about being transgender. They changed his life, he said, helping him understand that despite being assigned female at birth, he felt closer to male.

KOSA “makes me angry,” said Jax, who asked that his full name not be used, fearing retribution. “The videos were like a learning tool” he said, adding that they “made me feel a little better about myself.”

At meetings in Congress last week, many teenagers sent by the A.C.L.U. described how meeting other peers online had helped with feelings of isolation.

Ms. Verma, the rising high school senior in Pennsylvania, met with the staff of two lawmakers, including Representative John Joyce, a Republican from her state. She described how the internet was an important source of information and support after she was cyberbullied. She asked lawmakers to focus on ways to help train and educate youths to be critical thinkers about what they experienced online.

“It’s called the Kids Online Safety Act, but they have to consider kids’ voices, and some of us don’t think it will make us safer,” Ms. Verma said.
