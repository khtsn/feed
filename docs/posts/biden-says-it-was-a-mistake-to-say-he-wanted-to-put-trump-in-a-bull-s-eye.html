Reporting from Washington

July 15, 2024

President Biden said on Monday it was “a mistake” to say that he wanted to put former President Donald J. Trump in “a bull’s-eye” but defended his descriptions of his rival in the 2024 election as a threat to the foundations of the nation’s democracy.

“How do you talk about the threat to democracy, which is real, when a president says things like he says?” Mr. Biden asked NBC’s Lester Holt in a 20-minute interview broadcast in prime time, as Republicans formally nominated Mr. Trump and his vice-presidential pick, Senator J.D. Vance, at the party’s political convention in Milwaukee.

“Do you just not say anything because it may incite somebody?” Mr. Biden asked.

The interview was the latest attempt by Mr. Biden to demonstrate to his allies and supporters that he remains sharp and vigorous enough to defeat Mr. Trump in November. With his every word under intense scrutiny since his listless performance at a debate last month, Mr. Biden at times came across as frustrated with the focus on his mental acuity.

When Mr. Holt asked about moments when the president “appeared to be confused” at the debate, Mr. Biden pivoted immediately to Mr. Trump.

“Lester, look. Why don’t you guys ever talk about the 18 to 28 lies he told?” Mr. Biden asked. “Where are you on this? Why didn’t the press ever talk about that? Twenty-eight times, it’s confirmed, he lied in that debate. I had a bad, bad night. I wasn’t feeling well at all. And I had been — well, I’m not going to make any — I screwed up.”

Mr. Biden also said he was not surprised that the federal judge overseeing Mr. Trump’s classified documents case threw out all of the charges against him on Monday, but he struggled to make his point clearly.

“I had an independent prosecutor to look at me,” Mr. Biden said. “They spent months on mine, going through — and I was totally cooperative — in and out of my house. And I’ll never, I don’t know.”

At the end of his answer, Mr. Biden noted that the prosecutor cleared him of any wrongdoing, and then added: “But my generic point is that it’s um, well.” He was quiet for several moments before explaining that he disagreed with a Supreme Court opinion by Justice Clarence Thomas related to the issue.

The president’s interview with Mr. Holt came just two days after a would-be assassin shot at Mr. Trump at a rally in western Pennsylvania, grazing the former president’s ear, killing one of his supporters and seriously wounding two others. Republicans have accused Mr. Biden of stoking violence against Mr. Trump, pointing to a comment that the president made last week to donors. He told them that “we’re done talking about the debate; it’s time to put Trump in a bull’s-eye.”

Mr. Biden told Mr. Holt that “it was a mistake to use the word.” He added: “I didn’t, I didn’t say cross hairs. I meant bull’s-eye. I meant focus on it. Focus on what he’s doing.”

He added that Mr. Trump has used incendiary rhetoric for years, citing the former president’s statement that he intends to be a “dictator on Day 1” and his comments in the lead-up to the Jan. 6, 2021, attack on the Capitol, in which he encouraged people to head there.

Asked by Mr. Holt whether he had done any soul-searching about his own language, Mr. Biden said he had not.

“Look, I’m not engaged in that rhetoric,” he insisted. “Now, my opponent is engaged in that rhetoric. He talks about there’ll be a blood bath if he loses, talking about how he’s going to forgive, although, actually, I guess suspend the sentences of all those who were arrested and sentenced to go to jail because of what happened in the Capitol.”

Mr. Biden has said repeatedly since the shooting that the country needs to turn down the heated rhetoric and disagree without violence or hateful language.

He said he would continue to “talk about the things that matter to the American public” and criticized Mr. Trump for talking about immigrants like “vermin.”

“That’s the kind of language that is inflammatory,” he said.

Mr. Biden said he was not sure whether the assassination attempt on Mr. Trump would alter the trajectory of the presidential contest. He said he was focused more on ensuring that the candidates in the race get the level of protection they require from the Secret Service.

And the president said he remained concerned about the way people talk about politicians.

“You ride through certain rural areas of the country, and people have signs they’re standing — big Trump signs with a middle — sign that says ‘F Biden’ and a little kid standing there, putting up his middle finger,” he said.

“I mean, that’s the kind of stuff that is just inflammatory and kind of vicious,” he added. “It’s a very different thing to say, ‘Look, I really disagree with Trump’s — the way he takes care of taxes.’”

When Mr. Holt turned the interview back to the question of his age and fitness for office in the wake of the debate, Mr. Biden all but dismissed the question and said he was the rightful nominee.

“Fourteen million people voted for me to be the nominee, in the Democratic Party,” he said. “OK. I’ll listen to them.”

“Me,” the president said, pausing for several moments. “I understand why people say, ‘God, he is 81 years old. Whoa. What’s he going to be when he’s 83? years old? 84 years old?’ It’s a legitimate question to ask.”

Asked whether he had talked to former President Barack Obama since the debate, Mr. Biden said, “I may have. I don’t think so.”

Also on Monday, an interview that Mr. Biden gave to Chris “Speedy” Morman, a journalist with Complex, a hip-hop news website, was released. The interview was recorded a day before the assassination attempt on Mr. Trump.

In it, Mr. Biden accused Mr. Trump of doing nothing for people in minority groups.

“What has he done when he was president? Tell me,” Mr. Biden said. “What has he done to help young people in America? What has he done to deal with racism?”

In the 18-minute interview, the president defended his support for Israel but also claimed to be “the guy that did more for the Palestinian community than anybody,” citing his efforts to open routes for humanitarian aid through Egypt. He also noted that he had recently denied Israel the use of 2,000-pound bombs that have contributed to mass casualties in Gaza.

The president has been heavily criticized by Palestinian Americans and others who say he has helped to enable the Israeli response to the Oct. 7 Hamas attacks that killed 1,200 people. Israel, which relies heavily on American weapons, has pummeled Gaza for nearly 10 months, killing tens of thousands of people living there.

When Mr. Morman asked whether there was one thing Mr. Trump would do well if he got elected to a second term, the president said he had no answer.

“I’m not being facetious,” the president said. “I can’t think of a single thing.”
