Vermont Storms Bring More ‘Demoralizing’ Flooding, Damaging Homes

Dozens of homes were destroyed and damaged in the latest wave of extreme rainfall in the state, officials said.

July 31, 2024Updated 11:45 p.m. ET

A storm that brought more than eight inches of rain to parts of northeastern Vermont early Tuesday destroyed and damaged dozens of homes, officials said, the latest blow to a state that has suffered a string of severe floods since last summer.

Preliminary information indicated that 50 or more homes were ruined or suffered major damage, Jennifer Morrison, the commissioner of the state’s Department of Public Safety, said in a news conference on Wednesday.

Ms. Morrison said swiftwater rescue teams made 12 rescues and 15 evacuations. Considering that the storm occurred in the middle of the night and that the initial rescue operations took place in darkness, “we had pretty remarkable outcomes,” she said.

The state has not yet received any reports of deaths caused by the flooding, according to Vermont Emergency Management.

The highest amount of rain recorded was 8.4 inches in St. Johnsbury, a town of about 7,000 people located an hour northeast of Montpelier, according to the National Weather Service office in Burlington. Other parts of the region drew six to eight inches of rain.

Additional showers and storms remain a threat to the region. A flood watch is in effect for all of northern and central Vermont through Wednesday night. In areas that have already seen heavy precipitation, little rainfall is needed to produce flooding, the National Weather Service said.

The latest damage comes just weeks after flash flooding from the tail end of Hurricane Beryl left two dead and forced more than 100 evacuations and rescues, and little more than a year after a record-breaking storm, described by some as an event that happens once in 100 years, flooded downtown Montpelier and destroyed homes and businesses across the state.

Gov. Phil Scott, who described the recent storm’s severity as a surprise, said in the Wednesday news conference that this week’s flooding has set back some parts of the state that were recovering from Beryl. He said municipal leaders, road crews and other workers have in some cases seen “all the progress they’ve made since the flooding three weeks ago being washed away again.”

“It’s simply demoralizing,” he said.

Mr. Scott said he planned to ask President Biden to declare the flooding from earlier this month as a major disaster, which would facilitate a range of federal assistance. He said it was not yet clear if this week’s storms would qualify for the assistance.

In Burke, a town of roughly 1,700 about an hour northeast of Montpelier, Jim Sullivan, the town administrator, said local crews had worked diligently to fix damage from the storm earlier this month and had mostly finished when the Tuesday storm dumped seven inches of rain in three hours. The rain washed out roads, endangered a key bridge and undid $20,000 of repairs to a local network of mountain biking trails that helps draw summer visitors to the tourism-dependent area.

The raging water, which overwhelmed local streams and brooks, swept several one-ton concrete cinder blocks out from under a snowmobile bridge, pushing them 100 to 200 yards downriver.

“The sound of those rocks in the river slamming against each other — it’s kind of disturbing to hear it, and realize the power of that water,” Mr. Sullivan said.

Mr. Sullivan said town leaders were first focusing on rapid road repairs to ensure that older residents could get to doctor’s appointments and that emergency vehicles could reach homes.

“You might have your head in your hands, and you can’t believe you’re doing this again, but you do it,” he said. Amid the shock, he added, “you’re out the door, putting on the face of confidence and optimism people are looking for.”

In Lyndon, near Burke, the storms have washed out several roads, taken out five bridges and destroyed multiple houses, according to Justin Smith, the municipal administrator. He said the rainfall forecast for Wednesday night could put additional homes at risk.

“We just need a break,” he said. “We need a chance to fix what is broken before more water comes.”

Trudy Brasure of Lyndonville, 56, said she drove out with her husband on Tuesday and saw that the road her house is on was blocked by water and a house sitting halfway on the road.

She said she was grateful that her house had not been damaged but was worried about what climate change could bring next.

“How much can we sustain this kind of regular damage?” she asked. “This is supposed to be happening between centuries, not in the same month.”

Researchers have found that climate change is increasing extreme precipitation in the Northeast, as warmer air can hold more moisture, resulting in heavier rainstorms.

Gillian Galford, a University of Vermont climate scientist, said that St. Johnsbury has experienced two days of extreme precipitation — one in the wake of Hurricane Beryl and another earlier this week — that have produced more rain in a single day than the town usually sees across the month of July.

Climate experts caution that it is difficult to draw conclusions from the two July storms about what the rest of the summer will look like for Vermont. But Jonathan Winter, a climate expert at Dartmouth who has studied extreme precipitation in the Northeast, said the longer-term trend is clear: “We’ve loaded the dice for more of these storms.”
