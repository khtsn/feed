Kyrsten Sinema, Behind on Fund-Raising, Faces Senate Re-election Decision

The Arizona senator is behind schedule on deciding whether to run for re-election as an independent, and she raised only $600,000 in the last quarter of 2023.

Senator Kyrsten Sinema is behind schedule in making a decision about whether to seek re-election in Arizona. She’s also running out of time.

Ms. Sinema, who left the Democratic Party just over a year ago to become an independent, is still considering whether to run for a second term, aides said. But new campaign finance reports show that she is lagging well behind the plan she and her team discussed last spring.

In April, Ms. Sinema and her advisers laid out a schedule for her decision that set a deadline of Sept. 30 for an initial round of public opinion polling and research into challengers, who include Representative Ruben Gallego, a Democrat, and Kari Lake, a Republican television-anchor-turned-politician who narrowly lost the governor’s race in 2022. By the end of December, Ms. Sinema would have a campaign staff in place.

But there is no sign that she carried out any significant polling, research or staff hires in the final six months of last year. She spent $6.9 million during the past year, new filings with the Federal Election Commission show, much of which went toward digital advertising, security and consulting.

New concerns also emerged on the other side of her campaign’s ledger. Ms. Sinema collected just under $600,000 during the final three months of last year — the fourth quarter in a row in which she had raised less than in the previous three-month period.

“There are no signs — literally or figuratively — that Kyrsten is going to run,” said Barrett Marson, a Republican strategist in Arizona.

Perhaps most urgently, Ms. Sinema’s expenditures also show little indication that she has started collecting the tens of thousands of signatures she would need to put her name on the ballot. That process must be completed by April 8 in Arizona and can cost about $1 million, a total that will probably grow more expensive the longer she waits to begin any campaign.

A spokeswoman for Ms. Sinema said the senator had been focused on her work in Washington, including negotiations over a high-profile immigration bill, and had not yet made her decision about pursuing a second term.

Ms. Sinema has $10.6 million in the bank, the new filings show, enough to start an effective campaign, said Hannah Hurley, Ms. Sinema’s spokeswoman.

“Kyrsten remains laser-focused on continuing her work making a meaningful impact in the lives of Arizonans across the state, not on campaign politics,” Ms. Hurley said. “She has the resources to keep delivering lasting results for Arizona.”

Ms. Sinema’s eight-figure campaign bank account means she has more money on hand than both of her two top challengers combined.

Mr. Gallego, the front-runner for the Democratic nomination for Senate, had about $6.5 million on hand after raising $13.8 million for the year, his filings showed. The top Republican candidate, Ms. Lake, raised $2.1 million in the final three months of last year, her first quarter as a candidate, and spent about half of that total, her report showed.

Similar trends are reflected across the map of battleground Senate races, where incumbents, many of them Democrats, are largely starting the year with significant financial advantages over their challengers.

In Montana, Senator Jon Tester, a Democrat seeking a fourth six-year term, raised $5.5 million in the fourth quarter of last year and had $11.2 million on hand, according to his filing.

Mr. Tester’s top Republican challenger, Tim Sheehy, a wealthy businessman, raised $2.5 million in the fourth quarter, including $450,000 of his own money, according to his filings. Republicans also expect Representative Matt Rosendale, a far-right agitator, to enter the race soon.

In Ohio, Senator Sherrod Brown, a Democrat also seeking his fourth term, raised $6.6 million in the fourth quarter and had $14.6 million on hand, according to his filings.

Among the three top Republicans seeking their party’s nomination in Ohio, Bernie Moreno, a businessman known for his chain of car dealerships in the state, collected $800,300 during the fourth quarter and ended the year with about $2 million in cash on hand, his filing shows. Mr. Moreno, who has been endorsed by former President Donald J. Trump, spent $3 million on his own campaign last year and added another seven-figure check this month, according to a person familiar with the matter who was not authorized to speak publicly.

Another Republican candidate, State Senator Matt Dolan, raised $357,700 during the fourth quarter and started the new year with $4.8 million on hand, his reports showed. The third major G.O.P. contender, Secretary of State Frank LaRose, collected $812,500 during the fourth quarter and started the new year with $771,600 in the bank, according to his fillings.

Much of the suspense in the fight for the Senate has surrounded Ms. Sinema, however. Since leaving the Democratic Party in December 2022, she has been ambiguous about her plans for a second term, keeping the political establishment on a razor’s edge.

If she runs again as an independent, amending her campaign’s initial timeline, which was previously reported by The Wall Street Journal, it will ignite a riveting and unusually competitive three-way race. If not, she’ll close one of the most eventful and colorful chapters in recent Senate history.

While Ms. Sinema has stayed cagey about her intentions, both Mr. Gallego and Ms. Lake began aggressive campaigns last year, but still have significant ground to make up with Ms. Sinema when it comes to fund-raising.

Mr. Gallego had one of the highest fund-raising totals last year for any Senate candidate who was not an incumbent. But his campaign has already spent half of that total without running a single television ad. This is Mr. Gallego’s first statewide campaign, and he is still relatively unknown to many voters.

Compared with other Republicans running for the Senate in battleground states, Ms. Lake’s total was relatively low: David McCormick, a Republican former hedge fund executive who announced his campaign for Senate in Pennsylvania in September, about three weeks before Ms. Lake started her bid in Arizona, raised $5.5 million, including $1 million of his own money. Mr. McCormick had $4.2 million in the bank at the end of the year.

Ms. Lake’s fourth-quarter total was roughly on par with that of Sam Brown, a retired Army captain and Republican running for Senate in Nevada, who raised $1.85 million in the fourth quarter of 2023 for his bid to unseat the incumbent Democrat, Senator Jacky Rosen.

Both Mr. Brown and Mr. McCormick are trying to topple incumbents with significant war chests for the coming year.

In Pennsylvania, Senator Bob Casey, a Democrat, raised $3.6 million during the same period and had $9.4 million in the bank, his filings showed.

In Nevada, Ms. Rosen raised $3.2 million in the final three months of last year, and had started 2024 with $10.6 million on hand, her reports showed.
