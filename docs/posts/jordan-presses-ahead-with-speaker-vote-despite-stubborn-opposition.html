The hard-line conservative from Ohio is likely to be defeated when the House holds another speaker vote on Friday, the third one this week.

Luke Broadwater

Reporting from the Capitol

  • Oct. 20, 2023Updated 9:51 a.m. ET

Representative Jim Jordan of Ohio made it clear on Friday morning that he was not giving up in his faltering campaign to be House speaker just ahead of a 10 a.m. vote in which he was expected to fail for a third time.

At a brief news conference at the Capitol, Mr. Jordan, a co-founder of the ultraconservative House Freedom Caucus who has risen to become the chairman of the Judiciary Committee, emphasized the need for the House to elect a new leader so the chamber could resume its business, including providing assistance to Israel as it responds to the attacks by Hamas.

“We need to get to work for the American people,” he said, adding, “We need to get a speaker as soon as possible.”

The House has been in a state of chaos for 18 days after hard-right rebels ousted Speaker Kevin McCarthy. The Republican conference’s initial nominee to succeed him, Representative Steve Scalise of Louisiana, failed to muster enough support to move forward. Mr. Jordan then received his party’s nomination to be speaker, but he failed to win the post in votes by the full House on Tuesday and Wednesday.

At his news conference on Friday, Mr. Jordan entered the room to applause from a small group of conservative allies backing his bid.

“He should keep going,” said Representative Scott Perry of Pennsylvania, the current chairman of the Freedom Caucus. “Because they’re going to come around.”

A favorite of former President Donald J. Trump, Mr. Jordan has seen his bid for speaker blocked by a group of mainstream Republicans harboring a litany of complaints against him. And a pressure campaign Mr. Jordan’s allies have waged against them, which has included threats and intimidation, has produced an intense backlash.

Many of those opposed to Mr. Jordan are angry at the way some of his allies treated Mr. McCarthy and Mr. Scalise, the No. 2 House Republican. Backers of Mr. Jordan failed to rally around Mr. Scalise after he drew support from a majority of Republicans in a conference vote.

Other lawmakers opposed to Mr. Jordan, including members from swing districts and lawmakers in charge of congressional spending, are worried about Mr. Jordan’s ultraconservative policies.

One holdout, Representative Ken Buck of Colorado, has said he is concerned about the way Mr. Jordan tried to overturn the 2020 election results at the behest of Mr. Trump.

Mr. Jordan met Thursday with some of the 22 holdouts against him. But they remained dug in and encouraged him to drop out of contention.

“He needed to know there is no way forward for his speakership,” Representative John Rutherford of Florida, one of the holdouts, told reporters after meeting with Mr. Jordan.

Allies of Mr. Jordan have urged him to force votes on his nomination throughout the weekend, even though opposition against him was expected to grow.

“I’ve heard it said in our conference that Jesus can’t get to 217,” Representative Thomas Massie of Kentucky, a supporter of Mr. Jordan, said, referring to the number of votes needed to become speaker. “And I’ve also heard it said that nobody can get to 217. What people really mean in our conference when they say that is nobody can get to 217 in two rounds and three legislative days. It’s just not possible.”

Mr. Massie said he could not think of another option in the Republican conference who could amass the needed votes. He compared the situation unfavorably to a previous speaker vacancy in which Representative Paul Ryan of Wisconsin emerged as a consensus pick.

“We’re all racking our brains right now,” Mr. Massie said. “If it’s not McCarthy and it’s not Steve Scalise and it’s not Jim Jordan, there’s not a Paul Ryan in the conference who’s left.”

The vote planned for Friday morning will cap a tumultuous 24 hours for House Republicans, who spent Thursday fighting among themselves in closed-door meetings, trading blame and insults, and casting about for a way forward as they failed again to coalesce around a speaker candidate.

Republicans on Thursday shot down a plan being floated by centrist lawmakers in both parties that would temporarily give the interim speaker, Representative Patrick T. McHenry of North Carolina, explicit power to conduct legislative business. Mr. Jordan backed that plan for a time on Thursday, only to reverse course in the face of furious opposition from rank-and-file Republicans.

The Republican Meltdown in the House

A Republican uprising has led to the ouster of a House speaker for the first time in American history.

  • Institutions Under Stress: Kevin McCarthy’s stunning removal as House speaker comes as Donald Trump is on trial and another government shutdown looms. Is American democracy veering out of control?

  • Electing a New Speaker: House Republicans nominated Representative Jim Jordan of Ohio, the hard-right chairman of the Judiciary Committee, to be their next speaker. But scores of Republicans have refused to back him.

  • A Key Issue: The Republican shift on Capitol Hill has highlighted how G.O.P. support for continued aid for Ukraine has decreased, and how opposition to helping Kyiv has become a litmus test for the right.

  • Matt Gaetz: The fourth-term Republican congressman led the push to oust McCarthy. His success has ratcheted up speculation that he already has his eye on his next target: the Florida governor’s mansion.

  • Biden Keeps His Distance: President Biden has chosen not to weigh in forcefully on the situation in the House, trying to contrast his own achievements with the chaos of his rivals instead.
