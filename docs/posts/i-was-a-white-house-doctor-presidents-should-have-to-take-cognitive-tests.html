I Was a White House Doctor. Presidents Should Have to Take Cognitive Tests.

Guest Essay

July 21, 2024, 9:00 a.m. ET

Jeffrey Kuhlman

Dr. Kuhlman is a former White House physician.

The job of president is physically and mentally demanding. I witnessed this firsthand as a White House physician for three presidents, including as the designated physician to the president for Barack Obama during his first term. My presidential patients often worked 12-hour days seven days a week. The leader of the free world travels constantly, and participates in or leads briefings in which he must retain huge amounts of information.

Health scares can happen at any moment. My role as White House physician was to keep the president healthy and performing optimally, and to provide the public with a candid medical assessment of his ability to carry out the duties of his office.

I participated in tabletop exercises in the Situation Room to go over how to follow Section 3 of the 25th Amendment, which deals with succession in the event the president is disabled or incapacitated. Typically, the 25th Amendment came into play when a president was going under general anesthesia for a colonoscopy or scheduled surgical procedure.

It is widely assumed that the physician to the president will gather and provide pertinent medical information to those contemplating whether the amendment needs to be invoked. This is not stipulated, but most in the medical community agree that the appropriate role for a physician is to offer a medical opinion, based on facts, that is then weighed by the patient — in this case the president — and those around him.

The debates around the fitness of Joe Biden and Donald Trump in the last several weeks have created new pressure to start having serious conversations about exactly how the White House medical team should evaluate presidents and determine their fitness for duty — cognitively as well as physically. This has been the subject of decades of discussion within the White House medical team as well as with the broader medical community.

Many Americans may want the White House medical team to take a more active role in declaring the president fit for duty. Many would probably like to see the same standard apply to candidates running for president as well. For those things to happen, these medical teams will need access to more data about these individuals than they now collect. And perhaps even more important, we should seriously consider the need for an age limit for those running for president, given the high stakes of the office and the realities of cognitive decline with aging.

During my time at the White House, I observed Bill Clinton, George W. Bush and Mr. Obama fulfill the duties of the office in their 50s, plus or minus a few years, in good physical health. Many presidents have had physical impairments, some to the point of serious disability. But while nearly all physical disabilities can be accommodated, cognitive impairments are a different issue. A president must be in mental shape to evaluate a complex situation, form a plan, consider the alternatives and possible consequences to a decision, and then provide definitive directives in a timely manner.

History is rife with examples of situations requiring such mental acuity: responding to the attacks on Sept. 11, tracking down the Taliban in Afghanistan, invading Iraq and taking out Osama bin Laden. The choices are ultimately the president’s to make, at times with tight deadlines and limited information.

This is why I believe that all candidates running for the office of president must agree to undergo a comprehensive neurocognitive assessment that follows critical decision-making guidelines, performed as a base line and periodically repeated. Currently, these tests are run only if the president’s physician specifically asks for them. Moving forward, the White House medical team should regularly administer a detailed battery of tests, rather than a mere screening exam. The review should be four to eight hours with components recommended by the American Academy of Neurology, and assess memory, language and problem-solving skills and other cognitive abilities. There should also be testing to ensure the individual is not taking controlled substances or exhibiting clinical effects of alcoholism. If objective evidence of dysfunction were found to exist, then further investigation with imaging studies by involved medical specialists should follow.

It’s critical that this testing be conducted regularly. It’s hard for experts to know if an episode of confusion, a memory lapse or verbal stumbling reflects or foreshadows mental decline, even for patients seen regularly. Fatigue, medications and transient events can confound any data point. Evaluation over weeks, months or years is far more valuable than any quick screening.

Mr. Bush reportedly said recently of Mr. Biden and Mr. Trump, “I’m younger than both of the candidates, and I’m too old.” It’s indeed a fact of biology that Mr. Biden and Mr. Trump cannot be as sharp as their younger selves. Age affects everyone differently, but past age 60, the average person’s cognitive decline accelerates.

Many cognitive abilities decrease with age

While we retain much of our vocabulary as we get older, cognitive abilities such as speed and reasoning tend to decline more rapidly after age 60.

The federal Age Discrimination in Employment Act forbids mandatory retirement before age 70 for many jobs, but it does allow exceptions for public safety, such as for F.B.I. agents and airline pilots. Given the physical and mental rigors of the presidency, shouldn’t we have an age limit as well on this position — the ultimate public safety position? Such a limit may mean that some sharp older people cannot hold the highest office in the land. But it may be worth that trade-off to avoid the debate we’re having about Mr. Biden and Mr. Trump.

I’m a doctor, but also an American. Our political parties need to step up and ensure we have candidates who, by every objective measure, are qualified to make the daily, critical decisions that significantly affect our lives, liberty and pursuit of happiness.

Jeffrey Kuhlman, who was the White House medical unit director for George W. Bush and the physician to the president for Barack Obama, is the author of the forthcoming book “Transforming Presidential Healthcare: Ensuring Comprehensive Care for the Commander in Chief Amid 21st Century Threats.”
