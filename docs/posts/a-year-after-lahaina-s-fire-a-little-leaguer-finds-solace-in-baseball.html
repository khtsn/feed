A Year After Lahaina’s Fire, A Little Leaguer Finds Solace in Baseball

A team from Maui is now steps away from the Little League World Series, including one player whose family lost almost everything.

By Tim Arango

Tim Arango reported from San Bernardino, Calif., and spent weeks on Maui reporting on the wildfire in Lahaina last year. He also played in the 1987 East Region tournament for Essex Little League, the Vermont state champion.

Aug. 7, 2024Updated 6:47 p.m. ET

In 2018, when a wildfire scorched the hillsides around Lahaina on the Hawaiian island of Maui, Kolten Magno was 6, and the experience left him with anxiety for years.

“Every siren he heard after that just triggered it,” his mother, Kim Ann Magno, said.

Then, last summer, a far worse disaster struck — a fast-moving inferno that incinerated much of Lahaina and killed 102 people. Kolten and his family escaped with their lives, driving out of the fire zone in a blizzard of smoke and flying embers. His home was destroyed, and his family moved into a resort where his father had worked for many years as a painter.

This time, his parents said, baseball has saved him.

“We knew we had to do something with Kolten,” Ms. Magno, whose uncle died in the fire, said. “We just had to keep him playing.”

There Kolten was on Tuesday afternoon, playing second base and looking a little like Pete Rose in the batter’s box, hunched over and showing bunt in the hopes of distracting the pitcher.

This week, as their community gathers back home to observe the one-year anniversary of the deadliest wildfire in America in more than a century, Kolten and his team of all-stars are in San Bernardino, Calif., playing baseball, with a trip to the Little League World Series on the line.

If nothing else, the team’s string of success this summer has represented a return to normalcy, a burst of joy for a still-grieving community — showing, parents said, the power of sports to inspire, and heal, after a long period of grief.

“We’re fighting for the title for them,” said Curstyn Pacheco, the team mother whose son, Gauge, started in right field and took a turn on the mound on Tuesday. “Not just for us but an entire island.”

It was something Kolten’s father, Dalton, experienced last fall as a coach for the Lahainaluna High School football team when the first game after the disaster attracted a sellout crowd of about 5,000 to Maui’s War Memorial Stadium. “Seeing the stands just full of people, it was so amazing for the community to support us like that,” he said.

The Magnos were sitting in the stands Tuesday down the right-field line, where they had hung a “Lahaina Strong” flag and reflected on their journey over the last year. For many months, they had lived in the Royal Lahaina Resort but were “kicked out about a month ago,” Ms. Magno said.

Now, they are living in her mother’s house with the families of her brother and sister, who also lost their homes. All told, 15 of them are living in a three-bedroom house that was among those that survived the fire.

Early on, with the future of Lahaina’s Little League uncertain, Ms. Magno asked people in the league on the other side of the island if they would accept Kolten. They did, and he often took a bus that was ferrying displaced children across the way for school or sports.

For Kolten, that meant making new friends, and adjusting to a new life. “They have just been super supportive,” Ms. Magno said. “They’ll have him sleep over for a week just to go to practice with them every day.”

The tragedy affected his new teammates and their families as well, even if they did not suffer the destruction that occurred in Lahaina. Ms. Pacheco, the team mother, is a health care worker who quickly jumped into action after the fire by bringing medications to people living in emergency shelters, filling a gap when government agencies struggled to deliver aid to West Maui. Her husband, a firefighter, was soon in Lahaina doing 36-hour shifts.

Kolten is the only one on the Central East Maui Little League team from Lahaina, but Ms. Pacheco said that his teammates know all about what he has been through and are “playing for him.”

Hawaii has long been a Little League powerhouse, with teams that not only have dominated the West Region but have won the world championship four times in the last 20 years, a remarkable feat for a state that ranks 40th in population.

To reach the Little League World Series in Williamsport, Pa., teams from the 50 states must first win tournaments at the local and state levels and then beat teams from other states in U.S. regional tournaments; California and Texas each get two teams because they are so populous.

To reach Williamsport — the dream of many youth baseball stars — Central East Maui must win this week’s tournament in Southern California.

At Al Houghton Stadium, built in 1969 and decked out in the familiar red, white and blue bunting that decorates major league ballparks in October, the Maui team won its first game against a Land Park Pacific squad from Sacramento. Then, on Tuesday, it lost to a formidable opponent from Eastvale, Calif., not far from San Bernardino.

The final, 11-3, suggested a blowout, but Hawaii was leading in the fourth inning — Little League games are six-inning affairs — and the overall feeling after the game was that the Hawaiian boys just had an off day at the plate.

Daniel Bolduc, the manager and an auto mechanic on Maui, said that as coaches, “we can teach but ultimately we’re along for the ride with them. They have to do it themselves.”

He said the pressure of the moment — the raucous and exuberant Southern California cheering section and the presence of ESPN, which is broadcasting the games — may have been a factor.

“For me, it’s the atmosphere,” he said. “It plays a lot on the 12-year-old brain.”

If the Maui team can beat another tough squad from Litchfield Park, Ariz., on Wednesday night, it will earn a rematch with Eastvale. Whoever wins the tournament will head to Williamsport on a private jet, along with the winners of the Northwest and Mountain regions who are also playing their championship tournaments in San Bernardino.

After Tuesday’s game, played in heat that soared past 100 degrees, Kolten sat in an air-conditioned room in the baseball complex that Little League officials had set up for players to meet with reporters. He had just come from a postgame meeting with his teammates where his coach’s message was, he said, “We’ve just got to battle back and not lose.”

Kolten, who roots for the New York Yankees and whose favorite player is Aaron Judge, said the team just had to find a way to “wake up the bats.”

Mr. Bolduc said he did not see a higher purpose to the season and did not want to speak about last year’s tragedy. After all, it’s just baseball.

“I don’t want to say we’re playing for anybody,” he said. He has told his players to, “just be in the moment.”
