Given weeks instead of years to cultivate donors, the vice president’s allies are armed with a new logo and burning up the phones.

Theodore Schleifer

Reporting from Washington

  • July 25, 2024, 12:54 p.m. ET

The high-dollar fund-raising world is whirring to life for Vice President Kamala Harris after weeks in which Democratic donors were dejected, demoralized and utterly battered.

Since the announcement of her presidential campaign, Ms. Harris has not only raked in $130 million primarily from small donors, but also gathered big check after big check from billionaires and millionaires as they stockpile money into the newly renamed Harris Victory Fund. Her fund-raisers, armed with a new Harris logo, went to work.

Major fund-raisers — one of whom told President Biden’s campaign just days ago that he thought the campaign could count on only about 25 percent of its allied donors to support Mr. Biden — are now swamped with a flood of interest from donors.

Ms. Harris has no time to waste, having taken over the top of the Democratic ticket with just over 100 days to go and without the years of lead time that Mr. Biden had to cultivate major donors, soothe them and coax them to hand over their money.

Harris campaign officials spent Tuesday and Wednesday energizing their supporters from Beverly Hills to the Hamptons.

On Wednesday, about 40 high-powered finance and Big Law executives joined a Zoom call organized by the superlawyer Brad Karp, the former New York City mayoral candidate Ray McGuire and a few others to discuss how Wall Street could best support a Harris ticket. Speakers included Rufus Gifford, the campaign’s finance chair, and donors inquired about Ms. Harris’s position on Israel, her posture on the economy and her selection of a running mate, according to two people on the call.

In Hollywood, Harris campaign officials joined calls on Tuesday and Wednesday with people from across the creative and entertainment industry to talk about fund-raising and organize celebrities on behalf of the Harris campaign, according to two people with knowledge of the outreach.

“Donors are calling asking how and where can I contribute to Harris for President,” said Andy Spahn, a top fund-raiser in Hollywood. He added that there was “tremendous excitement and enthusiasm for Kamala’s candidacy here.”

Farther north in California, in Silicon Valley, venture capitalists were circulating letters that they planned to publicly release in which they would announce their support for Ms. Harris, in part to combat the narrative that their industry is tilting rightward, according to two people with knowledge of the letters. Harris campaign officials in phone calls this week have asked tech leaders for help organizing their industry’s support, the people said.

Steve Spinner, who was a top Democratic fund-raiser in the tech industry for Barack Obama’s campaign in 2008, said there was something that was “beautiful about Sunday and Monday” that had reminded him of that year.

“It was the first time we’ve had an Obama-like moment,” he said of Ms. Harris’s swift ascent after Mr. Biden dropped out of the race, “a feeling where it was pure and it was good, and people were doing things bigger than themselves.”

A Silicon Valley fund-raising swing for the campaign is in the works, according to three people familiar with the planning. There is significant demand from within the tech industry to host one of the events, one of the people said, and one person expected to be involved is Reid Hoffman, the LinkedIn co-founder and Democratic megadonor.

But the tight calendar means that Ms. Harris may not have the time to engage in an aggressive high-dollar fund-raising operation, traveling from coast to coast and midsize city to midsize city. Instead, she may hope to be bolstered primarily by small-dollar donors and a few virtual events sprinkled throughout her travel to battleground states.

Surrogates could fill in the gaps: Doug Emhoff, now vying to become the first gentleman, has taken over a series of events in Cape Cod summer haunts like Martha’s Vineyard and Nantucket, according to copies of the invitations, which also mentioned a gathering that would feature a “conversation” between him and David Letterman. The events were originally going to be headlined by Jill Biden.

In mid-August, the playwright David Henry Hwang will put on a performance of his musical “Soft Power,” which will serve as a Harris fund-raiser in suburban Washington, and Senator Raphael Warnock of Georgia is scheduled to attend another event on Martha’s Vineyard.

Ms. Harris’s running mate might have to play a big role in fund-raising. Some donors are beginning to kick the tires early: Business leaders who are part of a group called the Leadership Now Project have invited four vice-presidential contenders — Governors Andy Beshear of Kentucky, Josh Shapiro of Pennsylvania and Roy Cooper of North Carolina, as well as Senator Mark Kelly of Arizona — to introductory sessions with their members over the next few days, according to a person with knowledge of the schedule.

In recent days, several Democratic fund-raisers connected to Ms. Harris, such as Stephanie Daily Smith, who has long raised money for her in Southern California, have started re-engaging with their networks to bring prominent supporters of the vice president — not all of whom were involved with the Biden campaign — into the fold.

Mr. Biden’s existing fund-raising leadership team, including Mr. Gifford, is expected to remain in place, according to two people close to the team, although allies of Ms. Harris like Ms. Daily Smith will most likely rise in prominence. Members of Mr. Biden’s team sticking around include Jeffrey Katzenberg, the Hollywood mogul, who helped lead fund-raising for the campaign as one of its co-chairs. Mr. Katzenberg, who was quiet publicly as the Biden campaign deteriorated, broke his silence on Wednesday in a New York Times guest essay that saluted both Mr. Biden and Ms. Harris.

Big donors are important not only to the campaign but also to the constellation of outside groups that are able to accept checks of unlimited size. Indeed, seven- and eight-figure donations are again flowing into super PACs like Future Forward, a Democratic big-money group that was beleaguered by donor anxiety after Mr. Biden’s catastrophic debate but has since rebounded to the tune of $150 million.

Major Democratic donors say privately that they do not anticipate any significant changes to the outside groups supporting Ms. Harris.

But one group that is poised to play a more prominent role than it would have under Mr. Biden is Emily’s List, which has fiercely defended Ms. Harris and runs a super PAC that supports female candidates who back abortion rights.

The super PAC said this week that it would spend at least $20 million to help Ms. Harris, beginning with new ads this week. Stephanie Schriock, a former longtime president of Emily’s List, is close with several major female donors and Ms. Harris. Ms. Schriock has been among the names floated by Harris allies as a possible addition to the campaign.

Time is of the essence. Preserve America, a conservative super PAC funded primarily by the casino mogul Miriam Adelson, is about to begin a television ad campaign around the time of the Olympics, which will start on Friday in Paris. It is planning to air $41 million in ads to help Donald J. Trump’s campaign through the end of August.

MAGA Inc., another pro-Trump super PAC, will air $21 million worth of ads, too, during July and August. Over that two-month period, allied Democrats are currently scheduled to have nearly $1 million booked in advertising, according to the media-tracking service AdImpact.

Once Ms. Harris and her donors make it to September, they will probably be in better shape: Future Forward and its affiliated nonprofit group have about $133 million in bookings reserved in that month and November.

Reporting was contributed by Nicole Sperling, Rob Copeland and Sharon LaFraniere.
