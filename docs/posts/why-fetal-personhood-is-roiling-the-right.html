An Alabama decision on I.V.F. has put an uncompromising principle on a collision course with political reality.

  • 82

Emily Bazelon

March 3, 2024, 5:02 a.m. ET

In vitro fertilization creates life, in the most literal sense. The procedure offers a chance to make a baby, with eggs that are fertilized and develop into embryos in a lab. I.V.F. has helped countless people have children, including couples struggling with infertility and an increasing number of L.G.B.T.Q. parents. In a sense, it’s the opposite of an abortion, which ends a pregnancy.

But in Alabama about 10 days ago, I.V.F. came to a sudden halt because of a State Supreme Court ruling that achieved a central goal of the anti-abortion movement. By a vote of 8 to 1, the court held that a fertilized egg outside the womb is a person. It said that couples could sue a hospital for the wrongful death of their embryos (which in this case were destroyed when someone dropped a frozen vial of them in a storage room) just as they could for the wrongful death of a child.

The ruling vaulted the question of “fetal personhood” to the center of the debate that has followed the U.S. Supreme Court’s 2022 decision overturning Roe v. Wade. Until now, the post-Roe battle consisted of familiar clashes over how much to restrict access to a single procedure — abortion.

Now, the framework has shifted. The Alabama court decision made clear that this stance on the definition of life can broadly rewrite reproductive rights and send states, and perhaps the country, down unpredictable paths. In response, Alabama legislators, led by Republicans who have opposed abortion, rushed to pass bills last week so that I.V.F. can resume.

Recognition of fetal personhood from the moment of conception has been a longstanding aim of leading conservative theorists. “The Alabama ruling gave the pro-life movement the closest thing in 50 years to what it has been striving for,” said Daniel K. Williams, a historian at Ashland University in Ohio who has written three books about evangelical activism. “The pro-life movement was never just about banning abortion. It was about codifying legal protections for human life at every stage. That is what Dobbs” — the 2022 decision overturning Roe — “did not do. Now they have that in Alabama.”

The anti-abortion movement did not script this moment; the major national groups that dominate its messaging, and bring high-profile legal challenges, did not file the wrongful death lawsuit. But the case follows from their stance against I.V.F. A month before the Supreme Court’s ruling in Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization, a lawyer for Americans United for Life said that I.V.F. is a form of “eugenics,” and the group Students for Life of America called it “damaging and destructive.” It’s not surprising, then, that Students for Life called for supporters to stand with the Alabama Supreme Court, or that a lawyer for the anti-abortion legal group Alliance Defending Freedom called the Alabama ruling a “tremendous victory.”

There is a debate in the anti-abortion tent over fetal personhood and the risk to I.V.F. But it’s not really among activists. It’s between the activists and the politicians they helped elect. Those politicians have reason to worry, in an election year, about preventing would-be parents from fulfilling their dreams by blocking a method that has the support of 85 percent of respondents generally and 78 percent of people who say they are pro-life, according to research by Kellyanne Conway, the Republican pollster and former Trump White House counselor.

Fetal personhood has other implications that depart drastically from many Americans’ settled expectations. It bars embryonic stem cell research and some forms of birth control. It dictates that people can claim tax breaks for “unborn children,” as Georgia allowed after Dobbs.

The politics have resulted in an odd scramble over the Alabama ruling. The National Republican Senatorial Committee told candidates that it was “imperative” to respond by aligning with public support for I.V.F. In Washington, Speaker Mike Johnson applauded the effort in Alabama to pass a law protecting I.V.F., even though he is a co-sponsor of the Life at Conception Act, a bill that in its House version would enshrine fetal personhood in federal law without exempting I.V.F. But in urging the Alabama Legislature to undo the effects of the court ruling, Johnson combined “working to protect life and ensure that I.V.F. treatment is available” into one seamless goal.

A Long-Held Principle

The idea that life begins at conception has the virtue of simplicity. Rooting this principle in law was the unquestioned goal of the modern anti-abortion movement when it began in the mid-1960s, as Williams shows in his book “Defenders of the Unborn.” In 1973, a week after the Supreme Court said in Roe v. Wade that the Constitution protects abortion rights, Republicans in Congress proposed a Human Life Amendment, which adopted the Constitution’s provisions for equal protection and due process of law for “any human being, from the moment of conception.” Introduced repeatedly over the next decade, the amendment was a priority for the movement and gained the endorsement of Ronald Reagan when he ran for president in 1980.

Soon after the first baby was born through I.V.F. in 1978, abortion opponents protested the opening of a clinic that planned to perform the procedure in Virginia. The Roman Catholic Church was opposed for reasons that the Vatican laid out in 1987, including the freezing and destruction of embryos and interference with marital sex for procreation.

But the church’s objections could not compete with the power of I.V.F. to address infertility. Evangelicals were mixed on its morality, and secular people embraced it with few qualms. Today, the procedure accounts for 2 percent of births nationally.

As I.V.F. grew in popularity, so did the concerns of its opponents. Standard practice involves creating multiple embryos, which are screened for genetic abnormalities, and the ones that appear healthiest can be transferred. Extra embryos are often frozen; by one count, there are a million and a half frozen embryos in the United States. After a designated time period, they may be donated to science or destroyed, just as the Catholic Church feared.

The anti-abortion movement won a partial victory for protecting life at conception in 2001, when President George W. Bush banned the use of federal funds for embryonic stem cell research, but President Barack Obama reversed the policy eight years later.

Starting in the late 2000s, voters rejected ballot initiatives to enshrine fetal personhood in at least five states. Voters in deep-red Mississippi looked likely to pass a personhood measure in 2011. But in the weeks before the election, doctors and abortion rights groups warned of the threat to I.V.F. and birth control, and the initiative failed, 58 percent to 42 percent.

In criminal law, however, fetal personhood became entrenched. In 1986, Minnesota passed a law that treated the death of a fetus as a homicide in some circumstances. More than 30 states now “give full recognition to unborn victims of violence,” in the words of the National Right to Life Committee, by applying fetal homicide laws at any point of development in utero. Some states have similarly extended child abuse laws to cover a fetus. Hundreds of women have been prosecuted based on these statutes, often for using drugs during pregnancy, or, in a few cases, after they miscarried.

Politically speaking, it is far easier to crack down on these women, who may struggle with poverty or addiction, than to target the often middle-class and affluent couples who turn to I.V.F. (The procedure costs between $12,000 and $30,000.) The spokespeople for I.V.F. include former Vice President Mike Pence, an evangelical Christian who opposes abortion. Pence and his wife, Karen, used I.V.F., he revealed in 2022. Fertility treatments “deserve the protection of the law,” he said then. “They gave us great comfort in those long and challenging years that we struggled with infertility in our marriage.”

‘Fetal Personhood 2.0’

After Mississippi voters rejected the 2011 fetal personhood initiative, abortion opponents changed their political strategy. They campaigned for six-week abortion bans, which did not threaten I.V.F., and which several states passed, as symbolic measures, before the end of Roe.

During the Trump administration, the anti-abortion movement increasingly focused on the federal courts. Conservative judges, unlike politicians, would not have to answer to voters. In 2017, the year the first Trump nominee, Justice Neil Gorsuch, was confirmed to the Supreme Court, conservative scholars made a new push to argue that “preborn human beings are legal ‘persons’ within the original meaning of the 14th Amendment,” as Joshua Craddock, then a Harvard law student, wrote in a law review article. The legal philosopher John Finnis made the originalist argument for fetal personhood in a 2021 essay in First Things, a journal about religion. And in Dobbs, Finnis and the Princeton professor Robert George made the case directly to the Supreme Court in an amicus brief.

“This is fetal personhood 2.0,” said Mary Ziegler, a law professor at the University of California, Davis who is writing a book on the subject. “It’s the argument the anti-abortion movement is making because the courts are their main audience. It doesn’t work to say to a voter, ‘You can’t have a biological child with I.V.F. because people at the time of the founding thought personhood applied at fertilization.’ But conservative judges are self-professed originalists.” Indeed, in Dobbs, Justice Samuel Alito’s majority opinion relied in part, correctly or not, on the history of abortion bans in the 19th century to overturn Roe.

The Alabama Supreme Court cited Dobbs while largely basing its ruling last month on state law. The justices focused on an Alabama statute, passed in 1872, that allows parents to sue for damages for the wrongful death of a “minor child.” In 2011 and 2012, the court decided two cases that interpreted “minor child” to apply to any “unborn child,” regardless of stage of development. The question before them now, the justices said, was whether to make an “unwritten exception” for “extrauterine children.” The answer was no. Responding to concerns about the future of I.V.F. in the state, they said it was not their role “to craft a new limitation based on our own view of what is or is not wise public policy.”

But Americans have never lived with the Alabama court’s version of fetal personhood, so the court opened the door to an uncertain future. Clinics stopped offering I.V.F. for fear that the destruction of any embryo could lead to a lawsuit or even criminal prosecution. If an embryo has all the rights of a person, then in theory, destroying it could be murder.

These are the possibilities that some Alabama lawmakers rushed to head off. That’s the thing about philosophical purity: In practice, it’s often untenable.

“A lot of conservatives didn’t necessarily foresee this happening,” Williams said. “But there were plenty of signs that it would along the way.”
