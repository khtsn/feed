A Pioneering New York Rabbi Says Goodbye to Her L.G.B.T.Q. Congregation

Rabbi Sharon Kleinbaum, the senior rabbi at Congregation Beit Simchat Torah in Manhattan, is retiring at a challenging time for the gay rights movement.

Three decades later, the intense weekslong controversy has largely faded from memory. A New York rabbi had asked permission for her congregation to march in the city’s annual Salute to Israel Parade up Fifth Avenue. But the rabbi was Sharon Kleinbaum, the new leader of a predominantly gay synagogue. Officials from various Orthodox Jewish organizations were threatening to boycott the event, and the rabbi was getting hate mail.

The rabbi brokered a deal with parade organizers, but it fell apart. So she came up with a Plan B.

The congregation was not allowed to participate in the 1993 parade, but it did hold a separate tribute to Israel, which drew grateful members of the city’s political class who had sought to somehow accommodate all sides in the controversy. It was not until 2012 that the congregation was allowed to march.

“I never let it upset me,” Rabbi Kleinbaum said. “I didn’t need them to tell me that I, or our community, are worthwhile. My job, I felt, was to listen and help people see that their words and actions were coming from a place of fear and anxiety and that hate wasn’t the only response to have.”

In the years since, Rabbi Kleinbaum’s synagogue, Congregation Beit Simchat Torah in Midtown Manhattan, has grown to become the largest L.G.B.T.Q. congregation in the country. And she has become a national leader in the civil rights movement for L.G.B.T.Q. Americans and their fight for acceptance within Judaism.

Rabbi Kleinbaum, 64, is retiring this summer after a career that put her at the forefront of social change in New York and beyond. Her progressive vision for Judaism was embraced by her congregation, and she became a confidante to liberal political leaders.

The climate for gay Jews has improved dramatically in the United States, said Rabbi Ayelet Cohen, the dean of the rabbinical school at the Jewish Theological Seminary. And she gives much credit to Rabbi Kleinbaum’s work.

“She made this possible by insisting on claiming a religious Jewish identity and a proud L.G.B.T.Q. identity within the tent of organized Jewish life,” said Rabbi Cohen. “And she has mentored so many emerging leaders.”

When she was first hired, however, Rabbi Kleinbaum’s very presence was seen by some as a provocation. When word got out that a lesbian rabbi was leading a gay synagogue, she said, she received menacing letters and phone calls. In 1993, a death threat led to a police presence inside and outside the synagogue during Sukkot, the Jewish harvest celebration.

Even within her congregation, some members initially objected to having a woman as their spiritual leader.

Congregation Beit Simchat Torah began in 1973 when a small group of gay men gathered for Shabbat services in a children’s classroom in the annex of the Church of the Holy Apostles in Chelsea. Over the next two decades women joined the congregation and lay people led the services. Rabbi Kleinbaum was the synagogue’s first spiritual leader and is retiring as its senior rabbi.

Her early days were filled with visiting AIDS patients, comforting mourners and burying young men from her own generation.

“My community was under attack by a virus, neglected by the government and hated by the society,” she said. “I understood the power of prayer, of creating community and of being present.”

After the presidential election of Donald J. Trump, who had campaigned against allowing people from certain Muslim countries into the United States, Rabbi Kleinbaum made a point of reaching out to Muslims in New York. She and some of her congregants stood in front of the Islamic Center at New York University handing out flowers and holding up signs reading “Jewish New Yorkers support our Muslim friends” and “Love your neighbor as yourself.” They returned every Friday after the inauguration until the coronavirus pandemic began in 2020.

The congregation also built a clinic to help immigrants receive asylum in the United States.

“She is really a rabbi’s rabbi,” said Rabbi Sharon Cohen Anisfeld, president of Hebrew College in Newton, Mass. “She’s so highly regarded across a lot of divides and brings together the pastoral and the political seamlessly while drawing from a very deep well of Jewish learning.”

Unlike at the start of her career, Rabbi Kleinbaum now performs gay weddings that are legally sanctioned and celebrates b’nai mitzvah of children born to openly gay parents. L.G.B.T.Q. rabbis are ordained by all liberal Jewish movements, and many congregations, including some that are Orthodox, openly welcome L.G.B.T.Q. Jews.

The synagogue, now located on West 30th Street, has 950 member households. Its free annual Yom Kippur services, held at the Javits Convention Center, attracts several thousand people. The congregation’s internship program has trained 50 rabbis and seven cantors.

And yet Rabbi Kleinbaum is retiring at a challenging moment for the L.G.B.T.Q. rights movement, especially for transgender people. Her retirement also comes at a fraught moment for American Jews, who are watching the war in Gaza from a remove of 5,000 miles.

Rabbi Kleinbaum receives alerts on her phone every time a missile enters Israel’s airspace and often joins a weekly vigil in Union Square organized by Israeli Jews calling for the return of the hostages, a cease-fire between Israel and Hamas and humanitarian aid for the Palestinians in Gaza. She has been called “anti-Zionist” by supporters of Israel and “Rabbi Genocide” by the country’s critics in the same week, she said.

Last month, while marching alongside her wife, Randi Weingarten, the president of the American Federation of Teachers, and Gov. Kathy Hochul in the Pride Parade in Manhattan, Rabbi Kleinbaum spotted some pro-Palestinian protesters.

She stepped away from the governor’s contingent, reached into her pocket and unfolded a handwritten sign that said, “This Rabbi loves Palestinians and Israeli Jews.”

In late June, Rabbi Kleinbaum wrapped her prayer shawl over her head, whispered a blessing in Hebrew and walked to the front of her congregation’s sanctuary, which was enveloped by giant rainbow banners. As she began to lead one of her last services as senior rabbi, she was overcome with feelings of “inconsolable sadness and ecstatic joy,” she said.

As it came time to sing “Lekha Dodi,” a 16th-century mystical poem welcoming the “Sabbath bride,” she exhorted her congregants to sing from the heart.

“Everybody has it hard,” she said. “But we can model what it means to find joy even in the midst of hardship. Even in the midst of despair, even in the midst of fear and anxiety. And we can create a different future.”

“Joy,” she told the gathering, “is an act of spiritual and political resistance.”
