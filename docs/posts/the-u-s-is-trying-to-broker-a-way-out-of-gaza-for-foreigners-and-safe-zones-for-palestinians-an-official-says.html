Frightened Palestinians packed belongings and left their homes in northern Gaza on Friday after Israel’s military demanded that more than a million civilians move to the south of the blockaded coastal strip, a possible precursor to a ground invasion but one that the United Nations warned could be calamitous.

Israel’s buildup of soldiers near the border with Gaza has fueled speculation that it is preparing to invade the Hamas-held territory in response to last weekend’s incursion that killed more than 1,300 people. Israel last sent troops into the enclave in 2014.

But many Palestinians were reluctant to leave their homes for southern Gaza, which has even fewer resources, and routes to get there have been damaged by a week of Israeli strikes. Hamas officials urged Palestinians not to comply with what they called Israel’s “psychological warfare.”

Israel’s retaliatory airstrikes since Saturday, deadlier and more widespread than in its past campaigns in Gaza, have wiped out entire neighborhoods, brought the medical system to the brink of collapse and forced some 400,000 people into temporary shelters as they face dire shortages of food, water and fuel. Gaza’s health ministry said that 1,799 Palestinians, including 583 children, had been killed since Saturday, and that 7,388 people had been injured.

The United Nations pleaded for Israel to rescind the demand for a forced relocation out of fear of a humanitarian disaster. The Israeli military said on Friday morning that there was no firm deadline for people to leave the north and acknowledged that it “will take time.”

The U.S. government is talking with Israeli and Egyptian officials about getting safe passage for American citizens, including Palestinian Americans, and other foreigners out of Gaza through the Rafah border crossing with Egypt, but are not discussing the possibility of getting Palestinians out, a senior State Department official said on Friday.

The official said the United States is instead talking with the International Committee of the Red Cross and United Nations relief agencies about setting up “safe zones” for Palestinians inside Gaza. Israeli officials support that idea, the American official said. In theory, he said, Israel would not attack people in the safe zones.

The comments appeared to sharply narrow the idea of a “safe passage” that Secretary of State Antony J. Blinken mentioned at a news conference in Tel Aviv on Thursday, when he implied that if such a route were to be negotiated with Israel and Egypt, any civilian would be able to use it.

“We also talked about possibilities for safe passage for civilians who want to leave or get out of the way on Gaza, and that’s a conversation and discussion that we will pursue in the coming days in some of the countries that we’ll be visiting,” Mr. Blinken said on Thursday when describing his discussions earlier that day with Israeli officials in Tel Aviv.

The State Department official spoke on Friday in a briefing with reporters flying with Mr. Blinken from Jordan to Qatar. He spoke on condition of anonymity to discuss sensitive diplomatic discussions.

The official said that Arab nations in the region do not want Palestinians to leave the enclave, though he did not specify which countries. In the past, analysts have said that Arab governments do not want Palestinians to be forced to leave because it could mean that Israel takes the land. The governments are also reluctant to deal with a flood of refugees in the region.

About two million people live in Gaza. U.S. officials estimate that about 500 to 600 Palestinian Americans are in the narrow seaside strip of land.

There are only two ways out of Gaza: via Israel, which has said it is imposing a “complete siege” on the blockaded strip, and via Egypt, which is adamantly opposed to allowing Gazans to cross the border into its territory for fear it could be sucked deeper into the crisis. On Thursday, responding to growing calls to allow safe passage for civilians fleeing Gaza, President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi of Egypt said Gazans must “stay steadfast and remain on their land.”

Egypt has signaled that it would help to get emergency aid into Gaza, and its Foreign Ministry on Thursday urged countries and aid groups to send shipments to the international airport at El Arish, in Egypt’s North Sinai region, 35 miles from the border. But it has not shown any sign of opening the border crossing to fleeing Palestinians.

When asked whether the U.S. supported Israel’s call for one million Palestinians to leave northern Gaza, the American official avoided answering the question.

The U.S. secretary of defense, Lloyd J. Austin III, said on Friday that American security assistance “is already rapidly flowing into Israel” as he met with the Israeli defense minister in Tel Aviv.

Mr. Austin said the supplies included ammunition and interceptors for Israel’s Iron Dome missile defense system. Hamas assailants appeared to overwhelm the air defense system at the opening of its devastating surprise incursion last weekend, firing hundreds of rockets from Gaza in rapid succession, potentially more than the system’s interceptors could handle.

“We stand together and we stand strong,” Mr. Austin said, in a strong statement of support for Israel. “The U.S. has Israel’s back and that is not negotiable and never will be.”

Israel’s defense minister, Yoav Gallant, said that Israel was fighting “a war for freedom” and for “our common values.” He compared the atrocities committed by Hamas in its assault over the weekend — “murder, rape, kidnapping” — to those of the Islamic State, as other Israeli officials have also done.

“This is a war for the existence of Israel as a prosperous state, a democratic state, as the homeland of the Jewish people,” he said.

Echoing those comments, Mr. Austin said that the “deliberate cruelty” of the Hamas attacks “vividly reminds me of ISIS.”

The Pentagon chief said he had come to Israel to demonstrate “ironclad” U.S. support for Israel, and to emphasize that the Biden administration would continue to “coordinate closely” with Israel to help secure the release of the estimated 150 people, including Americans, believed to be held hostage by Hamas after incursion.

Relating indirectly to the fears that Israel’s retaliatory strikes in Gaza would exact a huge toll, Mr. Austin said, “I’ve worked with Israeli forces for many years.”

“They are professional, disciplined, and focused on the right things,” he added.

Mr. Austin described the international coalition’s fight against forces of the Islamic State who, he said, were in some cases embedded deeply in built-up areas, in an analogy to Hamas in Gaza. That coalition, he said, “fought valiantly, protected civilians, and created corridors for humanitarian movement, even in the midst of a pretty significant fight.” Referring to the Israeli military, he said, “It is well-led, and I have every expectation that it will be disciplined.”

“As the Israelis prosecute this fight we will learn lessons from them,” he added.

Mr. Austin said that the message the United States — which has deployed an aircraft carrier strike group to the eastern Mediterranean — was sending to any government or armed group in the Middle East thinking of taking advantage of the unrest was simple.

“Don’t,” he said.

Last weekend’s attack on Israel by Hamas has brought renewed scrutiny of the armed Palestinian group’s longstanding relationship with Iran, and questions about whether the Gaza-based group could have pulled off such a sophisticated and devastating operation on its own.

Iran has a long history of training and arming proxy militia groups in the region, from Gaza to Lebanon, Iraq and Syria. It supports Hamas militarily and has helped it design and produce a domestic missile and rocket system to match the capabilities and material available in Gaza — an impoverished, densely populated coastal strip that has been blockaded by Israel and Egypt for the past 16 years.

And over the past year, there have been signs that Iran and its proxies were preparing to take a more aggressive approach toward Israel.

Gen. Esmail Ghaani, who is in charge of supervising Iran’s network of proxy militias as head of the country’s paramilitary Quds Force, repeatedly traveled to Lebanon for covert sessions with leaders of Hamas and Hezbollah, a Shiite Lebanese militia that Iran also supports.

Over the past year, Mr. Ghaani worked to coordinate and unify all of Iran’s proxies, according to public statements from Iranian analysts and five Iranians familiar with the work of the country’s Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps.

Hassan Nasrallah, the leader of Hezbollah, held an hourslong online meeting in March with an elite group of strategists from all the Iran-backed militias and told them to get ready for a war with Israel with a scope and reach — including a ground invasion — that would mark a new era, according to two participants from Iran and Syria. The participants spoke on the condition of anonymity because they were not authorized to publicly discuss the meeting.

There are conflicting accounts of whether these activities were leading specifically toward last week’s attack by Hamas, which left 1,200 Israelis dead and shattered the country’s sense of security.

Thirteen French citizens have been killed in the attacks and 17 are missing, several of whom are most likely hostages, officials say. At a news conference in Tel Aviv on Thursday, tearful relatives of missing French-Israeli citizens appealed to Mr. Macron for help.

“You have the power to bring them back home,” said Meitav Journo, whose sister, Karin Journo, 24, was at an open-air music festival that was targeted by Hamas.

“We don’t know if she is dead, or if she is in Gaza,” her father, Doron Journo, said. “My daughter didn’t go to fight a war. She went to dance.”

Ido Nagar, an Israeli whose wife, Céline Ben David-Nagar, is missing, said, “Macron should act today just as if the attack had happened in Paris.” He held the couple’s 6-month-old baby in his lap at times during the news conference.

In his address, Mr. Macron promised, without elaborating, that France was doing everything it could to bring the hostages back, and he also appealed for calm in France, where the Israeli-Palestinian conflict has in the past exposed bitter rifts between the country’s Jewish and Muslim communities, which are some of the largest in Europe.

The latest violence has also thrown the French left into turmoil, with the main leftist party in Parliament, France Unbowed, accused of being too tepid in condemning Hamas, including by its own allies.

“Those who conflate the Palestinian cause with the justification of terrorism are making a moral, political and strategic mistake,” Mr. Macron said.

Gérald Darmanin, France’s interior minister, told France Inter radio on Thursday that over 100 antisemitic acts, mainly graffiti or verbal threats, had been reported around the country since Saturday, and that over 20 people had been arrested.

Mr. Darmanin has asked state representatives to ban pro-Palestinian protests “because they are likely to generate public disorder.” But that did little to prevent hundreds of people from gathering on the Place de la République in Paris on Thursday evening, some chanting “We are all Palestinians” or “Israel murderer.” They were cleared out with tear gas and water cannons by riot police officers.

In his address, Mr. Macron vowed to protect France’s Jewish community. The authorities have deployed 10,000 police officers to nearly 600 Jewish sites, including synagogues and schools, and they have tightened security for top politicians of Jewish descent.

Mr. Macron urged the French not to add “national divisions to international ones.”

“It is this shield of unity that will protect us from all excesses, all abuses, all hatreds,” he said.
