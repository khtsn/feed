New York City Failed to Halt Major Overspending on Migrants, Audit Finds

An audit by the city comptroller, Brad Lander, found that a city agency allowed a troubled contractor to bill for unnecessary expenses like unused hotel rooms.

Aug. 6, 2024, 3:00 a.m. ET

Last year, as Mayor Eric Adams sought to relieve the pressure on New York City’s shelter system, he began to send hundreds of asylum seekers to hotels, often in counties north of the city.

But in many cases, the city spent taxpayer funds for rooms that were never used. In Rockland County, the city’s primary migrant-services contractor, DocGo, billed the city $833,340 for 4,902 vacant rooms over 61 nights, according to an audit the city comptroller is set to release on Tuesday.

The audit by the comptroller, Brad Lander, concluded that the Department of Housing Preservation and Development had failed to exercise sufficient oversight of a no-bid $432 million contract given to DocGo, a firm that has come under scrutiny for its handling of migrant services.

The rooms in Rockland County were booked at the Armoni Inn and Suites in Orangeburg, N.Y. At the time, a temporary restraining order had been issued that prohibited the use of the Armoni for migrants from the city. Yet DocGo held the empty rooms and the city paid for them.

DocGo also charged the city $569,500 to rent out the Crowne Plaza JFK Hotel in Queens for 10 nights, even though not a single room was used over that period, auditors said.

City housing officials said they viewed the extra rooms as “planned insurance” to guard against a situation where the demand for emergency housing unexpectedly rose, and that they saw no reason to seek a refund because the steps taken were necessary in the midst of an unprecedented migrant crisis.

Yet investigators concluded that the city agency had allowed DocGo to understaff the hotel shelters, overbill for security and hire dozens of subcontractors without the agency’s properly vetting and approving them as the rules require.

All told, only 20 percent of the invoices reviewed by investigators should have been paid, according to the audit.

“It was a catastrophically poor decision to hire DocGo in the first place,” Mr. Lander said in an interview. “It’s an appalling lack of oversight to pay them when they can’t properly document 80 percent of the expenditures.”

As tens of thousands of migrants began arriving in New York from the southern border, DocGo was awarded separate contracts by different city agencies to assist with processing, feeding and housing the new arrivals. Under its contract with Housing Preservation and Development, DocGo was to care for thousands of migrants at over two dozen hotels in New York City and upstate.

Of the $182 million paid to the company as of mid-June, Mr. Lander is recommending the city initially try to retrieve more than $11 million, after a review of two months of invoices. He said the amount of misspent money could exceed $100 million over the last year if a similar rate of noncompliance is found in unchecked invoices.

Housing officials said in written responses to auditors that they had often given oral approval for services because the task of caring for migrants was urgent enough to obviate the need for a paper trail of authorizations.

They said many of the identified problems had been fixed, and that they did not intend to seek refunds for most of what Mr. Lander called overpayments.

Liz Garcia, a spokeswoman for Mr. Adams, said the city had put “people’s well-being before paperwork,” and given migrants a roof over their heads and beds to sleep in rather than “a subway seat or slab of concrete.”

“The comptroller can nitpick the first two months of an emergency contract over a year after the fact and long after new safeguards were put in place, but he cannot claim to have saved a single migrant family from sleeping on the streets,” Ms. Garcia said.

Mr. Lander, who is challenging Mr. Adams in next year’s Democratic primary, has been a sharp critic of the mayor’s handling of the migrant crisis.

In a written statement, a DocGo spokesman, Thomas Meara, said the company was proud to have “helped the city navigate this complex emergency by rapidly scaling and financing large programs to move more quickly in response to urgent situations,” and that it had received assurance it would be paid for its services.

While the city is phasing out DocGo’s emergency contract with the housing agency, the company is poised to rake in tens of millions of dollars more for migrant services from other contracts, mostly with the city’s public hospital system, according to the comptroller’s office.

In field surveys, Mr. Lander’s auditors found that “most asylum seekers expressed satisfaction with conditions” at the hotels. Their biggest complaint was the food. The New York Times previously reported on staggering food waste and widespread complaints about food quality at the hotels.

Auditors also found that the amount of money DocGo billed for food “far exceeded what the contract provided.” They said that on a single day last year at the Crossroads Hotel in Newburgh, which housed 97 migrants, DocGo charged the city for supplying 1,288 meals at a cost of $3,276, even though it was supposed to supply only 388 meals and receive $987.

City Hall officials disputed the finding, saying DocGo had significantly lowered food costs over time.

The surveys turned up over a dozen instances of bedbugs and four dozen reports of roach infestations at the hotel shelters, mirroring some of the complaints in internal DocGo incident reports obtained by The Times.

A caseworker told of a “bedbug outbreak” last year at the Central Motel Courtyard in White Plains after encountering a mother and child and seeing “bug bites on the child’s arm.”

And at the Ramada Plaza in Albany, a caseworker said that 20 cockroaches had once spilled out of a malfunctioning smoke detector and “rolled over my hand.”

Mr. Meara, the DocGo spokesman, said that pest issues were “common in congregate, multifamily homes and hotels” and “unrelated to cleanliness,” adding, “Once we are made aware of an issue, we act immediately.”
