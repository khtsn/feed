While Hurricane Milton’s path was in flux as it approached Florida, the Gulf Coast city of some 55,000 seemed sure to receive a blow.

Patricia Mazzei

Reporting from Sarasota, Fla.

  • Oct. 9, 2024, 2:25 p.m. ET

A drizzle fell on Bayfront Park and Marina in downtown Sarasota, Fla., on Wednesday morning as Marilyn Borysek walked her dog, Nemo, a Pomeranian-Chihuahua mix, in a stroller.

“I’m just taking pictures, because this probably won’t look the same in 24 hours,” she said, pointing to Sarasota Bay, thought it was hard to tell where the dark, heavy sky ended and the choppy water began.

Behind her stood the gleaming condos of Sarasota, a city of about 55,000 on Florida’s Gulf Coast about 60 miles south of Tampa that has grown dramatically in recent years, attracting wealthier retirees and artsy types drawn to the area’s museums and theaters.

While Hurricane Milton’s path was in flux as it approached the state, the Category 4 storm appeared to be heading toward the area of Sarasota, rattling the city’s newer arrivals as well as its longtime residents. While previous hurricanes — most recently Helene — have caused power losses and damage, none has directly struck Sarasota since 1946, according to a New York Times analysis of hurricane information from a National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration database. And that storm made landfall at Category 1 strength.

Two years ago, when Hurricane Ian veered south toward Fort Myers, “we dodged a bullet,” said Ms. Borysek, 60. This time, she and others feared that Sarasota would not be so lucky.

Ms. Borysek was staying with a friend just a few blocks away. The area was under a mandatory evacuation order, but she said that her friend’s 17-story building on Main Street felt safe, adding that other friends also planned to ride out Milton in downtown high-rises.

One of the people staying in the same building was Dale Parks, an architect who has lived in Sarasota for 40 years (and designed the local bus station). Standing outside on Wednesday morning, Mr. Parks looked at the deserted downtown streets, with businesses boarded up and sandbagged, and said he was nervous. Not about the building — “it’s concrete and high-impact glass,” he said — but about the storm’s aftermath.

“Sarasota is going to be so torn up,” he said. “It’s the first time I’ve seen a storm do this. That’s what’s making me nervous.”

Back at Bayfront Park, Alan Kehrer, 56, and his son, Luke Kehrer, 18, stood quietly staring over the slate-gray bay, getting what the younger man said were “last looks.”

“It’s sentimental,” said his father, who recalled his houseboat being tossed by storm surge in 1985 during Hurricane Elena, which did not come ashore in Florida.

The Kehrers saw Milton’s forecast path on Tuesday night and thought that Sarasota would be spared. But Wednesday morning’s forecast suggested the possibility of a far greater risk to the city.

The Kehrers live inland, outside of the evacuation zone. But their street flooded in August during Hurricane Debby, which made landfall hundreds of miles north in Florida.

“I’m going to try to get some sleep today,” the elder Mr. Kehrer said, anticipating that he would “stay up all night tonight, worrying.”

Annie Correal and Bea Malsky contributed reporting.

Patricia Mazzei is the lead reporter for The Times in Miami, covering Florida and Puerto Rico.
