Hall of Fame: cogu

The former superstar AWPer is the first player from outside Europe to join the hall after a distinguished career crowned by a world championship in 2006.

If you find it inconceivable to picture Counter-Strike without Brazil as a major force, that's because Raphael "⁠cogu⁠" Camargo helped lay the groundwork.

Without the Brazilian former AWPer, there might not have been Gabriel "⁠FalleN⁠" Toledo, the two-time Major-winning Luminosity/SK team, or the current FURIA squad. He helped put an underdeveloped scene on the map during an era of European and North American dominance, and inspired a new generation of talent in South America. Decades later, his MIBR legacy lives on.

But he was more than just the best player on a championship-winning team. He was, in his own right, one of the finest AWPers of the 2000s, an extremely accurate sniper who didn't shy away from the big moments and was also phenomenal with pistols, especially the USP. It's no surprise then that he makes the Hall of Fame's second class after a first year dominated by Swedish talent.

"I always thought that he was a stable player," Danny "⁠fRoD⁠" Montaner, another elite AWPer during the 2000s and himself a Hall of Fame candidate, said of cogu in a 'Reflections' video with Duncan 'Thorin' Shields.

"He used to make a lot of smart decisions for his team. I always knew that he was gonna be someone who was gonna be very methodical in the way that he approached his game. He was not gonna overextend himself. He wasn't really going to let people get many picks on him. We had to strategize around him.

"cogu was a pillar that you had to go around or try to plan everything around to destroy him. He was one of the more heavily anti-stratted guys that I can think of individually."

Like many other players back then, cogu took his first steps in the game at a local cyber café. Counter-Strike was still in its beta stages, and wielding the AWP was like love at first sight for the Brazilian.

"There was a Korean guy who played a lot with the AWP, João, and we would play until late, sometimes it would just be me and him left on the server," cogu tells HLTV. "And then we would have a lot of these AWP duels.

"I think that even back then, in the very early days of the LAN house, I developed a great fondness for the AWP."

A friend introduced cogu to the competitive side of the game, and by 2003, he was already playing for g3x, one of the best teams in Brazil. Later that year, he got his first taste of international Counter-Strike as the team competed at CPL Winter in Dallas, placing top-12. MIBR, who had in Eduardo "⁠eduzin⁠" Chagas their AWPer, came in fifth place.

g3x had lived in MIBR's shadow throughout 2003, losing out on ESWC and WCG spots to their rivals. That changed in 2004. Four months into the year, the two teams squared off in Peru in the grand final of the Latin Cup, with g3x beating MIBR in both the upper bracket final and the grand final to claim the first prize of $5,000.

"I played very well, I think I was named the MVP of the tournament," cogu recalls. "Coming back to Brazil, (MIBR founder and owner) Paulo Velloso called me. 'cogu, come here,' and so on.

"He talked a lot with my mother, and I ended up accepting."

It's difficult to overstate how big a change that was for cogu. With Velloso's financial backing, MIBR provided conditions no Brazilian team could match: apartments overlooking the beach in Rio de Janeiro, good salaries, and bootcamps at the renowned Inferno Online cyber café in Stockholm.

"I think it was the best time in my life," cogu says. "I was living a dream."

In 2005, MIBR won the CPL World Tour stops in Brazil and Chile, yet struggled elsewhere. They failed to qualify for ESWC and posted 17th-24th finishes at CPL Summer and Winter — worse than their 2004 placements at those tournaments.

cogu was unhappy. He didn't see the same type of commitment from his new teammates, whom he felt had succumbed to complacency.

"Paulo Velloso nicknamed the team 'the smoke squad,'" he says, laughing.

"I wanted to be on the best team, to win tournaments, but we couldn't improve. We had stagnated."

cogu was the embodiment of dedication and passion for the game

To make sense of cogu's decision to walk away from a dream contract to jump into the unknown, you need to understand that his greatness came from his competitive drive and obsession with the game. There's a famous anecdote that he would play all night at Inferno Online during bootcamps, having asked staff to lock him inside the building after closing.

"Back in the 1.6 era, I don't think there was anyone who played more than me," he says. "And when I say that, I don't mean quality, I mean time on the server.

"I was a big nerd. Nowadays, that's more common, but if we go back 20, 25 years, there were few, or almost no one, who did it.

"I put Counter-Strike as my number-one priority, over family, friends, girlfriend, even my own health. Oftentimes I would practice even when I was sick with the flu or whatever, because CS came first."

Velloso gave cogu his blessing to rejoin g3x, expecting their paths would cross again eventually. That reunion came within days.

cogu flew back to São Paulo to link up with his new teammates, but as he made it to the cyber café for his first day of practice, Alexandre "⁠gAuLeS⁠" Chiqueta — the player he was meant to replace on the team — showed up with g3x's registration papers and refused to give up his spot.

Renato "⁠nak⁠" Nakano and Bruno "⁠ellllll⁠" Ono left g3x in response, and the trio set about creating a team that could dominate the scene, with Velloso agreeing to support the venture.

They convinced Carlos "⁠KIKOOOO⁠" Henrique Segal to come back from retirement to captain the team. Daniel "rdz" Alves was supposed to be the fifth player, but he couldn't be reached by phone. Since Velloso wanted to announce the roster that day, the final spot went to Lincoln "⁠fnx⁠" Lau, a rising talent at the time.

The team was unveiled as mibr.sp (for São Paulo, where the players were based), joining cogu's former squad—the main MIBR roster—and mibr.rj (for Rio de Janeiro) under the organization's umbrella. Velloso organized an internal tournament to determine which of the three teams would receive the organization's full backing.

"We played every map against every team," cogu recalls. "We didn't lose a single side. We completely thrashed everyone from beginning to end.

"And then when the tournament ended, and since our team won everything and we got the best sponsorship, we went to get the shuttle PCs, those small PCs that you carried with you everywhere, from the guys' [main team's] room.

"Just imagine. The guys had the best sponsorship, the owner's son, and we just walked in and took the PCs."

A fourth-place finish at DreamHack Summer turned heads, but MIBR remained underdogs heading into ESWC 2006 — a massive 44-team tournament with $160,000 up for grabs. Picture a modern Major in terms of the level of the teams, but played entirely in a cutthroat best-of-one format.

ESWC 2006 was the tournament where everything changed for Brazilian CS

MIBR came through the two round-robin group stages almost unscathed, playing out a draw against the Koreans of Lunatic-hai on Nuke and losing a Dust2 game to fnatic. The first two playoff rounds were an emotional roller coaster: after a 16-5 thrashing of Spanish team x6tence, they almost lost to ALTERNATE aTTaX because of a flash bug on Train. After over two hours of deliberation, the referees deemed that the bug had been accidental. MIBR went on to win the game in overtime, 19-16.

The grand final was a rematch against fnatic, this time on Inferno, MIBR's best map. The Brazilians delivered a masterclass performance as they ran out 16-6 winners, with cogu pulling off a spectacular quad-kill in the CT pistol — one of the plays he is most remembered for.

"Teams would go against us, and you know when you're playing FIFA with your friends, and there's a guy who plays so poorly that when you face him, you win by a landslide to guarantee several points?" cogu says. "That's what all the gringos did to us, right? They'd get us and destroy us.

"It was something that changed a lot [after ESWC]. We showed that it wasn't going to be so easy from that moment on."

And then, to everyone's surprise, cogu left the team right after ESWC.

Earlier that year, cogu had met his father for the first time, and the two bonded over their shared love of football. They started recording videos of cogu playing and sending them to his uncle, who coached at a club in Qatar. His uncle liked what he saw and managed to convince the club's board to sign him.

Everything was already arranged by the time ESWC came around. cogu kept it to himself throughout the tournament and only told his teammates and Velloso after it ended.

The football dream didn't last long. Because he hadn't trained in a long time, he was put on twice-daily sessions to regain fitness as quickly as possible. He ended up sustaining a serious injury that effectively ended his chances of a professional career in the sport.

On October 6, less than four months after leaving the team, cogu was back on the roster. He showed no signs of rust as he played the final leg of the season. "When I came back, I was better than I had been before, I think," he says. A few weeks into his return, MIBR won the WCG Pan-American Championship in Mexico by beating a Team 3D side with fRoD as a stand-in.

But the Brazilian team never quite lived up to the hype generated by their ESWC run. In early 2007, Bruno "⁠BIT⁠" Fukuda Lima was brought in to fill the void created by KIKOOOO's retirement. MIBR won shgOpen in Denmark by beating Wiktor "⁠TaZ⁠" Wojtas's Pentagram in the final before placing fourth at ESWC — whose prize pool had been halved compared to 2006 —, second at GameGune and third at the four-team WEG eStars, where Jonas "⁠bsl⁠" Alsaker Vikan stood in for fnx after the Brazilian missed his flight.

Shortly after falling to g3x in the WCG qualifier, the players made the switch to CS:Source, drawn by the financial security that the Championship Gaming Series (CGS) had to offer.

2008 was an uncertain year, with the players bouncing between 1.6 and CS:Source before ultimately settling back on the former following the collapse of the CGS that summer. The roster continued to shift, with cogu eventually becoming the last remaining member of the ESWC-winning lineup still on the team.

With the game's future looking increasingly unclear in 2009, he decided to step away from competitive play entirely before the end of that year. He announced his retirement and enrolled in law school.

"It's tough thinking about regrets in those terms," he says. "I come from a very conservative family. So me doing nothing was unacceptable."

There was a largely fruitless comeback in the early years of CS:GO, when cogu played for teams like ProGaming.TD, INTZ and Gorilla Core. In late 2017, he stood in for Immortals's Vito "⁠kNgV-⁠" Giuseppe in the group stage of ELEAGUE Premier. He also tried his hand at coaching, working with MIBR, O PLANO and 00NATION.

More than three years on from his last competitive project in any role, cogu has fully embraced the streaming and content creation sides of the scene.

Many in today's scene never got to witness cogu at his peak. But FalleN has no hesitation placing him among his country's finest talents. "He was insane in 1.6," he said last year. For the FURIA captain, the AWP spot on Brazil's all-time team comes down to a coin toss between cogu and himself. There are few compliments that carry more weight.

cogu can't help but feel immensely proud that FalleN has carried the torch that he left behind in 2009.

"That side that I was telling you about: crazy, obsessed, super-focused on playing and training," cogu reflects. "I think I definitely ended up inspiring other generations, right?

"We played a few matches against each other back then, but he played with many teammates who played with me. nak, ellllll, fnx. So I believe that many of those players passed on to him the way I did things. My dedication."

How good does he think he would be if he were playing today?

"If I were 15 or 16 years old, maybe it wouldn't be molodoy [in FURIA]," he says, laughing.

"I ended up leaving a legacy that hard training, focus and determination will beat anything.

"CS was my life. It still is. And I'll be forever grateful for everything and everyone who has been part of my life."
