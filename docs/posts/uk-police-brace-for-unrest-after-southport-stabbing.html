By Ali Watkins and Megan Specia

Aug. 4, 2024Updated 4:06 p.m. ET

Anti-immigration rioters attacked a hotel in the town of Rotherham in northern England on Sunday, breaking windows and appearing to set fires at a hotel that has housed asylum seekers, as riots continued in Britain.

The police in cities across the country have braced for continued far-right and anti-immigration protests spurred by a deadly stabbing attack last week in the northern English town of Southport. Disinformation spread rapidly that erroneously claimed the suspect in the knife attack was an immigrant. On Saturday, dozens of people were arrested as demonstrations from Liverpool to Belfast descended into violence.

Sunday saw a new wave of clashes, with groups gathering in Rotherham, Bolton, Hull, Southport, Middlesbrough and other towns and cities scattered across the country that devolved into varying degrees of violence.

In Rotherham, protesters smashed windows and set fires at a Holiday Inn Express before storming into the building as riot police officers struggled to control the rampaging crowd. The hotel had been used to house asylum seekers, but it was unclear if they were still staying at the site.

At least 10 police officers were hurt and one was left unconscious after a head injury, the South Yorkshire police service, which oversees the area, said in a statement on Sunday evening. No employees or guests of the hotel were injured, the police said.

In Middlesbrough, a group of mostly men, some of them masked, hurled bottles and rocks at riot police officers. Cars were set alight, and the local police said at least nine people had been arrested on Sunday. In Bolton, near Manchester, things turned violent when two separate groups clashed, despite a large police presence.

The weekend riots have prompted a heavy police response in cities across Britain. The National Police Chiefs’ Council, which represents law enforcement, said that nearly 4,000 additional officers had been deployed to deter the violence, and some 147 arrests had been made across the country since Saturday night.

Prime Minister Keir Starmer, in a statement given from Downing Street on Sunday afternoon, condemned the “far-right thuggery we have seen this weekend.”

“Be in no doubt: Those who have participated in this violence will face the full force of the law,” Mr. Starmer said, noting that the police would be making arrests and that charges would follow. “I guarantee you will regret taking part in this disorder, whether directly or those whipping up this action online and then running away themselves. This is not protest. It is organized, violent thuggery.”

The crisis has emerged as the first test for Mr. Starmer’s fledgling Labour government, which scrambled on Sunday to tamp down reports that he was set to go on vacation this week. His office said he was not scheduled to be out of town and would remain at 10 Downing Street to manage the fallout.

Mainstream politicians from across the spectrum have been united in condemning the violence. Rishi Sunak, the former prime minister and current leader of the opposition Conservative Party, which had governed Britain for the last 14 years, said on X that the strife had “nothing to do with the tragedy in Southport” and that the police had “our full support to deal with these criminals swiftly.”

Tension has gripped some communities since a knife attack at a Taylor Swift-themed dance class left three young girls dead. Eight other children and two adults were wounded in the town of Southport, near Liverpool, and a 17-year-old suspect was later named.

Disinformation about the attacker’s identity spread quickly online, falsely identifying him as a recently arrived asylum seeker and fueling a far-right riot in which a mosque was attacked and dozens of police officers were hurt in Southport on Tuesday.

The wave of protests and unrest has been driven by fast-spreading misinformation, much of it promulgated by far-right voices on the social media platform X and the Telegram messaging app. They include neo-Nazis, violent soccer fans, anti-Muslim factions, and social media influencers who have amplified their messaging to followers.

Pockets of disorder have since cropped up in other parts of Britain, with violent protests in Liverpool, Manchester, Leeds, Nottingham and other cities over the weekend. A protest was also held in Belfast, Northern Ireland, and also turned violent.

BJ Harrington, the head of public order for Britain’s National Police Chiefs’ Council, said that disinformation had been “a huge driver of this appalling violence” and that many who were involved were responding directly to what they had read online.

“Often posts are being shared and amplified by high-profile accounts,” he said.

“We’re working hard to counteract this,” he said, adding, “They won’t win.”

Mr. Harrington added that the disorder had declined significantly on Sunday. He also issued a warning for those stoking the disorder.

“Intelligence teams, detectives and neighborhood officers are working round the clock to identify and apprehend those involved,” he said. “And make no mistake, if you haven’t had a knock on the door yet, your time will come.”

On Saturday, larger groups had gathered in areas of unrest. In Liverpool, more than 300 people were involved in what the police called a “violent disorder,” when businesses were looted and rioters hurled bricks and other projectiles at police officers. Two police officers were taken to the hospital with non-life-threatening injuries. By Sunday morning, at least 11 people had been arrested, the police said.

Far-right protesters and counterprotesters also clashed in Manchester, at least 10 people were arrested in unrest in Stoke-on-Trent, and there were clashes in Leeds, where the police said they had made a “handful” of arrests.

In Belfast, Northern Ireland, where anti-immigration messaging has also simmered, clashes broke out between anti-Muslim protesters and people at an antiracist demonstration at City Hall, with fireworks and projectiles being hurled between the two. Several businesses and vehicles were reportedly set on fire, and an apartment building had to be evacuated, the police said.

The Police Services of Northern Ireland said in a statement that while only four people were arrested, “every tool” would be used to identify those who caused damage.

Much of the initial violence in some cities over the weekend had been focused on the Muslim community, with mosques and local businesses owned by Muslim people targeted. Yvette Cooper, Britain’s home secretary, announced a new rapid response process to provide mosques at risk of violent disorder additional security personnel, “providing communities with vital support and reassurance,” she said in a statement.

“Britain is a proud and tolerant country, and nobody should make any excuses for the shameful actions of the hooligans, thugs and extremist groups who have been attacking police officers, looting local shops or attacking people based on the color of their skin,” Ms. Cooper said in a written statement.

The new security provisions have already begun, she said, and specific security for mosques across the country was already being provided this weekend, in addition to the police response.

“As a nation, we will not tolerate criminal behavior, dangerous extremism and racist attacks that go against everything our country stands for,” she added.
