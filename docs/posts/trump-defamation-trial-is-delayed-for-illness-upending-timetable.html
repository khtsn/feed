Trump Defamation Trial Is Delayed for Illness, Upending Timetable

New York|Trump Defamation Trial Is Delayed for Illness, Upending Timetable

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/01/22/nyregion/trump-defamation-testify-e-jean-carroll.html

The judge hearing the case, in which E. Jean Carroll is suing Donald J. Trump, put the trial on hold after a juror and the parents of one of Mr. Trump’s lawyers fell sick.

Jan. 22, 2024Updated 11:23 a.m. ET

The trial of the writer E. Jean Carroll’s defamation suit against Donald J. Trump was suspended on Monday after one of his lawyers said she had been exposed to Covid and a juror fell ill.

The juror was on his way to court when he began to feel flulike symptoms and called the federal courthouse in Manhattan to report that he was feeling ill, the judge in the trial, Lewis A. Kaplan, told lawyers for Mr. Trump and Ms. Carroll in the courtroom.

Mr. Trump’s lawyer, Alina Habba, who was exposed to Covid during a visit to her parents, said she had tested negative but was still feeling symptoms.

She asked Judge Kaplan to postpone court for the day, and then asked for the trial to be delayed until Wednesday. She noted that Mr. Trump, who was in court and had been whispering to her, had reminded her that New Hampshire was holding its presidential primary on Tuesday.

“I would need his testimony to be Wednesday,” Ms. Habba said.

Roberta A. Kaplan, Ms. Carroll’s lawyer, said she would prefer to continue on Tuesday.

“I’d like to get this trial over with,” she said.

Judge Kaplan said he would consider Ms. Habba’s request. “Circumstances may result in your getting what you ask for,” he told Ms. Habba. “And maybe not.”

Thus ended the question of whether Mr. Trump would take the stand on Monday and testify in the civil trial, which is beginning its second week and which he has used as an opportunity to reach voters and complain that he is being mistreated by the courts.

On Monday morning, Mr. Trump’s motorcade had headed to the downtown Manhattan courthouse where a jury is deciding how much money, if any, he must pay Ms. Carroll for defaming her in 2019. That was the year she first accused him of raping her decades earlier in a department store dressing room.

In May, jurors in a separate trial, awarded Ms. Carroll a little more than $2 million after finding Mr. Trump liable for sexually abusing her. They also found that Mr. Trump had defamed her in separate instances and awarded her $3 million in damages for those.

Judge Kaplan, who has presided over both cases, ruled that the defamation finding in the previous trial would carry over to the current one, limiting what Mr. Trump could say on the stand.

Mr. Trump’s and Ms. Carroll’s lawyers have been debating the parameters of what the former president should be allowed to discuss.

Judge Kaplan has said that Mr. Trump cannot dispute Ms. Carroll’s version of events like he has done in social media posts, campaign speeches and news conferences, where he has called her a liar who fabricated her story of the rape in order to sell a book. Ms. Carroll is asking for at least $10 million in damages.

Ms. Habba has said that even with the limitations placed on her client, Mr. Trump could “still offer considerable testimony in his defense.”

She said he “should be allowed to testify about the circumstances surrounding his statements” and whether he was acting with “hatred or ill will.”

On Monday, just before court was adjourned for the day, Mr. Trump appeared calm and sat still with his hands grasped together on the defense table. He occasionally scanned the members of the jury, but looked frustrated after Judge Kaplan dismissed the jurors.

Susan Necheles and Todd Blanche, two of Mr. Trump’s criminal defense lawyers who are representing him in other cases, also watched the proceedings and left with him after Judge Kaplan sent the jury home.

Benjamin Weiser, Maggie Haberman, Anusha Bayya and Olivia Bensimon contributed reporting.
