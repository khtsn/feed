Norfolk Southern CEO Apologizes for East Palestine Train Derailment

The railroad chief executive testified that he was “determined to make this right” but did not endorse bipartisan rail safety legislation or commit to paying for long-term damage.

March 9, 2023Updated 5:16 p.m. ET

WASHINGTON — The chief executive of Norfolk Southern told Congress on Thursday that he was “deeply sorry” for the effects of the train derailment last month in East Palestine, Ohio, but stopped short of promising to pay for long-term damage to the community, as senators pressed him for answers about safety measures and the possible environmental consequences from the accident.

Appearing before the Senate Environment and Public Works Committee about five weeks after one of his company’s trains carrying hazardous materials skipped the tracks and ignited a fire, Alan H. Shaw, the chief executive, fielded questions from angry senators in both parties about how far Norfolk Southern was willing to go to improve rail safety and address the fallout from the disastrous derailment.

“I am determined to make this right,” said Mr. Shaw, whose apologetic tone turned defensive at times during the hearing as senators in both parties vented their concerns. “Norfolk Southern will clean the site safely, thoroughly and with urgency. You have my personal commitment.”

But he declined to endorse bipartisan rail safety legislation introduced last week, which would strengthen notification and inspection requirements for trains carrying hazardous materials and increase fines for safety violations by rail carriers. It is unclear whether the bill will have enough support to make it through the closely divided Senate, where most major legislation needs 60 votes to advance, or the Republican-led House.

Senator J.D. Vance, Republican of Ohio, criticized Mr. Shaw for his refusal to support legislation that would create stricter safety policies, noting that rail companies turned to the federal government last fall to settle a labor dispute that could have resulted in an economy-crippling strike.

“You cannot ask the government to bail you out and then resist basic public safety,” said Mr. Vance, who is leading the bipartisan legislation.

Pressed by senators in both parties, Mr. Shaw also declined to commit to paying for long-term medical costs, health care and economic damages from the derailment.

Mr. Shaw testified that Norfolk Southern had committed more than $21 million to supporting East Palestine, provided support for over 4,000 families, hired a liaison in town and signed a lease in the community to ensure a continued presence.

He also said the company had taken steps to enhance safety by increasing its network of early-warning sensors. That process is still underway.

“The preliminary report found that the Norfolk Southern crew was operating the train below the speed limit and in an approved manner, yet it is clear the safety mechanisms in place were not enough,” Mr. Shaw said.

After the hearing, Senator Thomas R. Carper, Democrat of Delaware and the chairman of the committee, said he was disappointed that Mr. Shaw did not commit more resources to the community and urged him and other rail executives to embrace legislative action to put in place stricter safety measures.

Federal investigators found that a wheel bearing on one of the train’s cars had been heating up as the train passed through Ohio, but that an alarm did not sound to alert the crew until it passed a sensor not far from where it derailed. Safety experts say the crew could have averted the disaster if there had been more sensors closer together on the route that the train took.

The National Transportation Safety Board said on Tuesday that it had opened a special investigation into the safety practices at Norfolk Southern, which had suffered five significant accidents since December 2021. Another of the company’s freight trains derailed near Springfield, Ohio, on Saturday. Hours before the hearing, another Norfolk Southern train derailed in Alabama. According to Katelyn Byrd, a company spokeswoman, there were no reports of injuries or released hazardous materials. Norfolk Southern’s accident rate has increased over the past four years, according to a recent company presentation.

On Capitol Hill on Thursday, Debra Shore, a regional administrator of the Environmental Protection Agency, and Anne Vogel, the director of the Ohio Environmental Protection Agency, assured lawmakers that the air and water quality surrounding the community was safe and that the agencies were continuing to conduct testing.

“If the company fails to complete any of the E.P.A.-ordered actions, the agency will immediately step in, conduct the necessary work and force Norfolk Southern to pay triple the cost,” Ms. Shore said.

Senator Bernie Sanders, independent of Vermont, questioned Mr. Shaw about whether precision scheduled railroading, which involves cutting back on rail yard workers, inspectors and equipment to adhere to stricter train schedules, has had a detrimental effect on safety.

“I have been told by workers who work for your company, and other rail companies, that they are now being asked to do more work with fewer workers, and that includes safety inspections,” Mr. Sanders said. “So well before this disaster in East Palestine, we have been told about the potential safety hazards.”

Mr. Shaw sidestepped Mr. Sanders’s repeated questions about cutting safety workers within the company, including one about whether he would do away with precision scheduled railroading.

Several senators criticized Norfolk Southern for paying out billions of dollars to its shareholders — nearly $18 billion through stock buybacks and dividends over the past five years, double the amount it invested in its railways and operations — instead of using the money to make the company’s network safer.

Senator Jeff Merkley, Democrat of Oregon, asked Mr. Shaw if he would pledge to stop stock buybacks until the company put into effect measures aimed at reducing the number of derailments.

In East Palestine, residents have complained about what they consider a slow federal response and a lack of clarity about what precautions would be taken to ensure their safety. They have pointed out that shortly after the evacuation order was lifted, trains began rumbling back through town.

Residents are concerned that Norfolk Southern will not be held accountable for the damage inflicted upon the town of roughly 4,700 people and that they will be forgotten as the weeks go by.

Lawmakers also questioned whether the company had properly communicated with officials and emergency medical workers in its response to the incident.

Eric J. Brewer, an emergency worker who was on the scene, testified about what he called a “jaw-dropping” moment of miscommunication between Norfolk Southern and local officials.

According to Mr. Brewer, the company told emergency agencies that it wanted to vent and then burn the contents of a single derailed train car containing toxic substances to release the fumes, but the emergency agencies later learned that the company wanted to do so for four additional cars. Tank cars are vented to prevent the buildup of pressure inside the car and avoid an explosion.

Republicans on the committee, who have criticized the Biden Administration for its handling of the ecological disaster, questioned the E.P.A.’s response. Mr. Vance said that piles of contaminated dirt were sitting in the community because the E.P.A. had temporarily halted waste removal.

Ms. Shore said the E.P.A. had to pause the process in order to ensure the facilities receiving the toxic chemicals complied with agency standards.

“We expect waste to be moving as soon as today,” she said, adding that test results from a site in Indiana had found low levels of dioxin, a toxic chemical.
