The protests in Israel began almost as soon as the word came down on Tuesday that Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu had fired Defense Minister Yoav Gallant. Crowds began to gather near the prime minister’s residence in Jerusalem and in Tel Aviv, Haifa, Beersheba and other towns to protest the firing.

The opposition leader, Yair Lapid, said it was an “act of madness” to sack the defense minister in the midst of a regional conflict against Hamas, Hezbollah and their Iranian patron and called on “all Zionist patriots” to take to the streets.

Netanyahu no doubt anticipated the reaction. The last time he fired Gallant, in March 2023, after the defense minister had publicly criticized Netanyahu’s legislative efforts to subvert the independence of the judiciary, huge street protests compelled the prime minister to rescind his order.

The Hamas attack on Oct. 7, 2023, and the massive Israeli assault in Gaza that followed temporarily pushed the personal animosity of Netanyahu and Gallant into the background. But the defense minister’s disagreements with Netanyahu over the conduct of the war, as well as on other issues — including the drafting of ultra-Orthodox Jews into the army (which Gallant supported) — became increasingly evident and rancorous.

Especially galling for Netanyahu was that the Biden administration, fed up with the prime minister’s refusal to heed Washington’s calls for restraint in Gaza, turned instead to dealing with Gallant. The Israeli public, too, having grown tired of Netanyahu’s machinations to stay in office at any cost to avoid prosecution on corruption charges, began viewing Gallant as the antithesis of Netanyahu and his ultranationalist allies.

Gallant was the no-nonsense politician who spoke out against the judicial changes; the defense minister who insisted on the priority of bringing home the hostages over an all-out, murderous campaign; the former commando who contradicted the ultranationalist hawks with the argument that “not every goal can be achieved by military action alone.”

The breaking point was apparently a letter Gallant sent last week to the government arguing that “the war is being conducted without a compass” and proposing new war goals for Gaza, Lebanon, Iran and the West Bank. This direct challenge to Netanyahu and his far-right allies drew an icy response from the prime minister, who said he found the letter “quite puzzling.” In a video posted on his X account, Netanyahu said he had fired Gallant because of a breakdown in trust and “significant differences in managing Israel’s war.”

Less puzzling was the timing of the firing: Election Day in the United States. Netanyahu no doubt counted on political fever in the United States to drown out any reaction from the Biden administration or critics of Israel’s brutality in Gaza. Israelis are also closely watching the election, the outcome of which will strongly affect their relations with America, and thus might be slow to once again take to the streets against Netanyahu.

Gallant, for his part, had only a brief, one-sentence, response, posted on X: “The security of the state of Israel is and always will be my life’s mission.”

Nov. 5, 2024, 3:33 p.m. ET

The nature of terror is that a threat doesn’t have to be fulfilled in order for it to work.

On Election Day here in Fulton County, Ga., the county with the largest Black population of any county in the state, authorities had to contend with several bomb threats aimed at polling places.

I met Khalid Kamau, the mayor South Fulton, at Feldwood Elementary School, one of the polling places in his city that was targeted with a threat. South Fulton bills itself as the “Blackest city in America.”

He said at that point, around noon, there had been seven bomb threats around the county, they had all been deemed “noncredible,” and none of them had resulted in a polling location being closed for more than 30 minutes. Brad Raffensperger, the Georgia secretary of state, said the source of the phony threats was in Russia.

About an hour later, the mayor texted:

“Just got word of another threat at SW Arts Ctr.” The Southwest Arts Center is where the City Council meets. He texted that the police and fire departments were on the scene.

When I asked Kamau, who is changing to the one-word name Kobi, what he made of the bomb threats, he said, “I think that there’s folks that are scared about people voting.” He continued, speaking specifically about the citizens of South Fulton:

“We’re the sons and daughters of people who have faced down lynch mobs, water cannons and canines to vote. So this isn’t going to turn us around.”

Even if there are no bombs — and we must all hope that these threats continue to ring hollow — the objective of the threats can still be achieved: the interruption of voting and an ambient danger associated with the casting of ballots.

It is impossible to know if these threats will have any measurable effect on voter turnout, but the tactic is one that many Black people remember in their bones. It represents the opponent of their enfranchisement using every available tactic to deter them from voting.

And in cases like this, you must ask yourself two questions:

If you believe your vote doesn’t matter, why would people go to such extreme lengths to keep you from casting it?

And who would benefit if people in this largely Black county had a lower turnout?

Like everyone else on the planet, I’ve been wondering who will win this neck-and-neck nail-biter of an election. So I called up Shannon Mulcahy to ask how she’s voting.

Mulcahy, who lives in Whitestown, Ind., and lost her job as a steelworker when her plant moved to Mexico in 2017, became my personal bellwether on the American mood when I wrote a book about her factory closing and what became of all the workers in the years that followed.

I noticed during my research for that book that whenever she shrugged off some outrage like the Stormy Daniels affair, Donald Trump weathered the storm. Whenever she got angry about something Trump had done, like the child separation policy at the border, he was forced to change course. That’s because Mulcahy’s people are Trump people, the very essence of his base.

Mulcahy loved Trump in 2016 because he promised to renegotiate NAFTA, which she blamed for the loss of her job. By 2020, she had soured on him because of his behavior during the pandemic. She ended up protesting Trump at rallies and cast her ballot for Joe Biden. (It wasn’t her first time voting for a Democrat: She supported Barack Obama in 2008.)

So how is she feeling now?

Two weeks ago, she told me that she was torn about how to vote, much like the country itself. On the one hand, she likes Kamala Harris. “She’s tough,” she told me, after watching the debate. Mulcahy, a blue-collar feminist who had to fight for every opportunity she got in her male-dominated factory, believes in a woman’s right to choose and likes the idea of a female president. She doesn’t like what took place at the Capitol on Jan. 6.

On the other hand, she lost four close relatives, including a young niece, to fentanyl overdoses over these past four years. Fox News, and Trump, blamed the situation at the Mexican border for such deaths. And now groceries have become unaffordable. A family-size bag of Doritos now costs $8, she told me.

“I just feel like things were better when Republicans were in,” she said. As for Harris, Mulcahy said, “I feel like she’s had her chance,” adding: “She’s been in there four years. What’s going to be different this time?”

On Election Day, she answered my call just as she left her polling place.

“I wouldn’t be unhappy if Kamala won,” she told me. “But I just voted Republican, all the way.”

Of all the things that Elon Musk has done to get Donald Trump elected — magnifying misinformation on his social media platform, X; jumping up and down onstage at rallies; and pouring eye-popping sums into pro-Trump propaganda — nothing has generated more excitement than his strange and dubious contest.

Musk announced recently that he would “randomly” award $1 million every day until the election to people in swing states who registered to vote and signed his super PAC’s petition. More than a million people have signed the petition, the PAC has claimed on social media. In court, lawyers said some 18,000 of them were owed some sort of payment for referring others to the petition, and that 17 people from across the country would receive $1 million.

It’s very on brand for allies of Trump, a reality TV star, to turn our elections into a game show. In a way, you have to admire the chutzpah. While Kamala Harris’s campaign is focused on getting out the vote through a massive door-knocking campaign fueled by volunteers, the Trump campaign had the world’s richest man handing out million-dollar checks to winners of the Trump rally lottery.

Except that on Monday in a Philadelphia court, his lawyers had to defend the giveaway against a charge brought by the local district attorney that it violated state consumer protection laws and constituted an illegal lottery. Musk’s lawyers were forced to describe the “winners” not as people who “won” money but as ones who “earned” money for performing a service. They weren’t randomly chosen at all, the lawyers said, but selected for their unique abilities to boost the campaign. Monday’s winner will be from Arizona, and Tuesday’s will be from Michigan. So, no, not random at all.

“We ask people to sign a petition and refer others to sign a petition. There are payments made in exchange for that,” Chris Gober, a Republican Party lawyer, explained. “We then take that pool of individuals across the country and determine which ones of those individuals would serve as effective spokesperson” for the America PAC, a super PAC that lists Gober as treasurer.

As a defense against accusations of running an illegal lottery, it worked. The judge declined to stop the giveaway. But as a defense against accusations of grift and dishonesty, it was essentially an admission.

“Our case got better this morning,” Larry Krasner, Philadelphia’s district attorney, said from the stand.

It was a rare moment when the public gets to peer behind the curtain of a Trump P.R. stunt, which turned lucky winners into paid actors and transformed earnest voters signing a petition to support the Constitution into people looking for a check in the mail. (Musk’s lawyers said that anyone who signed the petition and persuaded others to sign would get paid $47 in addition to the chance of “earning” a million.)

Is it illegal? Maybe not. But it is disturbing. An oligarch is playing games with our democracy. It might turn out to be expensive to issue all those checks. But for the rest of us, it’s a cheap trick.

Nov. 4, 2024, 3:40 p.m. ET

Working variously as a writer, producer, conductor and arranger for more than half a century, Quincy Jones — who died on Sunday in Los Angeles at the age of 91 — brought a jazzy originality and sophistication to recordings across genres.

One way to grasp his extraordinary range is to listen consecutively to three disparate recordings that bear his stamp. The first is “Sinatra at the Sands,” which finds the singer at his earthiest and most relaxed, backed by Count Basie and his orchestra. The second is Michael Jackson’s iconic “Thriller,” the ’80s juggernaut that set a high bar for moneymaking and pop music freshness.

The third is “We Are the World,” performed by a super chorus of more than 40 artists — including Jackson, Diana Ross and Stevie Wonder — to raise money for famine relief.

As a young man in the 1950s, Jones studied with the renowned French composer and composition teacher Nadia Boulanger, whose students included Aaron Copland, Virgil Thomson and Philip Glass. Boulanger, he said, volunteered the best advice he would ever receive, telling him: “Your music will never be more or less than you are a human being. So I started to work on the human being.”

He was already music royalty by the time he joined forces with Michael Jackson for the 1979 album “Off the Wall” and the collaboration that would transform pop music. But what started out as a fruitful and respectful collaboration ended in acrimony.

Jones sued Jackson’s estate in 2013, asserting that the King of Pop had cheated him of his rightful due. Later, during a now-famous interview in New York magazine, he described Jackson as a thief who had stolen music from others and declined to compensate them. “He was as Machiavellian as they come,” he said.

The Civil Rights Act was signed into law in the summer of 1964, and the Voting Rights Act was signed into law the following summer. One would think that all Black people would have been rejoicing at the progress. Yet, a month after the Voting Rights Act became law, frustrated Black people in Watts rioted.

That’s an emotion that may explain why some Black voters, and particularly men, are not readily lining up behind Kamala Harris this year, according to one prominent Black mayor.

In the fall of 1964, Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. wrote an article in the weekly magazine Saturday Review about how the civil rights movement had been “essentially regional, not national,” with Black people in the South reaping most of the benefits and creating in them a particular form of optimism.

On the other hand, King pointed out, the plight of Black people in the North — in education, housing, police brutality and employment — grew worse.

King described Black people in Northern ghettos as living in a “schizophrenic social milieu” as they watched a “role reversal” in which energy flowed South to help Black people there while “the North, heretofore vital, atrophied, and the traditionally passive South burst with dynamic vibrancy.” In King’s estimation, part of the riot’s energy came from a feeling of being left behind, a feeling of a sort of advancement envy.

That sense of being overlooked and outpaced is precisely what Khalid Kamau, mayor of South Fulton, in the battleground state of Georgia, described to me last week. South Fulton, which has roughly 111,000 people, of whom more than 90 percent are Black, bills itself as the “Blackest City in America.”

Last week I asked Kamau, who is changing to a one-word name, Kobi, what he was hearing from his constituents on the verge of the election.

He said that generally, among women and people over 50 there was definite excitement for Harris’s candidacy. But among those under 50 there was some noticeable indifference, in part because they had once before voted to elected a Black president, Barack Obama, only to decide that it didn’t make a material difference in their lives.

He says the sentiment is essentially that “we had a Black president before,” and “then fake cops in Florida, and real cops in Ferguson, like lynch our sons in front of his face.” He’s referring to the killings of Trayvon Martin in Florida and Michael Brown in Missouri.

He is particularly worried about young Black men. He says that these men have the sense that they are losing ground while other groups, including women and the L.G.B.T.Q. community, are moving up. As he put it, “they see these other constituency groups making advancements and the issues that are important to Black men just not even being in the conversation at all.”

As he put it: “There are definitely some Black men that feel like this election is all about Black women and white women. And like, you don’t need my vote.”

He says that some of these men may vote for Donald Trump, but he fears that more of them simply won’t vote at all.

Nov. 4, 2024, 5:04 a.m. ET

Every Monday morning on The Point, we start the week off with a tipsheet on the latest in the presidential campaign. Here’s what we’re looking at this week:

After Donald Trump won in 2016, his campaign manager, Kellyanne Conway, made a comment about voter behavior that stayed with me, and I think applies to the Trump-Harris race too. Conway said:

I think one thing that was missed all along in this election is something we noticed early on, which is that there’s a difference to voters between what offends you and what affects you. And they were being told constantly, “stare at this; care about this; make this the deal-breaker once and for all.” And yet, they voted the way voters have always voted, on things that affect them, not just on things that offend them.

I thought of her comment as I dug into Sunday’s final New York Times/Siena College polls of the seven swing states. Trump looks more competitive in Pennsylvania, Michigan and Wisconsin than he did in 2020 and 2016 — three states that are critical for Kamala Harris. And we’ve found, in 61 focus groups by Times Opinion over the past three years, that voters in those three states and other battlegrounds have complained much more about inflation, grocery and gas prices, housing costs, interest rates, health care costs and other things that affect them financially than Trump’s offensive behavior and language.

In 2016, Democrats thought Trump’s “Access Hollywood” comments would sink him; today, they think voters will be offended by a Trump ally calling Puerto Rico “garbage” or Trump saying he will protect women “whether they like it or not.” But as Maureen Dowd wrote this weekend, this is pretty standard fare for Trump. Many undecided and late-breaking voters are casting ballots on what affects them — and Kamala Harris has never had a great argument on the economy. She has been vice president in an administration that many voters blame for their economic problems, and she has not successfully separated herself from President Biden.

But here’s the twist about Trump, Harris and what affects voters. Trump’s conduct shifted from offending people to affecting them during Covid, the aftermath of the 2020 election and on Jan. 6, 2021. He put our lives, our nation and our democracy in peril. His actions affected voters again when his conservative majority on the Supreme Court overturned Roe v. Wade.

Looking at Election Day, Republicans think voters will choose Trump again because of how the economy affects them. Some may — but not everyone. Will the nation really re-elect a guy who barely won in 2016, lost in 2020 and is widely unpopular now? I think the G.O.P. is underestimating how many voters feel Trump affected their lives since 2020 for the worse. On Tuesday, I’ll be watching four swing states especially closely: North Carolina, Michigan, Wisconsin and Georgia. I think we’re likely to get vote results and trending direction in those states earlier than in the other three battlegrounds. And if Harris shows strength in North Carolina, or Trump in Michigan, that will tell us a lot about how the night will go.

I’m also now keeping an eye on Iowa after J. Ann Selzer’s shock poll there this weekend showing Harris ahead and up by 20 percentage points with women. Count me among those who know Selzer as the best in the business — and that margin with women as evidence of movement away from Trump because of how he’s affected them. Joy may not be a strategy, but neither is taking away people’s rights and their place in a democracy.

The 11th-hour New York Times/Siena poll on Sunday showing Kamala Harris ahead of Donald Trump in Georgia by a single percentage point is no fluke.

Many political insiders put Georgia into the Trump column months ago. When I crisscrossed the state last week, though, it was clear that voters there had not given up.

Atlantans had festooned homes and businesses with “The South for Harris” signs and posters of John Lewis. “Freedom is not a state. It is an act,” one poster read, quoting Lewis, the civil rights leader and congressman who died in 2020.

Lifelong conservatives were showing up at Republicans for Harris events, where they told me they loved American democracy enough to vote for a Democrat.

“What he did on Jan. 6 is unforgivable,” Hilda Bishop, a white woman from Duluth, Ga., told me of Trump at one such event. She said she voted for Trump in 2016 but not in 2020 and would not do so again. She said his handling of the pandemic and his response to the foiled kidnapping plot against Gov. Gretchen Whitmer of Michigan had repulsed her as well.

Across Georgia, Black voters wore shirts with political slogans like “Voting is my Black job.” All year, polls have suggested Trump is gaining ground with racial minorities. But a vast majority of Black voters will almost certainly vote Democratic. In Georgia they are turning out in large numbers.

Democratic strategists said this weekend that Black voters accounted for 27 percent of the early vote in Georgia, which ended Friday. That matches turnout in 2020, when the state went for Biden by 11,779 votes, fueled largely by these voters.

On the campus of Morehouse College, a prominent historically Black institution in Atlanta, Ajani Bond, a 19-year-old sophomore, told me he didn’t know anybody who planned to vote for Trump.

“He’s a fascist. A racist. Then there’s the sexual assault cases, even how he treated his dead wife,” Bond told me. “He’s inciting white men who think they can do whatever they want.” Bond said he and his father, a U.S. Capitol Police officer, watched the Jan. 6 attack together on cable news, horrified. “Seeing my father’s face, I’ll never forget it,” Bond told me.

The South’s growing pro-democracy coalition of Black, Asian, Hispanic and now white college-educated voters is being underestimated.

Watching those voters at work these past days gave me faith as nothing else has that the project of democracy will continue, no matter the outcome of Tuesday’s election.

Nowhere did I see the ferocity and power of this commitment more strongly than in South Georgia, where Black and some white Democrats live alongside cotton fields and Trump signs. They are greatly outnumbered but play a critical role in statewide Democratic victories by shaving down Republican margins in these rural areas.

As they prepared to knock on doors last week in Albany, a small city in South Georgia, a small group of Americans, Black and white, exchanged hugs and prayed.

“We’re the heart of the old Confederacy and the cradle of the civil rights movement,” Jon Ossoff, a Democrat and the state’s first Jewish U.S. senator, told me at the event. “In the South, it is within living memory that decisions made by elected officials have had such a transformative impact on daily life, on whether schools are integrated, on, for goodness sakes, whether people are even allowed to vote.”

While many across the country wring their hands, these Americans have been hard at work. The balm for these perilous times may be to join them.

Nov. 1, 2024, 6:00 p.m. ET

As Election Day approaches, I’ve been having trouble sleeping. So I thought it would be useful to try to separate out those things that keep me up at night from those that don’t.

What Doesn’t Concern Me: Vote Certification

There is no shortage of doomsday scenarios about electoral chaos floating around out there, and it’s not unreasonable to be concerned. We have already seen cases of officials refusing to certify vote totals and taking matters into their own hands because they don’t like the outcome.

The good news is that there are many buffers in place, more than in 2020. First, the decentralized nature of American elections means that no one person’s actions can spread beyond a certain area.

Second, all states, including swing states, have legally binding deadlines for certification of their vote tallies, most in late November. Meeting these deadlines is not a choice; it is a requirement.

Third, Congress in 2022 passed the Electoral Count Reform Act, which, among other things, sets expedited procedures for federal courts to hear election disputes, including cases in which a local official somewhere refuses to certify a vote count.

I’m not including all the poll workers and election officials who have left or been pushed out of their jobs since 2020, in part because of harassment or threats of violence. That brings me to:

What Does Concern Me: Violence

The history of American elections is replete with threats, intimidation and violence by those who apparently lacked the confidence that they could win through voting and majority rule. In recent decades, the most glaring examples of such behavior have come from Republicans.

There was the Brooks Brothers riot in 2000 that helped shut down the recount in the Miami-Dade area, the precursor to George W. Bush’s victory; the caravan of Trump supporters who aggressively surrounded a Biden campaign bus on a Texas highway; armed militia members standing near ballot drop boxes; and, of course, the attack on the U.S. Capitol that Donald Trump incited on Jan. 6, 2021, in an attempt to overturn the election and install himself as president against the will of the people.

This year the threats are pouring in, putting decent Americans in fear for their lives and undermining the basic functioning of civil society. Nov. 5, 2024, 6:09 p.m. ET

Mara Gay

Editorial Board Member, reporting from Detroit.

In recent weeks, volunteers for Kamala Harris have assembled in Detroit from states as far away as Washington and New York to knock on doors. Democrats have executed an exhaustive field operation in Michigan and other battleground states. Voters in Michigan have sometimes received up to a dozen text messages and phone calls a day reminding them to vote, some including a photograph of their polling sites.

We’ll know soon whether it was enough. But if Harris wins, these field operations may be the reason.

By Election Day though, many likely voters had already cast ballots, and some volunteers were met by residents who said they didn’t plan to vote at all.

“I don’t like this male-versus-female thing,” Herbert Miles, a 36 year-old Black man, told canvassers at his door in Detroit, explaining his decision not to vote. Behind him, his three small children pressed their faces against the window and smiled and waved at the canvassers. “I have two daughters and prefer Kamala,” he said. But then he added, “We don’t need a woman president to inspire them.” Miles said he hadn’t voted in 2020 either.

The threat to democracy isn’t only Trumpism, but also disaffection among wary voters in complicated times. Listening to these voters drives home in real time the vital importance of Black women to the Democratic coalition, and, increasingly, college-educated voters of all backgrounds.

When I asked Emma Mitchell, a 73-year-old Black woman from Detroit, why she was volunteering for Harris, Mitchell told a story about growing up in the Jim Crow South. Mitchell, who was raised in Louisiana, recalled an incident during a trip to Mississippi when she and her siblings were subjected to racial slurs and attacked when they tried to visit a burger shop. “We couldn’t even get a doughnut unless we knew a white person,” she said. Mitchell said she believed Trump wants to ban abortion in part because he is concerned about the decrease in the white birthrate.

In Ann Arbor, students at the University of Michigan said they understood the added weight of their vote in an election that was too close to call. Droves of students walked through campus wearing “I voted” stickers. But the campus was unusually quiet. Many students I spoke to said they were filled with anxiety about the possibility of Donald Trump returning to office.

“We were in fifth grade when he was elected,” Allison Jackson, a 19-year-old freshman from Dallas, said of Trump. Jackson told me that she remembered that time as “very dark.” She voted for Harris, as did most students I spoke with here.

Several students here said they had considered voting for Jill Stein because they were angry about American support for Israel’s war in Gaza. But all said that after doing their research, they had ultimately chosen to vote for Harris instead, in many cases because of women’s rights.

“I decided, we just need to get Trump out of the way, then we can deal with the rest of it,” Zoe Vorwald, 18, told me.

If Harris wins, it will be because of voters like these.
