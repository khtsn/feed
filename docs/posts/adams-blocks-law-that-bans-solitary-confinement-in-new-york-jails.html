Adams Blocks Law That Bans Solitary Confinement in New York Jails

New York|Adams Blocks Law That Bans Solitary Confinement in New York Jails

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/07/28/nyregion/adams-suspends-solitary-confinement-law.html

Mayor Eric Adams declared a state of emergency in New York City jails and suspended parts of a law banning solitary confinement, a day before it was to take effect.

July 28, 2024Updated 9:43 p.m. ET

Mayor Eric Adams declared a state of emergency in New York City jails on Saturday and issued an executive order that blocked key parts of a local law that would have banned solitary confinement in the jails.

The order, one of three Mr. Adams issued on Saturday that pertained to the jails, was an unusual step that came only one day before the law was set to go into effect. It was the latest move in a protracted battle over the legislation between the City Council and the mayor, a former police captain who ran for office on a public safety message. After Mr. Adams vetoed the bill in January, arguing that it would make jail staff and detainees less safe, the Council issued a rare override of his veto.

The law would have banned solitary confinement for detainees who were accused of breaking jail rules, beyond a four-hour “de-escalation period” during an emergency. It would also have limited the use of handcuffs or shackles to restrain detainees riding in Correction Department vehicles.

“The Department of Correction has been laser focused on reducing violence in our jails to protect both the people in our care and correctional staff who boldly serve our city,” Amaris Cockfield, a City Hall spokeswoman, said in a statement, noting that the federal monitor who oversees the jails had raised concerns about the law.

The state of emergency is expected to remain in effect for 30 days, though Mr. Adams can extend it for additional 30-day periods. He has declared states of emergency before, including in response to the migrant crisis and the outbreak of monkeypox, but Ms. Cockfield noted that the mayor has never before issued an emergency executive order in response to newly passed legislation.

It was unclear on Sunday what steps the Council would take in response. Emergency executive orders can only be challenged through the court system. But the mayor’s actions were attacked by elected officials who had backed the bill, including Jumaane Williams, the city’s public advocate, who called the decision an “abuse of power.”

Referring to the city’s troubled jail complex on Rikers Island, Mr. Williams said in a statement, “Any state of emergency on Rikers continues to be caused by an administration that has refused to engage with partners to meaningfully improve conditions, with a preference for covering up the crisis and spreading misinformation.”

Shirley Limongi, a City Council spokeswoman, said in a statement that “the law already included broad safety exemptions that make this ‘emergency order’ unnecessary” and called it “another example of Mayor Adams overusing executive orders without justification.”

Critics of solitary confinement, also known as punitive segregation, say that holding detainees alone for long periods amounts to torture. Other states and municipalities have banned the practice; Democrats in the House and the Senate have introduced bills to ban it nationwide.

City officials have said that temporarily separating violent detainees is the only way to keep them from hurting other detainees and staff members. They have called a ban on solitary confinement unnecessary, saying it has been effectively outlawed in city jails since 2019. The practice that is at stake now, officials contend, involves housing detainees in solitary cells for much of the day but allowing them some time outside the cells.

According to Mr. Adams’s order, which was first reported by The New York Post, the new legislation would “remove key tools necessary to mitigate the risk of violence” in the jails and “pose a direct threat to the safety of incarcerated individuals and staff” in Correction Department facilities. The mayor has received strong support from the correction officers’ union, which has maintained that provisions in the law would endanger its members. Benny Boscio, the union’s president, noted that in the months since the law’s passage, more than 250 correction officers were assaulted by inmates.

The Council passed the legislation in December in a 39-to-7 vote, making New York the first major American city to ban solitary confinement in city jails in most cases. The mayor vetoed the legislation on Jan. 19. The Council overrode his veto later that month in a 42-to-9 vote.

Then, in July, Steve J. Martin, a federal monitor who has overseen the jail system since 2015, said he had “grave concerns” regarding the law.

“There is simply no question that situations arise in correctional settings where an immediate risk of harm must be addressed regardless of arbitrarily imposed limitations,” Mr. Martin wrote in a letter to Laura T. Swain, a federal judge presiding over an ongoing court case involving the jails.

According to the mayor’s order, the Correction Department can keep a person in custody isolated for more than four hours in “exceptional circumstances,” though the person must be removed from such confinement “as soon as practicable” once the detainee no longer poses a risk of serious injury to themselves or others.
