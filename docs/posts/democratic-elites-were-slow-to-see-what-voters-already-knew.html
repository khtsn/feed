Democratic Elites Were Slow to See What Voters Already Knew

Ezra Klein

July 21, 2024, 6:00 a.m. ET

President Biden and Representative Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez agree on this much: It is the elites who are trying to take Biden down, ignoring the sentiments of legions of Democratic voters. But when I started arguing in February that his age would mortally wound his candidacy, it didn’t feel that way to me. I saw the elites propping him up, ignoring the sentiments of legions of Democratic voters.

Who’s right?

Maybe we both are. In any system, elites are most visible when they are fractured and factions are acting against each other. In July 2023 — before the primaries, before last month’s debate — a Times/Siena poll found that Democratic primary voters, by 50 to 45 percent, preferred that the party nominate someone other than Biden in 2024.

But the Democratic Party’s elites were in lock step around Biden. They refused to listen to what their voters were saying. The fact that he was barely campaigning or giving unscripted interviews was rationalized rather than criticized. No major Democrats decided to challenge him for the nomination. Representative Dean Phillips’s effort to draft alternative candidates was rebuffed and his subsequent primary challenge ignored. Some of this reflected confidence in the president. Some of it reflected the consequences of challenging him.

The White House and the Democratic Party apparatus it controls are powerful. Congressional Democrats will not get their bills prioritized or their amendments attached if they are too critical of the party leadership. Nonprofit leaders will stop getting their calls returned. Loyal party donors will abandon you if you’re branded a heretic. “I would be crucified by them if I spoke out of line,” an anonymous Democratic state party chair told NBC News early this month. “I know when you get out of line, they all of a sudden have a shift of priorities, and your races, your state is no longer on the map.” That was far truer a year ago, when Biden’s position in the party was unchallenged.

These actions, decisions and calculations by Democratic Party elites were neither unusual nor conspiratorial. This is simply how parties work. But it meant that Democratic voters were given neither a real choice of candidates nor a demonstration of Biden’s fitness for the campaign. What they were given instead was signal after signal that every power broker in the party was behind Biden and confident in his ability to win re-election. Who were they to argue? Biden won the primary contest in a landslide.

In February, after Biden skipped the Super Bowl interview and flubbed the news conference intended to defend his memory, I published a series of columns and interviews arguing that he should step aside and Democrats should choose a new ticket at the convention. My argument was that his age had become an insuperable problem — visible in every poll and appearance, omnipresent when you spoke to ordinary voters — and the way his team was insulating him from unscripted interviews reflected a recognition of his diminishment. Biden was trailing Donald Trump even then. He was not showing himself capable of the kind of campaign needed to close the gap. And the risk of frailty or illness causing a catastrophe across the long months of the campaign seemed unbearably high.

The response I got from everyday Democrats was overwhelming — and almost entirely positive. Nothing else I’ve published has gotten such enthusiastic feedback. The backlash I got was from Democratic elites, and it was often fierce. Some of them thought I was wrong. Some of them thought I was right but was making the problem worse by pointing it out. Some of them thought I was right and told me so privately but wouldn’t dare say so publicly. I’ve never published anything else for which the gap between regular and elite readers was so vast.

Who are the Democratic elites? There’s no official list. They are Democrats who have significant influence among other powerful Democrats: members of Congress, governors, Biden administration staff members, donors, media figures, state party chairs, union heads, nonprofit heads. After the debate, this informal, disorganized coalition fractured. Some converted to the view that Biden should step aside and began working toward that end. Some believed that was an overreaction to one bad night and he just needed a few strong appearances to restore faith. Most were paralyzed by fear, anxiety and uncertainty.

In the weeks since, Biden’s public appearances and private calls have eroded his position. With each outing, more top Democrats have come to believe he’ll lose in November — and that he might not have the capacity to effectively serve a second four-year term even if he won. They have exercised their influence in the ways they can. Calling on Biden to step aside, giving anonymous quotes to reporters, writing opinion essays, withholding donations, organizing petitions, circulating polls.

But many Democratic elites have stuck by Biden. They believe that he has lost a step on the campaign trail but that his record is strong, his judgment is good and his agenda is admirable. And they, too, have exercised their influence in the ways they know how. Doubling down on their endorsements of Biden, giving quotes to reporters, writing opinion essays, pressuring wavering members of Congress, organizing calls on which they harangue donors.

I’ve talked to many on both sides of this divide. One thing that’s true about all of them: They see themselves as acting on behalf of voters. But they disagree about what that means, at this late hour.

Here’s the position of Biden’s critics: Would he win the Democratic primary contest today, given all that Democrats now know? Few believe he would. Would he run in the Democratic primary contest today, given all that voters now know? Probably not. Would he run unopposed today? Absolutely not. Polls clearly show that most Americans want Democrats to field a candidate other than Biden and that most Americans believe he is unfit for a second term. The Democratic Party is courting disaster if it does not listen.

Here’s the position of his backers: Biden already ran in the primaries. He won the primaries. There is no mechanism for voters to weigh in yet again. For him to be pushed out by Democratic power brokers would be illegitimate. Polls are broken — Biden and Ocasio-Cortez have made this argument — and so the only valid public input is the primary contest he ran in, functionally unopposed, which began months before the debate and the procession of interviews that have so damaged faith in his capabilities.

The truth is that all this talk of elites is one faction of Democratic elites trying to discredit another. It is an elite action aimed at other elites. And that reflects the reality of where the party is right now: It cannot feasibly hold a new primary contest. There is no perfect process to get (or hide) behind. There is only this diffuse network of people, with varying levels of power and influence and information, working and arguing under chaotic, uncertain conditions, trying to do the best they can. That’s true on both sides of the Biden divide.

So be skeptical when you hear that this is all a plot of the elites. The Democratic Party of 2023 had elites, too. They were less visible because they were unified. All the elected officials were endorsing Biden. All the donors were backing him. All the staff members were certain he would be the nominee. Now they are fractured, and we are seeing what we should have seen earlier: a genuine debate over the best path forward for the Democratic Party.

If the nomination goes to the convention, to be decided by the thousands of teachers and union members and party faithful who chose to be delegates, believing they would travel to Chicago to cheer on Joe Biden, there will be many who try to paint those delegates as a shadowy, organized elite. But that’s not who they are. They are Democrats, and they will be there to do the work of their party and of democracy — work that the elites kept primary voters from doing last year.
