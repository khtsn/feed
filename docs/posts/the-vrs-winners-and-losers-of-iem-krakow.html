The VRS winners and losers of IEM Krakow

Who extracted the most VRS value out of the biggest event of the year so far?

Though Vitality were the ones who came away with the trophy at IEM Krakow, a handful of other teams managed to avoid departing the event empty-handed, netting crucial gains in the Valve Regional Standings (VRS) to help cement their appearances at future events.

Four events to send invites from February VRS

IEM Krakow carried extra importance, as the invitation deadline for four sizable events — BLAST Open Rotterdam, PGL Bucharest, IEM Atlanta, and CS Asia Championships — came around on February 2, following the conclusion of Stage 1 of the event. As such, IEM Krakow gave teams an important opportunity to make a last-minute leap in the VRS.

Of course, not everyone came away from IEM Krakow with an improvement in the rankings. Some teams took big hits in the VRS as a result of their Krakow results, leaving them in difficult positions as the IEM Cologne Major deadline of April 6 approaches.

G2 (#17 -> #10)

Skipping over BLAST Bounty to start 2026 left G2 with limited opportunities to climb up the VRS ahead of the February 2 invitation deadline, but they certainly capitalized during their IEM Krakow campaign. Starting in Stage 1 gave them the chance to secure more points ahead of the deadline, with their victories over Liquid and Passion UA earning them a place at #13 overall heading into Stage 2.

This boost in the rankings left them tantalizingly close to a BLAST Open Rotterdam invite, but with all twelve teams above them in the rankings locking in their spot, G2 were ultimately left in the dust for the Dutch event.

Their Stage 1 efforts were not entirely fruitless — Nemanja "⁠huNter-⁠" Kovač's men sit just inside the cusp of the global top fourteen for a CS Asia Championships invite in May, should they choose to attend, and they are first in line for PGL Bucharest if any other invitations are declined. While G2 would have certainly liked to have earned a BLAST Open Rotterdam spot ahead of the Cologne Major cutoff, it's hard to be frustrated at the outcome given their delicate spot in the VRS at the start of the event.

G2 made up for skipping BLAST Bounty with their Krakow showing

Of course, G2's rise in the VRS did not stop after their Stage 1 campaign ended. With wins over The MongolZ, FUT, and an eventual quarter-final finish, G2 managed to wrap up the Polish event with the #10 spot in the world. As such, G2 are also in a solid position to push for PGL Astana and BLAST Rivals invites at the March 2 deadline, contingent on a respectable finish at the upcoming PGL Cluj-Napoca.

FUT (#26 -> #16)

FUT entered Stage 1 of IEM Krakow as the second-lowest VRS-ranked team in attendance at #26 in the world, dragged down by woeful losses to EYEBALLERS and BIG Academy in the recent IEM Rio closed qualifier. As such, their Stage 1 victories over #13 Legacy and #12 Liquid carried immense weight, allowing the young international side to break into the 19th spot in the world ranking heading into the February 2 cutoff.

Like G2, FUT were within touching distance to some event invites, but ultimately came up just shy, netting only an invitation to IEM Atlanta as a result of a substantial amount of declined invites from higher-ranked teams, likely due to the event's overlap with PGL Astana and difficulties attaining visas for an American event. FUT are also in a good position to capitalize on any declines for PGL Bucharest, with the potential to earn a closed qualifier spot or even potentially a direct place in the main event, depending on the number of declined invites.

FUT managed to improve on their Stage 1 success even further once in the top sixteen of the event, claiming the VRS #1 FURIA's scalp in their opening match. Though they faltered in their next two games against G2 and Astralis, FUT still came out of Stage 2 with a net positive, ending the event as #16 in the world, a ten-spot climb from where they started.

FUT have now given themselves a fighting chance to vie for an Astana, Rivals, or FISSURE Playground invitation come March 2, with PGL Cluj-Napoca being their best opportunity to make a push for it. Looking further, while much can still happen between now and the April 6 IEM Cologne Major invitation deadline, Aulon "⁠Krabeni⁠" Fazlija's troops have also given themselves some margin for error as they aim for their organization's debut Major appearance.

Astralis (#16 -> #11)

Rounding out the victors of IEM Krakow's VRS battle is Astralis, who climbed five places with their newly international roster, coming just shy of a stage appearance before narrowly falling to FURIA.

Though Astralis started in Stage 1, they did not make substantial gains from their first few matches in Poland. Wins over comparably ranked paiN and GamerLegion were certainly helpful, but a loss to #21-ranked NRG dashed Astralis's hopes of making a last-ditch climb up the rankings before the February 2 cutoff. As such, Astralis only came away with an invitation to IEM Atlanta by virtue of the numerous declined invitations, and at minimum a closed qualifier spot for PGL Bucharest.

Astralis are keeping up despite the substantial changes they made to their lineup

Astralis continued to put in work in Stage 2, however, suffering a loss to Spirit before stunning BLAST Bounty champions PARIVISION for a whopping 25 points, following that up with a win over FUT. All told, Astralis rounded out their IEM Krakow campaign as #11 in the world, hardly missing a beat after transitioning to an English-speaking lineup. Astralis are now in a prime position to gun for some of the March 2 cutoff events as they gear up for PGL Cluj-Napoca, while their ESL Pro League outing will also allow them to stay competitive for an IEM Cologne Major spot.

HEROIC (#18 -> #25)

HEROIC had a difficult set of circumstances heading into IEM Krakow, deprived of AWPer Alimzhan "⁠Alkaren⁠" Bitimbay due to visa issues, forcing them to call up Simon "⁠yxngstxr⁠" Boije from the bench for the second event in a row. Christoffer "⁠Chr1zN⁠" Storgaard's men struggled to get things going in Stage 1, claiming a map against PARIVISION before faltering to the Russian side, ultimately bowing out of the event without a series win after getting sent packing by GamerLegion.

HEROIC suffered without Alkaren by their side

HEROIC's fall in the rankings was no doubt steep, falling a whopping seven places, but things are not as scary as they seem for the European side. This is in large part thanks to the fact that they have already secured invites for PGL Cluj-Napoca and ESL Pro League Season 23, giving HEROIC some chances to at least stay competitive for invitations to the March 2 deadline events and an IEM Cologne Major spot.

Passion UA (#24 -> #29)

Passion UA started Stage 1 at #24 in the world, the third-lowest ranked team in attendance, and consequently fell to #29 by February 2 after suffering losses to G2 and Ninjas in Pyjamas.

Passion UA, unlike HEROIC, will not be attending PGL Cluj-Napoca, giving Johnny "⁠JT⁠" Theodosiou's troops zero meaningful opportunity to make a run in the rankings prior to the March 2 cutoff. Passion UA are not completely out of luck — their solid ranking at the beginning of the year has secured them ESL Pro League and IEM Rio berths, and they are currently in the midst of an IEM Atlanta closed qualifier campaign against fairly limited opposition.

Furthermore, with ten spots tentatively reserved for the Americas for the Major, Passion UA do not really face a risk of missing out on a Cologne appearance.

GamerLegion (#15 -> #18)

Rounding out the VRS losers of IEM Krakow are GamerLegion. Though the European side did not drop substantially, moving just from #15 to #18 after a win over HEROIC and losses to Aurora and Astralis, GamerLegion suffered dramatically with regards to the February 2 cutoff.

GamerLegion will be limited on opportunities throughout the first half of the season

Unlike HEROIC and Passion UA, GamerLegion unfortunately do not have the advantage of prior invitations to PGL Cluj-Napoca and ESL Pro League, owing to their lower VRS placing at the start of the year. Given that they started the event at #15, a flawless run through Stage 1 could have made the difference between netting invites to PGL Bucharest, BLAST Open Rotterdam, and CS Asia Championships, instead of just IEM Atlanta and a failed run through the BLAST Open Rotterdam closed qualifier.
