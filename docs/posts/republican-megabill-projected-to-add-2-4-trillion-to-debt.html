Republican Megabill Projected to Add $2.4 Trillion to Debt

The nonpartisan Congressional Budget Office announced today that the broad Republican bill to cut taxes and slash some federal programs would add $2.4 trillion to the already soaring national debt over the next decade.

The analysis was all but certain to inflame intraparty tensions between fiscal conservatives and President Trump, who does not share the party’s longstanding aversion to government debt. The bill, which was designed to enact the president’s agenda, has already passed the House. But some Senate Republicans have vowed to oppose any legislation that, in their view, would lead to excessive government borrowing.

Trump said that his critics were misguided and insufficiently loyal. His allies have sought to undermine the budget experts whose job it is to analyze the effects of legislation. The White House has argued that the tax cuts would lead to major economic growth, thus paying for themselves. The Joint Committee on Taxation, another nonpartisan office, disagrees.

Trump said that Putin would retaliate against Ukraine

Trump said that Vladimir Putin told him during a phone call today that he planned to retaliate against Ukraine for its major drone attack. Trump described the call on Russia’s war in Ukraine as “a good conversation, but not a conversation that will lead to immediate peace.” He did not say whether he had tried to dissuade Putin.

During a meeting in Russia today, Putin lashed out at Ukrainian leaders. He accused them of launching attacks to intentionally disrupt the peace process. Volodymyr Zelensky, Ukraine’s president, also accused Putin of stalling peace talks in order to delay a new package of American sanctions.

U.S. has delayed or cut about 2,500 medical research grants

During his first months back in office, Trump radically transformed the country’s medical research system. His administration has eliminated or delayed funding not just for Ivy League universities and politically disfavored topics, but also for research on Alzheimer’s, cancer and substance use.

To capture the cuts, my colleagues trawled through grant data and interviewed dozens of researchers. They found that the National Institutes of Health, the world’s premier public funder of medical research, has ended 1,389 grants and delayed sending funding to more than 1,000 additional projects. Remembering Edmund White

Edmund White, who died yesterday at 85, was a pioneer of gay literature. He mined his own life story, including his vast and varied catalog of sexual experiences, in more than 30 books of fiction and nonfiction as well as hundreds of articles and essays. Many were critical successes, and several were best-sellers. The Chicago Tribune labeled him “the godfather of queer lit.”

Galleries are plumbing art’s forgotten talents

The art market isn’t just about selling Picassos and van Goghs. There’s also a cottage industry of dealers who comb through storage racks and old photographs in hopes of discovering a breathtaking talent that slipped through the cracks of history.

The upside can be considerable. Consider the painter Lynne Drexler, who lived on a remote island in Maine. Before she died in 1999, she sold her work to tourists for as little as $50. In recent years, her lyrical landscapes have sold for more than $1 million at auction. Experts told us what they look for in overlooked artists.

Don’t underestimate the cockatoos

Most birds are satisfied to quench their thirst by sipping water from rivers, puddles and birdbaths. But not the sulfur-crested cockatoos in the suburbs of Sydney, Australia. They have taught themselves how to use public drinking fountains — a challenging task that requires one foot to twist the handle open and another claw to grip the spout.

It’s not clear why they go through all that effort; perhaps it’s just more fun than the local creek. But the mohawked parrots have become well known for their smarts, much to the chagrin of humans who have tried to keep them from opening trash cans around town.
