Pandemic Divisiveness in the U.S. Wasn’t What It Seemed

I’d like to tell you a story about the pandemic, one that may sound so gauzily hopeful, it would qualify today as a public health fairy tale.

The story is this. When Covid arrived on American shores, the United States did not have to collapse into Covid partisanship, with citizen turning against citizen and each party vilifying the other as the source of our national misery. Instead, political leaders could have moved forward more or less in unison, navigating epidemiological uncertainties unencumbered by the weight of the culture war.

You may be laughing, but this is actually a pretty good description of what genuinely happened in the spring and summer of 2020, despite how you may remember those days now. Back then, the president was a lightning rod who seemed to polarize the country’s response all by himself, although he had rhetorical help from podcasters and radio hosts, governors and members of local school boards. But at the state and local levels, for many months, red and blue authorities moved in quite close parallel. For the most part, red and blue people did, too.

This is one of the revelations of “Lessons From the Covid War, an Investigative Report,” by 34 experts, published in April by PublicAffairs. Over the next few weeks, inspired by this book and a few other efforts at pandemic autopsy, I’ll examine the experience of 2020 and how it is already distorted in our memory.

One of the biggest distortions, the authors of “Lessons” argue, concerns that familiar bugaboo of Covid polarization. As the years unfolded, the country’s pandemic response began to suffer under the pressures of familiar red-blue conflict. But early pandemic partisanship, they suggest, is something of a myth.

Don’t believe it? Let me take you on a tour of those early months.

Forty states issued stay-at-home orders in late March and early April 2020, not long after the country’s first confirmed Covid death on Feb. 28.

These stay-at-home orders were riddled with exceptions, and all but one state lifted them by the end of June. On average, those orders lasted for a couple of weeks longer under the 23 Democratic governors than 17 Republican ones in those states — seven weeks compared with five, at the beginning of a public health emergency that lasted 171 weeks.

There are many reasons to regard these comparisons as crude, including that several states conventionally believed to be culturally and politically red were led by Democratic governors (like John Bel Edwards of Louisiana) — and vice versa (as with Charlie Baker of Massachusetts). The dilemma faced by states was also not uniform, since the pandemic made its initial beachhead in outward-facing places like New York, Washington and California. The objective logic of shutting down faster and sometimes staying closed longer was often strongest in blue states. Measuring crudely, though, the differences were real but very small.

But stay-at-home orders are only the most draconian of the public policies often described as lockdowns.

At Oxford, a team of researchers at the Blavatnik School of Government have been tracking global mitigation somewhat more broadly, measuring about a dozen metrics in terms of what they call stringency since the very beginning of the pandemic.

The states that reached the highest peaks of policy stringency — mostly from late March to early May 2020 — were Maine, Idaho, New Mexico, Kentucky, Maryland and Alaska. Not far behind were Montana, Minnesota, Wyoming, New York, Delaware, Rhode Island and Hawaii. Just try to draw an ideological line connecting these groupings.

In May 2021 the Oxford researchers published an eye-opening review of the American response. What did it reveal? Let’s start with the limits placed on business — the closing of retail stores, for instance, or caps on the number of people in a given workplace. By June 2020, the researchers found, just three states had no recommendations or restrictions on business activity; by September, just four. The rest of the country was relatively evenly divided between various levels of restriction, and while there was some regional variation, it didn’t map very neatly onto partisanship.

For social distancing policies, the Oxford maps do just as little to reveal clear patterns of partisanship. In August 2020, the whole West Coast had prohibited social gatherings. But so had Louisiana, Georgia and West Virginia. Texas and Florida had the same level of restrictions as Maryland. New York, New Jersey and Massachusetts all had lighter ones. By December, Texas, California and New York had the same level of limitation; Florida was lighter, but so was Massachusetts.

How about masking? By September, according to the Oxford researchers, every single state had some form of masking policy or recommendation in place. The two strictest states at that point were Pennsylvania and North Carolina. In general, across the summer and fall, the Great Plains and Big Sky states had somewhat lighter mask guidance, but the difference wasn’t stark, and policy was relatively uniform down the West Coast, across the South and up the Eastern Seaboard.

By the late summer of 2020, there was already different rhetoric coming from state and local leaders, depending on the party. But in terms of actual policy? “Almost everyone was well within a standard deviation of what everyone else was doing,” one of the “Lessons” authors writes.

Probably the most explosive and long-lasting fight was over school closings, but those fights didn’t take off in earnest until September 2020 at the earliest. According to a database maintained by Education Week, all but nine states ordered their schools closed for the remainder of the academic year in spring 2020. Of the nine that didn’t, three with Democratic governors, including California, and four with Republican governors, including Florida, recommended it. Two controlled by Republicans left the question up to local school districts.

The 41 states that ordered statewide closings did so almost in unison. The earliest closings were announced on March 16 by 18 states — half run by Democrat governors and half by Republican governors at the time.

Even when states began to relax restrictions on in-person instruction in the fall of 2020, Oxford researchers found a remarkable uniformity. In September 2020, 36 of the 50 states required the same level of stringency, and by December, 42 of the 50 states exhibited the same level of guidance.

On the local level, where districts could have considerable latitude, more partisan division emerged in the pandemic’s second school term. Though the state-level guidance was quite similar, schools in blue states, overall, took a different course than those in red states. According to a state-by-state database maintained at the Covid School Data Hub, during the school year that began in the fall of 2020, the average red state offered 54 percent in-person instruction, 32 percent hybrid and 14 percent remote; the average blue state was 25 percent in-person, 44 percent hybrid and 32 percent remote.

Even so, there was considerable variation within the two sets of states — larger variation than between them. In New York, for instance, 97 percent of students had access to at least some in-person schooling; in California, almost three-quarters of students had no in-person learning at all. In Arizona only 2 percent of students had access to in-person schooling, and in Kentucky only 3 percent, whereas in South Dakota and Florida it was 96 and 97 percent, respectively. Overall, partisanship appears to have had an effect when it comes to actual in-person schooling. But it was hardly the only factor, even in the more divisive second school year of the pandemic.

What about patterns of actual individual behavior? After all, policy and guidance go only so far.

If you recall the endless social media videos of arguments over masking policies in Walmarts and Whole Foods, it may be hard to believe that surveys show only a small partisan gap on the issue through 2020. And yet, according to a large survey conducted by Pew in August 2020, 92 percent of Democrats said they had worn a mask when in stores and other businesses all or most of the time over the previous month, and 76 percent of Republicans said the same. (The gaps widened considerably over the next six months.)

Somewhat creepily, you can also track the actual social behavior of people through cellphone data, as well. In the summer of 2020, according to Google’s mobility data, the states with the biggest declines in retail activity were, in order, Hawaii, California, New York, Florida, New Jersey, Arizona, Massachusetts and Texas. By the fall, the biggest declines in grocery activity were in Hawaii, Florida, California, Arizona, New Mexico and Texas — and the overall impact, across all 50 states, was less than 4 percentage points below prepandemic levels of activity.

So what happened? How was pandemic unanimity transmogrified into perhaps the clearest illustration of our self-destructive culture war?

First, there was, you might remember, a presidential campaign, with the challenger faulting the incumbent for the pandemic’s brutality and the incumbent claiming it was all no big deal.

Second, there was simple exhaustion over mitigation policies — closed schools, limited socialization, suspended professional and family and romantic life — with each party offering different perspectives on how to put the previous six months behind us.

Third was the normalization of mass death, with many Americans tuning out the ongoing illness and mortality as a kind of annoying background noise.

And then there was vaccination, which could have offered an exit from mitigation partisanship and instead ushered in a much more intense period of Covid polarization. In December of 2020, according to polling by the Kaiser Family Foundation, not quite half of Democrats and a bit more than a quarter of Republicans said they had already gotten their shots or would get them as soon as possible. But by February, 75 percent of Democrats said they had been vaccinated or would be soon, and only 2 percent said they definitely would not. Forty-one percent of Republicans said they had been or would be vaccinated, and 28 percent said they definitely would not be. By October 2021, 90 percent of Democrats said they’d gotten at least one shot; for Republicans, the figure was just 61 percent.

Vaccine mandates created another partisan wedge. By November of 2021, when President Biden announced new vaccine requirements for not just federal employees but all those working for large companies, 86 percent of Democrats said they supported the policy; 79 percent of Republicans said they opposed it.

For the authors of the “Lesson” book, these divisions, while significant, are notable also because of how late in the pandemic they developed. The partisanship of our pandemic response wasn’t a pre-existing condition, they argue; it was, at least partly, a result of that response.

“There is a common view that politics, a ‘red response’ and a ‘blue response,’ were the main obstacle to protecting citizens, not competence and policy failures,” they write:

We found, instead, that it was more the other way around. Incompetence and policy failures, including the failure of federal executive leadership, produced bad outcomes, flying blind, and resorting to blunt instruments. Those failures and tensions fed toxic politics that further divided the country in a crisis rather than bringing it together.

Quite early, governors began to realize that ongoing shutdowns were unsustainable, the authors write.

Meanwhile, without a system of biomedical surveillance, policymakers were still flying blind until people started showing up in emergency rooms. The health experts were still arguing about asymptomatic spread and how the virus was transmitted. It was not clear how much lockdowns would slow community spread. State and local leaders were anxious, correctly, about whether their fraying health care systems could stand any large margin of added strain.

And even those hoping to move quickly toward total reopening were delayed by rising caseloads. Gov. Greg Abbott of Texas was in that period about the most outspoken critic of pandemic restrictions in the country. But even he could not proclaim Texas “100 percent open” until March 2021. “By that time,” the “Lessons” authors point out, “most Democratic governors had reopened their businesses too.”

Throughout this prevaccine period, what was most different between red and blue states wasn’t the policy, the authors write, but the rhetoric. Red officials “stated that they were doing a phased operation because we wanted to reopen the economy and get their states moving again,” writes one of the “Lessons” authors, Alexander Lazar, who served on the Governor’s Strike Force to Open Texas and took part in over 250 multistate planning meetings throughout the pandemic. “Blues stated that they were doing a phased operation because they want to protect their people.”

But the effects of the rhetoric intensified in a familiar political feedback loop, as gestures that were at first primarily performative churned into something much more powerful.

Elsewhere in the world, the authors note, governments faced the same challenges, easing restrictions and acclimating themselves to levels of infection and death they had previously defined as unacceptable. But mostly they did so without the same erosion of trust that occurred in the United States. The reason Germany, for instance, achieved one of the lowest pandemic death rates among the most affected large, wealthy countries “was not that they found the perfect policy formula,” the “Lessons” authors write. “It was that their government tended to work the problem in a more organized, practical, and proactive way. They made rapid decisions about how to strike a balance, gave out protocols to implement those decisions, balanced the risks, and monitored what happened.”

The result wasn’t perfect; Germany had about 175,000 Covid deaths, a death rate about two-thirds as bad as that of the United States. And there were protests there against lockdowns and pandemic restrictions as in almost every nation of the world. Such resistance wasn’t unique to America any more than such mitigation policies were.

What was unique to the United States was the intensification of pandemic partisanship that followed the arrival of vaccines. In Britain the major threat of Covid mortality ended in March 2021, with mass vaccination. The peak that followed reached just one-sixth of the mortality peaks that had come before. In the United States, there was a nearly nine-month surge beginning in the summer of 2021. The United States was unusual among the world’s wealthy countries for having a deadlier 2021 than 2020.

In terms of pandemic partisanship, it was considerably uglier too.
