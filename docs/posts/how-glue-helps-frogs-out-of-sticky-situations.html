How Glue Helps Frogs Out of Sticky Situations

Trilobites

You’ve heard of a “frog in your throat,” but probably not like this.

Aug. 7, 2024, 5:02 a.m. ET

On a hike through a sweaty Peruvian jungle in 2016, Kim Roelants, a biologist at Vrije University Brussel in Belgium, caught a tree frog. As the large amphibian squirmed, the mucus it left on Dr. Roelants’s hands turned to glue.

“My fingers were just stuck to each other,” he said. “Before I knew it, both of my hands, my flashlight, everything was stuck.”

Similarly sticky secretions are found among other frogs as well as salamanders. The animals use this natural super glue to foil both predators and overcurious scientists. The mechanism behind this speedy adhesive — including how it evolved in unrelated amphibians — had been a mystery, Dr. Roelants said. In research published last month in Nature Communications, he and his team provided an answer: Noodle-like proteins found across the amphibian family tree have been adapted by a number of species to make their own fast-acting glue.

Many amphibians produce a layer of mucus over their skin. It keeps them moist and helps them breathe. And several, including common toads and more exotic, lethal species like poison dart frogs, secrete potent poisons to deter predators. But unfortunately, such poisons don’t always act quickly, leading to unhappy results for an attacked amphibian, Dr. Roelants said.

Amphibian glues, however, “work almost immediately,” Dr. Roelants said.

The stress of a bite “triggers the release of viscous fluid that rapidly solidifies,” added Shabnam Zaman, a doctoral student at Vrije University Brussel and an author of the study.

That’s bad news for anything with an appetite for amphibians.

“When an amphibian gets stuck in the mouth of a predator like a snake, that snake’s in trouble,” Dr. Roelants said. “At some point, you’ll see a snake decide this is going nowhere, give up and start trying to get rid of the frog.”

However, the question of how frogs produce such a glue has not received much research attention, Ms. Zaman said. To figure out how the glue worked, the team looked at the tomato frog, a plump red frog from Madagascar that often produces a viscous white glue when pestered.

The team carefully handled a few captive frogs and used the resulting ooze to stick toy bricks together, measuring how much force it took to pull them apart. They also subjected the fluid to tests, including scrutiny through high-powered microscopes. Other organisms, like mussels, affix themselves to rocks using a biochemical adhesive that their bodies produce. But the proteins that made amphibian glue were unusual, Ms. Zaman said.

Most proteins have rigid and defined structures, which help them carry out their specific functions. However, the protein the team found in the frog glue was distinctly floppy. “It acts like a cooked spaghetti,” Dr. Roelants said. “You throw its shape against a surface and it adapts to that surface.”

The protein in question is also full of sticky sugars. Add in a second protein that actively binds the first proteins together, and you have a viscous, pressure-sensitive slime “that’s basically a glue,” Dr. Roelants said.

The research, Ms. Zaman said, is a first step toward “helping us identify more glue proteins,” in other amphibians as well as in the prey-lassoing slime of velvet worms or the defensive glue of a slug. “It sets a precedent for being able to identify the components more easily,” she said.

The research also hints at how glue might have evolved in separate amphibians, Dr. Roelants said. When the scientists looked at the Mozambique rain frog — a tiny, chubby frog also known to produce a glue — they found its sticky protein was related to that found in tomato frogs. The two species, separated by over a hundred million years of diverging evolution, had converged on a similar adaptation. They were not alone: While the phenomenon has been formally reported in only five families of frogs, anecdotal reports suggest it’s more widespread.

Similar proteins are found in most other non-glue-producing amphibians in much lower numbers. “We find the genes to make these proteins are in nearly all amphibians we investigated,” Dr. Roelants said, including frogs, salamanders and caecilians, a wormlike amphibian. The genes manifest differently in different animals, but most amphibians carry the biological tools to produce their own glue, even if a majority don’t use it.

“Once you have a good template that’s widespread, these things can show up,” Dr. Roelants said. “This glue is a beautiful example of how evolutionary innovations can evolve.”
