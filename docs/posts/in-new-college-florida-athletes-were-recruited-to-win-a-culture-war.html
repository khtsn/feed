In New College, Florida, Athletes Were Recruited to Win a Culture War

They came to be somewhere sunny and warm. Some came because their parents lived nearby; others came from far away — from abroad — because it was a way to study in the United States. Madison Garcia, a softball player who previously attended Lincoln University, a historically Black college in Missouri, came because she was lonely and cold in the Midwest. The school recruiting Garcia and the others, New College of Florida, offered all freshmen free laptops, which was a nice perk. But the main reason most of the athletes showed up for the very first season of varsity sports at New College was that someone there was offering them the chance to keep doing what they loved. “I would do anything to be on that field,” said Nahuel Garcia, a freshman soccer player from Miami. “I’ll be water boy. I’ll be ball boy. It’s like, I need soccer in my life.”

Jayleigh Totten came to New College because she wasn’t happy on her team at Southern Nazarene University in Oklahoma City, a Division II school where students were expected to attend church twice a week. She didn’t mind the church part, but she did mind that her coach wasn’t giving her any playing time. She was starting to think that she might as well drop out of college and start working her way toward her dream career as a real estate agent. Still hoping that some great softball opportunity might pop up, she put her name in the transfer portal, which allows athletes to be contacted by interested coaches around the country. That led her, last spring, to a conversation with a coach who was starting a softball team at a school on the Gulf Coast of Florida. Totten would have the chance to make history, she was told. The coach was a young woman, and Totten thought she sounded sane and real and caring. And besides, how bad could it be? She would be playing softball by the ocean!

Totten committed. It didn’t matter to her that she knew little about the school, or that the players might not even be part of an official league in the first year. The school was small, but it was a public honors college for top students, which also appealed to her.

Totten did not fully realize that she was signing on for more than just a new team. She and about 140 other athletes who showed up last fall at New College, a school of about 700 people in Sarasota, were all, knowingly or not, also consenting to be change agents, just by being themselves. They would be athletes — baseball players, basketball players, soccer players, softball players — at a progressive liberal-arts school that had long differentiated itself by not having varsity athletics. Many of the new athletes came from religiously affiliated high schools and universities or from conservative counties; they were arriving at a school that was seen as culturally and politically far to the left, the kind of place where shoes were optional (except in labs), where L.G.B.T.Q. students felt comfortable — a space considered a haven, in Republican Florida, for brainy kids with a countercultural sensibility.

The athletes were brought to campus at the indirect behest of Florida’s governor, Ron DeSantis, who seized on New College — which he has criticized as a “Marxist commune” — as an ideal political target. In January 2023, citing declining enrollment, DeSantis applauded the decision of the school’s board to fire and replace New College’s president; DeSantis had recently appointed six new conservative members to the board (including the activist Christopher Rufo, a prominent opponent of critical race theory and diversity, equity and inclusion programs). The board immediately moved toward dismantling the gender-studies program and disbanded the D.E.I. office. To fill the role of head baseball coach and vice president for athletics, the school brought on Mariano Jimenez Jr., who served as the president of baseball operations at Inspiration Academy, a Christian sports-focused school founded by another Florida conservative who briefly served on New College’s board.

To DeSantis, who often invoked New College during his stump speeches in the Republican presidential primary, the school was a symbol of his assault on left-leaning academia, the athletes a harbinger of a new conservative era. Rufo has celebrated the school’s “new cohort of mostly male student athletes who will begin to rebalance the hormones and the politics on campus.” But as DeSantis campaigned last fall, and the news media aired student and faculty concerns about academic freedom, the athletes were also left to navigate the new reality on campus, many of them thrust into a role they hadn’t fully understood or agreed to play.

Totten is a pitcher, which means she’s not easily rattled. But even she acknowledged that arriving at New College was an adjustment. In August, when Totten got to campus, the softball field was lacking a fence, a dugout, a turtle for catching stray pitches and consistent grass. The academic changes were more confounding: Totten studied marketing at Nazarene and assumed she could continue at New College — except New College didn’t have a marketing major, she learned when she arrived. What kind of college didn’t have a marketing major? Also, even weirder, the school didn’t have grades. Instead, students received written evaluations at the end of each semester. That made Totten nervous. She liked metrics; she understood scores, ratings, grades. No one had given her a heads-up about any of these academic features. Amy Reid, the faculty chair and director of the embattled gender-studies program who also serves on the board, told me that many student athletes were surprised to find, upon arrival, that their intended majors — business, sports management, communications — were not offered.

The culture shock was also real. At New College, Totten never saw anyone going to church. But even more surprising to her was what happened during the first week of classes. A professor asked, in a routine manner, that the students introduce themselves by name and say where they were from and their preferred pronouns. When Totten’s face clearly reflected her confusion, someone sitting next to her leaned over and broke it down. “You’re a she/her if you’re straight and you’re a girl,” the student explained in Totten’s recollection. This was all somewhat baffling to Totten: In Oklahoma, as far as she knew, people’s pronouns weren’t a thing that was talked about. She had just never, ever heard a discussion of it, or even known that others had such discussions. “I’m being so for real,” she told me, her eyes wide, as we sat at a small table outside the dining hall in October.

Totten tried to be respectful of everyone she met, but many returning students reported that some of the male athletes, especially, were having a hard time accommodating difference — they pointed and laughed if someone was dressed in high goth or in gender-nonconforming ways. One faculty member, who did not want to be identified for fear of professional reprisal, said she saw a group of athletes jump-scare a nonbinary student who was walking by. The athletes, in turn, sensed hostility from returning students, many of whom told me they considered the athletes insensitive, jeering, academically incompetent or uninterested — precisely the kind of students they put up with in high school and expected to be free of at New College.

Andrés Rivera Calderón, a first baseman and outfielder, arrived at New College well aware of the tensions on campus. The Sarasota Herald-Tribune had run at least two columns critical of the idea of athletics at the school, and many New College students had shown up for impassioned protests against DeSantis’s overhaul the previous spring.

Rivera Calderón, who had just finished community college in Grand Rapids, Mich., did not let that faze him; he had serious dreams about making it to the Major League Baseball draft, and even though New College had no track record as a feeder school, he thought Jimenez, who had been a hitting coach for some major-league players, knew the right people.

During orientation, Jimenez offered the athletes an inspirational presentation that was clearly geared toward giving them the strength to persevere in a hostile environment. You might feel that some students don’t want you here, he told them; they would have to rise above it with grace.

However well intended the speech, it led many of the athletes to feel even warier of the returning students, or even “paranoid,” as one athlete who left the school put it. “The kids hated the athletes,” he said. “Everyone did.” The possibility that student athletes might feel that even professors did not want them there troubled Reid. “We are the students’ allies,” she told me. “That’s what we’re here for.”

Some athletes felt misunderstood — uneasy with being part of an “anti-woke” agenda that they themselves did not support. Nahuel Garcia, who came from Miami and had L.G.B.T.Q. friends, had no objection to the school’s trying to attract a broader range of students, but he resented that the change happening at New College was being used to make a “political statement.” “And it’s like, damn, I’m literally in the middle of that and a part of that, whether I like to be or not,” he said. “I know it’s bad. I feel bad about it.”

Garcia was aware of negative Instagram posts — he wasn’t sure if they were being posted by current or former New College students — implying that the athletes were so dumb that they would never be up to the task of a calculus test or a major research project. Garcia received high scores on numerous Advanced Placement tests and knew fellow athletes at New College who could more than hold their own academically. But it was also true that many of the athletes’ academic records were mixed or poor by the standards of an honors college. One baseball player cheerfully told me he got no higher than a 2 out of 5 on all but one of his A.P. exams. I obtained an internal spreadsheet showing that several students who were admitted for the spring semester had grade-point averages below 2.5 and that others had ACT scores in the low teens (a perfect score is 36).

Jacob Stangle, who came to New College to play basketball, felt immediately out of depth in class. Stangle believed he was recruited for a junior-varsity team, but it never materialized. (“We originally planned to have a junior-varsity team,” said Andrew Wingreen, the head basketball coach. “However, we didn’t have enough student athletes to do that this first year.”) Stangle didn’t feel culturally aligned with either the returning or the new students. “I didn’t fit in,” he said. He was uncomfortable when the players who were recruited for basketball prayed as a group during preseason practice. The prayer was optional, but he joined anyway, because it would have been awkward not to. By the time Stangle realized the mismatch, his parents were committed to paying thousands of dollars. He left New College after only two weeks; four of the original 18 varsity basketball players have since left, at least one of whom told teammates that he could not keep up in his classes.

For athletes who considered themselves diligent students, some of the assumptions stung. Lily Urbina, a freshman softball player from Jacksonville, Fla., came to New College to study photography. She was waiting in line at the dining hall when two students, oblivious to her presence, loudly expressed their frustration about the way the school was changing — about how depressing it was that the athletes were so academically inferior, that the standards of the school were going to be brought down. “It was rough,” Urbina said. Being new to college and away from home for the first time was hard enough; feeling unwanted and insulted made it all the harder.

On many college campuses, athletes live together, study together and practice together (it’s not uncommon for them to refer to nonathletes as NARPs — nonathletic regular people). But at New College, the gap between the two groups was widened by a barrage of messages that each side was hearing about the other. Sitting outside the New College cafeteria in early October, I watched the entirety of the lunch rush without seeing so much as a wave or a nod across the cultural divide.

By January, 10 percent of returning students had decamped for Hampshire College, a progressive school in Massachusetts that committed to helping them continue their schooling in a more welcoming environment. Many others were taking a break or had enrolled elsewhere. The New College board had sought this outcome, rather than worried about it: “The student body will be recomposed over time: some current students will self-select out, others will graduate,” Rufo tweeted last February. “We’ll recruit new students who are mission-aligned.” Ryan Terry, who was fired from his position as communications director last fall, reported that at a high-level meeting, an administrator said they would try to recruit — in the administrator’s words, he clarified — “weird home-schoolers and Christian private-school kids.”

In the meantime, more “mission-aligned” students tried to establish a presence on campus. Anna Chavez, whose mother, Kristina Heuser, is a prominent conservative constitutional lawyer in Florida, transferred to New College from Belmont University, a private Christian college in Tennessee. (In June, DeSantis appointed Heuser to the district board of trustees at another public college in Florida.) Chavez helped start the New College chapter of Turning Point U.S.A., a national group founded by Charlie Kirk, a prominent defender of Donald Trump who amplifies the lie that President Biden’s election was fraudulent.

Soon after, Chavez decided to run for president of the Student Senate, and she had posters and banners made that read, “Academics, Athletics, America.” The posters were stolen, stomped on and defaced (stickers reading “anal” were placed over the word “America”). Chavez sent a frustrated email to the entire school on Sept. 20, accusing those administering the election of making last-minute changes that benefited her rivals. “I obviously do not live under a rock: I am aware of the controversy surrounding changes this school is currently undergoing at the behest of Governor DeSantis,” she wrote. “I am not Governor DeSantis, nor do I know him or have any dialogue with him about his vision for New College. I AM NOT YOUR ENEMY.”

Chavez lost the election, and a majority of returning New College students remained hostile to her politics, but as the semester wore on, some of the chilliness between them and the athletes started to thaw. By early October, Rivera Calderón had gotten to know enough returning students, especially in class, to be impressed by what he saw. “In fact,” he said, “some of them were geniuses!” He loved hearing the way they thought about things. “My coach told me we have a lot to learn from these kids,” Rivera Calderón told me. “And now I know what he means.” He said he thought it would be a shame if the kinds of students who had historically come to New College were no longer part of its future. “I think it’s great that they have a place where they don’t feel they’re going to get criticized,” he said. “I respect them for having the guts to be themselves.”

Rivera Calderón, who emerged as a leader on his team, was invited to work at the student-affairs office, and he was asked to help interview a potential senior hire at the school, along with several returning students who were established leaders on campus. After the interview in late November, the other students joined Rivera Calderón for lunch, which is how he found himself sitting down, for the first time, for a meal in the dining hall that did not include any athletes. The returning students assured him, as they all talked, that their problem was with the administration, not the athletes. But they did wish the athletes wouldn’t segregate themselves so much from the rest of campus. For Rivera Calderón, the message was a huge weight off his shoulders. “I actually realized the returners don’t hate the athletes,” he said. “They actually want to get to know us. I always thought they don’t want us there. That was the moment I realized a lot of people have this whole thing wrong.” Around the same time, someone wrote in chalk on a campus sidewalk: “Your coaches are lying to you. We don’t hate you.”

‘My coach told me we have a lot to learn from these kids. And now I know what he means.’

The last New College party of 2023 (theme: “Nearly Naked”) struck Maya Rish, a freshman on the school newspaper, as a breakthrough. More athletes attended that event than any other, even if they stayed in their own circle, doing a standard dance: hands waving in the air and chanting, “Ay, Ay, Ay!” Nearby, the returning students were dancing in their own way — Rish described it as more like a Grateful Dead scene — but the presence of so many athletes at the party suggested a new degree of openness.

Garcia, the softball player, was happily reigniting her high school interest in theater and set design. As a result, she was one of the rare athletes who was spending a lot of time with returning students, some of whom were fascinated by what she did as a catcher on a softball team, even if it was clear that they didn’t totally understand it. How exactly was softball different from baseball? they asked her. How on Earth did she squat for that long? Was she OK? Their curiosity was touching to her, and a little funny. She answered all their questions, and she knew they came to appreciate her dedication to her sport. Now she wanted the athletes to appreciate a little bit more of what the theater students were about. “I’m going to try to force all the athletes to come to the play,” she told me in January.

If the athletes were changing the school, there were also indications that the school was influencing the athletes. Totten, having no other choice but to switch majors as a senior, had visited a bunch of classes and decided she might be interested in sociology. Her sociology classes were opening her eyes about a lot of things, she told me, like “how once you change your community and your society, your perspectives and your ideas change.” She herself, for example, had a new understanding of the L.G.B.T.Q. community at New College. “I just wasn’t exposed to it — and now that I am, I realize where they’re coming from and why they feel threatened in the world the way they do,” she said. “Just getting yourself out there and exploring, finding new things, is how you’re going to learn and get along with more people.”
