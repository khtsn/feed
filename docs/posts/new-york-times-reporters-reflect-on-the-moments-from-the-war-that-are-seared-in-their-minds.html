President Volodymyr Zelensky of Ukraine vowed on Friday that his country would defeat Russia, as the first anniversary of Russia’s invasion prompted shows of solidarity from around the world and a mix of anxiety and resolve in Ukraine.

“We will be victorious,” Mr. Zelensky of Ukraine told reporters at a lengthy news conference in Kyiv. He said that Ukraine could win the war this year as long as its allies remain united “like a fist” and continue delivering weapons.

There will be no negotiations with Russia, Mr. Zelensky said, until Moscow stops bombing Ukrainian cities and killing Ukrainian people.

“Go ahead and stop doing all of that, and only after that we’ll tell you what form will be used to diplomatically put an end to it,” Mr. Zelensky told reporters, on a day when allies rallied around Ukraine with new pledges of weapons and shows of support.

Even as leaders in Ukraine and around the world commemorated the anniversary with ceremonies and speeches, the fighting continued much as it has for the past year.

The war has already done untold damage: Tens of thousands have been killed on both sides, millions of Ukrainians have been made homeless, and Ukraine has sustained tens of billions of dollars worth of damage that has left cities flattened and people around the country grappling with dark and cold.

But Ukrainians have also found strength in shared sacrifice, and hope in the setbacks their country’s forces have dealt Russia on the battlefield. Ukraine has largely stopped the offensives of its much larger and better-armed neighbor and has regained swaths of captured land, aided by the United States and its European allies, which have remained united, funneling billions of dollars of weapons to Kyiv.

Here’s what else to know:

  • The United States announced new economic sanctions on more than 200 individuals and entities in Russia and other countries that are helping to support Russia’s invasion. And the Pentagon said on Friday that it would spend $2 billion to supply the Ukrainian military with new drones and anti-drone systems, as well as additional ammunition for artillery and long-range rocket systems to repel Moscow’s attacks.

  • Russian opposition groups in more than 100 cities in 44 countries around the world — from Berlin to Seoul to Los Angeles — are noting the anniversary of the invasion of Ukraine with three days of demonstrations.

  • NATO and European leaders dismissed a position paper issued by Beijing that called for an end to fighting while avoiding demands, or words like invasion, that could hurt its ties with Russia. But Mr. Zelensky took a more restrained approach, saying, “I think that China spoke its mind on the matter.”

  • The Treasury secretary, Janet L. Yellen, confronted senior Russian officials at a meeting of the finance ministers of the Group of 20 nations in India on Friday, calling them complicit in Mr. Putin’s war and saying they had a “moral imperative” to end the conflict.

  • As The Times covers the first anniversary of the war, we invite readers to share their thoughts and observations here. Here is a selection of excerpts from readers’ comments.

  • The war has reverberated around the globe, reshaping and strengthening alliances, and affecting everything from grain prices to energy policy. But even though Russia has found itself more isolated from the West, sanctions have failed to bring the country to its knees, and much of the rest of the world has continued to provide economic or diplomatic support to Moscow.

The United States said on Friday that Russia may be planning to provide Iran with fighter jets as part of a deepening military relationship between the two nations as Moscow’s war in Ukraine enters its second year.

John F. Kirby, a spokesman for the National Security Council, did not offer evidence to support the accusation, but said that U.S. officials believe the possible provision of jets to Iran follows shipments of artillery rounds from Iran to Russia.

“We have additional information that Iran’s support for Russia’s war is expanding,” Mr. Kirby told reporters. He said that Iran in November had shipped artillery and tank rounds to Russia for use in Ukraine, and that now Russia is planning to seek even more military equipment from Tehran.

“In return, Russia has been offering Iran unprecedented defense cooperation, including on missiles, electronics and air defense,” Mr. Kirby said.

Since September 2022, Iranian military and political officials have publicly promoted the supposed purchase of 24 Su-35s, one of Russia’s most advanced combat aircraft. While Russia has not confirmed the deal, a video released in February and satellite images suggest Iran was at least making preparations for the planes’ arrival.

Mr. Kirby’s announcement comes days after the United States warned that China may also be considering whether to provide military support to Russia for the war in Ukraine. On Friday, Mr. Kirby declined to elaborate on what evidence the United States had to back up that assertion.

“We haven’t seen them make that decision or move in that direction,” Mr. Kirby said of China. “And we certainly don’t want them to. China should not want to move in that direction.”

Together, the potential new support for Russia that might come from Iran and China would underscore the far-reaching ramifications of the yearlong Ukraine war, which has significantly reshaped global alliances on both sides of the conflict.

If Iran helps President Vladimir V. Putin get around the Western sanctions aimed at crippling his ability to equip his forces, the implications for the Ukraine war could be significant, Mr. Kirby said.

But he noted that there would also be implications for the Middle East, saying that Russian delivery of fighter jets to Iran could alter the power dynamic in the region.

KYIV, Ukraine — In a wide-ranging, hourslong news conference in a basement in the heart of Kyiv, the Ukrainian capital, President Volodymyr Zelensky said Friday that he was confident his country could win the war this year, expressed hope that China would not provide lethal military aid to Russia and implored Israel to get off the fence and side with his nation.

On the anniversary of Russia’s full-scale invasion, Mr. Zelensky offered a broad overview of where the war stood, the challenges Ukraine faced and the opportunities in the months ahead.

Not one to shrink from questions or to shy away from calling out even allies if he feels they could be doing more to help Ukraine, he nevertheless tread carefully when talking about China.

Asked about reports that Beijing was considering providing Moscow with lethal weapons, Mr. Zelensky initially ignored the question. When it was raised a second time, he said that working to ensure China did not arm Russia was one of his top priorities.

“I really want to believe that China is not going to supply weapons to Russia,” he said. Such a move would not only cause problems for Ukraine, he said, but would raise the risks of a third world war, adding, “I want to believe China is going to side with the idea of peace and fairness.”

About an hour later, the subject of China came up again, this time from a Chinese journalist.

Why should countries as geographically distant from Ukraine as China side with Kyiv?

Mr. Zelensky had the question translated a second time and then engaged the reporter directly, telling her that it was not about geography or geopolitics but basic principles.

Does China believe that innocents should not be killed? That a nuclear power plant should not be occupied? That respect for the sovereign rights of a nation means an invader should withdraw?

“I am already starting public diplomatic relations here,” he said, before switching to English to stress his openness to direct talks with China. “We have so many common issues, we have to speak between us with no one else.”

Mr. Zelensky’s confidence in Ukraine’s victory is premised on the idea that Moscow will not find a way to quickly and robustly rebuild its battered military.

If China begins supplying the arsenal for Putin’s army, all current thinking about the course of the war could be upended. But it is not just China that Mr. Zelensky is concerned about.

Iran is already providing attack drones to Russia, and the West has warned the Moscow is also seeking to acquire ballistic missiles. It was in this context that Mr. Zelensky was asked about Israel. Israel and Ukraine share a common enemy, he was asked, so why are they not stronger allies?

Again, Mr. Zelensky was careful not to alienate a potential ally. Israel’s targeting of Iranian drone and missile production facilities in Iran is probably more important than words of public support.

He said that he understood Israel’s long and complex history with Russia but said that he had wished for Israel to take a firmer stance against it since the start of the war.

“I really want them to be more than a mediator, but to choose a side,” he said. “The Ukrainian side.”

There were hard questions about possible internal disputes in his government. There was also a journalist from Azerbaijan who simply wanted a selfie with the Ukrainian leader, for his son. (Mr. Zelensky obliged.)

When asked, Mr. Zelensky briefly ruminated about his personal life and the difficulty of being away from his wife and children.

The Ukrainian leader said the darkest moment of the war for him personally was when he saw the atrocities committed by Russian soldiers for the first time, in Bucha. “It was horrible,” he said.

The best moment, he said, is yet to come.

“Victory Day is going to be the happiest day for me and every Ukrainian,” he said.

Over more than two and half hours, questions about how and when Ukraine could win the war, were the most frequent — even if that answer would only be determined on the battlefield.

Ukraine can win the war this year, he said, as long as its allies remain united “like a fist” and Western weapons continue to be delivered on agreed-upon timelines.

“I am confident we will have this victory, and hope it will happen this year,” he said.

Mr. Zelensky dismissed the idea of negotiations with Moscow, saying that at this moment they would make no sense. Before any talks, he said, Russia would have to stop the destruction and killing, and respect the right of Ukrainians to live on Ukrainian land.

“You go ahead and stop doing all of that, and only after that we’ll tell you what form will be used to diplomatically put an end to it,” he said.

The leaders of the Group of Seven nations — Canada, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, Britain and the United States — on Friday reaffirmed their support for Ukraine and again condemned Russia’s invasion of the country a year ago.

In a strongly-worded statement after a virtual meeting that included President Biden and President Volodymyr Zelensky of Ukraine, the group vowed to continue helping to finance and arm Kyiv in its effort to defeat Russian forces.

“Our solidarity will never waver in standing with Ukraine, in supporting countries and people in need, and in upholding the international order based on the rule of law,” the joint statement from the leaders on the first anniversary of the invasion said.

Mr. Zelensky said in a separate statement that he had presented the G7 leaders with detailed requests for more support in five areas: weapons, sanctions, finances, justice and unity.

“I thanked the partners for their unity and help during this year,” Mr. Zelensky wrote on the Telegram messaging app. “We withstood. We started to free our land from Russian evil. We are in a return of security to international relations. And we know that our coalition of defenders of freedom can become a coalition of winners in 2023.”

The public declarations of unity from Mr. Biden, Mr. Zelensky and the other leaders came as the United States announced a series of broad new actions aimed at helping Ukraine on the battlefield and increasing the economic pressure on Russia.

The Biden administration on Friday announced new economic sanctions on more than 200 individuals and entities in Russia and other countries that are helping to support Russia’s invasion. The Pentagon also said on Friday that it would spend $2 billion to supply the Ukrainian military with new drones and anti-drone systems, as well as additional ammunition for artillery and long-range rocket systems to repel Moscow’s attacks.

In the statement from the G7, the leaders vowed to continue imposing punishments and sanctions in an effort to hold Russia and its war machine accountable.

“In the past year Russian forces have killed thousands of Ukrainians, caused millions to flee, and forcibly deported many thousands of Ukrainians, including children, to Russia,” the leaders said. “Russia has destroyed hospitals, schools, and energy and critical infrastructure, and left historic cities in ruins.”

Reflections From Ukraine

New York Times correspondents in Russia and Ukraine are looking back on the past year and recalling moments that remain in their memory.

I had been covering the escalation of artillery shelling in eastern Ukraine that preceded the full-scale invasion, visiting villages that smelled of wood smoke and the livestock kept in backyard corrals. Windows had been blown out from explosions, and people were afraid.

But the fighting in the east at that point seemed to be a continuation of what had come before in Ukraine: years of violence in a remote, largely forgotten corner of Europe that had seemed all too easy for the world to ignore.

Returning to Kyiv amid Russia’s full-scale invasion brought home the scale of what was happening: The artillery and destruction had arrived in a modern, European capital in an all-out war.

I drove into the capital on the sunny, late afternoon of the war’s second day. A city I knew well, where restaurants were typically packed and streets clogged with traffic, was deserted, eerily silent, except for the distant booms of explosions. It suddenly looked like a sprawling set for a movie about the apocalypse. Empty.

Seeing familiar streets so quickly transformed was shocking.

The next day, my colleague Sabrina Tavernise and I drove around to report. We visited the scene of a violent fight: Burned, blown-up trucks and tracked vehicles cluttered Victory Avenue, a few miles from the city center. As we walked, our footsteps tinkled from stepping on broken glass. There were bloodstains on the pavement and spent shell cartridges all about.

The day was notable, too, for inspiring scenes.

The Kremlin intended for the Russian assault on Kyiv to be a blitz attack, with paratroopers expected to quickly seize the city after overrunning Ukraine’s defenses. But Ukrainians were having none of it. Overcoming the shock and accepting the grim new reality would be key to their country’s defense. People were turning up by the hundreds at military recruitment offices to fight, picking up Kalashnikovs and heading out onto the streets in small, loosely organized groups of volunteers.

We visited one site where a few dozen people stood in line for guns. Asking several of them about their backgrounds and why they wanted to fight, I found a cross-section of Kyiv: a university professor, a bookish economist so scared that his lips were trembling, and a nightclub owner, all ready to fight against what seemed impossible odds.

Explosions and air-raid sirens rang out. The sunny, almost balmy weather only accentuated the horror of the transformation we were witnessing.

At a clinic where Ukrainians had come to donate blood, I spoke with Iryna Koziienko, a 42-year-old psychologist who lamented that she did not know how to fight but wanted to do what she could by donating blood. She said she was frightened but also angry, a common sentiment heard in the early days of the invasion.

“You see this wonderful weather today?” she said. “It is sunny, it feels like spring. The birds are singing. I don’t want to have war on my land.”

Maya King, Sarah Eckinger and Guillermo Hernandez Martinez

Over the past year, the war has carved a path of destruction and terror across Ukraine, for civilians and soldiers alike. Russia has attacked cities in nearly every corner of Ukraine. Some continue to be targets while others are a reminder of the war’s enduring toll.

Kyiv

Russian forces made Kyiv, Ukraine’s capital, a prime target at the start of their invasion last February as they attempted to take down the government. But Moscow’s troops lacked coordination and underestimated Ukrainian fighters, who pushed the Russian Army out of Kyiv in the Kremlin’s first major setback of the war. As the Russians retreated, they left behind a trail of atrocities, notably in Bucha, a Kyiv suburb that was the site of some of the war’s worst atrocities. This week, President Biden paid a surprise visit to Kyiv to underline America’s support for Ukraine.

Kharkiv

As Ukraine’s second-largest city, lying 30 miles from the Russian border, Kharkiv holds acute military and historical significance for the Kremlin. Since the invasion, Russia has tried multiple times to take Kharkiv, in northeastern Ukraine. Ukraine’s forces expelled the Russians from the entire surrounding region in September, a key moment in the war. Much of the city’s population has returned and many businesses have reopened, but as Russian strikes continue, uncertainty in Kharkiv remains.

Lysychansk and Sievierodonetsk

Russia viewed Lysychansk and Sievierodonetsk, twin eastern cities sitting on opposite sides of the Siversky Donets River, as big prizes after its troops failed to take Kyiv. Russian forces occupied the two cities over the summer after weeks of brutal fighting, solidifying their foothold in Ukraine’s Donbas area, an industrial center that is home to Russian-backed separatist groups. Those groups and Russian forces control close to 90 percent of Donbas, according to Ukrainian officials.

Kherson

The southern port city on the Dnipro River remains the only regional capital that Russia successfully captured. In November, Ukrainian forces liberated the city from Russia as part of a counteroffensive in the south, evoking memories among some citizens of the day in March 1944 that the city was freed from Nazi rule. But Russian forces that pulled back to positions on the eastern side of the river continue to shell the city with abandon. This week, Ukrainian officials said that a Russian attack on a bus station killed at least six people.

New York Times correspondents in Russia and Ukraine are looking back on the past year and recalling moments that remain in their memory.

I will never forget reporting at a Moscow draft office in early October, watching as a motley group of men in varying stages of physical fitness were blessed by a priest and sent off for training and eventually, most likely, to the front.

One of the recruits, Evgeny, was from 1,000 miles away. He had been picked up that morning at the hostel where he was staying while working in construction. He wasn’t keen to fight, but he thought the authorities would punish him if he tried to evade service, and then draft him anyway.

I could feel the emotions of the women left behind, and I wondered how many of the conscripts would return — and what kind of men they would have become by then. In Ukraine, I interviewed women who had been raped by soldiers young enough to be their sons and visited homes that had been looted and destroyed by occupying forces. “The purpose of these false flag operations would be to accuse Ukrainian forces of violating the territorial integrity of, for example, Belarus” and in this way coerce Belarus to join the war on Russia’s side, the Institute for the Study of War, a Washington-based research group, wrote on Thursday.

In an ominous development that has precipitated previous Russian attacks, Russian cellphone operators switched off service in occupied portions of the Luhansk region in eastern Ukraine, apparently to prevent locals from reporting troop movements, according to the Ukrainian military. Russia has been bombarding Ukraine’s coal and hydroelectric power plants since September, causing blackouts that military analysts say are intended to demoralize the population. But in the days that followed, I learned something else: Fear might be infectious, but so, too, is courage.

I saw janitors and baristas, accountants and teachers join to form an army. A brewery turned into a Molotov cocktail factory. Grandmothers sewing camouflage netting. Villagers digging trenches and erecting barricades.

In those early days of the war, I would often visit Nadiya Opryshko, 29, and a growing band of volunteers helping families escape violence in eastern Ukraine. “The military of Russia, they are fighting for nothing,” she said in an interview, adding: “Ukrainian people, we know what we are fighting for. We are fighting for peace. We are fighting for our country. And we are fighting for freedom.”

Every time I return to Kyiv, I start my reporting trip at a funeral, looking to see whether that resolve I encountered so frequently in those first months of war has faded, whether fear has taken hold as the war’s toll mounts.

One year later, even in moments of deep sorrow, they have not.

There is an image seared in my memory: a blue and yellow bracelet around the wrist of a dead man, Serhiy Sova, exhumed from a pit in Izium after Russian forces were driven out. The bracelet had been a gift from the man’s children, one that he had worn every day.
