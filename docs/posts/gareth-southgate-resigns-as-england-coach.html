Gareth Southgate Resigns as England Coach

Southgate’s eight years in charge made him the most successful England manager in decades. In stepping down two days after losing the Euro 2024 final, he got the last word.

Rory Smith

Rory Smith first interviewed Gareth Southgate in 2013, and has watched his England teams at four major tournaments.

July 16, 2024Updated 11:25 a.m. ET

Outside Gareth Southgate’s office at St. George’s Park, the remote training facility that serves as the home of English soccer, a series of portraits had been mounted on the wall. They depicted the 14 men who had preceded him in what was once described as the second-most important job in the country: manager of the England men’s soccer team.

The images were supposed to act as inspiration, to remind Southgate of the rarity and the sanctity of a duty that had fallen to him almost by default. The impression they gave, though, was one of warning. On a daily basis, Southgate was presented with a reminder of a fate that appeared to be unavoidable.

There have, over the years, been many ways to lose the job of England manager. Underperforming on the field was the easiest, of course, but a nonexhaustive list would also include: suggesting disabled people were paying for sins in a previous life; refusing to drop a player embroiled in allegations of maintaining an affair with a teammate’s wife; using an umbrella on the touchline; drinking something that looked like, but was not, a pint of wine.

In the eight years that he has occupied that office, though, Southgate has proved himself more than capable of breaking patterns. England famously did not win penalty shootouts; Southgate did. England did not win semifinals; Southgate did. England did not reach finals on foreign soil; Southgate did.

It should not be a surprise, then, that even his departure broke with convention. On Tuesday, with England still mourning its loss in the Euro 2024 final to Spain, Southgate published a letter on the website of the Football Association, confirming that the final in Berlin would be his 102nd and last as England’s manager.

The letter was vintage Southgate: conciliatory, generous, written in that curious, corporate argot that encourages the use of phrases like “positive change.” “It has been the honor of my life to play for England and to manage England,” he wrote. “It has meant everything to me, and I have given it my all. But it’s time for change, and for a new chapter.”

This is not how England managers are supposed to leave. They are meant to be ejected in either humiliation or disgrace, victims of a bloodletting or of a witch hunt, castigated by a spittle-flecked tabloid press and scorned by the public at large. They are not, under any circumstances, allowed to retain their dignity.

That Southgate has managed to do so, though, is fitting. His record, of course, warrants respect: Under his leadership, England reached the semifinal of the 2018 World Cup and the final of the European Championship in both 2021 and 2024, making him the most consistently successful England men’s manager since Alf Ramsey, who delivered the country’s first and only World Cup in 1966.

But more significant is the timbre of Southgate’s reign. He has overseen arguably the most talented England team of all time at possibly the most tendentious moment in its history. His position, as well as the outsize role the England soccer team plays in the country’s broader consciousness, meant he found himself unwittingly caught in a series of roiling culture wars.

Southgate, at various times, was expected to take a position on the coronavirus vaccine, on lockdowns, and on the protests that erupted in the aftermath of the murder of George Floyd. He was asked to act as a unifying national figure at a time when England, the country rather than the team, was convinced that it was more divided than ever.

Out of instinct rather than calculation, he succeeded. Southgate risked the ire of at least a portion of his fans when he supported his players in taking the knee, the gesture borrowed from Colin Kaepernick that became synonymous with athletes’ response to the Black Lives Matter movement. His personal assistant grew used to — and weary of — opening the abusive letters that poured into his office.

Slowly, his relationship with England’s fans seemed to curdle. During the 2018 World Cup, he was so popular that he single-handedly turned the waistcoat into a fashion statement. Polling found that a substantial portion of the population wanted him to be prime minister. Atomic Kitten’s “Whole Again,” reworded as a paean to Southgate, served as one of the team’s unofficial soundtracks.

And then, the songs started to fade. Occasionally, fans would boo Southgate after a disappointing performance. A feeling had taken hold that he was too cautious, too rigid, too insipid to make the most of England’s lavish playing resources.

Some of the criticism was, in a sporting sense, probably justified. The story of Southgate’s England has always been somehow perfect for the era in which it was set: the endless churn of social media, the reduction of everything to a hot take, the acquiescence to the idea of competing facts.

Southgate had restored England’s men’s team to the front rank of soccer’s great powers. But he also had an impossibly rich generation of players. Southgate had taken a country that as recently as 2016 was losing to Iceland into a regular presence in finals and semifinals. But he also seemed to draw the likes of Slovakia, Slovenia and Denmark in the random draws for knockout tournaments.

Off the field, though, even he acknowledged that his social stances had alienated a portion of the fans. There seems little doubt that some of the criticism he faced and some of the opprobrium he engendered was rooted less in what he did than who he was. Southgate was a mild-mannered technocrat, a centrist dad, a high-performance podcast with a social justice lilt. He was not what at least some England fans want: a roaring lion.

The team’s performances, early in this summer’s European Championship, did not help. For the first time in eight years, Southgate seemed to develop an irascible edge in the early weeks of the tournament, bridling at questions he considered unfair. Criticism from former players — including some of his erstwhile teammates — cut through in particular.

That the pressure amplified everything is hardly surprising: There was, after all, a point during the tournament at which it felt like the only options for Southgate were an ignominious dismissal after an embarrassing defeat or a knighthood, handed to him after a glorious coach parade.

In the end, of course, he found a different path. (There is every chance he will still get the knighthood.) It would be a stretch to say this is how Southgate wanted his England career to end. The memories of the two finals he lost, against Italy in 2021 and Spain on Sunday, of how close he came, will stay with him. It is hard to make the case that his England team has established a defined style, something that can be passed down from one generation to the next.

But he will know that it could have been much worse. His image will, soon enough, hang from the walls of St. George’s Park. His successor — whether that is Eddie Howe or Graham Potter or Thomas Tuchel or Jürgen Klopp or someone else — will have to see him every day. And when they do, that series of portraits will seem just a little less minatory.

Their tenure might end, too, with an umbrella or a pint of wine or a good old-fashioned tabloid witch hunt. Or, if they are lucky, if they somehow manage to navigate all of the off-field issues that come with the role, if they pick the right piece of clothing, if they bring England to within a whisker of glory, they might get to go out like Southgate: on their own terms, in their own time, and with their dignity intact.
