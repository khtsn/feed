Severe weather, including heavy rain and potentially damaging winds and hail, was expected to hit much of the Eastern United States on Monday after a weekend of storms that killed at least 21 people and left hundreds of thousands without power.

In Kentucky, where Gov. Andy Beshear declared a state of emergency on Monday, four people were killed and more than 150,000 people were without power at midday after reports of tornadoes and damaging winds.

Elsewhere, a tornado left at least seven people dead in North Texas, including two children aged 2 and 5. Eight people were killed in Arkansas, and two died in Oklahoma as severe storms swept through the region, flattening hundreds of buildings and blowing away homes.

Thousands of others were without power from Arkansas to West Virginia, according to the tracking site poweroutage.us.

Later Monday, severe thunderstorms were expected from Texas to New York, the National Weather Service’s Storm Prediction Center said. Flash flooding was possible from Maryland to the Catskill Mountains in New York, the Weather Service said, and tornadoes were possible in the Mid-Atlantic region.

Governor Beshear confirmed four deaths in the state — a 67-year-old woman in Mercer County, where a tree fell onto a house; a 62-year-old woman in Hardin County; a 48-year-old woman in Hopkins County; and a 34-year-old man in Louisville. Governor Beshear said that an additional person was severely injured, and added that there were no people unaccounted for.

“The storm knocked out power for thousands of Kentuckians, temporarily shut down numerous roads and interstates, and caused massive damage to homes and businesses” Governor Beshear said, speaking from a command center on Monday morning.

The system is the latest to pummel the region over the past few days. Five people were killed and part of a city was obliterated in Iowa last week after a powerful tornado hit.

On Sunday, severe thunderstorms in Howell County, Mo., produced hail the size of baseballs and tornadoes that downed trees and damaged homes near the town of Mountain View, said Kelsey Angle, a meteorologist with the National Weather Service in Springfield.

In Kentucky, the governor deployed the National Guard to help state forestry workers clear downed trees and dangerous debris. He activated the state’s price-gouging statute to prevent price increases on necessary supplies. Governor Beshear said that he believed Kentucky would qualify for public assistance from the Federal Emergency Management Agency.

Emergency workers have reported high water, fallen trees and other dangerous conditions in about a third of Kentucky’s 120 counties, mostly in the western part of the state, Jim Gray, the Kentucky transportation secretary, said at a news conference. The storms also impacted nearly 100 state highways.

In Texas, Governor Greg Abbott signed a severe weather disaster declaration that covered 106 counties. Officials estimate that nearly 100 people were injured, more than 200 homes were destroyed and more than 220 buildings were damaged by the storms.

Katie Benner contributed reporting.
