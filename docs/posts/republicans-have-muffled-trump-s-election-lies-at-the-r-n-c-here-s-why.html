Republicans Have Muffled Trump’s Election Lies at the R.N.C. Here’s Why.

The conspiracy theories about fraud in the 2020 election have largely been absent in prime time. But false claims about elections are still circulating.

July 18, 2024Updated 1:02 p.m. ET

On the main stage at the Republican National Convention this week, some Trump campaign trademarks have been missing. There have been no celebrations of Jan. 6 rioters and no talk of election “mules” smuggling ballots. Even references to Democrats “cheating” in elections are so spare they could be easy to miss.

But across the street it was a different story.

There, in so-called media row, the hive of right-wing activists galvanized by former President Donald J. Trump’s lies about the 2020 election was buzzing. Podcasters chatted about plans for paper ballots and hand counts. Mike Lindell, the pillow company founder who is one of the most prominent promoters of conspiracy theories about election fraud, gave interviews about rigged voting machines. Rudolph W. Giuliani, Mr. Trump’s leading lawyer in the effort to subvert the 2020 election, held court nearby.

Republicans have largely kept this cohort on the fringes of its big, prime-time show, while more politically palatable messages take center stage. Though the majority of the Republican base still distrusts the validity of elections, despite no evidence of widespread fraud, some in the party blame losses in the 2022 midterms on candidates who held the most extreme positions on voting and democracy. Democrats made combating Republican “extremism” a core message in 2022, and it remains one of President Biden’s most consistent attacks.

And, with polls showing a widening lead for Mr. Trump, some in the denial movement are signaling how they might legitimize a victory in a system they’ve decried for years.

Some denizens of media row accepted their party’s strategy as practical and savvy. There is no reason to risk driving away skeptical voters who are just casually tuning in and uninitiated in the finer points of their election theories, they say.

“At this point whoever is on board will stay on board and we don’t need to keep compounding that message,” said Vish Burra, a consultant for “War Room,” the popular podcast hosted by Stephen K. Bannon, a former Trump adviser who is serving a prison sentence for contempt of Congress. “Once Trump is elected, we still want that adjudicated, but now we are full steam ahead on winning this election.”

Other signals from the convention stages have placated the election activists. Mr. Vish said that the selection of Senator J.D. Vance of Ohio as Mr. Trump’s running mate was all the message that “War Room” listeners needed to indicate that the right’s agenda — including changes to the election system — would be a priority in a second Trump administration. Mr. Vance embraced Mr. Trump’s effort to overturn the 2020 election results.

Figures in the election denial movement were given some notable roles in the convention. On Monday, the delegate chosen to second Mr. Trump’s nomination was Michael McDonald, the chair of the Republican Party of Nevada, who has been indicted on a charge stemming from his role in the so-called fake elector plan, the strategy that ultimately led to the riot at the Capitol on Jan. 6, 2021. Mr. McDonald has pleaded not guilty.

Shelby Busch, an election activist who has spread conspiracy theories, spoke for the Arizona delegation during the roll call nominating Mr. Trump. As other delegates used the moment to talk up their state’s wildlife or landscapes, Ms. Busch said: “Arizona needs to secure its southern border to prevent the atrocities that are happening and fix its broken election system.”

On Wednesday, Peter Navarro, Mr. Trump’s former trade adviser who served a four-month sentence for refusing to comply with the House committee investigating Jan. 6, received boisterous applause.

But, in the first three days of the convention, as Mr. Trump has watched the show from his box, that sort of heated rhetoric has largely been missing on the main stage.

The most prominent claim blends the G.O.P.’s immigration rhetoric with its focus on voter fraud. Several Republicans have said Democrats want to make it easier for noncitizens to vote. The accusation is based on Democrats’ opposition to legislation tightening identification requirements for voters.

There is no evidence of noncitizens voting in any significant numbers, and it is already a federal crime. A few municipalities, such as Oakland, Calif., allow noncitizens to vote in municipal elections.

The most high-profile promoter of falsehoods about elections, Mr. Trump, hasn’t delivered his speech yet. But Republicans have twice aired prerecorded video, in which he tells supporters to “keep your eyes open because these people want to cheat and they do cheat, and frankly it’s the only thing they do well.”

But Mr. Trump did not directly say that the 2020 election was stolen or rigged, as he does so often in his stump speeches.

His son, Donald Trump Jr., did rehash some of the falsehoods about the 2020 election in an interview with Axios far from the convention stage, and repeated his father’s focus on Democrats “cheating” in November.

The messaging onstage at the R.N.C. reflects a slight shift offstage as well. Some prominent Republican-aligned groups have increasingly encouraged Trump voters to cast their ballots early and by mail. Mr. Trump has claimed both methods are rife with fraud, putting his party at a disadvantage when it comes to driving up turnout.

And increasingly, as polls have shown Mr. Trump leading, there are signs that Republicans are trying to restore their voters’ trust in the result. Notably, the party’s new platform seems to suggest voting issues are a thing of the past, referring to “fixing our once very corrupt elections.”

As Mr. Lindell worked crowds in media row on Tuesday, supporters chanted, “too big to steal!” The slogan urges Trump supporters to vote in such large numbers that they overwhelm any of the supposed fraud or wrongdoing.
