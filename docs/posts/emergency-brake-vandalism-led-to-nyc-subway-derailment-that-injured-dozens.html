Emergency Brake Vandalism Led to NYC Subway Derailment That Injured Dozens

New York|Subway Delayed Again? Might Be Caused by a Brake Prank.

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/01/11/nyregion/subway-derailment-emergency-brakes.html

Each year, hundreds of subway riders pull the emergency brake for no good reason. Last week, that kind of vandalism led to a derailment that injured dozens.

Jan. 11, 2024, 3:00 a.m. ET

The collision between two subway trains that injured dozens of people in Manhattan last week began with what has become a routine problem in New York City’s mass transit system — pranksters pulling emergency brakes.

All New York City subway cars are equipped with emergency brakes, Metropolitan Transportation Authority officials said, which means that most riders can suddenly stop a train as long as they can reach and yank the mechanism — often a dangling cord or handle.

Emergency brake vandalism has been on the rise for the past five years. For every time the brakes are used in response to a legitimate emergency, there are more than 50 instances where they are needlessly deployed, adding up to thousands of subway delays a year, according to data from the M.T.A., which operates New York City’s transit system.

In 2023, the agency counted 1,748 unjustified cases of pulled emergency brakes, which rippled through the subway system causing 7,365 delays. Only 30 uses were justified, the authority said. The number was down from 2022 but more than double the 2019 number, even as ridership has slumped because of the pandemic.

But last week, transit officials said, was the first time in recent memory that the everyday annoyance contributed to a serious incident.

Even though they are often misused, M.T.A. officials said that emergency brakes remain an important safety feature that are common in most transit systems in the United States. They can be pulled for valid reasons when a passenger collapses, if a rider gets caught in a subway door or if there is a violent quarrel on a train.

But some experts said that the M.T.A. is operating with antiquated technology and that emergency brakes should not be so accessible to passengers.

“This is a little bit alarming,” said Katsuo Kurabayashi, chair of the mechanical and aerospace engineering department at the N.Y.U. Tandon School of Engineering.

The incident on Jan. 4 began when a No. 1 train stalled at 79th St. after vandals activated its emergency brakes, M.T.A. officials said. Transit workers reset all of the brakes except for one that remained stuck.

The train went out of service and continued uptown to a storage yard, forcing the rerouting of other No. 1 trains — which link the west side of Manhattan to the Bronx — onto parallel tracks. Near the 96th Street station, a slow-moving crash occurred when a train carrying about 300 passengers attempted to change back to its regular track and moved in front of the out-of-service train.

The prank became more than an act of vandalism, said Richard Davey, president of New York City Transit, the M.T.A. division that oversees the subway. “There are consequences to what people might think are innocent or pranking kinds of activity,” he said.

Emergency brakes typically work by using compressed air. When a brake is activated, it releases air pressure, Mr. Davey said. The brake mechanism that gave transit crews trouble on the out-of-service No. 1 train was damaged after the vandals set it off, he added. To get the train to move, workers disabled much of its braking system, which turned off a fail-safe that could have prevented the collision, Mr. Davey said.

The collision injured 26 people, derailed both trains, and continued to interrupt service on some of the busiest transit lines in the city until service was fully restored more than 60 hours later. None of the injuries were serious. Mr. Davey said that the crash will cost at least tens of thousands of dollars in damages.

On Wednesday afternoon, another subway train derailed in Brooklyn, less than a week after the earlier derailments. There were no injuries. Officials said that the incidents were not related, and they tried to reassure riders that the transit system was safe.

Federal law requires emergency brakes be available to passengers on commuter rail trains like Long Island Rail Road and Metro-North Railroad. And most local transit agencies, though governed on the state level, have adopted that standard as a safety best practice.

“Historically and routinely, transit systems allow the riding public the ability to activate the emergency brake system in case of safety-related and medical incidents onboard trains,” Mr. Davey said.

Bryan Sooter, the director of standards for the American Public Transportation Association, an industry association, said that most subway systems in the United States have emergency brakes similar to those in New York City. Mr. Sooter said that transit officials must weigh preventing the misuse of emergency brakes with keeping them accessible in case of true emergencies.

“A thing that can really probably help is trying to educate people and certainly highlighting a situation like this where several people were injured,” Mr. Sooter said.

The M.T.A. is trying to crack down on emergency brake vandalism. During a radio interview on WNYC earlier this week, Janno Lieber, the chairman of the M.T.A., noted that surveillance cameras are being installed in every train car.

The authority has tried to dissuade vandals by placing advertisements in train cars to let them know that it is a crime potentially punishable by a $100 fine to pull an emergency brake when it is not necessary to do so.

The technology used in New York’s emergency braking system was invented more than a century ago, said Mr. Kurabayashi, who noted that New York City’s subways have primitive safety features compared with cities abroad. He said that systems in some Asian cities can prevent brakes from being engaged when it is dangerous to do so, such as inside a tunnel.

“I think people really need to invest more on safety here,” Mr. Kurabayashi said. “Otherwise, we may end up having a really big tragedy in the future.”
