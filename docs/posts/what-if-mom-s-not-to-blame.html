What if Mom’s Not to Blame?

The Great Read

In a recent crop of films and television shows, grown men are obsessed with their mothers — even if they’re not the monsters audiences expect them to be.

Mark Harris

Artwork by Keita Morimoto

Feb. 27, 2024Updated 5:19 p.m. ET

IN ANY DISCUSSION of the enduring cultural madness surrounding the subject of mothers and sons, it would probably be natural to start with Sigmund Freud. But let’s treat as a given all the ways in which he blazed a trail for decades of mother bashing and jump one century forward, so that we can examine the philosophy of a different scholar of male frailty and its relationship to unresolved maternal trauma. “Boy, I love meeting people’s moms,” Ted Lasso says in a 2021 episode of the character’s namesake Apple TV+ series. “It’s like reading an instruction manual as to why they’re nuts.”

It’s a funny line, it’s a brutal line, it’s an unfair line — and it’s also an exquisite setup for a big reveal that wouldn’t air until two years later, during the show’s third season, when, for the first time, we meet Ted’s mother and finally learn (or think we do) just where his own blind spots, irrationalities and insufficiencies come from. It’s an “aha!” moment of a kind in which television and movies have long specialized: If you’re seeking the deepest understanding of why any man is the way he is and can’t be anything more, different or better, there’s someone you have to meet. And it’s all her fault.

The tortured dynamic of mothers and sons in popular culture has always been its own peculiar thing. Mother-daughter narratives have as profuse a tradition, but with a key difference: In those stories, no matter how complex the interplay of competition and control and selfishness and sacrifice and ego, there’s the base line belief that a relationship is supposed to exist into adulthood. Mothers and sons, though? Not so much. As movies and television have often had it, theirs is a bond that, if both parties are healthy, is meant to reach some kind of tacit endpoint that results in male independence and maternal letting go. If a grown man and his mother are still somehow at each other’s throats or in each other’s business, that’s pathology. Whether played for laughs, tears or shrieks, it’s almost always treated as a sign of dysfunction. Over decades, the pop-psychology conversation about mothers and sons has evolved from a fixation on all the ways in which the former can ruin the latter to more nuanced fretfulness about toxic masculinity and the difficulty of raising proud boys who don’t become Proud Boys or turn horrible in any of the other ways the world encourages them to be. But when that talk turns toward mothers, the verdict, as reflected in movies and television, has never moved all that far beyond “You’re doing it wrong.”

That may, at long last, be changing. The past year has brought an exceptionally varied and thematically rich crop of movies exploring men and their — to use the proper scientific term — mommy issues. They range from the extremely dark comedies “Beau Is Afraid” and “Saltburn” to the more heartfelt and sincere “All of Us Strangers” to the coming-of-age period piece “The Holdovers” to the singular mash-up of character study and tabloid scandal excavation that is “May December.” The movies all showcase mothers and sons; many of them seek to untangle relationships knotted and gnarled by neediness, selfishness or cruelty. By the end of most of them, blood is on the floor, and the collateral damage is steep. But as different as their approaches are, what these films have in common is a questing, thoughtful desire not simply to return to an old trope but to complicate, undermine or even explode it.

That said, old tropes die hard, and this one — the hapless son who’s been emotionally mangled by a monster mother — has been entrenched in movies and television for close to 75 years. Freud himself may not have been around to watch them emerge but, by the 1950s, references to psychiatry and analysis were ubiquitous in movies and on TV comedies and talk shows, and mothers, in the cultural parlance of that era, were a necessary evil — something for healthy and well-adjusted men to get past and get over. Men who couldn’t, or worse, didn’t want to, were portrayed as marionettes tied to and practically strangled by their mothers’ apron strings. They were labeled neurotic, and often implicitly labeled homosexual — an accusation that couldn’t then be made overtly in entertainment but could definitely be winked at. Doting mothers, not to mention distant or domineering or strong or fragile ones (for mothers, there was no winning path except quiet self-sacrifice), could make their sons timid, unstable, sexually dysfunctional, effeminate, perverse or outright mad. It became a kind of cruel, knowing joke: Think of Robert Walker’s simpering, coy, mommy-obsessed murderer in Hitchcock’s “Strangers on a Train” (1951) or Anthony Perkins as Norman Bates, whose macabre credo “A boy’s best friend is his mother,” earnestly stated to Mommy stand-in Marion Crane (Janet Leigh), is the closest thing “Psycho” (1960) has to a punchline. Or in a more benign mode, consider the hanging-on-to-heterosexuality-by-a-thread beta male that Tony Randall used to play in all those Doris Day-Rock Hudson movies. “I’ve been talking to this psychiatrist about my mother for two years now,” his character says in “Pillow Talk” (1959), adding, “It’s perfectly healthy. He dislikes her as much as I do!”

By the dawn of the 1960s, Mike Nichols and Elaine May had gained national fame with a set of improv sketches, including an oft-repeated one in which a brilliant rocket scientist is reduced to a prelanguage babbling toddler by a single ill-timed phone call from his ruthlessly manipulative mama. Overbearing Jewish mothers — the archetype is the one insistently banging on her son’s bathroom door in Philip Roth’s 1969 novel, “Portnoy’s Complaint” — were mostly comic at the time and have remained so. The true dramatic terrors were the WASPy ones, epitomized by Angela Lansbury as the parent who all but castrates Laurence Harvey in “The Manchurian Candidate” (1962). Here’s what mothers with too much aggression and ambition can do to their sons, the movie says — turn them into hollow-eyed zombies, sapped of the very autonomy that defines maleness.

Many of those stereotypes eventually evolved into something more self-aware, ironic and amused. The next decade brought Sian Phillips as Livia, the homicidal schemer in the popular 1976 mini-series adaptation of “I, Claudius,” plotting a path to power for her son while savagely minding her own interests. Some 20 years later, the character was drolly contemporized as Livia Soprano (Nancy Marchand), who may not have made her son into a mobster but, far worse, made him into the kind of mobster who needed therapy. Although “The Sopranos” found several new big bads after its first seasons, none was as big or as bad as Livia, who was killed off after Marchand’s death early on but effectively haunted the show’s entire run. That long-shadow idea — the nightmarish threat that even in death, monster-mothers never go away — was literalized by Woody Allen in his comic “Oedipus Wrecks,” the director’s segment of the 1989 anthology film “New York Stories.” When a middle-aged attorney finally gets rid of his nagging, critical mother with the aid of a magician who makes her vanish, she soon reappears as a parade balloon-like ghost in the sky, humiliating him in front of all Manhattan. Filmmakers as different as Albert Brooks (in 1996) and Darren Aronofsky (in 2017) have used movies to explore the notion of the matriarch as creator-underminer-destroyer. Both movies are, naturally, titled “Mother”; Aronofsky’s is so scary, it comes with an exclamation mark.

Incidentally, it’s not a coincidence that this phenomenon has been, and continues to be, largely restricted to white mothers. Nonwhite family stories remain too much of a scarcity economy for many filmmakers of color to feel that they can afford to play games with the idea of terrible, son-wrecking moms.

TODAY, DRAMAS AND comedies that take on mothers and sons tend to be much more engaged with issues not just of parenting but of trauma and recovery. They also grapple with, in the term of the moment, adulting, and with the very 21st-century question of just how long you can classify yourself as a young person before you have to own the fact that, as much as you may be the product of flawed parenting, you are responsible for your own behavior. Are monster-mothers real, or are they essentially self-serving rationales invented by men? The question is now being asked, but the dynamic is still irresistible. And why not? To quote the safely quotable part of that famous Philip Larkin 1971 poem about what your mom and dad do to you, “They fill you with the faults they had / And add some extra, just for you.”

Television in particular has run with that idea. The recently concluded “The Crown” spends six seasons reframing 60 years of the British monarchy as a mommy-issues psychodrama, and caps its final season with the dubious notion of Prince Charles, not previously known as an expert on human emotion, patiently explaining the phenomena of grief and catharsis to his blinkered mother after Diana’s death. And the most recent season of “Only Murders in the Building” has knowing fun with the decades-old stereotype that smothering mothers create semi-incompetent gay sons, with Linda Emond and Wesley Taylor as Donna and Cliff DeMeo, a possibly homicidal Broadway producing team. “Do you remember what my doctor told me on my eighth birthday?” Cliff fondly asks. “That it was time to stop breastfeeding,” Donna recites. Cliff: “And what did you say?” Donna: “That you should never give up on something you love.”

But more often, current TV series have taken advantage of their serialized, multiseason structures to use mothers the way “Ted Lasso” does — as decoder rings, teasingly withheld and then deployed to help us unravel the mysteries of their charismatically screwed-up sons. The seriocomedy “The Bear,” about Carmy (Jeremy Allen White), a driven Chicago restaurateur who runs a tight ship but seems filled with a lifetime of lost boy inner pain, waits until a pivotal second-season flashback to introduce his mother, Donna (Jamie Lee Curtis), who is plagued by mental illness. In that episode, we see that she holds her family together while tearing the individual members apart; in the show’s family cosmology, she’s basically a version of Mary Tyrone from “Long Day’s Journey Into Night” (1956) — a center, a vortex, a fearsome living specter. And, it’s implied, the answer to the question “Why is Carmy the way he is?” The news-network drama “The Morning Show” similarly uses a third-season guest appearance, by Lindsay Duncan, as a way of stripping the veneer off of the charming, polished network chief Cory Ellison (Billy Crudup), sending him home to visit a matriarch who, in a single evening, morphs from diffident hostess to casual eviscerator. At last, we can understand why the alpha exec is one step from being a spiraling catastrophe.

Bad mothers like these often serve an exonerating function; once we meet them, we can no longer judge the misbehavior of their sons without adding an asterisk of pity. Even the most highly regarded series of recent years, “Succession,” goes there with its infrequent, shrewdly strategic deployment of the actress Harriet Walter. While the show makes sure we understand that Logan Roy (Brian Cox) is the primary architect of his broken-toy progeny’s monumental defects, Walter’s Lady Caroline is shown to be her own kind of malevolent force — a “Lion in Winter”-esque monarch in exile, but one who, it is suggested in the show’s final episode, is so bereft of basic mothering skills that she can barely feed her own adult children.

“Succession” understands to its marrow that adults, even adults who behave like children, are not children — and that, regardless of whether they’re still somebody’s children, adults should probably know how to feed themselves. Mothers still make convenient villains, especially in an era when every child-rearing decision can be chewed over, debated, Instagrammed and quote tweeted. But this is also a time in which many parents lie awake at night wondering if tomorrow will be the day their tween boy discovers Andrew Tate. Perhaps a dawning understanding that, for the most part, moms aren’t the primary problem with sons is beginning to take hold.

IN THAT REGARD, one of the most exhilarating aspects of the current spate of mommy-issues movies is how willing they are to move beyond scapegoating. In the last act of Ari Aster’s “Beau Is Afraid,” after we’ve watched Joaquin Phoenix as a middle-aged man in the process of what appears to be a long, agonizing and public nervous breakdown, the film takes him home, metaphorically backward in time, to reveal the answer to a question we didn’t quite know we were asking: His mother turns out to be Patti LuPone in full termagant mode. “Well, of course,” we think. But then Aster pivots. LuPone’s Mona may be a comical embodiment of devouring destructiveness, but she’s not the key to the three-hour riddle of Beau and, if you think she is, just wait until you meet his father.

Alexander Payne’s gentle film “The Holdovers,” about three unlikely companions enduring Christmas season at an empty New England boarding school in 1970, also offers a pointed reversal. Early on, Payne introduces the seemingly heedless mother of an angry, sensitive teenage boy whom she’s essentially abandoned for the holidays; she’s a voice on the phone, barely there. He’s so hurt, he has to beg for affection; she’s too detached to hear him. It feels like a setup for a reprise of the seminal 1980 mommy-issues drama “Ordinary People,” in which an adolescent on the cusp of adulthood must accept that his mother is incapable of loving him in order to ensure his own survival. (He ends up with two loving dads — his actual one and his psychiatrist.) But Payne has something different in mind: The mother in “The Holdovers” ends up not being a villain, just someone overmatched by tough circumstances and contradictory desires, hardly an anomaly for a movie that takes place before parenting was considered either a verb or a skill set. And Emerald Fennell’s excoriating satire “Saltburn” gives us not one but two terrible mother fake outs — it’s in part the story of a young outsider (Barry Keoghan) who ingratiates himself with others with a spurious account of his victimization by a terrible mom, and in part the story of his rich alpha friend (Jacob Elordi), whose louche, spectacularly inappropriate mother (Rosamund Pike) presides over decadent gatherings at the family’s English country mansion. When we first meet her, the observations that casually cascade from her mark her as a wicked queen. But in the end, she doesn’t turn out to be her son’s undoing; someone else’s son turns out to be hers.

In “May December,” Todd Haynes sifts through the detritus of ’90s headlines, melodrama, dark comedy and old-style network-TV “based on a true story” docudramas, and uses his profound understanding of the role of acting in everyday life to explore the long-term marriage of a woman (Julianne Moore) and the much younger man (Charles Melton) she began exploiting sexually two decades earlier, when she was 36 and he was 13. It’s a bold maneuver to probe the nuances of a mother-son relationship where one should absolutely not exist. In Moore’s Gracie, Haynes gives us a genuine monster-mother who may, ironically, reserve most of her malignity and manipulation not for her actual children but for the husband whose development she arrested in early adolescence — a character viewed and presented with wrenching compassion. It’s deeply upsetting, and a climactic scene in which we fully learn how Gracie views her own unforgivable behavior is all the more disturbing because it pulls us away from old stereotypes rather than back into them; like Mo’Nique’s character in “Precious” (2009), Gracie turns out to be a monster who explains herself to herself by believing she’s a victim, which makes her both more pathetic and more frightening. Haynes and the screenwriter Samy Burch contour their story to our era, one that’s informed by charged, competing ideas about fragile masculinity, reflexive misogyny and consent; at the same time, they take mommy issues to their most forbidden extreme. Under their penetrating gaze, no mother or son is inhuman, and neither is unscathed.

It’s no surprise that gay men, so often caricatured by popular culture as symptoms, victims or byproducts of bad parenting, are doing the most to demolish mothering clichés — partly by addressing them in a more direct way than the sidelong smirks with which straight culture has often contented itself. The most daring of the many daring things that Andrew Haigh does in “All of Us Strangers,” a portrait of Adam (Andrew Scott), a gay man undergoing a lonely midlife crisis, is to set us up for what decades of experience has taught us will eventually be a blistering confrontation before going a completely different way. Like “Beau Is Afraid,” Haigh’s film returns Adam to the threshold of his youth, literally depositing him at the doorstep of his childhood home, where he seeks answers from a mother (Claire Foy) who, for reasons not apparent until the story’s end, cannot provide them. Why introduce a mother in Act I unless she’s a loaded gun you’re prepared to fire in Act II?

But in “All of Us Strangers,” there’s no gun, just a sad man who has survived some brutal experiences to make it to his 40s but who still can’t get his emotional life together. Although the reason is partly rooted in childhood trauma, Haigh refuses to root it in bad mothering. Adam’s mother may have her limitations but, by the end of the movie, we’ve come to understand her and his father as loving, concerned and basically accepting. Haigh isn’t interested in blame laying; he makes clear that Adam’s life is not her fault or, for that matter, anything for which a culprit needs to be found. His real subject seems to be the chasms that prevent us — or that we use to prevent ourselves — from truly understanding the pain of those closest to us. It’s a tragedy without the relief of an easy villain. In a way, Adam can be seen as yet another man with an unhealthy mother fixation. But look closely and the movie is saying something different, something that feels like a step forward: She belongs in his life, and his problem isn’t her.
