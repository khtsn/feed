A Key Measure of Wages Grew at a Moderate Pace This Summer

The Employment Cost Index, which Federal Reserve officials watch closely as a gauge of pay trends, has come down since last year.

Oct. 31, 2023, 8:52 a.m. ET

A measure of pay and benefits that officials at the Federal Reserve have been watching closely as they try to gauge the heat of the labor market grew at a moderate pace over the summer.

The Employment Cost Index, a quarterly inflation measure from the Labor Department that tracks changes in wages and benefits, climbed 1.1 percent in the third quarter of 2023 versus the prior three months. That was slightly faster than the 1 percent that economists expected and up from the previous 1 percent reading.

Still, that pace of growth is a deceleration from a series of rapid quarterly gains in 2022. And on an annual basis, wage gains continue to slow: The employment cost measure picked up by 4.3 percent on a yearly basis, down from the 4.5 percent reading in the previous report.

The index averaged 2.2 percent yearly gains in the decade leading up to the pandemic, underscoring that although the pace of wage gains is coming down, today’s pace remains quick.

Rapid wage gains are good news for households, but they can present a challenge for economic policymakers. Officials at the Federal Reserve often worry that it will be hard to fully snuff out inflation if pay gains are climbing quickly. Companies that are paying workers higher wages are likely to try to charge more to cover their costs.

Fed officials are meeting this week to discuss what to do next with interest rates, and are widely expected to hold borrowing costs steady at the conclusion of their two-day meeting on Wednesday. They have already raised interest rates to a range of 5.25 to 5.5 percent, up from near-zero in March 2022, in a bid to slow inflation.

Higher rates make it more expensive to borrow money to buy a house, purchase a car or expand a business. As companies hire less voraciously and demand wanes, wage growth should slow and companies should find it more difficult to raise prices without losing customers. That chain reaction is expected to put a lid on inflation.

But the labor market’s cool-down has been an unexpectedly bumpy one. Job gains have slowed somewhat, but they remain much faster than many economists had expected after so much Fed action. That is why Fed officials are so closely watching wages.

If pay growth is more modest even as companies are hiring, it suggests that continued job gains are being driven by an improving supply of applicants, and that the labor market is still coming back into balance.

The logic is simple: If the job market were running hot, companies would be paying more and more as they tried to poach needed employees from one another. That would keep pay gains climbing swiftly. If it was cooling toward a more normal level of tightness, economists would expect wage gains to pull back.

Another closely watched measure of wage growth, the average hourly earnings index that is released each month as part of the nonfarm payrolls report, has been showing steady moderation.

That gauge is useful because it comes out frequently, but it is also susceptible to data quirks. It tends to move around as the composition of the work force shifts. If a lot of low-wage workers gain jobs, for instance, the hourly earnings measure can drift lower.

Given that, Fed officials closely monitor the Employment Cost Index, which avoids some of the data pitfalls that afflict other wage measures.
