Lawmakers voted to repeal an 1864 law that bans nearly all abortions. Here’s what to know about the fight over the law.

  • 294

Anna Betts

May 1, 2024, 5:59 p.m. ET

Arizona lawmakers on Wednesday voted to repeal an 1864 law that bans nearly all abortions, which went into effect after a State Supreme Court ruled in April to reinstate it.

Wednesday’s vote was a culmination of a fevered effort to repeal the law that made abortion a central focus in the battleground state, coming after two failed efforts last month.

A bill to repeal the law narrowly passed in the Republican-controlled State Senate with the support of every Democratic senator and two Republicans who were breaking with anti-abortion conservatives in their own party. It now goes to Gov. Katie Hobbs, a Democrat, who is expected to sign it.

Here’s what to know about the fight over the 1864 law.

What is the 1864 law?

The law, which was on the books long before Arizona achieved statehood in 1912, outlaws abortion from the moment of conception, except when necessary to save the life of the mother, and it makes no exceptions for cases of rape or incest. It bans all types of abortions, including medication abortions.

The U.S. Supreme Court’s 1973 decision in Roe v. Wade overrode many state abortion laws, but after the court reversed that decision nearly two years ago, some states began once again to enact restrictions. In Arizona’s case, the Legislature passed a law in 2022 banning the procedure after 15 weeks of pregnancy.

Though the 1864 law had lain dormant for decades, however, it was never formally repealed. So supporters and opponents of abortion rights in Arizona have been fighting in court over whether it can be enforced, or whether it had effectively been superseded by decades of other state laws that regulate and restrict abortion, including the 2022 statute.

Doctors prosecuted under the 1864 law could face fines and prison terms of two to five years for providing, supplying or administering care to a pregnant woman.

What did the Arizona Supreme Court ruling say?

On April 9, the Arizona Supreme Court ruled in a 4-to-2 decision that the pre-statehood law was “now enforceable.”

The court said that because the federal right to abortion had been overturned by the U.S. Supreme Court, there was no federal or state law preventing Arizona from enforcing the near-total ban from 1864. It noted that the State Legislature had not created a right to abortion when it passed the 15-week ban in 2022.

“Physicians are now on notice that all abortions, except those necessary to save a woman’s life, are illegal,” the court wrote.

What happens now?

If Ms. Hobbs signs the repeal bill, the state would abide by the 2022 statute banning the procedure after 15 weeks.

But that bill would not take effect until 90 days after the legislative session ends, so it is possible there would be a period with the near-total ban in place.

The 1864 ban did not go into effect when the court reinstated it on April 9, and it was unclear how it would be enforced. The ruling has been put on hold, and the court sent the case back to a lower court to hear additional arguments about the law’s constitutionality.

How could this affect the 2024 election?

The stakes could be significant for races up and down the ballot in Arizona this fall.

President Biden and Democratic officials have blamed former President Donald J. Trump for the dwindling of access to abortion care in America. On April 8, Mr. Trump said that abortion rights should be left up to the states.

Democrats, who seized on abortion to win campaigns for governor and attorney general in Arizona in 2022, said the issue of reproductive rights would galvanize their supporters.

Representative Ruben Gallego, running unopposed in the July Democratic primary for the U.S. Senate, criticized the April 9 ruling and tied it to his Republican opponent, Kari Lake, who called the near-total 1864 ban a “great law” when she was running for governor in 2022.

Ms. Lake has been emblematic of a Republican shift on abortion. She came out against a federal abortion ban last year, while still backing Arizona’s 15-week restriction, and said on April 9 that it was “abundantly clear that the pre-statehood law is out of step with Arizonans.” Then, in an interview with The Idaho Dispatch on April 20, she flipped her stance and supported the ban.

Representatives Juan Ciscomani and David Schweikert, two Republicans facing re-election challenges in closely divided districts, criticized the court’s ruling and urged state lawmakers to address it.

Where else is abortion restricted?

Twenty-one states currently ban or restrict abortion earlier in pregnancy than what Roe v. Wade established.
