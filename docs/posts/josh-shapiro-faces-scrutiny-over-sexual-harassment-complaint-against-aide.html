By Sharon LaFraniere

Aug. 3, 2024

Gov. Josh Shapiro of Pennsylvania, who is on the short list to be Kamala Harris’s running mate, is facing renewed criticism for his handling of a sexual harassment complaint against a longtime top aide.

The state agreed to pay $295,000 last September to settle the complaint against Michael A. Vereb, then Mr. Shapiro’s head of legislative affairs and one of his closest advisers. An employee had accused Mr. Vereb of making repeated and graphic sexual overtures and then criticizing her job performance after she refused him. She resigned rather than continue to work for him, her only other option, she said in her written complaint.

Mr. Vereb, 57, kept his post for six months after his accuser first alleged misconduct. He resigned only after local reporters obtained a copy of the employee’s complaint, weeks after the settlement had been secretly reached. The governor’s office praised Mr. Vereb for his “dedicated service” when he left.

Mr. Shapiro weathered scrutiny last fall over his office’s response to the case, but it reignited in recent weeks as he became a front-runner to join Vice President Harris on the ballot. She is expected to announce her choice by Tuesday.

The National Women’s Defense League, a nonpartisan group founded in the wake of the #MeToo movement, asked the Harris campaign this past week to look into the case, saying Mr. Shapiro’s office “should have done a better job” in both preventing sexual harassment and handling the complaint. Democrats, including a candidate for Pennsylvania treasurer, have also taken aim at the governor for his office’s response to the allegations.

In a statement on Friday night, Manuel Bonder, a spokesman for Mr. Shapiro, said the governor “was not aware of the complaint or investigation until months after the complaint was filed.” Mr. Shapiro should have been notified of the allegations sooner, Mr. Bonder said, and he has now ordered that he be immediately informed of any such complaint against a senior staff or cabinet member.

“Governor Shapiro has no tolerance for harassment in the workplace or anywhere else,” the statement said.

Mr. Vereb declined to comment. The New York Times is withholding the name of his accuser, who has chosen not to be publicly identified.

It is unclear whether the incident would hurt Mr. Shapiro’s chances of securing a spot on the ticket. Pennsylvania is a critical swing state, Mr. Shapiro is broadly popular and supporters say he could help Ms. Harris expand her appeal to moderate Democrats and independents. Supporters also note that Mr. Shapiro’s nearly two-decade record in public office has been relatively free of controversies. The Harris campaign did not respond to a request for comment.

But the case could undercut Mr. Shapiro’s best-known effort: a grand jury report he oversaw as Pennsylvania’s attorney general in which some 300 Catholic priests were accused of molesting more than 1,000 children over 70 years in Pennsylvania. Mr. Shapiro has also presented himself as a staunch defender of women’s rights.

Until recently, criticism of how the governor’s office reacted to the complaint was largely restricted to female Republican lawmakers. Kim Ward, a high-ranking Republican state senator, was among the most vocal, calling Mr. Shapiro’s response “appalling and infuriating.”

Then last week, Erin McClelland, the Democratic candidate for Pennsylvania state treasurer, posted a message on X saying — without naming Mr. Shapiro — that she favored a vice president who “doesn’t sweep sexual harassment under the rug.” Colleen Kennedy, a member of the state’s Democratic committee, criticized the nondisclosure agreement that bars the parties from discussing the complaint, telling The Times that the case showed a “systematic failure” that should disqualify Mr. Shapiro as a vice-presidential pick.

Nondisclosure agreements have been increasingly attacked on the grounds that they cover up sexual misconduct. At least 18 states have restricted them in sexual misconduct cases, but not Pennsylvania.

Mr. Shapiro’s spokesman said the governor supported eliminating nondisclosure agreements when the complainant did not want one, including in sexual misconduct cases.

Mr. Vereb, a Republican, met Mr. Shapiro about two decades ago when they served in the state legislature. Both represented one of the state’s richest, most populous counties, bordering Philadelphia. When Mr. Shapiro became attorney general in 2017, he appointed Mr. Vereb as head of government affairs, and then picked him as his secretary of legislative affairs when he became governor in 2023.

The woman who accused Mr. Vereb of sexual harassment had been recently hired as one of his deputies. Her complaint accused him of a string of inappropriate comments, including suggesting that he could manage a female lawmaker because she wanted to have sex with him.

After she had been on the job less than a month, the employee alleged, Mr. Vereb suggested she wear more revealing clothing and proposed sex in the office, comparing her to another aide who he said was too assertive to have sex with. When she declined, she said in her complaint, he cursed at her. She said she refused again when he called that evening, apparently drunk, and suggested a sexual relationship.

About a week later, the complaint said, Mr. Vereb, his top deputy and the director of administration for the governor’s office called her into a meeting to raise concerns about her job performance.

She then recounted Mr. Vereb’s behavior in detail, according to the complaint, and said she believed that the surprise performance review constituted retaliation. She said she repeated her allegations to Mr. Shapiro’s deputy chief of staff, asking her to tell the chief of staff, and saying that she felt she “had no choice but to leave.”

The administrative director told her that an investigation would be launched but no action would be taken until it was over, according to the complaint. That evening, the employee said, the director called her repeatedly to ask if she wanted to resign. She received a written confirmation of her resignation the next day, she said.

An official with the governor’s office, who spoke on the condition of anonymity to discuss sensitive information, said the employee was not offered a different job away from Mr. Vereb because she resigned immediately. Her formal complaint, filed in March 2023, was ultimately handled by the state Human Relations Commission, which enforces civil rights laws.

Debra S. Katz, a civil rights lawyer who has handled many high-profile sexual harassment cases, reviewed the complaint at the request of The Times. She said the incident was badly mishandled.

As soon as the employee alleged the harassment and retaliation to her superiors, she said, officials should have asked what she needed so she could continue to work — including reassignment — while her claims were investigated. Ms. Katz also said the amount of the settlement reflected the seriousness of the accusations.

The case settled at the start of September, but the outcome did not become public for weeks. Mr. Vereb resigned at the end of the month.

The complaint was not the first time that Mr. Vereb had been accused of inappropriate behavior toward a woman. Cathleen Palm, the founder of a nonprofit child advocacy group, said in an interview that she complained in 2018 that Mr. Vereb, then a top aide to Mr. Shapiro in the attorney general’s office, verbally abused her for a half-hour on the phone after she failed to support a proposed change to child protective services laws.

“You are nobody, and by the time Josh and I are through with you, you will be less than nobody,” she said he screamed at her. She said he also called her “a friend of pedophiles,” even after she protested she had been sexually abused as a child. “I was devastated,” she said.

Ms. Palm said she complained about the encounter to the chief deputy attorney general. The deputy died unexpectedly a few weeks later, she said, and she never received a reply to her complaint.

Mr. Shapiro’s spokesman said Mr. Shapiro was not notified of Ms. Palm’s complaint. “This alleged behavior would be completely inappropriate,” he said.

Susan C. Beachy, Jack Begg and Kitty Bennett contributed research.
