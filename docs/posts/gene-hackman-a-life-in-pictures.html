Gene Hackman, a Life in Pictures

Gene Hackman, a celebrated actor whose death at 95 was announced on Thursday, stood out in Hollywood for his ability not to stand out.

Not until he was 42 did he make his star turn, winning the Oscar for best actor for playing a gruff narcotics detective in “The French Connection.” But at that point he already had more than 30 television and film credits and a reputation for charming intensity that would stay with him throughout his career.

A tall man with thinning hair and a deep voice that was befitting a former Marine, he is easily remembered for distinctive mustaches and tweed jackets. Yet he was equally convincing in roles as a paranoid communications expert, an archnemesis of a superhero, a big-hearted basketball coach, a sinister sheriff and an eccentric patriarch of a family of troubled geniuses.

And if he seemed to some to have appeared out of nowhere in the 1970s as a fully formed star, he disappeared just as abruptly, doing one final film in 2004 and then walking away without any formal declaration that he had retired. He spent his remaining years in Santa Fe, N.M., painting and sculpting and staying out of the spotlight.

He was Hollywood’s Everyman, but had a career — and a life — that few could even attempt to recreate.

Mr. Hackman made an impression on Warren Beatty in 1964 despite a small part in the film “Lilith.” Mr. Beatty subsequently brought Mr. Hackman along for “Bonnie and Clyde” (1967), in which he managed to thrive in a cast that included, from left, Estelle Parsons, Mr. Beatty, Faye Dunaway and Michael J. Pollard. The performance earned Mr. Hackman an Oscar nomination for best supporting actor.

Mr. Hackman played Jimmy Doyle, a detective nicknamed Popeye who was not particularly concerned with the rules, in “The French Connection” — for which he won the Academy Award for best actor — and in “The French Connection 2.” In The New York Times’s review of the sequel, Vincent Canby said of Mr. Hackman’s character, “Popeye hasn’t gone soft or genteel in ‘French Connection II.’ If anything, he appears to have become a bit more of a magnificent boor.”

Mr. Hackman celebrating his first Oscar, for best actor, for “The French Connection,” which won best picture; alongside Jane Fonda, who won best actress for “Klute,” in 1972. They were flanked by Philip D’Antoni, a producer of “The French Connection,” and William Friedkin, its director, who also won an Oscar.

Mr. Friedkin directing Mr. Hackman during the filming of “The French Connection.” The black hat that Mr. Hackman wore in the “French Connection” films was instantly iconic, and they surrounded him with talent, like his co-star Roy Scheider.

In “Poseidon Adventure” (1972), Mr. Hackman was part of an ensemble cast in a film that was a huge hit even if critics did not seem to care for it. In The New York Times, Mr. Canby, the film critic, described an action sequence and then added: “Shortly after this big scene, you stop worrying about acting and writing and logic and probability. You simply enjoy the engineering feats of the moviemakers, which are so effective that they touch even outrageous things with credibility.”

In 1975, Mr. Hackman starred in Richard Brooks’s entirely forgettable “Bite the Bullet” alongside James Coburn. The film was panned by critics — a headline in The New York Times read “ ‘Bite the Bullet’ Is a Richard Brooks Error” — but featured a top-tier mustache from Mr. Hackman.

Mr. Hackman’s career saw him cross paths with numerous stars, including Liza Minnelli, who was his co-star in “Lucky Lady” (1975).

Mr. Hackman was unforgettable as Lex Luthor in the “Superman” films, and he attended the premiere of the first installment in 1978 with his children.

Mr. Hackman, pictured here in 1980, was known for his gruff demeanor, though that only made the occasional smile look even larger.

Mr. Hackman had a brief stint in professional auto racing in the 1980s, competing in a few events, but ultimately decided he wasn’t a fit for the sport. “I think that, to be really good, you have to be extremely selfish, which is probably true in a lot of professions, including acting,” he told The Los Angeles Times. “So I could maybe fulfill that part of it. But I think you need a tougher personality than I have. You have to be harder than I’m capable of.”

“Hoosiers,” which was released in 1986, has its share of critics, but Mr. Hackman’s performance as Coach Norman Dale is arguably his most memorable. Janet Maslin’s review in The New York Times summed up much of Mr. Hackman’s career: “Mr. Hackman is exactly right for the role of Coach Dale, bringing shrewdness and a varied temperament to a man who might otherwise have seemed bland.”

In 1985, Mr. Hackman managed to produce a commanding performance in “Target,” a film that was largely written off by critics as an implausible adventure that was being carried by an actor capable of far better fare.

Mr. Hackman and Morgan Freeman, standing behind, memorably starred together in “Unforgiven” in 1992 — garnering Mr. Hackman his second best actor Oscar. They did not recapture that magic among audiences and critics in 2000 with “Under Suspicion,” a remake of a French film.

Mr. Hackman gave what Ms. Maslin, the Times critic, called a “superlative performance as a wily old racist” in “The Chamber.” The film came out in 1996 and lived in the shadows of a more successful death row film (“Dead Man Walking”) and a more successful adaptation of a John Grisham novel (“A Time to Kill”).

In “Behind Enemy Lines,” Mr. Hackman, a former Marine, starred as a rear admiral in the Navy. That he was a military man with a renegade streak fit in with the rest of his career.

Despite having their acting primes coincide over a period of 40 years, Mr. Hackman and Dustin Hoffman did not work together until “Runaway Jury” in 2003. Mr. Hackman’s performance was the penultimate of his career. (His last was “Welcome to Mooseport” in 2004.)

Robin Williams and Michael Caine joined Mr. Hackman in 2003 to celebrate his Cecil B. DeMille Award, an honorary Golden Globe for “outstanding contributions to the world of entertainment.”

Mr. Hackman saved one of his most memorable roles for his final years of acting, bringing an irreverence to “The Royal Tenenbaums” in 2001. He stood out in an ensemble cast that included Anjelica Huston, Gwyneth Paltrow, Ben Stiller, Luke Wilson, Owen Wilson and Bill Murray. Mr. Hackman’s antics with his grandsons showed a playful side that was sometimes missing from his earlier films. He also delivered showstopping lines like: “Hell of a damn grave. Wish it were mine.”
