Harris Has Made Up Ground in Nevada. But the Road Ahead Is Steep.

Polls show Democrats’ chances have improved in Nevada since Vice President Kamala Harris was elevated. But she could face an uphill battle to win over undecided voters who had tuned out completely.

Aug. 1, 2024Updated 2:44 p.m. ET

Bryce Brady, a former high school English teacher turned presidential campaign operative, took to the front of the room with first-day-of-school enthusiasm.

It had scarcely been two days since President Biden had ended his re-election bid and passed the reins to Vice President Kamala Harris, and the North Las Vegas campaign office was still plastered with Biden-Harris stickers and signs.

But every one of the dozens of volunteers in the room spoke of a newfound optimism about Ms. Harris’s chances. A few had come for the first time, suddenly happy to get involved in a race they had almost given up on.

“I know I’m preaching to the choir here, but you all maybe are talking to others in the community who say their vote doesn’t matter,” Mr. Brady said. “That could not be more false.”

He pointed to a central fact: registered nonpartisan voters now outnumber both registered Democrats and Republicans in Nevada. There would be no winning without them.

Mr. Brady compared these voters, who make up roughly a third of registered voters in the state, to drivers sitting in a parking lot. They could go either way, and choose to vote for either Ms. Harris or Donald J. Trump. Or they could just not move — they could simply stay home.

These voters had something else in common too, he said: They had lost trust in all kinds of American institutions, from the news media to political parties. But research showed they still trusted friends and neighbors.

“These are not going to be one-off conversations,” he told the assembled volunteers. “We want to share personal stories.”

It was a dose of reality. Even as the Harris campaign has seen a surge of support and enthusiasm from Democratic donors and volunteers, the party faces challenges as it works to win over the significant slice of potential voters who had tuned out the race for months, saying they were deeply unsatisfied with both major-party presidential candidates and unconvinced that their vote would make a difference.

After the hourlong strategy session ended, several volunteers paused to consider whether they had friends or acquaintances who were not already planning to vote for Ms. Harris and could be convinced to do so.

Most concluded they did not. They were Democrats who mostly spoke with other Democrats, and they were feeding off each other’s euphoric optimism.

“We’re all thrilled — but I do question how much bad information exists out there, and how we get past all that,” said Terry Adams, 70, who runs a small casino with his wife, Hiroe Adams, 55, who attended the session with him.

The couple recalled hearing their own daughter repeat falsehoods about Ms. Harris, including the incorrect belief that the vice president did not support gay rights.

They also mentioned a middle-aged man who works with them at the casino and has repeatedly told them he does not believe it matters who controls the White House. “Maybe he’s who we need,” Mr. Adams said.

There are few key states in which Democrats have as much room to grow as Nevada, the most diverse swing state in the nation. Though voters in the state have sided with the Democratic presidential nominee in each election since 2008, the party’s margin of victory has narrowed in recent cycles. Polls taken before Mr. Biden dropped out of the race showed him losing in Nevada to Mr. Trump.

Ms. Harris is widely expected to gain the most ground nationally with the demographic groups who had most soured on Mr. Biden — young voters, Hispanic voters, Black male voters and disengaged voters. And several polls released this week showed Ms. Harris erasing Mr. Trump’s lead in Nevada.

But winning a state that the Biden campaign had all but acknowledged had slipped out of reach will not happen easily. And more than three dozen interviews with voters, organizers and strategists in and around Las Vegas during the first week of Ms. Harris’s candidacy showed that her campaign still had much work to do in the sprint ahead.

“Of course I am excited, but the problems and the challenges for people are still the same,” said Ángeles Reyes, an organizer with Make the Road Nevada, a liberal advocacy group, who has spent the past several weeks knocking on the doors of potential voters. As temperatures have crept to 110 degrees, few people have even answered.

“Everyone talks about the cost of housing and how much their groceries cost,” Mr. Reyes said. “People come here for opportunities, but those feel like they have gone away.”

The sprawling Las Vegas region, by far the most populous portion of the state, has long been vulnerable to boom-and-bust cycles. First to suffer, last to recover, people here often say.

By all indications, the local economy has dramatically improved since the 2020 election, when casinos on the Strip were forced to shut down amid the pandemic.

Still, longtime residents frequently complain that the cost of housing has skyrocketed as more people have moved into the state, while wages have not kept pace.

And the economy is hardly the only challenge facing Ms. Harris and Democrats.

For weeks now, Stephone Revels Sr., a 56-year-old truck driver, and a group of his friends have texted about the ups and downs of politics.

Though many of those in the group chat, all Black men, are registered nonpartisan voters, most have only ever voted for Democrats. But they have begun to sour on the party, in part because they say elected officials have done too little to address their priorities.

“They came into office saying the first order of business was to pass the George Floyd act — and we are still waiting,” Mr. Revels said, referencing a bill meant to combat racial bias and excessive use of force in policing that has stalled in Congress.

“I’m looking for some direct, tangible assistance,” he said. “You’ve got to stop talking and start acting.”

Like Mr. Revels, Gerald Mayes has told the group he is leaning toward voting for Robert F. Kennedy Jr., who is running as a third-party candidate and who is working to qualify for the state’s ballot.

Mr. Mayes, 40, who works as a consultant, said he felt even more sure in that choice since Mr. Biden dropped out.

“It feels like the Democrats held Biden up, and now they are allowed to circumvent the vote and just place whomever they want in his place,” Mr. Mayes said. “That does not feel like democracy.”

Andre-Ashley Tarvin-Pyles, 35, a teacher, agreed, saying he was frustrated by so many years of promises that electing Black politicians would lead to systemic change.

“It’s too convenient for people to show up when it serves them exclusively,” he said. “And they are absent, vacant, any other time.”

He pointed to Ms. Harris’s time as a prosecutor in California as another reason he was skeptical about supporting her, saying he thought she should have done more to keep Black men from facing harsh prison sentences.

Even Harvey Perry, 59, a teacher who had helped bring the group together and who joined an early call of Black men supporting Ms. Harris, said he would not work to convince his friends to change their mind.

“Look, we’re all tired of just hearing things,” he said.

Still, Molly Forgey, a Democratic consultant who is helping the Harris campaign in Nevada, said she believed Ms. Harris’s candidacy put the party “in a totally different place.”

“Nevada is going to be a linchpin, and she can galvanize and turn out Black women and A.A.P.I. women in the way only she can,” Ms. Forgey said. “We know the makeup of the state and the work that requires — motivating and energizing voters to turn out.”

But at another campaign office, in nearby Henderson, Nev., there were signs of a challenge.

Chania Scott, a bank teller, came to an event at the office largely out of loyalty to a friend who works for the campaign.

The night was billed as a “paint-and-sip” event, and campaign officials instructed the small crowd to paint blue and yellow flowers as symbols of Democratic values.

“Honestly, I haven’t been paying that much attention, because it all seems negative,” said Ms. Scott, 23. She had brought along a friend from work, Alex Becerra, who took an even more pessimistic view.

“I don’t think our vote matters,” said Ms. Becerra, 22. “I mean, things are just going to continue as they always have, whether we vote or not. The people in charge aren’t listening to what we want.”
