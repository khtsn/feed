Meet Joan Jonas, One of America’s Most Elusive Artists

arts and letters

Joan Jonas’s maximalist, category-defying work combines video, performance, folklore, sculpture and ecology. At 87, she still has no intention of simplifying anything.

JOAN JONAS, 87, perched on a stool in a room behind the scenes at MoMA, was immediately recognizable as the artist she had been — compact, tense, intense — when she emerged as a figure in New York’s downtown scene in the late 1960s. In an essay published many years later, the composer Alvin Curran recalled Jonas’s stature in that environment: “On the streets, children cry out, ‘Here comes Joan Jonas,’” he wrote, adding that some even wanted to be what she was when they grew up: a performance artist.

Jonas — who prefers to be described as a visual artist, given the range of her work — has been a figure of fascination since 1968, when she started experimenting in 16-millimeter film with a short called “Wind,” featuring human figures struggling against the gales on a seashore, their enigmatic movements competing with the power of the elements themselves. Nature, the human form, the mysteries of rites and myth — all of it has featured prominently in the work of Jonas, who has, in the past 50 or so years, evolved, gone (briefly) underappreciated and resurged, all the while innovating, even as her recursive body of work kept referring back to itself. This month, she’s finally receiving a hometown retrospective at MoMA, a tribute on a scale she’s already had in cities such as Milan, London and Munich.

“You’re coming, right?” said Jonas, speaking into a cellphone at the museum in late December. There was a hint of tension in her voice — a curator from Europe on the other end of the line had better be coming — and her face relaxed upon hearing a reassurance. It was important to her that he, and many others in her world, see this collection of her work, its totality and its range.

Jonas is at least as influential and groundbreaking as her near contemporary Marina Abramović, 77, but has somehow never achieved quite the same name recognition. Whereas Abramović’s work is confrontational and direct — in her 2010 MoMA show, “The Artist Is Present,” the performance artist sat in a chair for hours a day as visitors lined up to sit across from her — Jonas’s work is, by contrast, multilayered, elusive, evocative and difficult to articulate concisely, if at all. Within one exhibition — “They Come to Us Without a Word,” her triumphant work representing the United States at the 2015 Venice Biennale — Jonas combined audio recordings of ghost stories from Cape Breton Island, Nova Scotia; video imagery of herself (masked, Druid-like, as she draws on a chalkboard); and various representations of bees and birds — all of it a celebration of a natural world both haunting and defamiliarizing. Mirrored glass made in Venice’s Murano for the installation invoked some of her earliest works, in which she used mirrors in her performances to show fractured images of the audience to itself.

The exhibit at MoMA opens, appropriately enough, with “Wind,” then moves more or less chronologically, taking the viewer quickly to Jonas’s iconic 1972 work “Vertical Roll.” On a screen, images of Jonas in the garb of a belly dancer or wearing a headdress or various other costumes appear on a monitor fleetingly, seemingly trapped in squares that keep rolling downward, as if demanding those watching to be conscious of the effort of perception. “Vertical Roll” takes a technical glitch familiar to TV watchers at the time — a problem with what was called the vertical hold — and turns it into art, relying on an elaborate setup of cameras and monitors. Jonas accompanies the images with the sharp, synchronized sound of wooden blocks colliding, a noise jolting enough to hold the threat of violence. It might also be heard as the sound of centuries of female iconography being smashed by this innovative art form. One watches the video, mesmerized, and later, the sound of those blocks echoes for hours: I heard it in the cracking of an egg, the banging of a fork against a bowl, the familiar rendered alien, worthy of being recorded and examined.

On the subject of her own work, Jonas, like many artists, is somewhat cryptic, which is wholly appropriate for someone whose pieces require viewers to engage actively, giving the work meaning — or even completing it — themselves. “Perception is something that really is an important part of my thinking,” she said. “How does the audience perceive my work? How does my work change their perception?” If an artist like Abramović wants her audience to stare her in the eye, Jonas seems to want viewers to turn inward as they look outward, to question the very act of how they see and through what lens.

As one of the first artists who used video while simultaneously performing live for that medium, Jonas was a pioneer in certain kinds of art that rely on technology. She also came up with what she calls a “viewing box,” a mounted oblong for the display of props and video. Jonas, in addition to her innovations in video, is now considered to be among the forerunners of artists training the full force of their creative powers on the climate crisis. “I would say that Joan Jonas is a foundational and transformative figure not just in the realm of video and performance and feminist art but in art that moves away from anthropocentrism,” says the artist Michael Rakowitz, a former student of Jonas’s at M.I.T., where she taught regularly from 1998 to 2014.

Most artists would consider a MoMA retrospective — even more so than an appearance in the Biennale — a gratifying recognition of one’s lifelong efforts. “I don’t think that,” Jonas responded, still sitting on the stool in the room where she was meeting with Ana Janevski, the curator overseeing the show. Janevski exchanged glances with two colleagues standing nearby, eyebrows raised, before Jonas clarified: “Because it would be too disconcerting to think about it that way.” Her voice, which often features in her work, had a low, lulling timbre; Jonas, who’s barely five feet tall, has a stillness about her that sometimes reads as wary, sometimes as highly controlled. “I can’t get excited until the whole thing happens,” she said. “I’m superstitious.”

Rather than looking exclusively backward, Jonas said she considered it essential to add new work to the retrospective. As much of her art does, the piece includes imagery of wildlife, in this case stunning, rare footage of sea creatures captured by the marine biologist David Gruber. One sequence shows a sperm whale giving birth. Positioned at the end of her survey, it would serve as a harbinger of great beginnings. The show’s title, “Joan Jonas: Good Night Good Morning,” is taken from the name of two video works, one from 1976 and one from 2006, that both depict the artist, albeit at different stages of her life, right before sleep and shortly after awakening. Her retrospective is as close to a full view as we are likely to get of everything that came between.

JONAS’S PATH TO becoming an artist started early, with her freewheeling, unpredictable parents, who sent her to the Upper East Side’s Walt Whitman School, the kind of progressive institution that let the students choose what they wanted to do each day. Because her answer was always the same — “paint” — she was 7 before she learned to read. Her parents exposed her to a wide range of culture at a young age in New York — a Richard Wagner opera at the Metropolitan, musicals, magic shows, circuses. So much of her own kaleidoscopic work could be seen as an attempt to reharness the bewildered wonder that a young child feels when encountering great art or spectacle.

Jonas’s parents eventually transferred her to a more academically driven private school, in one of many rude transitions that she would endure. When she was around 5, they divorced, with her father, an aspiring writer who drank, spending his winters in Mexico, marrying a wealthy woman and living on a sailboat. Her mother, a former debutante whose grandfather founded a wrought-iron business, remarried (“same problem,” Jonas said of the new husband), and they eventually moved to Long Island, with its then-endless potato farms. The family dog, and closer proximity to nature, were the consolations for this new, painful distance from the dazzle of the city, as were summers in New Hampshire. Jonas’s connection to and curiosity about the ocean, her deep love of the natural world and animals (she drew one of her dogs so frequently that she wondered if the habit was “sick”), can be traced back to these days.

When Jonas was a young woman, her relationship to art was both deeply personal and possibly a projection of her parents’ wishes. Asked if she saw her father regularly, she said, “I wanted to, but I didn’t.” When he did appear in her life, he always reminded her that his dream for her was that she pursue art. While convention in the late ’50s called for early marriage and safe, temporary career choices for women, her father, upon learning that she was considering teaching, was outraged. “He really wanted me to be an artist,” she said.

Jonas studied art history and sculpture at Mount Holyoke College in Massachusetts, and then moved to Boston to concentrate on making art of her own at the Museum School and married the writer Gerald Jonas. The two moved to New York and, through a friend of Gerald’s, his colleague at The New Yorker Calvin Trillin, who followed contemporary art, the couple started showing up to view Happenings. The events were precursors to performance art that were loosely conceived, densely staged and unpredictable — something happened, but it wasn’t like anything that would take place in a theater. Picture the artist Jim Dine in an East Village storefront in head-to-toe silver, charging his way through an audience of men in ties and women in shirtdresses, making car noises as other artists made a cacophony of sounds (the 1960 piece, a hit, was called “Car Crash”). Although male artists like Dine and Allan Kaprow are usually associated with the movement, the novelty of the form — its freedom from millenniums of the art canon — created room, Jonas said, for women. This was especially true when the work incorporated experimental dance. After moving to New York, Jonas attended workshops with dancers who were part of the Judson Group, an experimental troupe influenced by the choreographer Merce Cunningham. “There were very few men doing that, so you didn’t have that hierarchy,” she told me when I visited her at her loft and studio on Mercer Street, where she’s lived since 1974. (By the mid-60s, she and Gerald had divorced, although she kept her married name.) “It was a good time because people were experimenting. It was a wide-open field. I felt like I was on the edge of something.”

Inspired by the work of Jorge Luis Borges and his fascination with mirrors (what Borges called “the impenetrable glass”), Jonas started making pieces in which the performers either walked on or carried mirrors, fracturing the environment but also reflecting it. Her work with mirrors evolved to invoke concerns of the women’s movement, in which she considered herself an active participant. In “Mirror Check,” a 1970 performance, Jonas, nude, slowly inspects every part of her body with a small mirror, creating shadows that splinter her body. “The audience can’t see what she sees,” Jonas has said, a feature that subverts the notion of the nude as a passive, exposed object; in her performance, the object has power, privacy, a secret knowledge of the self.

That same year, Jonas was among the first artists to start experimenting with a Sony PortaPak video camera, a relatively affordable, democratizing force that was itself challenging hierarchies of media — the very idea that images being beamed out to the world needed to have institutional authority and money behind them. The eye of the video camera was new, and it seemed to act on Jonas in profound ways. “I would just step into the performance space” — the one being captured on video — “and I’d get ideas,” she told me. For her, the act of being filmed triggered an impulse to perform. She was both submitting to the implicit command of the camera — a medium designed for the spectator’s eye — and creating that opportunity herself. A 1972 performance called “Organic Honey’s Visual Telepathy” features Jonas, in elaborate headdress and costume, as Organic Honey, an “electronic erotic seductress” who performs for the audience while watching and seemingly interacting with her own performance on a video monitor. Here, the monitor is another kind of mirror, the two-way relationship placing the audience outside her experience but also drawing its members close, as observers of her actions. Minimalism, as practiced by the likes of Donald Judd, Dan Flavin and Richard Serra — her then partner, and a sometime collaborator — was ascendant; with her mirrors, masks and costumes, Jonas consciously rejected the imperatives of the day. “I wanted to go against all that,” she said. “I wanted to develop my own language.” She cited the work of another downtown artist, Jack Smith, who also performed live and worked with multimedia, who played with feminine masks and props and helped create a culture of camp, as an influence.

At the time, New York, like video itself, was wide open for imaginative artists. In “Delay Delay” (1972), we see viewers watching a performance from the roof of a loft building on Greenwich Street. In the distance, Jonas’s performers paint a large white circle in the street as passers-by — pedestrians, drivers and, on one memorable occasion, a police officer — interact with them. In her 1973 video “Song Delay,” a performer, banging wooden blocks overhead, is rendered small and hopeless by ships gliding behind him along the Hudson River. There’s something beautiful, wistful and comedic about it; today, it looks like a frame from a Wes Anderson film. “What I think she was doing was changing the world of sensory perception,” the artist Susan Rothenberg once wrote of Jonas’s early output. “You went to a Jonas work to see one of the excruciatingly odd minds of that time make a window into her world, that would in a few hours make a window into yours.”

IN HER VIDEO performances, Jonas is direct, confident and almost confrontational; in person, she has a quiet energy, an alertness that borders on worry. (“She was somewhat nervous, but very nervy,” the writer Joan Simon once said of her.) By the door inside her apartment on Mercer is a small sign that the daughter of a neighbor made for her. “Make sure the oven is off,” it says. A chronic insomniac, Jonas often reads at night, and her work has been inspired by literature (Borges, but also the Icelandic writer Halldór Laxness and the American poet Susan Howe). The floor of her home is littered with stacks of books that recall cairns, or even sculptures; the walls are hung with small pieces of art, mostly tributes from friends: “a drawing that’s poetic,” as Jonas described it, from the painter Pat Steir, as well as two other drawings by the conceptual artist Lawrence Weiner. Also hanging on the wall: a drawing that Serra gave her on her 70th birthday, a self-portrait of himself — styled after Picasso, no less. (“Typical,” commented an assistant in the kitchen.)

Jonas now rents out a third of the loft, which is filled with light and decorated with simply arranged things — wooden birds, shells, rocks — that evoke the beach, a beloved and rich creative space for Jonas, who bought acres of land on Cape Breton Island in the ’70s near other artists of her circle, from the director JoAnne Akalaitis to the composer Philip Glass. A quote from a Rainer Werner Fassbinder film title is transposed onto a tray by the artist Rirkrit Tiravanija and casually placed on a shelf in her kitchen among an earthenware jug from Japan and mugs made by a friend: “Fear eats the soul.”

It’s no accident that such a quote has pride of place in Jonas’s home. “I was interested in things that scared me,” she said of her own work. “I always trusted that fear is to get close to something that you’re scared of or is maybe interesting. Now I don’t work that way. But in the beginning, and for many years, if I was scared of something, I thought, ‘Oh, I have to do it’: making anything, getting in front of an audience. Certainly I was afraid of being nude. That wasn’t easy. That was just one obvious example.” Stage fright consumed her. “I used to wear masks to cover my face because I didn’t want my face to be seen.” She also confronted the fears she felt about making use of a wall that Serra constructed for her, a huge wooden structure, 8 feet high and 12 feet wide, that hung from the ceiling of a loft on Grand Street where she lived. Handles were attached to it so that she and other artists could climb it for a performance. She had a nightmare about that wall, although she ultimately thought it was the basis for a compelling performance that she conceived. “I never filmed it,” she said. “I’m sorry I didn’t.”

Even the act of arranging the MoMA show proves how much Jonas’s collective work defies easy collection or categorization. The process of choosing pieces, many featuring numerous elements — masks, clothing, drawings, headdresses, books, props — from so enduring and varied a career was a yearslong endeavor for the museum, a puzzle that needed solving by both Jonas and the show’s curator alike. Janevski and the curatorial team spent hours opening drawer after drawer full of sketches.

Drawing, for Jonas, has always been a kind of meditative practice, and she had bureaus filled with hundreds of works on paper. Many are simple, even spare, but also arresting — they evoke the energy and speed with which they were created. She draws to record the world around her, she said, the way some people use cameras; but drawings are also an innovative part of her performance art. She’s known for handicapping herself as she draws: sketching by looking not at the paper but instead at a monitor capturing her in the act of drawing, or drawing on canvas that covers her face, so that the audience sees her making her own mask, the act both self-obliterating and self-generating. “When I draw, I don’t have total control,” she said. “So for me, drawing is also kind of an adventure. That’s what interests me, is I get an image I didn’t expect.” (Jonas’s drawings will also be exhibited in a major new show that also opens this month, “Animal, Vegetable, Mineral,” at the Drawing Center in New York.)

That day at MoMA, Jonas and Janevski were talking in the museum’s model room, where doll-size configurations of exhibits are imagined, and maquettes of the museum’s various spaces, each for a different forthcoming show, clutter the tables. Staring at the 6-by-7-foot model for her exhibit, which even at that scale captured the sweep of her works, Jonas wondered about a few missing long mirrors (a callback to her 1969 and 1970 works “Mirror Piece I” and “Mirror Piece II”) that would make the audience visible to itself. Janevski assured her they would be there. Making the show clear and legible entailed difficult choices, given the vast amount of rich historical material in Jonas’s home and basement, including a trove of notebooks and sketches and journal entries from the ’70s. When the process was far enough along that few additions could be made, the curators discovered that Jonas had more notebooks from subsequent decades that she hadn’t exactly kept hidden but also hadn’t mentioned. In the process of being curated, she had revealed herself to be, as she is in her art, both visible and impossible to find.

The model revealed little about the new work for the show. Jonas already knew a lot: Along with that whale giving way to new life, it would feature a vampire squid, figures dancing, music and video projects. But like many of Jonas’s works, it would likely change over time and become part of something wholly new. “It’s not done,” she said and showed her small smile. “I’m never done.”
