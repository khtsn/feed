Ron DeSantis Launches His Campaign

Gov. Ron DeSantis of Florida — who rose to conservative stardom in recent years with a flurry of punches to America’s political left — made his long-awaited entrance into the 2024 presidential race this afternoon.

“We need the courage to lead and the strength to win,” DeSantis said in a video announcing his campaign. “I’m running for president to lead our great American comeback.”

DeSantis made an appearance on Twitter to declare his candidacy, but the event ran into major technical problems. Follow our live coverage here.

The entry of DeSantis, who drew attention for his restriction-averse handling of the coronavirus pandemic, comes at a pivotal moment for the Republican Party: Its voters must choose between aligning once more behind Donald Trump — who lost in 2020 and continues to rage falsely about a stolen election — or uniting around a new challenger to take on President Biden.

“The Republican primary is essentially a referendum on Donald Trump,” my colleague Trip Gabriel, who covers politics, said. “And DeSantis has long been seen as the candidate for Republicans who want Trumpism without the chaos."

So far, Trump seems to have the stronger hand. He leads by roughly 30 percentage points in recent polls and some major Republican donors have voiced skepticism of DeSantis. But, behind DeSantis is a political group with a $200 million plan — which includes building an army of organizers to flood the states with the first nominating contests — to help him secure the Republican nomination.

Tina Turner, the earthshaking soul singer whose rasping vocals, sexual magnetism and explosive energy made her an unforgettable live performer and one of the most successful recording artists of all time, died today at her home in Switzerland.

Ukrainians were probably behind Kremlin attack, U.S. says

U.S. officials said the drone attack on the Kremlin this month was most likely orchestrated by one of Ukraine’s special military or intelligence units. American intelligence agencies reached their preliminary assessment in part through intercepted communications in which Ukrainian officials said they believed their country was responsible. U.S. officials say their level of confidence that the Ukrainian government directly authorized the Kremlin drone attack is “low,” but that is because intelligence agencies do not yet have specific evidence identifying which operatives were involved.

U.S. finds malicious Chinese code in Guam

American intelligence agencies and Microsoft announced today that they had detected a mysterious computer code, which Microsoft said was installed by a Chinese government hacking group, in telecommunications systems in Guam and elsewhere in the U.S. The finding raised alarms because Guam, with its Pacific ports and vast American air base, would be a centerpiece of any American military response to an invasion or blockade of Taiwan.

Turning back the clock on baseball

We’re now roughly two months into the M.L.B. season, and one thing is clear: A series of rule changes — including the new pitch clock — has led to games that evoke the 1980s more than the 2020s. (For the first time in almost 40 years, an average game now lasts less than 2 hours and 40 minutes.)

As my colleague Tyler Kepner put it, a whole lot of baseball’s down time is gone, and nobody wants it back.
