Why Wasn’t DeSantis the Guy?

Katherine Miller

Right before the blizzard conditions hit Iowa ahead of the caucus, in a barbecue place with arcade games and waiters in red T-shirts weaving through reporters with beers and baskets of fried food, Ron DeSantis came onstage, as he does, to Poison’s “Nothin’ but a Good Time.”

This is a fratty song, and the vibe of the place was retro, much like a T-shirt I saw a guy wearing at a DeSantis event in 2022. The back read “Can’t Miss DeSantis” and featured a cartoon drawing of Mr. DeSantis, flanked by palm trees, playing beer pong.

The existence of the T-shirt suggests that it once seemed possible, to someone anyway, to adopt the MAGA intellectual ethos of using the state to rebalance society and smash ideological enemies, and also be relaxed, normal, above it. Or maybe the intellectual part was never involved: There was a kind of conservative who liked the idea of a young governor making the libs cry from time to time, but whose fundamental premise was “the Free State of Florida,” where a person could get back to living their lives, unbothered. And that’s where you’d find the theoretical Mr. DeSantis, ironically playing beer pong at a Bucs tailgate after church and a Home Depot run.

This is reading a lot into a T-shirt, but ideas and realities about who candidates are, and what voters really want, seem central to understanding the last few years in politics. And even in 2022 that T-shirt stood out for the way its relaxed, fun bro diverged from the harder, more lawyerly governor who promised an updated, aggressive social conservatism that would use most tools of the state to battle academics and bureaucrats.

The DeSantis presidential pitch often was about the governor as this singular force stopping societal decline. His vision encompassed schools, corporations, the markets and government bureaucracy. This, at times, uneasily subverted traditional conservative ideas about federalism and the individual — using government to create space for a specific conception of the individual and interfering with business and local government, if necessary, to do it. The pitch wasn’t especially about preserving the Everglades or raising teacher pay or getting bridges rebuilt after hurricanes, which he has worked to do and are actually the kind of problems people have now.

Mr. DeSantis marries intensity and action, which might be the source of some of the weirdness that followed him and his campaign, from the Twitter Spaces launch to the X.com videos with the edgelord aesthetics. He talks about physically rebuilding bridges, but in this way where only he can marshal the powers of the state to get a bridge rebuilt on time in a crisis, rather than as effective and collaborative management unfazed by events.

In November, Mr. DeSantis hosted a New Hampshire event dedicated to medical freedom, where he campaigned with Dr. Joseph Ladapo, the mRNA vaccine skeptic he made surgeon general. In contrast, the next day at a series of Nikki Haley events, the pandemic only came up in glancing mentions, about Covid relief fraud and mental health crises.

Dr. Ladapo talked about Mr. DeSantis and the pandemic in Ayn Rand-esque terms of historical sweep. “You could see that he recognized that this wasn’t some battle over policy, like ‘I think this is the way we should do it.’ ‘No, I think this is the way we should do it.’ No, that’s not what this was. That’s not what this is,” he told attendees. “This was a battle over the existence of human beings, the autonomy of human beings, the ability, the right or the position of human beings over institutions.”

Mr. DeSantis’s ascension into a plausible Trump challenger coincided with one of the most disruptive periods in American life. It’s clear that some people have had their lives reorganized by the pandemic in a frontal, tangible way that wrecked their health or intensified their political views, and other people never want to hear about the pandemic again, no matter how much those years may have changed any aspect of their lives. For Republicans who want to forget 2020 and 2021, the valor found back then in Mr. DeSantis might not have been all that much about crushing the woke left and a corroded society, and more just about permission to move on from the pandemic earlier than most states.

Mr. DeSantis’s vaccine politics coincided with his aggressive turn toward restricting trans rights and cracking down on what’s taught in schools. There’s a line that the way DeSantis has approached the last few years has been a political effort to get to the former president’s right and win over voters who wanted more from Mr. Trump. There is also the possibility that most things Mr. DeSantis does flow from his own convictions, maybe some newly formed, that he, too, was one of those people who had their lives rearranged by the pandemic years.

On some level, whether it’s genuine or cynical, only the real world consequences of Mr. DeSantis’s use of power matter, like changing abortion laws or a school librarian’s leaving a job she loved.

But if the campaign was a cynical move to outflank Mr. Trump and give voters what he thought they wanted, and it’s working out like this, it’s interesting to think about that “Can’t Miss DeSantis” T-shirt and the guy who can also channel his intense energy into teacher pay and bridges wrecked by hurricanes. It’s possible that Mr. DeSantis and the conservative intellectuals misunderstood what worked best for him in Florida, and by extension, many others misunderstood the nature of what people wanted most during a disruptive period in American life.

There was a little bit of all this — the emphasis on ideological wars, the strange occurrences — in that barbecue place the night the blizzard started. Mr. DeSantis, fairly relaxed and chatty about the brutal weather approaching, was interrupted by climate protesters. “Ron, I have a question!” a man shouted, then kept at it.

“Excuse me, I’m doing this,” Mr. DeSantis said, cutting him off, as the protester was led off by security. “I know, but you’re interrupting and you’re being rude. Everyone else is listening, and you’re butting in.” A minute later, a second protester apparently tried to get onstage with a banner, but from the back of the room all you could hear were shouts about the climate followed by a thump and an “oohh” from the crowd, as a security guard tackled him. Mr. DeSantis made a joke or two about the situation, then continued.

There’s one or 16 or 200 reasons a person can give for why a campaign doesn’t work. There’s the technical (Mr. DeSantis got in the race too late, Mr. DeSantis got bombarded with tens of millions in attack ads); the exogenous (the overpowering, almost mystical hold Donald Trump has on people); the personal (too intense, too impersonal); the policy (people’s animosity toward strict abortion bans, and perhaps their discomfort with restricting access to books). Maybe it all came down to being unable to withstand Mr. Trump’s attacks and return them in kind.

Or maybe this is the down moment before a shocking turn of events, with a candidate humbled by events. Nobody can mathematically prove why one person wasn’t the guy; this is one of the mysteries of politics. But the difference between who some people thought Mr. DeSantis would be like and what he was like this past year does seem to be part of why it’s not working how he imagined it might.
