Westminster Dog Show 2025: Behind-the-Scenes Photos

Follow along for live updates from the 2025 Westminster Kennel Club Dog Show

Things are finally back to normal for the Westminster Kennel Club Dog Show. Or, at least as normal as can be expected for a competition that features more than 2,500 dogs flooding into Midtown Manhattan to compete for the coveted title of best in show.

Traditionally a winter event held at Madison Square Garden — since before the venue even had that name — Westminster was thoroughly disrupted by the pandemic. For two years it moved out of New York City entirely, with the event being held at Lyndhurst, an estate in suburban Tarrytown. And for the last two years it took up residence at the Billie Jean King National Tennis Center in Queens. It was also moved to warmer months in hopes of limiting the spread of Covid.

But for the 149th edition of the event, which began on Saturday with a series of contests testing some dogs’ agility and obedience, the show is back to February and back to Midtown. The initial stages of breed and group judging are being held at the Jacob K. Javits Convention Center, and the main event — best in show — is slated for Tuesday night at the Garden.

Getting ready for the show involves a great deal of work, from humans and dogs alike.

Walking from the Javits Center to Madison Square Garden on a chilly day is no fun for dog paws, so Draco, a Neapolitan mastiff, rode a shuttle to group judging with his assistant handler, Maria Chiselko.

As Siberian huskies gathered in the ready ring, they were checking out their competition.

Intensity was not lacking for Jett, a male standard poodle.

Getting a dog’s curls just right ahead of the competition involves patience, for both dog and human.

Dogs may have been bred for hunting and fishing and other outdoor activities, but nothing in that process prepared them for escalators.

The long days and pressure-packed competition can wear out even a seasoned handler.

An elaborate collar worn by Joya, a smooth-haired dachshund, would stand out for most dogs. But Joya’s large eyes make it an afterthought.

The events at Westminster can be a family affair, with Brin, a Neopolitan mastiff, being joined by her owner and handler, Dr. Yves Belmont, left, along with his wife, Isis, and their son, Maximillien.

Teemu, a Russell terrier, could absolutely be mistaken for a stuffed animal while being carried by his junior handler, Lillian Petermann.

Dogs are everywhere at the Javits Center and Madison Square Garden, and so are clothes and accessories with pictures of dogs.

Van, a Great Dane, did not bother using one of the booths. His owner, Cynthia Neet, can reach him just fine when he’s sitting on the floor.

A trick of the trade? Using some cornstarch during grooming. In the case of Justine Spiers, who was working with an English setter named Caros, there was more than some.

A group of Samoyeds, or a glitch in the matrix?

Lewis, a Chinook, was all smiles after he was named the best of his breed.

Not much floor space is necessary when it comes to judging a group of pugs. But handlers hoping not to pick up the wrong dog would be wise to keep a hand on their leash.

The coats of the dogs at Westminster are luxurious enough to make anyone feel like they should rethink their hair care routine.

While blue booties are not the most flattering of accessories, they do help emphasize just how fluffy some of these dogs truly are.

Characteristics like fur color, eye color and gait are intended to follow strict specifications, but a group of Yorkshire terriers took uniformity to an extreme with matching red bows to keep their fur out of their eyes.

The wide variety of dogs necessitates a wide variety of seating solutions. Nun, a Borzoi, grabbed a nice large platform for a break with her handler and owner, Kelley Davis, while Tova, a Shih Tzu, just needed a portion of Judy Harding’s lap.

Standing at attention in perfect form is harder than it looks, whether you are a member of the hound group (Connor) or his handler (Chelsea Latch).

One can only imagine the crimes committed by this smooth coat Chihuahua.

The American Kennel Club describes the Bernese mountain dog as “a hardy dog who thrives in cold weather,” but Pipa, in a sparkly coat, wasn’t taking any chances.

Strict adherence to breed standards is crucial to a dog’s chances, which leads to many instances of seeing double. In this case, Sophie and Pixie are, indeed, two different silky terriers, and not one standing near a mirror.

Who said Westminster isn’t rough-and-tumble? Rhys, a Border collie, had to have his paw taped up before the new Flyball competition, which is a relay race for dogs.

While the quiet — and extremely serious — judging of breed and group winners is what most people expect at Westminster, Saturday’s calendar at the Javits Center was filled with competitions like Flyball that tested entrants’ physical capabilities.

Before the dogs go under the microscope of judging they are poked and prodded and vacuumed and inspected.

Dandy, a Pekingese, had no issues with people getting in his face.

The Javits Center is a big place and Auggie, a French bulldog, left the walking to the other dogs as he rode around in a stroller pushed by his co-owner Debbie Cowperthwait.

Loose fur? Not on this guy’s watch.

They may look like tiny gray ponies from an alien planet, but Chinese cresteds are members of the toy group and they come in hairless and furred varieties.

Blake, a Border collie, was one of the dogs competing at the 12th annual Masters Agility Championship on Saturday. The event was won by another Border collie, Vanish.

Some dogs think they are the center of attention. Albert, a Pomeranian, knows he is the dog people have come to see.

Perhaps jealous of all the terriers being toted around the Javits Center, Meadow, a Dalmatian, tried the same with her owner and handler, Jennifer Casapello.

The dogs need to run around the ring as they are being judged. When it comes to the smaller ones, a human can walk alongside them with ease.

With some of the smaller dogs, it can sometimes be hard to see any actual feet amid all the fluff.

When Kite, a Brussels griffon, is done with this dog show business he could start entering beard and mustache competitions.

The dogs at Westminster are all champions, and things can get awfully competitive, but as Luna, a Border collie can remind us, they are also still dogs. Extremely good dogs.
