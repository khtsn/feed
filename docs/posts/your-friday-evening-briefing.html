Your Friday Evening Briefing

Good evening. Here’s the latest at the end of Friday.

1. U.S. arms production can’t keep up as the war in Ukraine drains supplies, leaving the military watching as its stocks of some key weapons dwindle.

In the year since Russia invaded its neighbor, the U.S. has sent $33 billion in military aid to Ukraine. President Biden and other U.S. officials have committed to continue the support for Ukraine for as long as it takes.

But without an anticipated end-date, the outflows of American weapons are becoming a significant concern for Pentagon officials. By dispatching so much of its weapons stock to Ukraine, the U.S. discovered that it lacks the capacity to produce the level of arms needed at a time of heightened superpower tensions.

For example, the U.S. sent Ukraine so many Stinger missiles that it would take 13 years’ worth of production at recent capacity levels to replace them. To address the issue, the Pentagon is growing its procurement budgets and abandoning some of the cost-cutting changes embraced after the end of the Cold War.

In the U.S., a bipartisan group of senators asked President Biden to share evidence about Russian war crimes in Ukraine with the International Criminal Court.

In Ukraine, U.N. investigators found at least 40 cases where prisoners of war were executed by both Russian and Ukrainian forces.

2. India’s opposition leader was expelled from Parliament after criticizing the prime minister.

Rahul Gandhi — the leader of the once-powerful Indian National Congress party and a member of a storied political family — was disqualified from his seat in Parliament today, after a court found him guilty of defamation for likening Narendra Modi, India’s leader, to a pair of prominent “thieves.”

The move was the boldest stroke yet by Modi’s allies to winnow out potential rivals and move against sources of dissent ahead of next year’s elections. Modi’s critics have accused him of trying to twist the country’s democratic system into something akin to an electoral autocracy.

3. Donald Trump plans to hold a rally in Waco, Texas, amid a symbolic anniversary.

The former president, embattled by multiple investigations, will hold the first rally of his 2024 presidential campaign tomorrow at the regional airport in Waco. Both supporters and critics say the location is no accident: the event coincides with the 30th anniversary of a fiery federal raid on a doomsday sect encamped there that turned into a famous symbol of government overreach.

In an overnight social media post, Trump predicted that “potential death and destruction” may result if, as is expected, he is charged by the Manhattan district attorney in connection with hush money payments to a porn star made during the 2016 presidential campaign.

In Washington, the Manhattan district attorney is resisting demands by House Republicans that he provide information about the hush money inquiry. The standoff could test the limits of Congress’s oversight powers.

4. After a U.S. contractor was killed in a drone strike at a base in northeast Syria, officials said the base’s defense system was “not fully operational.”

The drone, which hit a coalition military base yesterday, killing a U.S. contractor and injuring six other Americans, was of “Iranian origin,” U.S. intelligence analysts said. Officials said that the Avenger missile defense system at the base may have been experiencing an unexpected maintenance problem at the time of the attack.

The strike came at a moment when U.S. forces in northeast Syria are on high alert for attacks from Iran-backed militias; there have been 78 since January 2021, an American general said. In response, the U.S. launched airstrikes against facilities in eastern Syria linked to Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps.

In other news from the Middle East, military leaders in Israel warned that the government’s planned judicial overhaul could lead to the resignation of military reservists — which might result in a need to scale back operations.

5. A group of young Montanans sued the state, saying its support for the fossil fuels industry is unconstitutional.

The young residents seized on language in the state Constitution that guarantees “the right to a clean and healthful environment” and stipulates that the state is responsible for maintaining and improving the environment “for present and future generations.” They’re arguing that Montana should not be allowed to continue to support its coal, oil and gas industries because the resulting pollution is heating the planet to dangerous levels and has robbed them of a healthy environment.

It is an untested legal challenge to a state government that has taken a sharp turn to the right in recent years, and is aggressively defending itself. The trial, which legal experts say is the first involving a constitutional climate case, begins on June 12.

6. Can the last two remaining No. 1 seeds survive in the men’s N.C.A.A. tournament?

Tonight, both Alabama and Houston — who entered the tournament with high expectations and top seeds — will be tested by upstart No. 5 seeds. Houston, which may have assembled its best team in 40 years, has never found a way to win it all. (The first game starts at 6:30 p.m. Eastern.)

In the women’s tournament tonight, it’s the Caitlin Clark show: Clark, an Iowa guard who is one of the best scorers in the sport’s recent history, has helped lead her team to two wins so far. However, tonight’s matchup against Colorado (at 7:30 p.m. Eastern) will be Iowa’s toughest game yet.

7. Faced with difficult situations in parenting, romance or work, some people are turning to chatbots.

8. Mushrooms are suddenly everywhere.

The past few years have seen a surge in the popularity of mushrooms. The recent legalization of psilocybin in U.S. cities and states has also helped push fungi more to the cultural forefront.

As mushrooms become more prominent in nearly all spheres of modern life — including wellness, pop culture and cuisine — they’re also becoming sought-after motifs in interior design. On Etsy, searches for mushroom gifts have more than tripled in the last 12 months, and the Museum of Modern Art Design Store said that its mushroom-shaped lamps are among its best selling products.

10. And finally, the science behind the “middle-aged groan.”

You likely know the sounds: “hrrg” when standing up, or perhaps an “oof” when sitting down. The groan is one of those things many aging people do without giving much thought to why. So we asked experts what’s behind all the noise.

They suggested that it could be a way of handling the pain and stiffness that come with age, or perhaps simply a way to tell others (or remind ourselves) that we’re having a tough time at that moment.

Have a painless weekend.
