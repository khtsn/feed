Now that Donald Trump is the Republican nominee — I know, it’s not official, but let’s get real — we can expect to hear a lot about how great the economy was on his watch. Which is strange, because he was the first president since Herbert Hoover to leave office with fewer jobs than when he came in.

What’s happening here is that Trump has been given a mulligan for 2020. And to be fair, the huge job losses that took place that year were caused by Covid-19, not Trump’s policies.

What’s really odd, however, is that this mulligan appears to be highly selective.

Also, when Trump supporters go on about his great economy, they play mix and match. They talk about low unemployment while gas cost less than $2 a gallon. But as the chart below shows, the only period when gas was that cheap was when unemployment was actually very high because of the pandemic. Yes, on the eve of the pandemic, the U.S. economy was indeed looking pretty good, with both unemployment and inflation low. But that’s also true now.

A pseudoscientific formula holds that today, the third Monday in January, is the most depressing day of the year — so-called Blue Monday. It’s a nonsense notion cooked up as a marketing stunt for a British travel company, but this year, thanks to the Iowa caucuses, it feels apt.

I can’t remember a major news story that was at once so terrible and so boring. A suspenseless race has turned out more or less exactly as everyone expected; The Associated Press called Donald Trump’s victory a half-hour into the proceedings, well before voting had ended at many caucus sites.

Now I guess we’re supposed to pretend that there are important stakes in the contest for second place, but ultimately, I don’t think it matters any more than the outcome of the Trump-less debates did. Barring some spectacular deus ex machina, the primary contest is over, and now the nightmarishly long slog to the general election — enough time to conceive, gestate and give birth to a baby — begins.

Maybe it could have been different; a year ago polls showed that most Republicans wanted to move on from Trump. But that would have required the party to unite against the ex-president’s stolen-election conspiracy theories and to take seriously both his indictments and a jury’s finding that he committed sexual assault — risking the ire of his fanatical base.

Instead, Republicans did what they always do, indulging the MAGA movement while hoping some outside force would make Trump go away. Rather than take him on directly, Nikki Haley and Ron DeSantis spent most of their energy and resources attacking each other.

Then again, maybe there’s nothing Republicans could have done, because their despairing base loves drama and yearns for a strongman. The one thing that unites Americans of all political persuasions, after all, is the conviction that our democracy is failing and our country is going to hell. Tonight, Iowa’s Republican caucusgoers have sent it a little further on its way.

I knew Monday’s Emmy Awards were going to be an exhausting exercise in navel gazing just before the first commercial break, when the camera panned to a framed photo of James Gandolfini (who died in 2013) and promised a postcommercial tribute to “The Sopranos” (which ended in 2007).

Cast members of “Martin” griped about how they never won an Emmy (last episode: 1997). The cast of “Cheers” reunited (last episode: 1993). There was even a “Grey’s Anatomy” celebration (still on the air, after almost two decades).

Most of the night's fanciful backward glances have taken place in life-size dioramas that evoke a sort of low-rent Disney ride. When the much-hyped “Cheers” reunion began, I was half expecting an animatronic Ted Danson to narrate the history of television in four acts.

All of this shameless nostalgia might leave the impression that TV is throwing its own funeral at the Emmys, expecting that an audience that has become so fractured by streaming services might find something unifying in looking back to the medium’s heyday.

But despite all the time devoted to such withered, wanting gestures at the past, the Emmy Award winners themselves pointed to TV’s promising future.

I was enthralled by the passing of the torch in comedy, when the icon Carol Burnett declared that the award for best actress in a comedy would go to the soon-to-be-iconic Quinta Brunson. She is the first Black woman to win the award in more than 40 years.

I could have spent much longer than 60 seconds listening to Niecy Nash-Betts accept her award for best supporting actress in a limited series. She spoke about Black women who have been victims of police violence and then brilliantly, audaciously thanked herself for believing in her when nobody else would.

When RuPaul brought a flock of drag queens onstage to accept yet another well-deserved award for “Drag Race” and declared, “If someone tries to restrict your access to power, they are trying to scare you,” I could feel groundbreaking television’s ability to disrupt, if not change, our culture.

Based on the award recipients tonight — “Succession” mania notwithstanding — it’s evident that the Emmy voters set out to award a diverse slate of shows that represent television’s future. Perhaps next time, the ceremony should trust that an audience might value that future as much as they might revere the past.

Deputy Opinion Editor, reporting from Van Meter, Iowa

In the 2016 Iowa caucuses, Donald Trump finished a lackluster third place, behind Ted Cruz and Marco Rubio in Dallas County, just west of Des Moines. This time around, his presidential campaign was banking on a show of strength among the same suburban voters — many of them professionals and college graduates in middle- and upper-income brackets.

And Trump had a happier night than eight years ago.

On Monday night, at the Van Meter school caucus site, southwest of Des Moines, voters told me that they were excited to support Trump and felt he had come a long way from his 2016 loss.

“People have seen he’s proven himself,” said Charleen Kinsey, a Trump supporter, when asked about 2016 compared with this year. “They have seen what he has done and how it’s worked. So I think there’s the difference.”

She added, “And as far as California and New York, we can just shove them off the — sorry — East, West Coast need to go.”

Dylan Book, another Trump voter, said there were no wars between 2016 and 2020.

“Everybody was thriving,” he said. “Stock market was thriving. Businesses were thriving. I grew my business from 2016 to 2020 like crazy. And the last four years have been pretty rough.”

As the caucus got underway, a Nikki Haley supporter, Joel Akers, made a pitch to the roughly 200 voters in the audience. She was “the only candidate who clearly beats” Joe Biden in the polls, he said, stressing her electability. But he also noted that he voted for Trump in 2016 and 2020.

“Then Jan. 6 happened,” Akers said. “He didn’t do a lot to stop it.” He said Trump was “disrespectful” to the peaceful transfer of power. No one booed Akers, but the next speaker, Jason Smith, a congressman from Missouri and a Trump supporter, got stronger applause for remarks that leaned on memories of Trump’s time in office.

Across Iowa, in town and city, you really feel an affinity for Trump among a lot of Republicans and conservatives. Few bring up Jan. 6, as Akers did. Of course, caucus voters tend to be the most hard-core segment of the Republican base.

But given their first chance in more than three years to cast a ballot once more for Trump, it’s clear that a lot of people are voting with their hearts even more than their heads.

In the end, Trump did much better than he did in 2016, but it wasn’t a blowout with the suburbanites. The final vote tally from the gym at Van Meter High School: Trump, 85 votes; Ron DeSantis, 79; Haley, 64; and Vivek Ramaswamy, 21.

It is now almost a cliché to observe that the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. was much more radical in reality than in his place in public memory. The actual, historical King cannot be used, for example, to bolster a colorblind ideology of studied neutrality toward racial inequality or to condemn political militancy in opposition to injustice.

Even if old hat, the point is still worth making, not the least because we celebrate King’s life as a national holiday today. This alone demands that we take his work as seriously and as holistically as we can.

It’s with this in mind that I want to note that more than eight million children will be left out of a new federal food assistance program for needy families this summer because they live in one of the 15 states, all led by Republican governors, that refuse to participate. Although some of these states cite logistical concerns with administering the program — which will serve 21 million children across 35 states at the minor cost, relative to the federal budget, of $2.5 billion — others have a straightforward objection to the concept of food assistance.

Mississippi won’t participate because its Republican governor, Tate Reeves, says that he rejects “attempts to expand the welfare state.” Nebraska won’t participate because, says its Republican governor, Jim Pillen, “I don’t believe in welfare.” And Iowa won’t participate because, says its Republican governor, Kim Reynolds, “an E.B.T. card does nothing to promote nutrition at a time when childhood obesity has become an epidemic.”

The children have too much food, you see.

In any case, when reading these comments, I was reminded of King’s 1961 commencement address at Lincoln University in Chester County, Pa., a historically Black institution of higher education founded in 1854 as the Ashmun Institute. Here is the relevant part of King’s speech:

We are caught in an inescapable network of mutuality, tied in a single garment of destiny. Whatever affects one directly affects all indirectly. As long as there is poverty in this world, no man can be totally rich, even if he has a billion dollars. As long as diseases are rampant and millions of people cannot expect to live more than 20 or 30 years, no man can be totally healthy, even if he just got a clean bill of health from the finest clinic in America. Strangely enough, I can never be what I ought to be until you are what you ought to be. You can never be what you ought to be until I am what I ought to be. This is the way the world is made.

You can find the full address in “A Testament of Hope: The Essential Writings and Speeches of Martin Luther King Jr.” It would not be a bad use of your holiday to take a few minutes to check it out.

In the weeks since the Colorado Supreme Court ruled that Section 3 of the 14th Amendment disqualifies Trump from holding the office of the president, there’s been an enormous amount of commentary noting that the decision is potentially dangerous and destabilizing. Knocking the Republican front-runner off the ballot could lead to further alienation, more anger and potentially even violence.

There’s no doubt that knocking Trump off the ballot would send shock waves through the American body politic, but why would anyone believe that it’s inherently less destabilizing if Trump runs?

We already know what he does when he loses. For him, counting the votes is only the beginning of the battle. If he loses, he’ll challenge the results, conspire to overturn the election and incite political violence.

And if he wins? Then you have an insurrectionist in command of the most powerful military in the world, who is hellbent on seeking vengeance on his political enemies. Does anything at all sound stabilizing about that?

Walking through the potential real-world consequences of another Trump defeat — or victory — only reinforces the original wisdom of the authors of Section 3. They had fresh experience with the potential popularity of violent demagogues and knew the bloody consequences of giving them access to power.

Indeed, one wonders how different American history would be if Congress maintained the courage of its convictions. Four short years after the amendment was ratified, Congress buckled. There’s more to say about this, but the Amnesty Act of 1872, which undermined the restrictions of Section 3 and permitted former Confederates to run for public office, was a key turning point in our nation’s post-Civil War history. As the Indiana University law professor Gerard N. Magliocca has argued, the act proved to be a “harbinger of Reconstruction’s doom.”

Taking Trump off the ballot has risks, but so does keeping him in place. There is no easy path, and the court should not overthink its task. It’s time to apply the plain language of the Constitution and disqualify Trump.

Thursday’s report on the Consumer Price Index came in a bit hotter than most analysts expected, and some of the news reports I read suggested that we may still be some distance from a “soft landing” of low inflation with low unemployment.

So here’s a quick note on why serious analysts think the soft landing is much closer than the commonly cited numbers might suggest.

Many reports focused on the change in “core” consumer prices — excluding volatile food and energy — over the last year. But in a rapidly changing economy, looking at year-on-year changes puts you literally behind the curve. The chart compares the core inflation rate over the last year with the annualized rate over the last six months; the latter gives you a much better sense of how much inflation has plunged.

And even the six-month change is largely driven by shelter prices, which is problematic in two ways. First, the way the Bureau of Labor Statistics measures shelter costs tends to lag well behind market rents, which are a better indicator of current inflation, and are barely rising at all. Second, shelter in general has a much lower weight in the price measures the Federal Reserve uses as a basis for policy than in the C.P.I.

So while you may have seen headlines about core inflation running at 3.9 percent, the underlying inflation rate is much lower, probably closer to 2 percent than 3 percent. The soft landing still seems to be happening.

The most culturally relevant television of 2023 already lost at the Emmys. I’m not talking about critical darlings like “Succession” and “The Bear.” I’m talking about “Vanderpump Rules,” a reality show that started as a program about waiters and waitresses in Los Angeles who aspired to acting, modeling and singing careers. But in its 10th season, a man named Tom Sandoval cheated on his long-term girlfriend, Ariana Madix, with her friend and castmate Raquel Leviss. As the cleverly dubbed “Scandoval” unfurled over the course of multiple episodes, “VPR” became the most scintillating and entertaining melodrama of the year.

Because it’s a reality show, “VPR" isn’t under consideration for an award in the mainstream Emmys. The show could be nominated only for a Creative Arts Emmy, which had its ceremony on Jan. 7. “Vanderpump” was nominated in the outstanding unstructured reality program category. Despite masterly editing, true pathos and incredible ratings, “Vanderpump Rules” lost out to the feel-good, A-list star pablum that is the soccer show “Welcome to Wrexham.”

I always wonder if there’s a bit of low-key misogyny in the way Bravo’s bursting slate of reality shows is terminally underrated and underappreciated, even though the programs are cultural juggernauts that spawn endless conversation, spinoff shows and memes online. “VPR” deserved an Emmy for the confrontation scene between Sandoval and Madix alone, where the two finally have it out over his transgressions. It is several minutes of perfect TV, especially the part where Sandoval asks Madix, “Do you want anything?” as he putters around the kitchen, and Madix deadpans to him: “For you to die.”

It is a talent to be a compelling reality star, producer or director, even if it isn’t one that is respected or compensated in the same way as being part of a scripted show. The participants on these programs are starting to demand better treatment, as some unscripted performers are discussing unionizing. It may take more time for the industry to come around, but for the amount of entertainment these people are bringing to the masses — and the considerable strain it puts on their personal lives — they deserve better.
