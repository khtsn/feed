The Search for Tulsa Massacre Victims Finally Reveals a Name

U.S.|The Search for Tulsa Massacre Victims Finally Reveals a Name

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/07/12/us/tulsa-massacre-victim-identified.html

In 1921, an Army veteran who was traveling home stopped in Tulsa, Okla., only to be caught in one of the worst racial attacks in American history.

C.L. Daniel, an Army veteran, was in Utah looking for work after World War I and had planned to travel back to Newnan, Ga., his hometown.

He never made it back.

Mr. Daniel made a stop in Tulsa, Okla., and became a victim of the Tulsa Race Massacre, one of the worst racial attacks in American history, in which as many as 300 people were killed.

Five years after the city of Tulsa began searching for the victims, the investigation finally yielded a long-awaited identification, said Mayor G.T. Bynum on Friday. Other names had previously been identified in a state commission report on the massacre in 2001. Mr. Daniel is the first person named as part of the city’s effort to identify those killed in the attack.

Finally, some of what happened to Mr. Daniel is known.

“C.L. Daniel was a veteran who served our country in World War I, who was killed in the 1921 Tulsa Race Massacre, and whose family did not know where he had been buried for the last 103 years — until this week,” Mr. Bynum said.

Tulsa officials had been working with Intermountain Forensics, a lab assisting with DNA and genealogical analysis, to identify the victims.

Exhumed remains of individuals had provided enough DNA to create eight profiles that were tied to living relatives. The investigation connected the profiles to 19 possible surnames in seven states: Alabama, Georgia, Louisiana, Mississippi, North Carolina, Oklahoma and Texas.

Investigators positively identified Mr. Daniel as a victim of the massacre — based on research and independent documentation — and it is likely the remains in one of the cemetery burials belong to him or his brother.

“We can say two things: C.L. Daniel was a victim of the Tulsa Race massacre, according to his own mother,” and a burial in Oaklawn Cemetery in Tulsa where graves had been excavated “is consistent with the DNA we would expect to see in C.L. Daniel or one of his brothers,” said Alison Wilde, the genealogy case manager for the project and the director of the genetic genealogy program at Intermountain Forensics.

Mr. Daniel’s connection to the massacre was confirmed by combining DNA research and documents — a letter, written by a lawyer on behalf of his mother to the Veterans Administration in 1936 regarding his survivor benefits, that stated that “C.L. was killed in a race riot in Tulsa Oklahoma in 1921.”

It is unclear why Mr. Daniel was in Tulsa, but a second letter from a congressman in Georgia corroborated his death, stating that “C.L. Daniel was killed in Oklahoma a short time after his discharge.”

Records from the National Archives also show that Mr. Daniel, a Black man his 20s, had been drafted by the U.S. Army in 1918. He was discharged in December of 1919. Mr. Daniel was not married and had no children, according to the documents.

Some of the victims were identified in the aftermath of the massacre, but many were not. City records show that only 26 death certificates were issued in 1921 in connection to the massacre, and newspaper reports from June of 1921 noted that 18 adult male victims were buried in Oaklawn Cemetery. With so little documentation, the exact death toll is unknown. In 2020, the city began excavating a section of Oaklawn where evidence of a mass gravesite had been found. The city will begin a fourth excavation at Oaklawn later in the month.

The Tulsa Race Massacre began on May 31, 1921, after a young Black man was accused of attacking a young white woman. A white mob descended on the courthouse where he was being held. Shots were fired and a fight broke out when the group of white men confronted a group of Black men. The young man was eventually cleared.

The mob descended on Greenwood, a community so successful that it as was known as Black Wall Street. Less than 24 hours later, Greenwood had been destroyed. Beyond the deaths, hundreds were injured and around 8,000 to 10,000 were left homeless.
