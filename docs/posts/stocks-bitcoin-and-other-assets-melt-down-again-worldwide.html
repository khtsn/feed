Stocks, Bitcoin and Other Assets Melt Down Again Worldwide

What might the Fed do now?

Stocks, cryptocurrencies, the dollar, oil — they’re all down sharply again on Monday on concerns that the U.S. economy is slowing faster than expected. It comes after a rough week for global markets.

That has reignited criticisms that the Fed is moving too slowly to cut rates as economic conditions look shakier.

Here’s the latest:

  • S&P 500 futures were down 3 percent on Monday. Mega-cap tech stocks, which have driven much of the market’s gains this past year, were especially bruised: Nvidia was down 11 percent premarket, while Apple was off 7.5 percent. (Adding to the jitters was Berkshire Hathaway again cutting its stake in Apple and Nvidia reportedly delaying shipments of its most advanced A.I. chip.)

  • Japan was especially hard hit. The Nikkei 225 fell more than 12 percent, battered by concerns about tech stocks and worries that the Bank of Japan moved too quickly in raising interest rates last week.

  • Bitcoin tumbled more than 10 percent, wiping out more than $100 billion in value off the volatile digital currency.

  • Brent crude, the global benchmark, fell despite growing tensions in the Middle East.

  • Investors fled to safe-haven assets, helping lift sovereign bonds. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to a one-year low. (Bond prices rise when yields fall.)

Goldman Sachs economists this weekend raised the odds of a U.S. recession in the next year to 25 percent from 15 percent; they cautioned that such a risk was “limited.”

But the Goldman note — along with recent disappointing earnings results, and lackluster economic reports including Friday’s jobs report — have compounded global fears that the U.S. is headed for a hard landing. All this has stoked speculation that the Fed will need to take bold action, such as a prodigious half-percentage point cut next month or an exceedingly rare emergency cut within the next week.

Criticisms of the Fed are getting louder, especially from the I-told-you-so crowd. “The Federal Reserve has been late in cutting rates, but that has been true for some time,” Paul Donovan, a UBS economist, wrote in a client note on Monday. “The policy error is making things worse for lower income households.”

Companies have been warning for some time that lower-income consumers are pulling back, muddying their profit outlooks. “The tone for companies on earnings calls will be key to watch in the coming weeks,” Lori Calvasina, the head of global equity research at RBC Capital Markets, wrote in a note on Sunday.

Others say the worst fears are overblown. Volatility is common during bull market rallies, analysts cautioned. It’s worth noting that the U.S. economy is still growing, and while Friday’s unemployment data was weaker than expected, it was “not a disaster scenario,” Mohit Kumar, an economist at Jefferies, wrote on Monday.

What to watch this week: More data — including jobless claims on Thursday — and a flurry of corporate earnings reports could shed more light on how different parts of the U.S. economy are performing.

HERE’S WHAT’S HAPPENING

Warren Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway halves its stake in Apple. The conglomerate disclosed that it had sold about 390 million shares in the iPhone maker in the second quarter, as it continued to pare its exposure to the tech giant. (That still leaves Buffett with about 400 million shares in Apple, worth about $84.2 billion as of June 30.) The second quarter was the seventh straight of Berkshire selling more stocks than it bought.

Mars is said to weigh a potential deal for the maker of Cheez-Its. The snack giant is considering a takeover of Kellanova, the W.K. Kellogg spinoff that also produces Pringles chips and Eggo waffles, according to Reuters. It would be one of the biggest transactions in the packaged-foods sector — Kellanova’s market value as of Friday was $21.6 billion — but could face antitrust scrutiny.

Elon Musk revives his legal fight with OpenAI. In a complaint filed on Monday in a federal district court in California, the billionaire again accused the start-up of fraud and breach of contract by prioritizing profits over safety, saying the “deceit is of Shakespearean proportions.” The lawsuit comes weeks after Musk, who co-founded OpenAI, withdrew a similar lawsuit against the start-up.

Hurricane Debby begins to batter the Southeast. It made landfall as a Category 1 storm on Monday in Florida. That’s weaker than last year’s Hurricane Idalia, which hit the same coast, but forecasters warned that Debby appeared to be a slower-moving storm that could bring “potentially historic” rainfall to areas including Savannah, Ga., and Charleston, S.C. this week.

CrowdStrike hits back

The legal saber rattling between the cybersecurity company CrowdStrike and Delta Air Lines over last month’s global I.T. outage just ratcheted up a notch.

In a letter sent to Delta last night that DealBook has reviewed, CrowdStrike’s lawyers at Quinn Emanuel Urquhart & Sullivan pushed back against claims that it was responsible for the airline’s canceling hundreds of flights. The blame for Delta’s woes lies with the airline alone, the lawyers wrote.

The letter was in response to Delta lawyering up, hiring David Boies, a prominent litigator, and saying that it would seek damages over the outage, which it said cost it $500 million. Shares in both companies have plunged since the July 19 outage, with CrowdStrike down more than 35 percent.

In an interview with NPR, Transportation Secretary Pete Buttigieg — whose department is investigating Delta over the outage — provided preliminary observations about what went wrong: “Their systems seem to have been more dependent on pieces of software that were affected by CrowdStrike,” he said, “and we’re told also that their crew scheduling system for positioning people basically got overloaded.”

CrowdStrike questioned why Delta suffered more than its rivals. The security company cited Delta’s “I.T. decisions and response to the outage” and noted that the airline turned down “free onsite help from CrowdStrike professionals.”

Other carriers, including American and United, rebounded faster from the outage. Aviation experts told The Times that Delta leaned more heavily on cancellations than delays and had trouble getting operations back to normal.

CrowdStrike implied that its liability was limited. While Delta’s C.E.O., Ed Bastian, told CNBC that the $500 million estimate factored in lost revenue and “tens of millions of dollars per day in compensation and hotels,” the software company’s lawyers countered that “any liability by CrowdStrike is contractually capped at an amount in the single-digit millions.”

CrowdStrike’s lawyers added: “Should Delta pursue this path, Delta will have to explain to the public, its shareholders and ultimately a jury why CrowdStrike took responsibility for its actions — swiftly, transparently and constructively — while Delta did not.”

Asked for a comment, a representative for Delta referred DealBook to Bastian’s CNBC interview.

The business-minded adviser on Team Harris

As Vice President Kamala Harris is poised to assume the mantle of Democratic standard-bearer for November, many in the party are still assessing where she lands on key issues.

One figure who is providing reassurance to party moderates, especially business leaders, is one of their own: Tony West, a veteran of the Obama administration who is Uber’s chief legal officer — and Harris’s brother-in-law.

West is officially an unpaid adviser to Harris, The Times’s Kate Kelly, Noam Scheiber and Kenneth Vogel report. He has already made his mark, helping her reach out to would-be supporters once President Biden dropped out. He also recommended bringing in Eric Holder, the former attorney general, to help vet potential running mates.

West brings a wealth of experience in government and business. He served as the No. 3 official in the Justice Department, helping lead settlements with the financial industry after the 2008 financial crisis.

After leaving government, he oversaw legal operations at PepsiCo, then joined Uber, where he was brought in to clean up the ride-hailing company’s legal mess before its I.P.O. “I’ve had conversations with Tony and people on their team, and I find them very smart, very open to all business ideas,” Mark Cuban told The Times.

Progressive Democrats worry West may move Harris too far to the center. Critics of his work on the financial settlements say that banks were spared bigger penalties, and that no prominent executives were prosecuted. And labor advocates attacked his role in Uber’s fight against classifying drivers as employees.

That said, West has said he would stay at Uber after the election.

It’s another sign of the Democratic battle over how Harris might govern, as those on the left square off against donors and other moderates. That’s also playing out in the veepstakes, as liberals and union officials take aim at two potential running mates, Gov. Josh Shapiro of Pennsylvania and Senator Mark Kelly of Arizona.

The Times notes that most major donors would be fine with any of the leading candidates, who also include Gov. Tim Walz of Minnesota.

The Olympics’ toll on the Parisian luxury industry

LVMH is all over the Paris Olympics, and luxury brands have embraced athletes in their endorsement deals. That hasn’t necessarily translated into higher sales in the host city’s stores.

  • “I refer to my experience of London 2012 — that in the few weeks before the start of the Olympics, you see, usually, a decrease of the usual tourists coming to buy luxury,” Roberto Eggs, the chief strategy and global markets officer of the luxury outerwear maker Moncler, told analysts during a recent earnings call.

  • “Our experience for Olympic Games is that it’s not the best moment for us. We’ve seen that in London, we’ve seen that in Beijing. And we’re probably going to see that in Paris,” Éric du Halgouët, the executive vice president of finance at Hermès, told analysts.

That said, Both Moncler and Hermès delivered relatively upbeat sales outlooks. And brands said they expected a boost in traffic at other European stores as tourists reroute their shopping sprees around the Games.
