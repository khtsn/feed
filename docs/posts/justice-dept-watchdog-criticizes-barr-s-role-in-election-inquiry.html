Glenn Thrush

Reporting from Washington

July 25, 2024, 4:54 p.m. ET

It was an incident that seemed to bolster President Donald J. Trump’s claim that election workers were scheming against him: In September 2020, the Justice Department announced it was investigating the dumping of pro-Trump absentee ballots in Pennsylvania.

But prosecutors quickly determined it was an innocent error. A mentally impaired seasonal elections employee had mistakenly believed that nine ballots were invalid, and tossed them in a dumpster — yet the fact that no charges would be brought was not made public until well after Joseph R. Biden Jr. defeated Mr. Trump.

On Thursday, nearly four years later, the Justice Department’s in-house watchdog issued a scathing report criticizing the attorney general at the time, William P. Barr, and David Freed, then a Trump-appointed U.S. attorney in Pennsylvania, for publicly disclosing a continuing criminal investigation and allowing a mistaken perception of the incident to linger during an election.

The inspector general, Michael E. Horowitz, said he was particularly “troubled” by Mr. Barr’s decision to brief Mr. Trump on the inquiry, which, in turn, might have encouraged him to make false and exaggerated claims about election security.

“Department leadership was aware of information that substantially undercut” the narrative that election workers had thrown out ballots cast by Trump voters, he wrote in the 82-page report, which included four recommendations for reforms.

Mr. Barr and Mr. Freed chose not to tell the public before the election that “the subject of the investigation was mentally impaired, appeared to have discarded the ballots by mistake and would likely not be criminally charged,” Mr. Horowitz said.

Mr. Horowitz concluded that while Mr. Freed violated the procedures laid out in the department’s internal code of conduct, Mr. Barr had not because existing policies are written vaguely enough to give an attorney general a fair amount of leeway. But neither could be charged with misconduct, he added, because of ambiguity in federal law that gives department officials discretion if they believe they are acting “in the interest of the fair administration of justice.”

Mr. Barr provided the inspector general with some information early in the investigation but did not sit for an interview. He did not respond to a request for comment nor did Mr. Freed.

The report was the second by the inspector general this week to focus on Mr. Barr’s tumultuous tenure as attorney general. On Wednesday, Mr. Horowitz issued the results of an investigation that found no evidence of political interference in the department’s reduction of a prison sentence proposed for a longtime Trump ally, Roger Stone, in 2020.

Mr. Barr’s actions during protests at Lafayette Park, near the White House, in June 2020, appear to be the subject of a separate inquiry by the inspector general, according to a person who spoke on the condition of anonymity.

On Sept. 18, 2020, local law enforcement officials informed the F.B.I. that a temporary employee with the Luzerne County Bureau of Elections discarded nine military absentee ballots because he “was under the impression” they were fraudulent, according to the report.

Soon after, Mr. Freed publicly released a letter to local officials saying his office was investigating the improper disposal of nine military ballots filled out by Trump voters. He quickly corrected that number to seven when the F.B.I. pointed out two of the envelopes were unopened.

None of this was in line with proper practice, Mr. Horowitz determined.

Mr. Freed “violated the D.O.J. policy generally prohibiting comment about ongoing criminal investigations before charges are filed” and also violated an internal requirement to consult with the department’s public integrity section before going releasing such information.

Mr. Barr’s decision to inform Mr. Trump about such a minor investigation at such a sensitive moment in an election year fell into more of a gray area, investigators concluded.

Department policy “appears to leave it to the attorney general’s discretion to determine precisely what information can be shared with the president,” Mr. Horowitz wrote.

To avoid a similar scenario in the future, Mr. Horowitz recommended the department tighten its rules for disclosing open investigations and clarify guidelines for communication between prosecutors and the White House.
