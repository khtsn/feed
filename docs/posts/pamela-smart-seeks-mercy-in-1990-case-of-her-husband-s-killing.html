Pamela Smart Seeks Mercy in 1990 Case of Her Husband’s Killing

Pamela Smart hung up her green prison uniform jacket and walked down a hallway as fellow inmates swept the floor around her. A gold cross hung from her neck representing the Christian faith she credits with helping her survive what she calls her “sentence of extermination” — life without parole.

“As long as I have God, I have hope and I have a shot,” said Ms. Smart, who made national news in 1990 when she was arrested in New Hampshire at 22 on charges of persuading her teenage lover to murder her husband.

She is now 55 and an ordained minister who preaches sermons on Sundays to a large inmate congregation in the Bedford Hills Correctional Facility, a maximum-security prison for women just north of New York City where she was transferred in 1993 by New Hampshire officials, who cited “security reasons.”

Ms. Smart, who has exhausted her legal appeals, had long maintained her innocence, but is now more openly acknowledging responsibility as an accomplice to murder. New Hampshire officials call her remorse a contrivance as the State Supreme Court considers granting her a clemency hearing before the state’s executive council and Gov. Chris Sununu, a Republican eyeing a 2024 presidential campaign.

State officials have already denied Ms. Smart repeated petitions for clemency.

“They exiled her,” said Ms. Smart’s longtime lawyer, Mark Sisti. “She was getting too much attention so they said, ‘Let’s bury her in a 600-inmate prison in New York. Out of sight, out of mind.’”

So one of New Hampshire’s most notorious convicts is now also one of the longest serving female inmates in New York’s prison system, which lists her parole eligibility date as 99/99/9999.

“It’s a de facto death sentence that I’m serving — it’s death by incarceration,” said Ms. Smart, sitting in a visiting room last month as two prison staffers stood nearby monitoring her interview. She wore a pink sweater that matched her new Nike running shoes. Styled hair. A manicure.

A State Supreme Court ruling in Ms. Smart’s favor would order the executive council and Mr. Sununu to consider her petition for a sentence commutation, so she could appear before the parole board for release.

If that fails, Ms. Smart hopes to petition Gov. Kathy Hochul for clemency in New York on the grounds that she has been a de facto resident for three decades, incarcerated at New York taxpayer expense, and has the same rights as her fellow inmates.

Coming three years before the O.J. Simpson trial, Ms. Smart’s case was a video-driven sensation that spanned fact and fiction. It was one of the first fully televised murder trials, introducing the young defendant and her younger co-conspirators to a national audience. It inspired the 1995 film “To Die For,” based on a book of the same name and starring Nicole Kidman and Joaquin Phoenix, not to mention a television version that starred Helen Hunt.

It was impossible to keep a low profile coming into prison trailed by headlines like “Sex, Lies & Murder.” Early on, she was hassled, she said, including a beating in 1996 by two inmates that fractured her eye socket and required a plastic plate in the left side of her face. The enduring numbness and pain force her to wake daily at 5:30 a.m. to line up for pain medication. In 2003, she said a guard sexually assaulted her and leaked risqué photos that wound up in The National Enquirer.

As one of Bedford Hills’s older inmates, she has become a den mother and mentor, known to some inmates as Auntie and to others as Tinker Bell, whose antic nature she emulates.

At Bedford Hills, Ms. Smart managed to fall in with other prominent inmates. She said she helped develop educational programs with Judith Clark and Kathy Boudin, the Weather Underground radicals convicted of felony murder for a violent bank robbery in 1981.

Also, she said, she bonded with the rapper Remy Ma over enduring static from other inmates, as well as Amy Fisher, who was part of a similarly sensationalized case in the early 1990s.

She helped face her responsibility and remorse thanks to a writer’s workshop taught by the playwright Eve Ensler, who wrote “The Vagina Monologues” and who is now known simply as V.

She calls her incarceration a rehabilitative journey that includes earning two master’s degrees and a doctorate through prison education programs.

She hopes the court considers her record while in prison, not just her reputation.

“I’ve had inmates tell me, ‘I read your book, I saw your movie,’ and I have to tell them, ‘That wasn’t my book, that wasn’t my movie,’” she said.

Ms. Smart was a high school media coordinator when she began an affair with Billy Flynn, a 15-year-old student who later shot and killed her husband, Gregg Smart, in 1990. Prosecutors portrayed her as a seductress who coerced Mr. Flynn into killing Mr. Smart.

Ms. Smart was not present at the killing, but was charged with orchestrating the crime. She has long denied even knowing about any murder plot.

Mr. Flynn admitted to shooting Mr. Smart in the head during a bogus burglary in the Smarts’ home. He and three teenage accomplices pleaded guilty to reduced charges in exchange for testimony that helped convict Ms. Smart of being an accomplice to first-degree murder.

The four men have been free for years.

“How is that fair, that the ones who committed the killing are out and I’m in here for life?” she said.

In recent years, Ms. Smart has been more outspoken in taking responsibility for the murder, which she calls the result of a chain of events set in motion by the “terrible judgment by an immature, selfish young woman” to begin an affair with a 15-year-old.

In court this month, Mr. Sisti called the state’s rebuffing of Ms. Smart a politically motivated stance “advocating a long, painful extermination.”

But Laura Lombardi, a senior assistant attorney general for New Hampshire, argued that Ms. Smart “doesn’t have a legal right to a hearing” and that the court lacked the jurisdiction to order one.

Ms. Smart has “received all the justice to which she is entitled,” Ms. Lombardi said.

The court’s decision is keenly awaited by Ms. Smart’s supporters who believe that letting her die in prison is inhumane.

Roslyn Smith, who after serving nearly 40 years in Bedford Hills for murder was paroled in 2015, said she is one of a “sisterhood” of dozens of former inmates who support Ms. Smart’s bid for clemency.

“The way the media has portrayed Pam over the years in a derogatory and negative light, it doesn’t reflect who she is,” Ms. Smith said. “She’s a caring and kind person who’d give you the shirt off her back.”

New Hampshire has long made Ms. Smart’s failure to accept responsibility for the killing a key reason to oppose a commutation. But she said she has apologized to Gregg Smart’s family.

“Just because I wasn’t there and didn’t pull the trigger, doesn’t mean I wasn’t responsible for Gregg’s death,” she said. “Gregg is not alive because of me.”

New Hampshire officials remain unconvinced. An associate assistant attorney general for New Hampshire, Jeffery Strelzin, wrote in a 2022 letter opposing an earlier commutation request that Ms. Smart “has told a false narrative for over 30 years.” He cited “decades of deceit and the many lies” and urged councilors to be “skeptical of her apology.”

Donna Sytek, who served on New Hampshire’s parole board until early 2022, said that even if the court forces a hearing, Ms. Smart’s case would founder in the state’s executive council or parole board.

“I just don’t see it getting through the council, because of the notoriety of the case and her failure to take responsibility,” said Ms. Sytek, a former speaker of the state’s House of Representatives.

“Is she just saying what the council wants to hear or is it genuine?” she said. “She’s made repeated requests for clemency or a pardon but she’s never taken responsibility, and suddenly after 30 years, she says, ‘I apologize, I’m sorry.’”

Ms. Smart called her remorse genuine and the result of years of introspection and spiritual evolution with the help of prison programs.

During the 90-minute interview, Ms. Smart was straightforward, amiable and funny. But her positive mood cracked occasionally as when she admitted being worn out by incarceration and upset over the prospect of never seeing her ailing mother again.

“When New Hampshire says you’re going to die in prison, how do you have any hope?” she said.

“I’d rather have the death penalty than life in prison,” she added.

Ms. Smart is assigned to the prison’s honor floor for well-behaved inmates and has been repeatedly re-elected for years by fellow inmates as a head of a grievance committee that adjudicates complaints and disputes.

She called the requirements for clemency a conundrum.

“They’re trying to box me into confessing a crime I didn’t commit,” she said. “So if I say I’m a coldhearted killer, you’re going to let me out, but if I say I’m rehabilitated, you won’t let me out?”

Then she put her green inmate jacket back over her pink sweater and headed back to life in prison.
