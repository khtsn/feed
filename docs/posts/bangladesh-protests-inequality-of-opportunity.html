Bangladesh Protests Inequality of Opportunity

The demonstrations began over a quota system for government jobs but have extended to corruption, the cost of living and income disparities.

July 21, 2024, 4:07 a.m. ET

The video, taken this month, shows a Bangladeshi protester wearing a black T-shirt and standing on one side of an empty street. His arms are outstretched, and he is holding a stick in one hand.

Across the street stand several policemen, wearing bulletproof vests and helmets and pointing their guns at him. He does not move, daring the policemen to shoot.

They begin to fire.

As Bangladesh reels from one of its worst bouts of violence since it gained independence in 1971, the video — verified by the news agency Storyful and carried by multiple television channels in Bangladesh — has come to symbolize the helplessness and defiance of student protesters who have demanded the reform of a system of preferential treatment for coveted government jobs.

Late on Friday, the government of Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina declared a nationwide curfew and brought the army in to quell the protests. By then, the internet had already been switched off. The brutal crackdown that began last week has drawn condemnation from human rights groups and left members of the extensive Bangladeshi diaspora heartbroken as they frantically try to contact their families. On Saturday alone, the police reported that 12 people had died.

“When I pray, I can’t stop my crying,” said Silvia, 36, a Bangladeshi immigrant in New York who requested to be identified by only her first name out of concern for the safety of her family back home. “When police are killing my brothers and sisters, there is nothing called democracy right now in my country.”

The protests erupted out of students’ anger at a quota system for hiring in public-sector jobs that benefits certain groups, including the families of those who fought for independence from Pakistan, and that disadvantages everyone else. Analysts say the revolt also reflects a broader resentment over the uneven distribution of wealth and opportunity in an economy that has begun to wobble after years of rapid growth.

The protests are about the “frustration many people feel about how economic growth has been uneven, and there is huge inequality and corruption,” said Pierre Prakash, director of the Asia Program at the International Crisis Group. “The quota protest is just the manifestation of a widespread malaise that’s not just about quotas but also economic and political.”

In recent decades, Bangladesh’s economy has lifted millions out of poverty on the back of a robust garment export industry. But the coronavirus pandemic hit the country hard, as people around the world cut back on clothing purchases, and remittances from the diaspora fell. At the same time, consumers endured an inflationary burst, with food and fuel costs rising sharply.

Inflation remains stubbornly high at 10 percent, and the pace of job creation has slowed. Seven in 10 Bangladeshis are between the ages of 15 and 64; the median age is 29.6 years. As of 2022, the youth unemployment rate, at 16.1 percent, was about three times as high as the overall rate.

Public-sector jobs are desirable because they are stable and prestigious, and they come with generous benefits. But they are tough to obtain. Every year, roughly 4,000 government positions open up, and more than 300,000 students compete for them.

In such a setting, a quota system that reserves 56 percent of jobs for certain favored groups has increasingly become an affront to students.

Sheikh Mujibur Rahman, the country’s founding leader and father of the current prime minister, Ms. Hasina, created the quota system in 1972 to ensure that the thousands of men and women who fought in the war of independence from Pakistan would be taken care of.

The quota was extended in 1997 and 2010 to include the children and then grandchildren of war veterans, leading to the perpetuation of a favored class that many deem unfair, especially since many of the fighters have retired or died. Women; ethnic or religious minorities; those with disabilities; and people from certain districts are also allotted quotas, though the protesters have not called for the removal of these.

Ferdie Hossain, 34, and an alumnus of the University of Dhaka where the protests started, said the idea of quotas for those known as freedom fighters made sense as a reward at the time. People were even willing to tolerate the extension of those quotas for the offspring of those fighters, said Mr. Hossain, who left Dhaka in 2009 and now works as a financial analyst in Wales. “If it’s the family and children, it’s fine.”

But the anger began building after the quota was extended to their grandchildren in 2010, he said.

Over time, guaranteed government jobs have created a “political class” and a hierarchy, as well as a class of wealthy people, said Saad Hammadi, a policy and advocacy manager at the Balsillie School of International Affairs in Waterloo, Ontario. That, along with the rising cost of living and clampdowns on free expression through new legislation, brought Bangladeshis to this moment, Mr. Hammadi added.

“It was a volcanic eruption of all the frustrations people had been living with,” he said.

Other factors have fueled the anger, including corruption. Last year, Bangladesh scored 24 points on an annual Corruption Perceptions Index released by Transparency International, a global nonprofit. A score of 100 means “very clean.”

Students and analysts said it was not unusual for someone to bribe an official for a government post or for the questions to an exam. Local papers have recently reported on a long-running scheme to leak exam question papers, including those for the Bangladesh Civil Service exam, which is the qualifying test for a government job.

Anti-quota protests have erupted many times in the past two decades. The current protests have their roots in a student movement that started in 2018 and led Ms. Hasina to abolish the system. But after a lawsuit by the descendants of some freedom fighters, the High Court reinstated the quotas in June, which set off the protests.

Initially peaceful, the protests intensified after a news conference where Ms. Hasina called the protesters “razakars” — a derogatory term for those who supported Pakistan during Bangladesh’s independence war.

“We demanded rights, but we got called ‘razakar,’” students chanted for weeks on the streets of Dhaka. As their cries faded amid the government’s crackdown, Bangladeshi emigrants like Silvia, who left Dhaka in 2019, took up the cause. Last week, she was one of around 1,000 protesters in Times Square in Manhattan chanting, “We want justice.”

Mr. Hammadi, who is from Bangladesh, said he felt helpless and heartbroken, especially when he saw the video clip of the student with his arms outstretched. “It could be a heroic representation of a student protest for justice,” he said.

Saif Hasnat contributed to this report from Bangladesh.
