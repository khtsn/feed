Looking for something to do in New York? Lose yourself in the enchanting story-songs of Margo Price, or see the most romantic movie ever conceived by John Cassavetes.

Comedy

‘Tom D’s Big New York Show’

March 4 at 7 p.m. at Caveat, 21A Clinton Street, Manhattan; caveat.nyc.

Tom Delgado was once a lawyer, making obscene amounts of money, but he hated the profession. Instead, he fled for the greener but more impoverished pastures of standup comedy, and because he needed some way to pay the rent, he became a licensed New York City tour guide, too.

Over the past five years, Delgado has documented many of his tours on his YouTube channel, where he takes his nearly 70,000 subscribers through the streets of the Big Apple, framing the locations with a sense of humor and reverence. So it comes as no surprise that his latest venture, “Tom D’s Big New York Show,” a monthly showcase that debuted at Caveat in February, celebrates the city with a mix of comedy, banter and trivia.

Delgado will host the second installment on Saturday, which will feature performances by Janeane Garofalo and Gastor Almonte and an interview with Patrick Bringley, who left his job at The New Yorker to become a guard at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, an experience he recounts in his new book, “All the Beauty in the World.” Tickets are $15 in advance, $20 at the door and $10 for the livestream; they’re available at Caveat’s website. SEAN L. McCARTHY

Pop & Rock

Margo Price

March 4 at 8 p.m. at Webster Hall, 125 East 11th Street, Manhattan; bowerypresents.com.

Many listeners first got to know Margo Price through the hardscrabble back story that she laid bare on her splashy 2016 debut, “Midwest Farmer’s Daughter.” But even as the signifiers of her success — a Grammy nomination, sold-out shows, a book deal and more — have piled up, the singer-songwriter still manages to speak to and for outsiders. Never fully embraced by Nashville’s country music establishment, Price has gradually wandered from the homey roots rock of her early catalog toward more sprawling, psychedelic and classic rock sounds on recent releases. Her latest, “Strays,” which came out in January, is populated by characters on the margins: Price sings about an uninsured woman seeking an abortion, about a displaced resident of a struggling small town, and about her own battles with critics.

On Saturday, Price will bring her muscular story-songs to Webster Hall, with the folk singer-songwriter Tré Burt opening. Tickets are $30 and available from AXS. OLIVIA HORN

Jazz

Renee Rosnes Quartet

Through March 5 at Smoke, 2751 Broadway, Manhattan; smokejazz.com.

Jazz musicians typically start out by backing more established players, then ramp down those supporting roles once they start leading their own bands. But the Canadian-born pianist Renee Rosnes has been excelling in both lanes for more than three decades, working with giants such as Joe Henderson, J.J. Johnson and Ron Carter — whose Foursight band she still regularly performs with — while also heading up her own superb ensembles and, more recently, playing duets with her husband, the pianist Bill Charlap, and assembling the all-star collective Artemis.

This weekend at Smoke, Rosnes leads an intriguing, horn-free quartet featuring the bassist Peter Washington and the drummer Lewis Nash, her regular collaborators since the 1990s, and the vibraphonist Steve Nelson, who appears on two of her recent discs. Expect to hear top-notch post-bop, touching on both bustling swing and reflective elegance.

Sets are at 7 and 9 p.m. each night, with an additional 10:30 p.m. performance on Friday and Saturday. Tickets start at $35 and are available at Smoke’s website. Livestreams of Friday and Saturday’s sets will be broadcast for $15. HANK SHTEAMER

‘Reynier Leyva Novo: Methuselah’

Through March 26 at El Museo del Barrio, 1230 Fifth Avenue, Manhattan; elmuseo.org.

Few travelers are hardier than the Methuselah, a type of monarch butterfly that survives for months rather than weeks.

Young visitors to El Museo del Barrio can now experience its more-than-6,000-mile migratory journey vicariously in this augmented-reality installation. Reynier Leyva Novo, a Cuban American artist, has created a digital avatar of the Methuselah, which museumgoers, who are admitted four at a time, can view by donning a headset with holographic lenses. Novo designed the exhibit, which requires reservations, so that the avatar would appear to fly in the gallery, as if it were following the real butterflies’ seasonal path from southern Canada to central Mexico.

Now that the live monarchs are hibernating at their destination, their museum counterpart is mostly stationary, but children will enjoy watching it open and close its wings. They can also explore a website (and app) that provides project information and a map of this one imagined butterfly’s migration.

The complementary show “Juan Francisco Elso: Por América,” a retrospective devoted to another Cuban-born artist, includes family-friendly elements like massive sculptures, mythological references and globes with chalkboard surfaces on which to draw. A free tour (space is limited) is on Saturday at 2:30 p.m.

Museum admission, which is pay-what-you-wish, is free for children under 12. LAUREL GRAEBER

Shoot Me Like You Love Me

Through May 31 at Roxy Cinema New York, 2 Sixth Avenue, Manhattan; roxycinemanewyork.com.

“It’s annoying, living,” Seymour Cassel’s character says in John Cassavetes’s “Minnie and Moskowitz” (showing on Saturday and Sunday). “You not only have to learn their language. You have to learn their emotions.” That might be the key line in all of Cassavetes’s films, which posit that it’s folly to try to read other people — because everyone, in one way or another, is crazy. But if you’re lucky, and you’re Cassel’s Moskowitz, you meet someone like Minnie (Gena Rowlands). On the surface, the two couldn’t be more different. But in the most romantic and overtly comic movie the director ever conceived, they understand each other perfectly.

From the outside, that kind of mutual comprehension appears to have been the hallmark of Rowlands and Cassavetes’s long creative partnership — and possibly their marriage. This series highlights movies whose directors were romantically involved with their stars. The results aren’t always as obviously affectionate. Compare Brian De Palma’s “Dressed to Kill” (on March 9-11 and 19), in which De Palma’s wife at the time, Nancy Allen, plays a prostitute who stumbles onto a murder. BEN KENIGSBERG

‘Pictures From Home’

Through April 30 at Studio 54, Manhattan; picturesfromhomebroadway.com. Running time: 1 hour 45 minutes.

Nathan Lane, Zoë Wanamaker and Danny Burstein star in this new play, adapted by Sharr White (“The Snow Geese”) from the California photographer Larry Sultan’s 1992 photo memoir of his parents, Irving and Jean. Bartlett Sher directs. Read the review.

‘A Beautiful Noise’

At the Broadhurst Theater, Manhattan; abeautifulnoisethemusical.com. Running time: 2 hours 15 minutes.

The Brooklyn-born singer-songwriter who gave the world “Sweet Caroline” gets the bio-musical treatment in this new show starring Will Swenson in the title role. Directed by Michael Mayer, it has a book by Anthony McCarten, choreography by Steven Hoggett and a well-stocked catalog of hits to draw on. Expect “Cracklin’ Rosie,” “Holly Holy” and more. Read the review.

‘Funny Girl’

At the August Wilson Theater, Manhattan; funnygirlonbroadway.com. Running time: 2 hours 50 minutes.

Lea Michele stars as the striving then celebrated entertainer Fanny Brice — opposite a sizzling Ramin Karimloo as her roguish husband, Nick — in the first Broadway revival of this Jule Styne-Bob Merrill musical since Barbra Streisand was a sensation in the role in 1964. Tovah Feldshuh plays Fanny’s mom. Isobel Lennart’s book was newly revised by Harvey Fierstein; Michael Mayer directs. Read the review.

‘& Juliet’

At the Stephen Sondheim Theater, Manhattan; andjulietbroadway.com. Running time: 2 hours 30 minutes.

Spoiler alert: At the end of “Romeo and Juliet,” both lovers die. Not so in this musical comedy, which imagines — with an assist from Anne Hathaway, Shakespeare’s wife — what happens when Juliet goes on living without her Romeo. Running in London’s West End since 2019, the show has a book by David West Read (“Schitt’s Creek”) and a song list full of pop hits by Max Martin (“… Baby One More Time”). Read the review.

last chance

‘Meret Oppenheim: My Exhibition’

Through March 4, Museum of Modern Art, 11 West 53 Street; moma.org.

One of the great things about this show is that “Object” — a.k.a. “The Fur-Lined Teacup,” the little 1936 sculpture whose notoriety tended to overshadow the rest of Meret Oppenheim’s long, prolific career — gets lost in the shuffle. In fact, by the time you work your way through this sprawling exhibition of paintings, abstract and not, collages, drawings and objects, “Object” may well be the last thing on your mind, even if it holds an important key to Oppenheim’s art. There is so much else to think about. And there is this restrained, cerebral, sometimes irritating artist to factor in. Restless, undeterred, she deserves a place in any modernist pantheon you can assemble. Read the review.

‘Edward Hopper’s New York’

Through March 5 at the Whitney Museum of American Art, 99 Gansevoort Street, Manhattan; whitney.org.

The Whitney’s latest dive into its extensive Edward Hopper holdings sounds at first like the museum coasting on its hometown hero. Here is an occasion to trot out works like “Early Sunday Morning,” which makes a tidy Anytown of rowhouses along Seventh Avenue. But focus instead on that apostrophe in the title: This is Hopper’s New York, emphasis on the possessive, and for all its crowd-pleasing fare, this is a more challenging show about his dominion over the city. In paintings we know well and many we don’t, as well as some enlightening works on paper and writings, the artist long described as a Realist is recast as the architect of his own personal fantasy metropolis. Read the review.

Critic’s Pick

‘Thierry Mugler: Couturissime’

Through May 7, Brooklyn Museum, 200 Eastern Parkway, Brooklyn; brooklynmuseum.org.

This exhibition serves as a reminder of just how important Thierry Mugler was as a link in the chain that got us to now, and how innovative his fashion truly was beneath the grandiosity and glitz. (Though the grandiosity and glitz is also awfully fun to see.) The point is foreshadowed not just by the epigraph that opens the show — “In my work I’ve always tried to make people look stronger than they really are” — but by the first room, which showcases not a gown but a hologram of a gown: an elaborate gold cage built on a metal skeleton Mugler created in 1985 for a Comédie-Française production of “La Tragédie de Macbeth.” The dress was too fragile to travel, so instead the Québec artist Michel Lemieux preserved it in its embodied state via a ghostly image. The result frames Mugler’s vision of women as the engines of society and sets the stage for the series of rooms that follow. Read the review.
