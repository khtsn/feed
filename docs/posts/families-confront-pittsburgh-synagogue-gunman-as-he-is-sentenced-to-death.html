In the final hearing of the trial, survivors and relatives of victims spoke directly to the man who killed 11 people in the Tree of Life synagogue.

Nicholas Bogel-Burroughs, Jon Moss and Campbell Robertson

Reporting from Pittsburgh

Aug. 3, 2023Updated 5:52 p.m. ET

A widow spoke of how her husband went to the Tree of Life synagogue to praise God and was instead riddled with bullets. A police officer described racing to the scene and finding his colleague who had taken a bullet to the head. A woman recalled finding her 97-year-old mother’s fading pulse as they cowered in the chapel after both being shot.

Survivors of the massacre and relatives of the 11 worshipers who were killed on Oct. 27, 2018, in that Pittsburgh synagogue confronted the gunman in court on Thursday with stories of grief, anger and perseverance. Then, U.S. District Judge Robert Colville sentenced the gunman, Robert Bowers, to death, imposing the sentence rendered a day earlier by the jury.

One by one, the members of a grieving club formed against their will stepped up to a microphone in a fifth-floor courtroom and described the holes left in their lives by the gunman. All the while, Mr. Bowers, 50, who had raged against Jewish people during the attack and has expressed no remorse since, looked the other way and flipped through a stack of papers.

“He murdered a 97-year-old great-grandmother, but he did not get us all,” said Andrea Wedner, who had hidden under the chapel’s pews with her mother, Rose Mallinger, where they were both shot. Ms. Wedner said that as she waited 40 long minutes there for help, she wondered whether her husband and children thought she was dead. When help finally came, she recalled, she had to step over the bodies of people she knew.

“I am haunted and forever chilled as I think about what I saw as I was being rescued that day,” Ms. Wedner said.

At one point, a relative of two brothers who were killed, Cecil and David Rosenthal, tried to get Mr. Bowers to acknowledge him and other family members.

“You were never man enough to look up at us during the trial,” said Michael Hirt, a brother-in-law of the Rosenthal victims. “I wonder now if you are man enough to look up at us.”

He stood in silence for five or six seconds as Mr. Bowers continued to stare at papers, and then Mr. Hirt continued: “I didn’t think so.”

Daniel Leger, a member of Dor Hadash, one of three congregations in the synagogue that day, was shot in the chest in the attack. He spoke directly to the gunman on Thursday, also telling Mr. Bowers that he wished he would stop looking down “long enough to look at me, the Jew he tried to kill.”

The shooting took place on a Sabbath morning when members of three congregations — Tree of Life, New Light and Dor Hadash — had begun gathering in different parts of the building. Mr. Bowers entered and began firing, armed with an AR-15 rifle and three handguns. In addition to the people he killed, six others were wounded, including four police officers.

Many survivors attended the trial, which included about nine weeks of testimony. It was chilling and often painful for those who had lived through it, but some of them said the testimony gave them a fuller understanding of what had happened.

Martin Gaynor, who survived the attack, said it was “terribly and profoundly unsettling” to experience the same kind of hate that caused his grandparents to flee to the United States. While he said many of his fears brought on by the attack had subsided, Mr. Gaynor said it was important that the justice system send the strongest possible signal that antisemitism will not be tolerated.

“If we permit hate to enter our hearts, we ourselves are diminished,” he said.

After hearing from about two dozen people, Judge Colville thanked his court staff and the lawyers working on the case, as well as the victims’ families.

“Frankly,” he added, “I have nothing specific that I care to say to Mr. Bowers, and I mean no particular offense.”

Shortly after, he handed down the death sentence that jurors had unanimously decided on, as well as additional life sentences for crimes including the obstruction of the free exercise of religious beliefs and the use of a firearm during a crime of violence. Elsewhere in the courthouse, several jurors were watching a video feed of the proceedings. Mr. Bowers was led out of the courtroom in handcuffs.

During the trial, Mr. Bowers’s lawyers described his troubled childhood: His parents fought, and each had threatened to kill him when he was a baby. His father killed himself when Mr. Bowers was 7.

The jury evaluated nine possible aggravating factors put forward by the prosecution and 115 possible mitigating factors asserted by the defense and weighed them against each other. Ultimately, they accepted many of the defense arguments about Mr. Bowers’s difficult life, including that he was committed to a psychiatric unit at 13. But they did not agree with the defense on other key points, rejecting the argument that Mr. Bowers had schizophrenia and had carried out the shooting “under mental or emotional disturbance.”

Many relatives and survivors who spoke in the wake of the verdict said they were grateful that Mr. Bowers would be sentenced to death, though some people connected to the shooting had said they opposed it.

Rabbi Doris Dyen, who arrived at the synagogue with her husband during the attack and heard gunshots inside, said that Mr. Bowers had forfeited his right to live by carrying out the attack, and that executing him would keep him from sharing his harmful ideas.

“He must never again be allowed to spread his hateful, antisemitic views to others, whether in person, in print, through the media and especially online,” Rabbi Dyen told the court.

Alan Mallinger, the son of Ms. Mallinger, cited a biblical passage, Leviticus 24:21, telling the court, “One who kills a beast shall make restitution for it, but one who kills a human being shall be put to death.”

What happens after Thursday’s sentencing is not entirely clear. Like all people sentenced to death, Mr. Bowers was automatically granted an appeal, and his lawyers have indicated that they intend to pursue it.

He was also charged with 36 counts in state court, including 11 counts of murder. The district attorney of Allegheny County, which includes Pittsburgh, agreed to pause the state prosecution while the federal process unfolded.

In a statement on Wednesday, the district attorney’s office said that because of the “emotional strain” of the federal trial for victims, relatives and the community, “it would be inappropriate for us to comment on our charges until we have had a chance to meet with the families.”
