Why Menendez’s Meals With Egyptian Officials Worry Security Experts

  • Closing Arguments
  • Key Players
  • The Charges
  • The Trial’s Odd Moments
  • A Failed Last-Ditch Effort

The bribery case against Senator Robert Menendez has revealed how foreign intelligence officials cultivated casual access to one of the most powerful Democrats in Washington.

July 11, 2024Updated 8:18 a.m. ET

It was a shocking charge against one of the highest-ranking members of Congress: acting as an agent of a foreign government.

Senator Robert Menendez, prosecutors said, had steered millions of dollars in aid to Egypt and toned down his rhetoric about the country’s human rights violations to lock in a lucrative side hustle for his soon-to-be wife, who had reinvented herself as an international consultant.

After a two-month bribery trial in Manhattan, jurors are expected to begin deliberating Thursday on whether prosecutors have proved a complex web of conspiracy charges. But regardless of whether they render a guilty verdict, the volley of messages and photos extracted from phones seized by the F.B.I. and introduced as evidence have revealed how Egyptian intelligence officials were able to cultivate casual access to one of the most powerful Democrats in Washington.

Mr. Menendez, the New Jersey senator who at the time led the Senate Foreign Relations Committee and had broad authority over foreign military aid, was often only a text message away from some of Egypt’s most powerful leaders. His intermediary was not a trained committee expert but his unemployed wife, who holds a master’s degree in French language and civilization but had last worked as a restaurant hostess.

The electronic exchanges have shown the degree to which modern communication methods have altered criminal prosecutions. They have also exposed potential risks to U.S. national security, according to interviews with former and current U.S. diplomats, academics and officials with Washington-based think tanks that focus on Middle East policy.

“This is a massive breach, I think, that has been underestimated,” said Jodi Vittori, a retired U.S. Air Force intelligence officer who worked on NATO’s counter-corruption task force in Afghanistan and teaches at Georgetown University’s Edmund A. Walsh School of Foreign Service.

The bribery scheme had simmered since early 2018, according to prosecutors. But the direct outreach to the senator’s wife, Nadine Menendez, by Egyptian intelligence officers intensified in 2020, text messages show.

Days before marrying the senator, Ms. Menendez got a text from an Egyptian general, Ahmed Helmy, who wanted to have dinner with the couple. General Helmy, Egypt’s top spy in Washington, traveled to New Jersey the next week, and he brought along a female companion who was eager to meet the newlyweds.

The woman, Mai Abdelmaguid, gave Ms. Menendez a gift — a scarf — and quickly began to build rapport, complimenting her “charming personality” and asking in a text message for advice about life in Washington, D.C.

“The lady who gave me the scarf wants a name of a hair salon,” Ms. Menendez texted her husband weeks after their October 2020 wedding, according to trial evidence. “She seems very unhappy with all the salons.”

The group met for dinner again less than three months later.

Mr. Menendez’s own staff members expressed alarm when they learned of meetings that had not appeared on the senator’s official schedule. (“All of this Egypt stuff is very weird,” Damian Murphy, staff director of the Foreign Relations Committee, wrote to a colleague, Sarah Arkin, in September 2021.)

“I didn’t know exactly who he was talking to, or what info he had or didn’t have, or who he might want to meet, or where information was coming from,” Ms. Arkin, who worked for Mr. Menendez for years, testified.

In a closing argument this week, a prosecutor described for jurors the various ways the government believes Mr. Menendez had worked on behalf of Egypt, a crucial strategic partner in the Middle East regularly criticized for its poor human rights record.

The senator shared sensitive details about U.S. embassy personnel in Cairo, prosecutors say. He secretly drafted a letter for Egyptian officials who were lobbying his Senate colleagues for millions more in military aid. And he told Ms. Menendez to pass along a message that he was about to “sign off” on $99 million in tank ammunition for Egypt.

In return, prosecutors say, bribes of gold and cash made their way to the couple’s modest home in Englewood Cliffs, N.J.

The senator, who has strenuously maintained his innocence, declined to comment on whether he believed his wife had been deliberately targeted by the Egyptians to do their bidding in Washington. His lawyers also had no comment.

But they have argued that the foreign agent charge “defies common sense.”

“Were the U.S. government in possession of actual evidence that a foreign power had infiltrated the U.S. Senate, they would have done something about it long before filing the charges in this case,” they wrote in a filing before the start of the trial.

‘We will make everything happen’

Ms. Menendez had been corresponding directly with General Helmy since at least early 2020, telling him in a March text message, “Anytime you need anything you have my number and we will make everything happen.”

But Ms. Abdelmaguid, the general’s companion, had arrived in the United States from Egypt only a few weeks before the October 2020 dinner meeting at the River Palm Terrace restaurant in Edgewater, N.J. She would later be introduced to the F.B.I. as a replacement for General Helmy, who was returning to Cairo, according to an email disclosed at trial.

Ms. Abdelmaguid was soon asking Ms. Menendez for far more than beauty tips.

The two women coordinated details of a June 2021 visit to Washington by General Abbas Kamel, chief of Egypt’s General Intelligence Service and a close adviser to the country’s president, Abdel Fattah el-Sisi. Ms. Abdelmaguid urged the Menendezes to visit Egypt. And she told Ms. Menendez that her bosses were “keen to meet” Representative Gregory W. Meeks, a New York Democrat who, as the ranking member of the House Foreign Affairs Committee, held significant sway over the allocation of foreign military aid.

Ms. Menendez, 57, is charged in the bribery conspiracy, but her trial has been postponed because she is undergoing treatment for breast cancer. Her lawyer, Barry Coburn, declined to comment.

Gordon Gray, a former ambassador to Tunisia and deputy assistant secretary of state who now teaches at George Washington University’s Elliott School of International Affairs, said the off-the-books meetings were not on their own “evidence of misconduct.”

Diplomats and foreign intelligence officers regularly seek to nurture valued relationships, he said. But Ms. Menendez’s role as an intermediary, combined with the lengths to which Egyptian intelligence officials went to meet privately with the senator — including travel to New Jersey — was unusual, he said.

“It isn’t the normal course of events,” Professor Gray said, “and it shouldn’t be the normal course of events.”

The Foreign Agents Registration Act, or FARA, was adopted in 1938 and requires U.S. citizens who lobby on behalf of a foreign principal to register with the government and to disclose their activities in highly detailed reports. Members of Congress are not permitted to register as agents — or to do the work of foreign agents.

Until the last decade the law was rarely enforced, but Mr. Menendez appeared well-versed in its rationale. In letters urging the Justice Department to investigate a former Florida congressman, he stressed FARA’s importance to national security.

“When foreign agents fail to register under FARA,” Mr. Menendez wrote in May 2022, “their activities on behalf of foreign powers are hidden from public view, obscuring potential threats to U.S. national security.”

‘Completely burned’

Mr. Menendez has characterized his activities as acts of routine statesmanship, not corruption.

But the Egyptian officials he was dealing with appeared well aware of the value of the relationship.

Wael Hana, a New Jersey businessman with ties to Egyptian intelligence who founded a halal meat certification company that prosecutors say was used to funnel bribes to the senator, referred to Mr. Menendez as “our man” in a message to an Egyptian general.

Ironically, it was actions taken by Mr. Hana, who is on trial with Mr. Menendez, that threatened to undermine that trust. Mr. Hana, who has known Ms. Menendez since before she began dating the senator, had cheated her out of the full value of a diamond ring he had promised her, according to a court filing by prosecutors.

As a result, Mr. Hana “was about to ruin things with Bob,” a confidential F.B.I. source, who was in touch with Egyptian officials, said in a recorded 2019 conversation. “Bob who is starting to listen to us.”

The two people on the recording said that Mr. Hana’s betrayal would create problems when Egyptian officials learned that “he ruined the biggest relation with one in the Senate by swindling him.”

In August 2021 the F.B.I. was notified that the Egyptian intelligence service was no longer working with Mr. Hana because it had concluded that he was “completely burned due to F.B.I. activity,” according to a redacted F.B.I. report.

The intelligence service wanted to put a separate “asset” in charge of IS EG Halal — the company Mr. Hana had founded and that prosecutors say provided a financing stream that funded bribes to the senator and his wife.

Mr. Hana’s lawyer, Lawrence S. Lustberg, said that Mr. Hana’s fall from favor with Egyptian officials directly undermines the prosecutors’ theory “that he was currying favor with Senator Menendez, which is what he is charged with.”

Cigarette butts and intimidation

Mr. Hana’s meat certification company had hit it big in April 2019.

In an abrupt pivot, the government of Egypt made IS EG Halal the sole U.S. entity authorized to certify that halal meat exported to Egypt had been prepared according to Islamic law, work that had been shared for years by four companies. Months later, Mr. Hana’s company was awarded a worldwide monopoly, and certification costs for slaughterhouses promptly soared, leading meat prices to spike in Egypt, according to an analysis by the U.S. Department of Agriculture.

The United States did not need to approve, and could not block, the business monopoly. But the U.S.D.A. nonetheless raised objections, drawing a phone call from Mr. Menendez urging the agency to back off, prosecutors said.

The pressure apparently did not stop there.

Bret Tate, a foreign service officer who in 2019 was stationed at the U.S. Embassy in Cairo, told jurors during the trial that he had expressed concerns about the market risks associated with relying solely on IS EG Halal, a startup business with no prior experience in halal certification.

It was around this time that Mr. Tate noticed he was being followed and that people had been inside his house when he was away, Eli J. Mark, a prosecutor, told the judge outside the presence of the jury. On one occasion, Mr. Tate returned home to find a cigarette in his toilet. Another time, electronics that had been on a table were moved to the floor in what Mr. Mark described as an “intentional show that people had been at his home.”

It was not an uncommon form of intimidation, said Yezid Sayigh, a senior fellow at the Malcolm H. Kerr Carnegie Middle East Center in Beirut, who said that such intrusions are rarely “subtle or hidden.”

“It’s clear that they were meant to be noticed so as to deliver a message and discourage the diplomats affected from being too active,” Mr. Sayigh said in an email.

Timothy E. Kaldas, deputy director of the Tahrir Institute for Middle East Policy, said he had been disappointed by the U.S. response to the explosive investigation by its own Justice Department.

Foreign military aid to Egypt remained roughly $1.3 billion even after dozens of subpoenas tied to the alleged bribery conspiracy were issued in late 2022. “This is not the sort of recklessness that we should uncritically support,” Mr. Kaldas said.

A spokesman for the State Department, citing the ongoing trial, declined to comment. Officials with the Egyptian embassy in Washington and the consulate in New York did not return calls or emails.

After Mr. Menendez was indicted in September and required to step down as chairman of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee, his replacement, Senator Ben Cardin of Maryland, used his clout on the committee to block $215 million in military aid to Egypt.

He did not mention Mr. Menendez in a statement he issued at the time, and his office declined additional comment this week.

“Egypt’s stability is in the U.S. national interest,” Mr. Cardin said in October. “And that interest is best served when the Egyptian government is taking sustainable, concrete, and meaningful steps to improve respect for the human rights of its citizens.”
