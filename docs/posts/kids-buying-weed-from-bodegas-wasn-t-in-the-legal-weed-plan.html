Kids Buying Weed From Bodegas Wasn’t in the ‘Legal Weed’ Plan

Big CITY

The rollout of legal marijuana in New York has been a slow and laborious process. The corner stores aren’t waiting.

Ginia Bellafante

Ginia Bellafante writes the Big City column, a weekly commentary on the politics, culture and life of New York City.

Feb. 24, 2023, 5:00 a.m. ET

Not long ago, a mother in Westchester learned from her teenage son that he and his friends had gone to a nearby bodega and bought weed. She understood — they were kids, stifled and robbed by the pandemic of so many opportunities for indulging the secretive rituals of adolescence. Their parents only got through it all on Barolo and “The White Lotus.”

But it was deeply troubling to her that a store was selling weed to kids — New York State’s decriminalization statute makes it illegal to sell to anyone under 21 — so she embarked on an investigation. Predictably, when she confronted the bodega owners, they denied that they were distributing to anyone underage, so her next stop was a visit to the local police precinct, where she did not encounter the sense of urgency she had hoped for.

The cops greeted her with a kind of smug indifference, she said, an affect of I told you so, suggesting that liberals were now faced with the downstream impact of values that law enforcement had always disdained. Mothers in earthy, expensive footwear from the River Towns to Park Slope had supported the legalization of marijuana on the grounds that it needlessly funneled so many young Black and brown men into the criminal justice system. But now it was ubiquitous, and in the worst case scenarios possibly laced with fentanyl, and all too easy for their children to access. The bodega, in this instance, was a short distance from the local high school. Parental text chains, surely one of the best primary-source documents of 21st century anxiety, were operating at full intensity.

In New York City, Mayor Eric Adams has recently sharpened his focus on combating the proliferation of unlicensed cannabis vendors, which now number between 1,400 and 1,500, many of them targeting the young and impressionable. While marijuana is technically no longer against the law in New York, the slow pace of the state licensing process has brought just three legal recreational dispensaries to the city so far, leaving the sale of weed largely to an economy we might describe as underground were it not happening so explicitly at street level. As a city and a nation, the mayor said at a news conference this week, we were “losing our grip”; children were going to school high on gummies they bought at the corner store.

While his comments might have struck some as Nixonian hyperbole, they ran the risk of understating the scope of the problem. Last month, at a City Council hearing to address the issue, Lynn Schulman, chair of the Committee on Health, reported lab findings that showed “prohibitive levels” of E. coli, salmonella, nickel and lead in 40 percent of smokable marijuana, edibles and vape cartridges purchased from a sampling of 20 unlicensed smoke shops across the city. More troublingly, they contained amounts of THC — the primary psychoactive compound in cannabis, the effects of which are especially harmful to the developing brain — “inconsistent” with what was advertised. Other products were colorfully packaged, “imitating candies such as Rice Krispies and Skittles.”

Before the pandemic upended the lives of American teenagers with such catastrophic results, they were, at least by certain measures, doing relatively well. Over the past decade, rates of drug and alcohol use, as well as sexual activity, had been in decline. Between 2011 and 2021, according to data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, the percentage of high school students who reported using marijuana dropped from 23 percent to 16 percent. The fear now was that all this progress would roll back as the crises around learning loss and mental health, brought on by so many months of isolation, were meeting a moment of disinhibition, nihilism and a carnival culture around the sale of weed.

The city’s Department of Education tells us that the number of overall incidents involving the use or possession of drugs and alcohol at school has increased approximately 29 percent compared with the same time last year and that administrators have seen an even larger uptick among middle school students. There was every reason, a school nurse not prone to hysteria told me, for parents to be panicked.

Like so many urban maladies, this one is rooted, however maddeningly, in the confusions of bureaucracy and the limits to neighborhood control imposed by an astronomical real estate market. The labyrinthine web of state and city agencies and task forces entrusted to oversee the evolving cannabis industry have made attempts at enforcement directed at the illegal sector more difficult rather than less. The consumer protection office, the health and police departments, the state’s newly formed Office of Cannabis Management and the city sheriff are all directly or indirectly involved in the effort. But it is not an easy thing to determine which entity has jurisdiction over what.

Beyond that, the pandemic brought only more storefront vacancies to the city, creating an opening for smoke shops presumably not all that resistant to high rents. Last month, a City Council member, Gale Brewer, reported that 28 had opened on the Upper West Side.

A sign of potential reversal arrived earlier this month, when the Police Department filed suit against four East Village smoke shops after an underage auxiliary officer was able to walk in to each one and buy a $30 bag of weed packed in mylar. In a novel approach, the named defendants included the limited liability corporations that owned the buildings housing these outfits. At the same time, the office of Alvin L. Bragg, the Manhattan district attorney, sent letters to each of the known 400 smoke shops in Manhattan alerting them that it was prepared to begin civil action against store owners and, crucially, their landlords, who would be made to start eviction proceedings against any tenant involved in this kind of illegal trade.

After years of overly aggressive policing of marijuana merely at the level of possession, the state law legalizing it was designed with equity as a primary goal, giving small businesses in neighborhoods that had been devastated by the punitive tactics of stop-and-frisk an opportunity to build wealth selling safe, uncontaminated weed to adults. What was not supposed to happen was an undermining of the legal market by off-book vendors selling cheaply to the most vulnerable while making private equity landlords richer.
