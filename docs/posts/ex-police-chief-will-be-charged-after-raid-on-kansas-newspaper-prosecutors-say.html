Ex-Police Chief Will Be Charged After Raid on Kansas Newspaper, Prosecutors Say

The former chief, Gideon Cody, will be charged with interference with the judicial process, a felony, after he ordered the raid on The Marion County Record last year, prosecutors said.

Aug. 5, 2024, 5:32 p.m. ET

A former Kansas police chief who ordered a raid last year on the office of a local newspaper and the home of its publisher will be charged with interfering in the judicial process, a felony, prosecutors said on Monday.

In a 124-page report, two special prosecutors said that Gideon Cody, the former chief of police in Marion, Kan., would be the only person charged in connection with the raid on The Marion County Record, a weekly newspaper, on Aug. 11, 2023.

The report said there was no reason to believe that any of the other local police officers or county sheriff’s deputies who were involved in the search — or any of the newspaper’s staff members — had committed any crimes.

The raid was roundly condemned by First Amendment experts and thrust Marion — a small town that is home to less than 2,000 people — into the national spotlight.

It also led to several civil lawsuits, including one that was filed by Deb Gruver, a former reporter at The Record, against Mr. Cody and others, claiming that he had injured her hand while seizing her personal cellphone during the raid. Her lawsuit resulted in a $235,000 settlement in June.

The authorities had said the search was part of an investigation into how The Marion County Record obtained and handled the driving record of a local restaurateur, Kari Newell — and whether her privacy had been violated in the process.

The report concluded that no laws had been violated in the process of obtaining the record.

Only days after the raid, the top prosecutor in Marion County, Joel Ensey, said that there had not been sufficient evidence to support the search and that all of the devices and materials seized by the authorities would be returned.

The Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press also criticized the raid in a letter to Chief Cody last year, saying that it risked “chilling the free flow of information in the public interest.” The letter was signed by more than 30 news organizations, including The New York Times.

Mr. Ensey appointed the two special prosecutors — Marc Bennett, the Sedgwick County district attorney, and Barry Wilkerson, the Riley County attorney — to review the case, a sprawling small-town imbroglio involving local police officers, lawyers, city councilors, reporters and the mayor.

The prosecutors’ report concluded that the Marion police conducted an “inadequate investigation” before asking a judge to authorize warrants to search the newspaper office and the home of the newspaper publisher and editor, Eric Meyer.

But it found no evidence that Marion law enforcement officials had recognized the inadequacy of the investigation or intentionally or knowingly misled other law enforcement agents or the court.

“The evidence strongly suggests they genuinely believed they were investigating criminal acts,” the report said.

It said that Mr. Cody would be charged in connection with actions he took after the raid was carried out.

It found that on Sept. 29, 2023, the Marion city administrator, Brogan Jones, received several phone calls from attorneys for the city, and “it was brought to his attention that after the execution of the search warrants, Chief Cody had instructed Kari Newell to delete text messages sent between Chief Cody and Ms. Newell.”

Chief Cody was placed on administrative leave that day and resigned on Oct. 2, the report said.

In an email, Mr. Bennett’s office said that Mr. Cody would be charged later this week in Marion County District Court with interference with the judicial process, a felony that is punishable by up to 23 months in prison but may result in lighter sentences for defendants with no prior convictions.

There was no immediate response on Monday to a voice mail message and a text message left at a number listed in an online directory under Mr. Cody’s name. Two of the lawyers who represented Mr. Cody in the lawsuit filed by Ms. Gruver declined to comment.

Emily Bradbury, the executive director of the Kansas Press Association, said the prosecutors’ report “conclusively determined that the reporters involved acted within the bounds of the law.”

“This vindication reiterates the vital role of a free press in our society and the importance of protecting journalistic freedom,” she said in a statement. The pending charge against Mr. Cody, she said, is “a crucial step toward accountability and justice.”

Phyllis Zorn, a reporter at The Marion County Record who is still suing Mr. Cody and others, said she welcomed the report.

“There have been times over the last year, I was worried because there were so many law enforcement officers lined up against me,” Ms. Zorn said in an interview. “But I had faith that eventually justice will be done.”

The report concluded that no charges would be filed in connection with the death of Mr. Meyer’s mother, Joan Meyer, 98, who died a day after the police searched the newspaper office and the home she shared with her son.

The coroner noted that Ms. Meyer, whose death was “natural” and the result of “sudden cardiac arrest,” had found the raid “extremely upsetting,” the report said.

But there was no evidence to suggest that the officers intended to cause Ms. Meyer’s death, or that they knew that executing the warrants would cause her death, the report said.

Mr. Meyer said in an interview on Monday that he was still suing the police, the sheriff and others in connection with his mother’s death and the raid on the newspaper office.

He said he was thankful that Mr. Cody would be charged, but said the prosecutors’ report “glossed over a lot of wrongdoing.”
