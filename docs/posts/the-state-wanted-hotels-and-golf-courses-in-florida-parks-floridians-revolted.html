Patricia Mazzei

Reporting from Miami

Sept. 5, 2024

A plan to build golf courses, pickleball courts and hotels in some of Florida’s popular state parks, which became public late last month when a state employee decided to leak it, has set off extraordinary backlash against the administration of Gov. Ron DeSantis.

The plan, shared with The Tampa Bay Times through intermediaries, prompted a series of grass-roots protests across the state as Floridians demanded that the parks be kept intact. Republican leaders from Washington to the state capital, Tallahassee, including some of the governor’s stalwart allies, were among those questioning the plan, calling it an affront to long-held bipartisan conservation efforts in Florida.

James Gaddis, a cartographer for the state’s Department of Environmental Protection who leaked the plan, said in an interview that he had been asked to draw up conceptual land use maps that would have destroyed environmentally sensitive lands and imperiled plant and animal species.

The DeSantis administration initially defended the plan, which it called the “Great Outdoors Initiative,” as a way “to expand public access, increase outdoor activities and provide new lodging options across Florida’s state parks.” It announced a vague version of the plan on Aug. 19 — a day before a story with more details, based on the leak, appeared in The Tampa Bay Times.

But, faced with an unrelenting wave of opposition, Mr. DeSantis shelved it last week, a rare policy setback for a governor used to getting his way. It was the kind of bruising moment that some in Tallahassee think Mr. DeSantis could face more often after his failed campaign for the Republican presidential nomination left him politically weakened.

“They’re going back to the drawing board,” Mr. DeSantis said at a public event on Aug. 28, adding that the plan was “half-baked and not ready for prime time.”

By Saturday, Mr. Gaddis had been fired. On Monday, he opened a GoFundMe account disclosing his identity as the leaker and trying to raise $10,000 to help with his sudden unemployment. By Thursday, he had raised more than $211,000.

In an interview, Mr. Gaddis called the outpouring “overwhelming” after losing a “dream job.”

In response to a request for comment about Mr. Gaddis’s firing, the state provided a copy of his termination letter. It said he was being fired over “conduct unbecoming a public employee, violation of law or department rules, negligence and misconduct.” A spokeswoman for the Environmental Protection Department declined further comment on “personnel matters.”

Mr. DeSantis said the leak had gone to an unnamed “left-wing group,” which Mr. Gaddis denied. He declined to say exactly to whom he had leaked the information.

Mr. Gaddis said he had just finished a shift at his second job, stocking shelves at a store in Tallahassee, when he received the termination letter on Saturday. It hurt, he said, but he was buoyed by the broad opposition to the plan and the support for his actions.

“Seeing a lot of bipartisanship in the state of Florida,” he added, “was just awesome.”

Before Mr. DeSantis joined the presidential race last year, he appeared unstoppable. He had been re-elected by 19 percentage points in 2022 after his meteoric rise as a darling of the political right during the divisive culture wars that dominated the coronavirus pandemic.

But he ended his campaign in January after placing a distant second to former President Donald J. Trump in the Iowa caucuses.

With pandemic-era political fights, many of which revolved around education, further and further in the past, some of Mr. DeSantis’s chosen candidates for school board seats lost primary elections last month.

Polls show that a majority of Florida voters support constitutional amendments on the November ballot that would legalize recreational marijuana and guarantee abortion rights should they pass. Mr. DeSantis has vowed to defeat both of the questions, which would need more than 60 percent approval to succeed.

Nikki Fried, the chairwoman of the Florida Democratic Party, said that Floridians were tired of being “lab rats” for right-wing policies. “Voters are rejecting extremism,” she said last week. On Thursday, several Democratic members of Congress from Florida urged the governor to release all public records regarding the parks plan.

More striking, however, was the Republican pushback against Mr. DeSantis over the plan.

Among its proposals were a disc golf course, up to four pickleball courts and 10 cabins at Oleta River State Park in Miami-Dade County; three golf courses at Jonathan Dickinson State Park in Martin County; and a 350-room lodge at Anastasia State Park in St. Johns County.

Early on, a spokesman for Mr. DeSantis defended the plan. “It’s high time we made public lands more accessible to the public,” Jeremy Redfern, the spokesman, said in a statement.

To move the proposals forward, the Environmental Protection Department had planned to hold public meetings around the state on Aug. 27 that would each last one hour.

In a letter to the governor, the state’s two senators, the Republicans Marco Rubio and Rick Scott, and other officials called the brief time set aside for the rushed meetings “absolutely ridiculous.” Representative Brian Mast, a Republican whose district includes Jonathan Dickinson State Park in Hobe Sound, Fla., said his office would file public records requests to get answers.

“Over my dead body will there be a golf course at Jonathan Dickinson State Park!” Mr. Mast wrote on Facebook.

The president of the State Senate, the state agriculture commissioner and the state chief financial officer, all Republicans, also opposed the plan.

Representative Matt Gaetz, a Republican from the Florida Panhandle who criticized the plan, later credited Mr. DeSantis for withdrawing it. “He did the right thing,” Mr. Gaetz wrote on the X platform. “Others might have dug in but Ron was responsive and thoughtful.”

Protests had cropped up in several corners of the state, including in Mr. DeSantis’s hometown, Dunedin. A protest at Oleta River State Park in North Miami Beach was organized in part by a home-schooling mother who told CBS Miami that she frequently takes her children there.

Outside the Environmental Protection Department’s headquarters in Tallahassee, where about 150 protesters gathered on Aug. 27, Cris Costello, senior organizing manager for Sierra Club Florida, said pickleball and golf belonged in local parks, not state parks.

Eric Draper, who directed the Florida Parks Service until 2021 and has worked in parks advocacy for four decades, said the outcry to save the parks from development was unlike any he had ever seen: organized by the grass-roots and fueled by residents’ sense of collective ownership of the parks.

He added that, despite Mr. DeSantis’s suggestions to the contrary, he does not believe that the department would have prepared the plan without involvement from the governor’s office.

“They were definitely fast-tracking approval of this and would have been successful, I think,” if not for the leak, Mr. Draper said.

Mr. Gaddis, 41, who described himself as a “centrist” Democrat, had previously worked for a different state agency investigating Medicaid fraud, he said. But his passions were geography and history, and he jumped at the chance to become a parks cartographer. The job, which he held for about two years, paid about $49,300 a year.

On his GoFundMe page, donors praised Mr. Gaddis for what he did. Some urged him to run for office.

“You gave us all a great gift,” one wrote.

Valerie Crowder contributed reporting from Tallahassee, Fla. Kitty Bennett contributed research.
