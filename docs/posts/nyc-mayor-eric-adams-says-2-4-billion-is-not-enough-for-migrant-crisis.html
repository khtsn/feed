NYC Mayor Eric Adams Says $2.4 Billion Is Not Enough for Migrant Crisis

Mayor Eric Adams told state lawmakers in Albany that New York City needed $4.6 billion just to pay for half of its yearly costs related to the migrant influx.

Feb. 6, 2024Updated 4:18 p.m. ET

With the migrant crisis continuing to roil New York City, Mayor Eric Adams appeared to score a win last month when the governor vowed to commit $2.4 billion — more than double last year’s proposal — to cover the costs of caring for the tens of thousands of people who have arrived in New York to seek refuge.

But on Tuesday, Mr. Adams said even that would not be enough.

Testifying at the State Capitol in Albany, the mayor told lawmakers that the state would need to pony up at least half the cost of caring for migrants to keep the city from making drastic budget cuts, a figure his team put at $4.6 billion.

“We’re the economic engine of the state,” Mr. Adams said. “And we’ve always been here for the state. We need the state now to be here for us in the city.”

New York officials had hoped to divide the cost of sheltering migrants equally among the city, state and federal governments. But federal officials refused to commit to that arrangement, leaving the city to press the state for more funds.

In addition to more money for migrants, the mayor used his trip to Albany — an annual tradition known as Tin Cup Day when local leaders make their budget pitches to the state — to meet with legislative leaders and Gov. Kathy Hochul. He also pressed lawmakers for mayoral control of schools, the authority to address illegal cannabis vendors and an expansion of the city’s borrowing power.

New York legalized recreational marijuana use in 2021 with the goal of lifting up those communities of color that had been disproportionately victimized by the war on drugs. But licenses to sell cannabis have been slow in coming, as regulators sort through economic, environmental and legal concerns, allowing unlicensed vendors to proliferate.

Last month, Ms. Hochul called the rollout a “disaster” in an interview with The Buffalo News’s editorial board and has proposed measures that would allow the state and localities to clamp down on illegal vendors. Mr. Adams has said that he supports the proposal, but believes it could go farther.

While mayors often face tough crowds in Albany, Mr. Adams found a broadly sympathetic audience this year, fielding compliments from both sides of the aisle for different aspects of his handling of the migrant crisis, despite the partisan disagreements about the broader fixes for the problem.

The warm reception was no doubt welcome to Mr. Adams, whose popularity has waned amid local and federal investigations of people in the mayor’s orbit and into his fund-raising practices. Earlier today, an unrelated federal inquiry resulted in the indictment of 70 people associated with the New York City Housing Authority, an investigation that Justice Department officials have called one of the largest public corruption cases in history.

Mr. Adams, a former state senator, has framed his track record in Albany as a largely victorious one, citing the approval of red-light cameras, a favorable deal on Metropolitan Transportation Authority funding and two amendments to the state’s bail laws as evidence of his strong relationship with the Legislature.

But Mr. Adams’s decision to publicly and repeatedly push for changes to the state’s bail reform laws enraged many Democratic lawmakers who saw his stance as not only undermining the party’s work on criminal justice but offering election year fodder to Republicans who blamed the law for a pandemic-era rise in violence.

And while Mr. Adams was ultimately successful in winning changes to state law that would allow judges to jail more people awaiting trial, he failed to persuade lawmakers to grant him four years of mayoral control over city schools. Instead, Mr. Adams secured only two years, under the condition that he shrink class sizes.

This year, the mayor is once again asking for four years of school control, a request backed by Ms. Hochul.

Even so, some lawmakers raised concerns about the practice of awarding control of schools to the mayor at all, suggesting that another management system might be more effective. Others raised questions about the ways the mayor had chosen to spend the city’s current budget, noting the cuts to libraries and sanitation.

“If you do get this $2.4 billion, that’s a pretty big tin cup,” Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal, a Democrat from the West Side of Manhattan, told the mayor at one point, referring to the governor’s proposal. “Do we have your commitment that all the cuts will be restored?”

Mr. Adams deflected the question, reiterating that $2.4 billion would not be enough. “We need more,” he said.

During a later exchange about the request to increase the city’s borrowing limit, Senator John C. Liu, a Democrat from Queens, questioned the mayor’s priorities.

“So what will come first, schools or the jails?” the senator asked the city’s budget director, Jacques Jiha.

Mr. Jiha laughed, saying it was a good question.

“Say schools quickly before I run out of time,” Mr. Liu pressed.

“We have three major …” Mr. Jiha began, before Mr. Liu cut him off.

“Wrong answer,” he said.

Dana Rubinstein contributed reporting.
