Weight Discrimination Is Legal. Should New York City Change That?

The City Council is expected to approve a bill this spring that would ban weight discrimination in hiring and housing. Some states are considering similar measures.

April 6, 2023Updated 8:31 a.m. ET

Victoria Abraham has attracted a huge online following as a self-described fat activist who preaches body positivity and calls out bias. But after graduating from New York University last year with a degree in public policy, she cannot avoid wondering if her job search will be hampered by her size.

Ms. Abraham makes no secret of her weight, including her body in the photo on her LinkedIn profile so prospective employers know whom they are considering hiring.

“There is a perception that you’re lazy or unable to do the work,” she said. “People don’t even realize that they have that bias.”

Ms. Abraham was among a group of people who testified recently on behalf of a bill that would prohibit discrimination in New York City based on a person’s weight or height in employment, housing and access to public accommodations. Although the bill has not been scheduled for a vote, it has the support of a majority of the City Council, with 34 co-sponsors out of 51 members, and could be approved as soon as May.

Mayor Eric Adams, a Democrat who published a book in 2020 about losing 35 pounds on a plant-based diet, said on Wednesday that he was supportive of the bill and was still reviewing the details.

“We should never treat people differently because of their weight,” Mr. Adams said at a news conference at City Hall.

The effort is part of a growing national campaign to address weight discrimination, with lawmakers in New Jersey and Massachusetts considering similar measures banning the practice. Michigan and Washington State already prohibit it, as do some cities, like Madison, Wis., and Washington, D.C.

The momentum to add weight to the list of protected groups, which now includes race, gender, religion and disability, comes as the body acceptance movement continues to gain popularity. Podcasts like “Maintenance Phase” and social media creators have spread awareness that not all overweight people are unhealthy, and that health metrics like the body mass index are flawed.

Many Americans gained weight during the height of the pandemic as lockdowns kept people stuck at home. Obesity rates have risen in the United States over the last two decades, and more than 40 percent of American adults are considered obese.

The sponsor of New York City’s bill, Shaun Abreu, a councilman from northern Manhattan, said that he gained 40 pounds earlier in the pandemic and noticed a change in how people treated him. Someone touched his stomach, for example, and told him he was getting too big.

But it was Mr. Abreu’s background as a lawyer and his familiarity with labor law that prompted him to introduce the weight discrimination bill, working with the National Association to Advance Fat Acceptance, a nonprofit advocacy group founded in 1969.

“What you look like should not impact your ability to get a job,” Mr. Abreu said in an interview. “We should embrace body positivity and inclusion. It’s so important for people to feel comfortable.”

But some Republicans and business leaders have concerns about the legislation. Kathryn S. Wylde, president of the Partnership for New York City, a business advocacy group, said that the City Council should provide documentation to prove that weight discrimination was a “significant problem” in the city.

“This is another mandate where enforcement will be primarily through litigation, which imposes a burden on employers, regulators and the courts,” she said.

Joseph Borelli, the City Council’s minority leader and a Republican, said he worried the bill would “empower people to sue anyone and everything.” Mr. Borelli has posted on social media about his quest to lose weight.

“I’m overweight, but I’m not a victim,” he said. “No one should feel bad for me except for my struggling shirt buttons.”

Some of the bill’s supporters have questioned whether the mayor’s own experience of losing weight and his evangelism over his mostly vegan diet and workout routine might make him less likely to understand their concerns. On Wednesday, Mr. Adams downplayed any discrimination he might have faced when he was overweight.

“Even when I was heavier, I was still a good-looking guy,” he said, laughing.

The Council speaker, Adrienne Adams, has not said whether she supports the bill or when her office might schedule a vote on it. Her spokesman, Mandela Jones, said in a statement that “no one should be denied opportunities for work, housing or other essentials because of their physical attributes.”

State lawmakers in New York are also considering a weight discrimination law, with support from Brad Hoylman-Sigal, a state senator who chairs the judiciary committee, and from the powerful Retail, Wholesale & Department Store Union. Mr. Hoylman-Sigal, who said he was hopeful that the Legislature would pass the state law this year, said that he became interested in the issue after seeing how his 12-year-old daughter was being affected by discussions about beauty on TikTok.

“This was really in response to some personal observations and how social media is shaping her viewpoint around body image,” he said.

Mr. Hoylman-Sigal said he was also worried about employers who ask job candidates for photographs and whether that could lead to discrimination — a practice that Mayor Adams received criticism over last year.

The City Council bill also prohibits discrimination based on height, and a separate bill would ban discrimination against people with tattoos. It creates an exemption for employers who have a “bona fide occupational” reason to consider weight or height, and situations in which there is a public health and safety concern. Some jobs like police officers and fire fighters have physical requirements such as a timed run, climbing over a six-foot barrier or dragging a fire hose.

Under the bill, complaints about weight discrimination would be investigated by the city’s Commission on Human Rights, which already handles complaints over race, gender and pregnancy.

The commission has investigated at least two cases regarding weight when another issue like pregnancy or race was involved, city officials said, including one in which a supervisor made derogatory comments about a worker’s weight and race. In that case, the employer paid emotional distress damages and civil penalties.

Some workers have unsuccessfully filed lawsuits over weight discrimination, including a bus driver in New Jersey who lost his job after failing a medical exam and a New York City firefighter who was told to lose 71 pounds in 30 days. Another case involved a New Jersey casino monitoring the weight of the “Borgata Babes” cocktail waitresses.

Jennifer Shinall, a law professor at Vanderbilt University who studies weight discrimination, said that litigation shows that bias can be explicit or implicit. Colleagues might openly tease someone about their weight or say something under the guise of concern — comments that are often legal under federal law, she said.

“A lot of this discrimination is also implied and not necessarily spoken, and it appears to happen at the point of hiring,” she said. “We know that weight discrimination is the worst for customer-facing occupations — things like sales or anything that involves contact with the public.”

At the City Council hearing for the bill in February, New Yorkers spoke about hardships they have faced because of their weight. Ms. Abraham testified that subway turnstiles were too narrow for her to pass through easily and that the desks at New York University were too small for her, preventing her from taking notes during class. Now she is worried about her job search too.

“I graduated cum laude and I have all of the qualifications, but I’m terrified about trying to get a job right now,” Ms. Abraham said in an interview.

Tracy Cox, a soprano at the Metropolitan Opera, said at the hearing that she had faced discrimination and body shaming in her career as a performer where “a fat singer is the rare and remarkable exception.”

“Let me do my job,” she said, “without being casually encouraged to develop an eating disorder or have bariatric surgery, which has happened to me countless times in the workplace.”
