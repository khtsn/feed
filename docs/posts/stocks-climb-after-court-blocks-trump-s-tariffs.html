Stocks Climb After Court Blocks Trump’s Tariffs

The ruling added to uncertainty, but investors were encouraged that it could slow momentum for a trade war that threatens economic and corporate growth.

May 29, 2025Updated 9:41 a.m. ET

Stock markets rose on Thursday, the day after a panel of U.S. judges blocked President Trump from imposing some of his steepest tariffs, which have threatened to squeeze consumers, snarl supply chains and dent corporate earnings.

The S&P 500 index rose 0.8 percent in early trading. Although the ruling is subject to appeal, the latest twist in the on-again, off-again tariff drama is set to “lift investors’ spirits,” wrote Derren Nathan, head of equity research at the brokerage firm Hargreaves Lansdown.

Nvidia, the chip giant, pulled the market higher after it reported strong earnings growth on Wednesday and suggested that the tech industry’s embrace of artificial intelligence had ample room to run. Its stock, which exerts an outsize influence over major indexes, surged 5 percent.

Markets elsewhere were mixed, with stocks in Hong Kong, Japan and South Korea all rising more than 1 percent. But indexes in Europe fluctuated between small gains and losses.

Bond markets were choppy. The yield on the 30-year Treasury note — a focus for investors after Mr. Trump’s bill to cut taxes deepened worries about debt and deficits — briefly rose, crossing back above 5 percent, before turning lower.

Analysts at Panmure Liberum noted that the court ruling suggested that in the long run, “extreme tariffs and rapidly changing tariffs are less likely to happen going forward, which reduces uncertainty somewhat.” But in the short term, they said, the ruling and subsequent challenges to it “will add significant uncertainty to the mix, making it even harder for U.S. businesses to plan.”

White House officials sought to convey the message that little had changed as a result of the court’s ruling, saying that they had other avenues for pursuing tariffs. “Nothing’s really changed,” said Peter Navarro, President Trump’s trade adviser.

Markets could continue to bob up and down on tariff developments, as investors now parse court rulings and appeals in addition to Mr. Trump’s threats, delays and concessions.

Jason Karaian is the business news director, based in London. He was previously the editor of DealBook.

More on Trump’s Tariffs

  • Possibility for Economic Damage: The economic fallout on the American, European and global economies will be severe if President Trump follows through with his threat to impose a 50 percent tariff on all goods coming into the United States from the European Union.

  • Negotiations Begin: When President Trump delayed 50 percent tariffs on the European Union, officials on both sides of the Atlantic billed it as an opportunity to reach a trade deal. The path toward de-escalation, however, remains fraught.

  • U.S. Batteries Industry: Domestic battery factories depend on Chinese components and federal subsidies to meet America’s rising energy demand. President Trump’s tariffs on China and budget bill could knock the fledgling industry off course.

  • U.S.-Vietnam Trade Deal?: Among dozens of countries that were hit with steep tariffs, Vietnam was among the most publicly willing to meet the demands of the Trump administration. But China is a sticking point.

  • Apple and India: Apple and India have invested years and billions of dollars in teaming up against China. Things are looking tougher for India now, and for its American business partners. Trump has changed up his tactics with China, backing off his highest tariffs.

  • Outselling Tesla in Europe: Despite steep tariffs, the Chinese carmaker BYD of China leapfrogged Tesla for the first time, in what an analyst called a “watershed moment” for the continent’s auto market.
