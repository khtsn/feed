Anushka Patil

The cyberwar has expanded rapidly beyond Ukraine and Russia, new data shows.

The digital frontline of the war between Russia and Ukraine has expanded rapidly beyond the warring countries, driven by a handful of pro-Russian hacking groups who set their sights on Ukraine’s allies late last year, according to new data released on Friday.

Hackers carrying out attacks related to the conflict have targeted countries not directly involved in the fighting twice as many times in the first half of this year than in all of 2022, according to the data. Poland and Germany have both been top targets over their military support of Ukraine.

The data was shared by the CyberPeace Institute, an independent organization that tracks cyberattacks on nonmilitary targets. Its latest report, published Friday, documents the escalation of a decentralized, volunteer-driven digital war that security researchers said has been highly usual from the start.

Over 100 actors, ranging from nation-state hackers to loosely defined groups of volunteers, have carried out cyberattacks related to the war in Ukraine since the invasion, the report says. In 2022, 461 of those attacks targeted countries other than Russia or Ukraine. That number has surpassed 900 in the past six months alone, CyberPeace has found.

Poland, which has frequently led the way in marshaling Western nations to send Kyiv more advanced weaponry, has consistently been a top target. Major national news sites in the country were hit by what appeared to be a coordinated attack in May, and Polish officials have accused Russia of being behind an attack in March that blocked access to a government tax website.

Germany and Canada have also been top targets in recent months, CyberPeace found. Several German government websites and airports were hit by large-scale attacks in recent months, and officials have issued warnings about the security of critical infrastructure.

The majority of the cyberattacks on nonmilitary targets in countries other than Russia or Ukraine have been distributed denial-of-service attacks, or DDoS, that seek to overload or take down websites with a flood of traffic. Their impact can be disruptive but is often short-lived.

But the sheer volume of the attacks underlines how the volunteer groups are giving civilians a “frictionless capability to take part in the attack from anywhere else in the world,” said Stéphane Duguin, the chief executive of CyberPeace.

The pro-Russian hacking collective NoName057(16), for example, developed a tool that volunteers can download to join on DDoS operations. The collective offers cryptocurrency incentives to top participants , “encouraging people to contribute more technical resources for a more powerful attack,” threat researchers at the cybersecurity company Sentinel One found in January.

The collective has claimed responsibility for more attacks outside of Ukraine this year than any other group, according to CyberPeace data.

Security researchers cautioned in the early days after Russia’s full-scale invasion that low-impact cyberattacks by volunteer hackers were unlikely to change the course of fighting on the ground. But the proliferation of such groups in the cyberwar still has the potential to stir chaos, Mr. Duguin said.

If a cease-fire were established tomorrow, “do we really think that out of these 100 threat actors, everyone is going to respect the cease-fire in cyberspace?” he said. “When this is so disconnected, crowdsourced, decentralized? It’s almost impossible.”

The top U.S. general defends Ukraine’s counteroffensive, arguing it is making slow and steady progress.

WASHINGTON — America’s top military officer acknowledged on Friday that Ukraine’s counteroffensive against dug-in Russian defenses was going slowly, but he said Ukraine’s army was still making steady progress in “a fight for their life.”

Gen. Mark A. Milley, the chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, said the Ukrainian troops, many of whom were trained and equipped by the United States and other NATO allies, were “advancing steadily, deliberately working its way through very difficult minefields” at some 500 to 2,000 yards a day. “Slow advance is very deliberate,” the general said. “That is happening.”

He added that the fact the long awaited push to recapture occupied territory was not advancing as rapidly as many experts had predicted “doesn’t surprise me at all.”

“It’s going to be very long, and it’s going to be very, very bloody, and no one should have any illusions about any of that,” General Milley said in remarks to the National Press Club in Washington. “At the end of the day, Ukrainian soldiers are assaulting through minefields and in the trenches, and this is literally a fight for their life. So yes, sure, it goes a little slow, but that is part of the nature of war.”

General Milley confirmed published reports this week that the Biden administration is considering sending to Ukraine cluster munitions and a powerful weapon called the Army Tactical Missile System, or ATACMS, but he said that no decision had been reached yet.

The ATACMS can strike targets 190 miles away with a warhead containing about 375 pounds of explosives.

Asked whether last weekend’s aborted mutiny led by Yevgeny V. Prigozhin, head of the Wagner mercenary force, had weakened President Vladimir V. Putin of Russia, General Milley demurred.

“He’s a tough guy, a hard guy, and how he responds to what happened there over last weekend with Prigozhin,” General Milley said about Mr. Putin, “I don’t think we know yet.”

Satellite imagery reveals more than 300 new tents at a site that could be a base for Wagner in Belarus.

New satellite imagery analyzed by The New York Times shows that hundreds of large tents at an unused military base in Belarus have been erected in the past five days. The Times first reported Wednesday on rapid construction at the vacated base; the imagery captured Thursday and Friday reveals that a large military tent camp was built. The base could be a home for Wagner fighters who were given the option to relocate to Belarus after an aborted mutiny, and the images reveal that the buildup could fit the needs of thousands of fighters. But the imagery also shows little activity otherwise, suggesting that troops haven’t arrived.

The construction began within days of the aborted mutiny against President Vladimir V. Putin of Russia by Wagner fighters, and it is at a location that matches details given by President Aleksandr G. Lukashenko of Belarus about where mercenaries could be housed.

It appears that construction of the field camp, which is about 80 miles southeast from Minsk, the Belarusian capital, has been mostly completed on the site. A radar satellite image captured by Umbra Space on Thursday provided the first clear view of the site since Tuesday, because radar imagery is able to pierce clouds. There was little change during the 22 hours between that image and an image from Planet Labs captured Friday.

But there’s no evidence from the imagery or other reporting that any Wagner fighters had been moved to the camp as of Friday morning, and it remains unclear whether any fighters will move to Belarus. Unlike when it was an active base, no military vehicles are seen at the garages and buildings at the western side of the base, although sand had been cleared from a large, paved area there. The Belarusian Hajun project, which monitors military movements within the country, has not reported any recent large Russian troop movements this week, though it did say several Russian military vehicles were seen near Asipovichy, a town near the encampment, on Wednesday.

The Pentagon spokesman, Brig. Gen. Patrick S. Ryder, said on Thursday that he had no updates on the possible move of Wagner troops to Belarus. But, in response to a question about The Times report, he added, “Clearly, that’s something that we’ll continue to keep an eye on.”

A government real estate listing offering the buildings at the base up for free for business development that had been online since 2019 had been removed by Friday after The Times and others reported on it. However, the listing is still available in an online archive.

U.S. accuses Wagner boss of pushing U.N. peacekeeping mission out of Mali.

A senior White House official on Friday said that the founder of the Wagner mercenary company, Yevgeny V. Prigozhin, had engineered the departure of a United Nations peacekeeping force from Mali to help further his company’s interests there.

On Friday, the U.N. Security Council voted to end the decade-long peacekeeping mission in Mali, just weeks after Mali’s military junta asked the force to leave “without delay.” The peacekeepers have until Dec. 31 to leave Mali, which has opted to rely heavily on Wagner mercenaries to enforce security.

The official, John Kirby, the White House national security spokesman, said Mr. Prigozhin had a hand in the junta’s decision.

“We know that senior Malian officials worked directly with Prigozhin employees to inform the U.N. secretary general that Mali had revoked consent for the MINUSMA mission,” Mr. Kirby said, referring to the mission’s acronym for the United Nations Multidimensional Integrated Stabilization Mission in Mali, which was established in 2013.

The future of the Wagner network of businesses and paramilitary outfits in Africa after Mr. Prigozhin’s short-lived rebellion on Saturday remains up in the air. Russian officials have sought to reassure African governments that depend on the group for training and security that Wagner would continue its activities.

Mali, a landlocked nation of 30 million people fighting an Islamist insurgency, has been one of Wagner’s most important clients. For about $10 million a month, the group has deployed about 1,500 mercenaries who have fought alongside the country’s military against armed groups affiliated with Al Qaeda and the Islamic State.

The Wagner group has also sought to exploit gold mines in Mali, according to Western officials, with limited success. Still, Mr. Kirby said, the Malian authorities have paid $200 million to Wagner since late 2021.

Over 13,000 U.N. personnel have been deployed in the country as part of a decade-long mission meant to stabilize the country and protect civilians, particularly in disputed regions in the north. Despite mixed results, in many areas U.N. personnel protected local populations, provided job opportunities and helped connect hard-to-reach regions with flights and convoys.

“MINUSMA failed to deliver peace, but they still replaced an absent state,” said Ulf Laessing, the Bamako-based head of the Sahel program at the Konrad Adenauer Foundation.

Shortly after Wagner operatives arrived in Mali, the country’s military junta, curtailed the U.N. mission’s air and ground operations, limiting the mission’s efficacy.

“The mission really ended when Wagner arrived,” said one senior U.N. official who has worked in Mali, speaking on condition of anonymity because he was not authorized to talk about the mission’s withdrawal. “That’s when we lost control.”

Wagner mercenaries and the Malian military have so far struggled to reclaim control of areas targeted by various armed groups. Islamist insurgents have expanded into the country’s east and regularly attack civilians and security forces, including in central Mali, where most of the Wagner contingents have been deployed.

Civilian casualties in Mali quadrupled last year compared with 2021, a trend that observers have attributed to armed groups and the presence of Wagner fighters on the country, who have often targeted civilians in their operations.

In March last year, Wagner mercenaries and the Malian military killed more than 500 people during a five-day siege of a town in central Mali, according to the United Nations.

The Malian authorities have said that the town harbored terrorists, and accused the United Nations of fueling tensions with its assertions.

“Despite these hundreds of millions of dollars, Wagner has not improved Mali’s security situation,” Mr. Kirby said. “Wagner has instead brought more bloodshed, creating opportunities for terrorist exploitation.”

What Russians see: The ‘demilitarization’ of Ukraine during its counteroffensive.

We’re going to regularly break down how Russia is selling the war at home, as TV and other propaganda outlets create a distorted reality of what’s happening and who is responsible. Today, we look at how Russia describes Ukraine’s counteroffensive to retake occupied land.

In the version of the war in Ukraine that runs on Russian state media, the Ukrainian military is completely outmatched.

Splashy battlefield supercuts show victory after victory for Russian troops over Ukraine’s forces. Dead Ukrainian soldiers on the battlefield mix with explosions seen through blurry night vision. Pundits brag over footage of burning Western-made tanks.

That’s how Russian state media has portrayed the invasion since the beginning: a persistent string of victories, downplaying setbacks and losses. Now, state-run programs are not only claiming that Ukraine is making no ground, they were also asserting that Russia was achieving one of its original objectives of the invasion: the demilitarization of Ukraine.

These clips from state TV, taken before the mercenary march of Moscow and Vladimir V. Putin’s accusation of treason against an erstwhile ally, show how Russia’s state media argues that Ukraine is losing its ability to fight back — despite receiving dozens of tanks and armored vehicles, extensive training for its troops, and billions of dollars’ worth of aid from the United States and Europe.

And in the aftermath of the mercenary mutiny, as Mr. Putin sought to project an image of control again, he gave a speech that returned to these talking points, claiming that Ukraine had lost dozens of tanks and more than 100 armored vehicles.

Human Rights Watch accuses Ukraine of using prohibited land mines.

Ukrainian soldiers fired artillery rockets containing antipersonnel land mines into a Russian-controlled area of eastern Ukraine last year in apparent violation of international agreements banning the use of such weapons, a leading human rights group said in a report released Friday.

The group, Human Rights Watch, called the mines “inherently indiscriminate weapons” because of their inability to distinguish enemy soldiers from noncombatants. It urged the Ukrainian government to “act on its expressed commitment not to use banned antipersonnel land mines, investigate the military’s use of these weapons, and hold those responsible to account.”

Human Rights Watch has issued several reports about land mine use by Russian and Ukrainian forces since Moscow’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022. Russian troops have used at least 13 kinds of antipersonnel mines “in multiple areas across Ukraine” that have killed and injured civilians, according to the group.

The Ukrainian government promised to investigate the allegations against its soldiers during a June 21 meeting in Geneva of nations that have signed the treaty banning the use of the mines, the group said. Officials at Ukraine’s Defense Ministry in Kyiv and at its embassy in Washington did not immediately respond to a request for comment.

In 1997, more than 100 countries agreed not to use or maintain stockpiles of antipersonnel land mines, small explosive weapons that are typically buried just below the ground’s surface. When stepped on, they detonate with enough force to blow off a person’s leg. Injuries from mines of this kind can also be fatal.

Poland said on Friday that it had detained a professional athlete on charges of spying for the Russian government, in the 14th arrest stemming from the dismantling of what the authorities say was a Russian spy ring that aimed to sabotage arms shipments to Ukraine.

Polish prosecutors said in a statement that the athlete, a Russian citizen, was connected to a network that “conducted intelligence and propaganda activities against Poland and prepared acts of sabotage” for Russia. They did not name the suspect, though Polish news sources reported that he was a professional hockey player.

“Russian spies are falling one by one!” said Poland’s justice minister, Zbigniew Ziobro, in a Twitter post on Friday. “A spy who operated under the guise of a sportsman has been captured.”

Officials said the athlete was facing espionage charges punishable by up to 10 years in prison.

In March, Polish prosecutors detained nine foreigners who were accused of spying for Russia and plotting to sabotage Polish infrastructure used to transport Western arms into neighboring Ukraine.

Poland, a NATO member and one of Europe’s staunchest supporters of Ukraine, has been a vital transit point for weapons and ammunition provided by Western countries to help Ukraine defend itself against Russia’s invasion. The presence in Poland of a Russian spy ring trying to damage its infrastructure would signal a risky escalation by Moscow, which has so far avoided striking at targets inside alliance territory.

The Kremlin’s spokesman, Dmitri S. Peskov, on Friday suggested that Moscow’s embassy in Poland would “clear things up.”

“Of course the world of the embassy now is very hard,” he added, “because of the frenzied, Russophobic position.”

Prosecutors said that the suspect, who arrived in Poland in 2021, was paid to carry out “activities involving, among other, identifying critical infrastructure” in several provinces.

Polish news media reported that he played with the national hockey team, Zaglebie Sosnowiec.

The team’s coach, Grzegorz Klich, confirmed that the player was arrested earlier this month, but that he had signed to play with the team before Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in February 2022.

“We did not have any suspicions against him before that,” he was quoted as saying in an interview published Friday in Fakt, a Polish tabloid. “He behaved normally. That’s why we are surprised by this case.”

Polish intelligence officers said they detained the player in the province of Silesia, southern Poland, where he had carried out most of his activities.

Ukraine runs nuclear disaster drills amid warnings of an attack at the Zaporizhzhia plant.

KYIV, Ukraine — Ukraine held large-scale nuclear disaster drills this week as officials continued to warn that Russian forces were considering staging an attack at the occupied Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant.

Russian forces seized the plant in southern Ukraine, Europe’s largest, in the early days of the full-scale invasion last year. Shelling and fighting in the plant’s vicinity has repeatedly raised fears about a nuclear disaster, and the United Nations’ nuclear watchdog again sounded alarms last week over an “extremely fragile” security situation.

The Ukrainian and Russian militaries also have repeatedly traded allegations that the other side was preparing to attack the plant, which would risk a potentially catastrophic release of radiation.

Amid the uncertainty, Ukrainian officials have been working to prepare through simulations and drills.

“During the exercises, we tried to calculate all possible scenarios,” Ukraine’s energy minister, Herman Galushchenko, told reporters in the capital, Kyiv, on Friday.

About 8,000 people participated in “special exercises to practice an action plan” in the event of an attack at the facility, according to a statement from Ukraine’s Ministry of Reintegration, which said the drills were carried out in three regions of southern Ukraine and also involved hundreds of vehicles.

Central Ukraine’s small farming town of Zelenodolsk held a second day of emergency drills in case of an accident at the Zaporizhzhia plant, which lies about 60 kilometers, or 37 miles, away. They have involved practicing first aid, radiation screening, evacuations and other exercises to prepare for possibly tens of thousands of people being within a range of a nuclear accident.

Dmytro Neveselyi, the local mayor, said he had “no idea” what the Russians might do.

“Anything is possible,” he said. “There are many concerns among the people here — they are seeing warnings on media, social media, people are talking a lot about it.”

Warnings from top Ukrainian officials have become increasingly urgent. President Volodymyr Zelensky said last week that Ukrainian intelligence had information suggesting that Russia was considering carrying out a “terrorist” attack at the Zaporizhzhia facility. He did not provide evidence for the claim, which the Kremlin dismissed as a lie. The Biden administration said this week that it had not seen “any indication that that threat is imminent.”

On Friday, Ukraine’s Ministry of Defense, citing military intelligence, said that Russian forces were “gradually leaving the territory of the Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant” and decreasing their patrols of the facility. The ministry did not give details or explain how the intelligence had been gathered, and it was impossible to independently verify the claims. There has been increased military activity in the region surrounding the plant, as Ukraine wages a counteroffensive.

Although the warnings have alarmed some Ukrainians, the drills held in several parts of the country this week were aimed at showing preparedness and reducing panic.

On Thursday, hundreds of emergency workers wearing white protective suits and gas masks practiced responding to a radiation accident in the city of Zaporizhzhia, not far from the nuclear plant.

Ukraine’s State Emergency Service said more than 800 rescuers practiced deploying to treatment points, providing medical care and monitoring potentially contaminated areas.

“We work for safety,” the service said in a post on Facebook. Images of the drills were shared widely on social media across Ukraine.

Ukraine Diary: A visit to a village that looks serene, but only at first glance.

This is one in an occasional series of dispatches about life amid the war in Ukraine.

PREOBRAZHENKA, Ukraine — This small village in southeastern Ukraine looks serene at first glance, a typical Ukrainian village with abundant fields and lovingly tended yards. But it has not been spared by the war.

“In the night it’s silent, so we hear distant sounds of shelling,” said one resident, Tamara, 59, who asked to be identified only by her first name to avoid unwanted attention. “During the day, we’re planting as many vegetables as possible — nobody knows what winter will bring.”

When Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine started in February of last year, she and the three granddaughters she is raising moved to her home’s cellar, because it was “loud and scary outside.” But within days, they realized that it was impossible to live there in the dank cold.

“Many villagers left when it all started, but eventually most of them returned,” Tamara said on a recent afternoon. “Here we have a house, garden, and our own vegetables, but what will you do far from here without money and home? So we stayed.”

Days later, shelling left three people in Preobrazhenka with critical wounds, according to the local authorities.

But it was quiet as Tamara spoke. Her granddaughters were helping in the garden and playing with their little dog, Javeline. They were well aware that the village had already had two funerals for soldiers killed fighting the invaders, and a third was coming. “We’re not sure if we’ll go to the funeral tomorrow, but you’ll know where it will be, everybody will be there,” said the youngest, Yana, 9.

Another villager who asked to be identified only by his first name, Yurii, 69, was joking and laughing until he started to talk about his family. One of his sons is on the front line.

On the day of the third funeral, the village was crowded from the early morning on. People lined up along the main street, holding flowers and flags, waiting for the funeral procession so they could say farewell to Ruslan Serenkov, 37, a machine gunner who died on June 5 during a combat mission near Bakhmut.

His widow, Nadiia Serenkova, 34, is now faced with raising their two children, Sophia, 8, and Illia, 12.

“I can’t talk about him now,” she said of her husband. “I just can’t imagine my life without him.”

Misfortune was no stranger to the Serenkov family. His mother, Asiia, 81, is from Kazakhstan, and his father, Petro, 72, is from Belarus. After the Chernobyl nuclear disaster in 1986 sent radioactivity into Belarus, they fled their home there in the city of Homel, starting a new life in Preobrazhenka.

Asiia Serenkov said that her son liked the army. Shortly before his death, she said, he told her: “Mom, you can’t imagine how many good people are there. I should have gone to the army much earlier.”

In a Ukrainian field hospital, relentless casualties and a Russian P.O.W.

For more than a year, civilian doctors and nurses in Ukraine have been swapping their white coats for military fatigues, putting their lives on the line to treat an endless stream of casualties.

New York Times journalists spent a week inside a military field hospital in the Donetsk region of eastern Ukraine, filming a team of combat medics and doctors as they raced to save the lives of wounded soldiers.

“We’re working on two front lines,” said Oleksiy Nazarishin, a Ukrainian surgeon and the hospital’s chief medical officer. “The war against the enemy and the war for the patient’s life.”

About 13,000 soldiers have been treated at this hospital alone since it opened in October. While the Ukrainian military has not disclosed how many of its soldiers have been killed or wounded since the Russian invasion began, the relentless flow of patients reflects the staggering cost of 16 months of war on Ukrainian troops.

The severity and frequency of the casualties are the result of a long, protracted battle with Russian forces in the east, which has become mired in a bloody stalemate with no end in sight.

“I thought you could only see things like this in films about World War II,” said Anatoliy, a wounded Ukrainian soldier who spoke to us from his hospital bed. Under Ukrainian military rules, he is being identified only by his first name.

For the medics and physicians, the days are a grueling cycle of trauma, death and exhaustion. When an injured Russian soldier arrives at the field hospital after being captured on the battlefield, they must set aside their anger and uphold their medical oath to treat him like any other patient.
