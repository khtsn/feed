Trump’s Claim of Immunity Reaches Appeals Court

Such an assertion has never been fully tested by the courts, but then winning the appeal is only one of Mr. Trump’s goals. He is also hoping that the debate about immunity can chew up enough time to postpone the election trial — now set to start in early March — until after Election Day.

Already, Mr. Trump’s immunity defense, which was first raised in October, has bounced around various federal courts for more than three months, forcing the trial judge, Tanya S. Chutkan, to put the underlying case on hold until the issue is resolved. The Supreme Court declined a request last month to jump ahead of the appeals court and consider the question itself on an expedited schedule, although the appellate judges fast-tracked the case when it returned to them, ordering the defense and prosecution to file their papers on two successive Saturdays just before the holidays. The appeals court is also expected to issue a decision fairly quickly.

The question of immunity may end up back at the Supreme Court, but even if the justices eventually reject Mr. Trump’s arguments, the time consumed by the litigation could help the former president pursue his strategy of running down the clock. If that happens and Mr. Trump wins the election, he could seek to order the charges against him to be dropped or try to pardon himself.

Because no former president has ever been prosecuted before, there are few definitive precedents to guide the appellate judges in deciding the question of immunity. While the Justice Department has long maintained a policy that sitting presidents cannot be indicted, Mr. Trump’s bid to claim total immunity from prosecution is a remarkable attempt to claim the protections of the presidency even though he is no longer in office.

Prosecutors working for the special counsel, Jack Smith, who filed the case against Mr. Trump, are expected to argue that nothing in the Constitution or American history supports the idea that former presidents are above being subject to federal criminal law.
