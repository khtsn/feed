More Than 180 Academic Leaders Condemn Trump’s ‘Overreach’

The statement came a day after Harvard University sued the administration over its decision to freeze billions of dollars in federal funding, following the schoo’s refusal to submit to a list of demands.

  • April 22, 2025, 11:54 a.m. ET

A day after Harvard sued the Trump administration over its decision to freeze billions in federal funds to the school, more than 180 higher education leaders from around the country released a joint statement on Tuesday condemning the administration’s efforts to control universities.

The government’s “political interference” and “overreach” is “now endangering higher education in America,” they wrote.

The signers come from a variety of colleges and universities from across the country, as well as higher education associations, illustrating the breadth of the threat they say President Trump poses to academia. Joining in the statement were officials from large public research universities like the University of Virginia and the University of Wisconsin-Madison and smaller private colleges such as Amherst and Kenyon.

The statement, circulated by the American Association of Colleges and Universities and signed by a total of 187 people as of Tuesday morning, focuses on concerns that the Trump administration is attacking academic freedom.

“We must oppose undue government intrusion into the lives of those who learn, live and work on our campuses,” the statement said.

Many of the presidents who signed, including Alan M. Garber of Harvard, also face financial risks as a result of the administration’s deep cuts to research contracts and grants. Dr. Garber on Monday said his school had chosen to sue the administration after it issued a list of demands that included auditing its professors for plagiarism and appointing an outside overseer to ensure its departments were “viewpoint diverse.”

Harvard refused to comply with the demands, and the administration said it would freeze $2.2 billion in federal money.

Stephanie Saul reports on colleges and universities, with a recent focus on the dramatic changes in college admissions and the debate around diversity, equity and inclusion in higher education.

More on Higher Education in America

  • Harvard Under Fire: President Trump threatened Harvard University’s tax-exempt status after the school rebuffed the administration’s demands ostensibly aimed at purging “woke” ideology. Federal officials also said they would freeze $2.2 billion in multiyear grants to the university.

  • An Escalating Feud: The Department of Homeland Security also threatened to block Harvard University from enrolling international students unless the school handed over detailed information about the student body.

  • Columbia Student Arrested: An organizer of pro-Palestinian demonstrations at Columbia University was detained by immigration officials after arriving for an appointment in Vermont that he thought was a step toward becoming a U.S. citizen.

  • Cuts to Cornell and Northwestern: The Trump administration froze more than $1 billion for Cornell and $790 million for Northwestern amid investigations into accusations of discrimination stemming from efforts to promote diversity.

  • Students for Justice in Palestine: A Senate committee asked Columbia to provide extensive information about the group, which had been at the center of campus protests over Israel and Gaza.

  • Suing Over School Meals: The State of Maine sued the Agriculture Department, arguing that the agency violated the law when it froze funding to Maine because it threatened school meal programs.

  • D.E.I. in Public Schools: The New York State Education Department issued a defiant response to threats to pull federal funding from public schools over certain diversity, equity and inclusion programs, a departure from the conciliatory approach of other institutions.
