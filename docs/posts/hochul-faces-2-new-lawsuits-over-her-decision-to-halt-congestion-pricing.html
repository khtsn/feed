Hochul Faces 2 New Lawsuits Over Her Decision to Halt Congestion Pricing

New York|Hochul Faces 2 New Lawsuits Over Her Decision to Halt Congestion Pricing

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/07/25/nyregion/kathy-hochul-congestion-pricing-lawsuit.html

The lawsuits are among the first legal efforts aiming to force Gov. Kathy Hochul to move forward with the tolling program as planned.

July 25, 2024, 5:26 p.m. ET

A coalition of supporters of congestion pricing filed two lawsuits on Thursday that demand that Gov. Kathy Hochul allow the tolling program to move forward, arguing that she exceeded her authority when she indefinitely suspended the start of congestion pricing last month.

The first lawsuit was filed by the City Club of New York, a civic organization founded more than a century ago. It argues that Governor Hochul violated the law, approved by the State Legislature in 2019, that called for the creation of the tolling program, which would have charged most drivers $15 to enter Manhattan below 60th Street in an effort to reduce traffic and pollution.

The second lawsuit was filed by Riders Alliance, a grass-roots organization of transit riders, and two environmental groups. It contends that Ms. Hochul’s move undercut the state’s efforts to cut down on greenhouse gas emissions, which contribute to climate change.

“Governor Hochul may dislike congestion pricing; she may fail to grasp the threats of climate change or our need for a reliable, accessible transit system,” Betsy Plum, the executive director of the Riders Alliance, said at a news conference on Thursday morning.But congestion pricing is still the law, she said.

The lawsuits, filed in New York State Supreme Court, were produced by a coalition of advocacy organizations and other supporters of congestion pricing that was convened by Brad Lander, the New York City comptroller. The group has not ruled out additional lawsuits over the halting of congestion pricing, Mr. Lander said at the news conference on Thursday. But his office does not plan to be a plaintiff in any upcoming lawsuits, he added.

In a statement on Thursday, Ms. Hochul’s office noted that there were numerous pending lawsuits related to congestion pricing, and said that they had been filed by groups “trying to weaponize the judicial system to score political points.”

The lawsuits announced on Thursday, however, are among the first filed in response to the halting of the program. Ms. Hochul abruptly shelved the long-awaited tolling plan in June, just weeks before it was to go into effect. She said that she had called for an indefinite pause to the program because she feared that the new tolls could keep commuters and visitors out of Manhattan, which would hurt the city’s economic recovery from the coronavirus pandemic.

The 11th-hour decision shocked and angered environmentalists, transit advocates and economists. The M.T.A. had planned to use the money raised from the tolls to secure $15 billion in bond financing to pay for much-needed improvements to the transit system’s aging infrastructure.

Congestion pricing, which has been successfully implemented in places like London, Stockholm and Singapore, was passed in Albany in 2019 as part of the Traffic Mobility Act.

“Our lawsuit is very simple,” said Andrew G. Celli Jr., a lawyer who is representing the City Club in its suit. “It asks the court to order Governor Hochul and her transportation commissioner to abide by the law or get out of the way.” Mr. Celli said he expected to be in front of a judge arguing the case by September.

The lawsuit filed by Riders Alliance argues that in addition to violating the Traffic Mobility Act, the governor’s decision to halt congestion pricing runs counter to the Climate Leadership and Community Protection Act, which requires the state to meet aggressive emissions reduction targets. The measure, also passed in 2019, calls on the state to reduce its greenhouse gas emissions 85 percent by 2050.

The lawsuits come just a month after a federal judge dismissed a key argument behind a set of lawsuits against the congestion pricing plan, which gave supporters hope that the plan could one day be implemented despite the governor’s indefinite pause.
