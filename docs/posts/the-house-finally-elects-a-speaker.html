The House Finally Elects a Speaker

After three weeks of legislative paralysis and intraparty fighting, the narrow House Republican majority finally united this afternoon around a new leader. Representative Mike Johnson of Louisiana, with all 220 Republicans in favor and all 209 Democrats in opposition, was elected as the 56th speaker of the House of Representatives.

Johnson, 51, is an unusual pick: He is little-known and less experienced than all his recent predecessors. His elevation now places a socially conservative lawyer who opposes abortion rights and same-sex marriage, and who played a leading role in the efforts to overturn the 2020 election, second in line to the presidency.

Yet unlike the three nominees before him, Johnson was able to secure the support of both hard-right and mainstream Republicans. Many of them, worn down by a brutal stretch of leaderless infighting, jumped to their feet to celebrate his election. Democrats were sharply critical, with one leader saying that the speaker race had devolved into a contest over “who can appease Donald Trump.”

Analysis: Johnson, who wrote a brief offering a legal justification for trying to overturn the 2020 election, is fundamentally more right-wing than the ousted speaker, Kevin McCarthy. His podcast, “Truth Be Told,” offers an extended window into his politics.

Gaza is running out of fuel

Fuel shortages in Gaza have grown so dire in the weeks since Israel declared war on Hamas that U.N. aid officials there have said they might have to close some of their operations.

The U.N. agency in Gaza has said it needs fuel for its vehicles to deliver aid to shelters and health clinics. The World Health Organization said today that 12 of Gaza’s 35 hospitals were not functioning. But Israel has insisted that no fuel be allowed in on aid trucks, arguing it could be used for military purposes by Hamas, the Palestinian militant group that controls the territory.

President Biden called for more aid for Gaza, adding that U.S. officials were working to make that happen.

Hurricane Otis caught Mexico, and forecasters, off guard

Early this morning, Hurricane Otis made landfall near the resort city of Acapulco on the southern Pacific Coast of Mexico with Category 5 wind speeds, potentially as high as 165 miles per hour. The storm cut off communications, caused landslides and threatened thousands of residents with heavy winds and flooding.

Biden is hosting Australia’s leader at the White House

President Biden met in the Oval Office this afternoon with Prime Minister Anthony Albanese of Australia, whom U.S. officials see as key to countering China. The Biden administration is relying on Congress to pass legislation that would allow for the shipment of nuclear-powered submarines to Australia, but the wars in Ukraine and Israel took center stage at the meeting. Both Biden and Albanese said that they would continue to stand with Israel.

Later tonight, the White House will host Albanese for a lavish state dinner which includes root vegetables, short ribs and ice cream.

If you plant it, they will come

The common milkweed is a beautiful plant, with seas of nodding pink flower heads popping out left and right, but herbicides have largely relegated it to ditches and abandoned lots. Ecologists are hoping people will give milkweed another shot.

Those who do will quickly learn that a diverse, living fan base will follow close behind. That includes monarch butterflies, but also slugs, snails, rabbits and squirrels.

Chickens are probably smarter than you think

For decades, scientists have used mirrors to test animals for self-awareness. Chimps have passed the test because they can recognize their reflections — as can dolphins and elephants — but most other animals don’t.

Today, a group of researchers published a study showing that chickens could pass a similar mirror test, suggesting that they, too, are self-aware. They came to that conclusion after discovering that roosters would cry out with warning at an image of a predator when another rooster was visible, but not in the presence of a mirror — which they appeared to know was their own reflection.
