Flooding Latest News and Video: More Rain Expected Across Northeast

At least one person died as heavy rain inundated New York’s Hudson Valley and the surrounding areas. More flash flooding is expected in New York and New England.

July 10, 2023Updated 10:08 a.m. ET

After a night described as “complete chaos” that washed away roads and bridges, continued rain and widespread flash flooding are expected in New York and New England on Monday, a day after torrential rainfall flooded homes, stranded vehicles, and caused other damage in the Hudson Valley.

At least one person, a woman in her 30s, died in the flooding, the authorities said. Steven M. Neuhaus, the county executive in Orange County, N.Y., said on Monday that the victim had been trying to evacuate from her home while carrying a pet when she lost her footing and was swept into a ravine.

“Last night was complete chaos,” Mr. Neuhaus said in an interview with ABC’s “Good Morning America.”

While the water has receded in some places, the damage to roads and bridges has made it difficult for search and rescue teams to fan out and make sure residents are accounted for, he said.

“There’s some people that could have been swept away,” he said.

In Highland Falls, a riverside town about 50 miles north of New York City, a giant puddle was still sitting in the middle of Main Street on Monday morning as workers cleared debris and Gov. Kathy Hochul prepared to give a briefing. Roads in the area remained closed as crew members inspected bridges and worked to clear football-sized rocks and other slurry.

In nearby West Point, a military golf course was littered with branches and a wrecked cart paths, as motorists faced an arduous Monday commute.

Todd Steele, 50, a resident of Highland Falls, was examining the damage to a local bridge, where bits and pieces of broken asphalt and shattered sheds were blocking traffic. He said the village had seen flooding before, including during 2012’s Hurricane Sandy, but that Sunday’s deluge was worse.

“It just came up so fast,” he said, of the brook running down the local hills toward the Hudson. “I’ve never seen anything like that.”

More than 8 inches of rain has fallen at West Point since the rain began Sunday, with more than 7 inches falling in only a 4-hour period yesterday afternoon, according to preliminary weather data recorded at the United State Military Academy.

More rain is expected in the Northeast on Monday. A high probability of excessive rainfall is likely for the area across the Champlain Valley and Northern Vermont, where the most prolonged rainfall is likely.

The downpour was generating flash flooding in five counties across Northern Vermont, where up to 3 inches of rain have fallen so far, the National Weather Service office in Burlington said early on Monday. Up to an inch of rain was forecast to fall every hour.

In Central Vermont, Addison, Orange, Rutland and Windsor counties are at risk of a flash flood through the early afternoon, the Weather Service said.

“Expect significant to potentially catastrophic widespread flash flooding through Monday evening, with possible impacts not seen since Irene,” forecasters wrote Monday morning.

Flooding and relentless rainfall inundated Vermont and other states in 2011, as Hurricane Irene surged up the East Coast, washing away infrastructure and homes.

Forecasters with the Weather Prediction Center said that many areas in central and northern New England have seen 200 to 300 percent of their normal rainfall over the past 14 days. Streams are already running abnormally fast, with some at all-time record flows.

The Hudson Valley bore the brunt of the storm on Sunday, with as much as eight inches of rain recorded in some areas, according to the National Weather Service.

Parts of the heavily traveled Palisades Interstate Parkway were impassable, and several bridges collapsed, according to Trooper Steven V. Nevel of the New York State Police.

Transportation difficulties were continuing Monday through the region. As of Monday morning, dozens of flights had been canceled out of LaGuardia and John F. Kennedy airports in New York, according to FlightAware, a flight tracking service. More than 30 flights were also canceled out of Boston Logan International Airport.

Amtrak services were suspended between New York City and Albany on Monday. A New York City-bound Amtrak train had been halted as it approached Poughkeepsie on Sunday evening, with an Amtrak employee announcing that there had been a “complete washout of both tracks” south of the city, preventing any travel by train.

And with trees and other debris still covering the tracks, Metro-North on Monday suspended part of its Hudson Line between Croton-Harmon and Poughkeepsie.

Governor Hochul announced a state of emergency on Sunday, and expanded it later to include more areas of the state. “If you’re in an area impacted by tonight’s storms, please stay off the roads and take steps to stay safe,” she said on Twitter.

Claire Moses contributed reporting.
