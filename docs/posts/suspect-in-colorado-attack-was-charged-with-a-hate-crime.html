Suspect in Colorado Attack Was Charged With a Hate Crime

Briefing|Suspect in Colorado Attack Was Charged With a Hate Crime

https://www.nytimes.com/2025/06/02/briefing/suspect-in-colorado-attack-was-charged-with-a-hate-crime.html

The man who was arrested in Boulder, Colo., for throwing Molotov cocktails at people attending a march in support of Israeli hostages in Gaza was charged today with a federal hate crime.

Authorities said the man yelled “Free Palestine” during the attack. Twelve people were injured, including eight who were hospitalized with burns, officials said. Witnesses described “horrible” scenes.

The F.B.I. said the man, Mohamed Sabry Soliman, told investigators that he had been planning the attack for a year and that he wanted to “kill all Zionist people and wished they were all dead.” Police said he had brought more than a dozen incendiary devices to the event and disguised himself as a gardener in order to get as close as possible to the marchers.

Soliman, who is 45, entered the U.S. from Egypt in 2022 and stayed illegally after his visa expired in 2023. He told a detective that he used Molotov cocktails in the attack because he had not been able to buy a gun.

Ukraine peace talks yielded little after intense attacks

Negotiations in Istanbul today between Russian and Ukrainian officials produced little progress beyond an agreement to exchange prisoners and the bodies of fallen soldiers. It was the second round of peace talks since the two sides resumed direct dialogue two weeks ago.

As discussions have sputtered, attacks on the battlefield have intensified. Yesterday, Russia bombarded Ukraine with some of the biggest drone and missile attacks of the war, including a barrage of 500 drones and decoys.

The same day, Ukraine launched an ambitious, coordinated attack that damaged or destroyed long-range bombers located deep within Russia. Ukrainian officials said that the drones used in the attack were hidden in transport containers and launched from semi trucks.

In Britain, Prime Minister Keir Starmer vowed to bring his country to “war-fighting readiness,” citing “growing Russian aggression.”

South Korea is voting for a new leader

It is already Tuesday in South Korea, and voters there are heading to the polls to elect a new president. Surveys suggest that Lee Jae-myung, the leader of the centrist Democratic Party, is the favorite to win. He is facing Kim Moon-soo, a conservative from the governing People Power Party.

The election is seen as a referendum on former President Yoon Suk Yeol, who sent the country into political turmoil when he declared martial law in December. Whichever candidate wins will face a country in crisis. “South Korea is deeply divided between generations, and between young men and women,” our Seoul bureau chief Choe Sang-hun said. “The economic growth rate has sputtered to a snail’s pace. The income gap is larger than ever.”

Coffee was linked to healthy aging

If you're at all like me, you probably don’t hesitate to reach for a third, fourth or fifth cup of coffee on any given day. According to a new study, that might be a good thing.

Researchers followed a large number of women over several decades and found that those who drank the most caffeine (up to seven cups of coffee a day) in midlife were 13 percent more likely to age healthfully than those who consumed the least.

A historic move in the fashion world

LVMH announced today that Jonathan Anderson — who transformed Loewe from a niche house into a fashion powerhouse — would become the creative director of women’s wear, men’s wear and couture at Dior. That makes him the first designer to unite all sides of the brand since Christian Dior himself.

Anderson’s appointment signifies the end of one of the most drawn-out succession dramas in modern fashion history. And, as our critic Vanessa Friedman explained, it could be a broader paradigm shift. Taking on both the men’s and women’s lines — 10 collections a year for a $9 billion brand — is an immense burden, and Anderson will be expected to help revive the fashion world from its sluggish state.

Shirley Manson has become the godmother of rock

As the frontwoman of Garbage, Shirley Manson has continued to carry the torch for the alternative, female-led rock music of the 1990s. The band is out with a new album, its first in four years.

Listen up, space aliens

If there is extraterrestrial life out there, it might eventually hear Johann Strauss’s “Blue Danube” waltz. The Vienna Symphony Orchestra made sure of that on Saturday when it performed the piece and transmitted it into space via an electromagnetic wave at the speed of light.

Vienna’s tourist board organized the event to right what it described as a historical wrong. It believes that “Blue Danube,” Strauss’s most famous work, deserved to be included on the Golden Record, a phonograph record that NASA launched into space in 1977, one copy aboard Voyager 1 and another on Voyager 2.
