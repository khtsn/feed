Senate Passes Child Online Safety Bill, Sending It to an Uncertain House Fate

The legislation would impose stricter privacy rules and safeguards for children on the internet and social media, but concerns about free speech and fierce industry lobbying pose challenges.

Maya C. Miller

Reporting from Capitol Hill

July 30, 2024Updated 3:11 p.m. ET

The Senate on Tuesday passed bipartisan legislation to impose sweeping safety and privacy requirements for children and teens on social media and other technology platforms, voting overwhelmingly to send the measure to the House, where its fate was uncertain.

Passage of the measure, which has been the subject of a dogged advocacy campaign by parents who say their children lost their lives because of something they found or saw on social media, marked a rare bipartisan achievement at a time of deep polarization in Congress.

Despite the lopsided support among Republicans and Democrats, the package faces a fierce lobbying effort by technology companies that are resisting new regulation, and deep skepticism among free speech advocates who argue that it would chill individual expression and potentially harm some of those whom the bill aims to protect.

The vote was 91 to 3 to approve the measure, sending it to the House, which is in a summer recess until September. The legislation is the product of years of work by lawmakers and parents to overhaul digital privacy and safety laws as social networking sites, digital gaming and other online platforms increasingly dominate children’s and teens’ lives.

“I used to run marathons, and I feel like I used to feel halfway through: ‘Wow, I got this far, and I’m feeling good,’” Senator Richard Blumenthal, Democrat of Connecticut and one of the chief sponsors of the legislation, said in an interview on Tuesday. “The second half can be the toughest in some ways, but also the easiest psychologically because you see the end in sight.”

The centerpiece of the legislation would create a “duty of care” for social networking platforms that mandates they protect minors against mental health disorders and from abuse, sexual exploitation and other harms. Companies could be held liable for failing to filter out content or limit features that could lead to those adverse impacts.

Led by Mr. Blumenthal and Senator Marsha Blackburn, Republican of Tennessee, the legislation also would require technology service providers to turn on the highest privacy and safety settings by default for users under 17 and to allow youths to opt out of some features that can lead to compulsive use, such as auto-playing of videos.

A second measure included in the package would strengthen privacy protections for anyone under 17 and ban targeted advertising to children and teens. It would create an “eraser button” for parents and children, requiring companies to permit users to delete personal information.

The uncommonly bipartisan legislative push reflects widespread concern about the omnipresence of social media in children’s lives and the potential risks associated with it. The surgeon general, Vivek Murthy, recently called for a warning label on social media platforms — similar to the one required on tobacco products outlining potentially deadly health risks — informing parents that their use could damage adolescents’ mental health.

Senator Chuck Schumer, Democrat of New York and the majority leader, told reporters he tried every avenue possible to advance the bill swiftly out of the Senate. But when those efforts were blocked by a few senators who opposed the measure, Mr. Schumer kept a promise he made to the parents and put the bill on the floor for a vote. He profusely thanked the parents for their relentless advocacy and called on the House to take swift action on the bills when legislators return from the summer recess.

“Now that the Senate has done its job, the House should do the same,” Mr. Schumer said at a news conference as the vote wrapped up. “The bipartisan momentum behind these bills is real, and we should seize this opportunity to make them into law.”

Mr. Blumenthal acknowledged that he and Ms. Blackburn had “diametrically opposed” voting records on most issues — he is the third-most liberal senator, and she the second-most conservative, according to the nonpartisan service GovTrack. But they united around the goal of making the internet safer for children. And they were particularly motivated by the parents who told gut-wrenching stories of losing their children, some to suicide, and young people who described the mental distress that social media imposes on them.

As the vote unfolded on Tuesday, some of those parents sat watching from the gallery above, holding hands and wiping away tears. One of them was Julianna Arnold of New York, who lost her 17-year-old daughter Lucienne Konar, known as Coco, to fentanyl poisoning after an Instagram drug dealer sold her pills laced with the deadly drug.

“It makes you feel like you have a purpose after all of this horror,” Ms. Arnold said in an interview. “It feels good to be doing something good and taking something so dark and bringing light to it.”

Also in the gallery was Joann Bogard, who said she watched her 15-year-old son Mason Bogard take his final breaths on life support after he accidentally strangled himself as part of the viral “choking challenge” on social media. Every week for the past five years, Ms. Bogard has searched for choking challenge videos on platforms like TikTok and Instagram and reported as many as she could to content moderators. Rarely do the companies remove the videos, she said.

“These platforms have gone too long without having any responsibility” to protect children online, Ms. Bogard said. “They have to mitigate those harms.”

The bill faces strong pushback from technology companies, who argue it would place unacceptable burdens on them to moderate content and verify users’ ages, and groups like the American Civil Liberties Union that contend it would restrict free speech. In the Senate, the measure generated narrow but intense opposition despite several rounds of redrafting to address such concerns.

Senator Rand Paul, Republican of Kentucky, recently called the bill “the Pandora’s box of unintended consequences,” arguing it would stifle First Amendment-protected speech. Other critics, such as Senator Ron Wyden, Democrat of Oregon, said they were worried that limiting children’s access to certain content on social media could further isolate vulnerable youths, including L.G.B.T.Q. young people who often rely on online communities for a sense of belonging and acceptance.

Mr. Wyden was the only Democrat to vote against the measure on Tuesday.

“I think the bill has been improved. There’s no question about that,” Mr. Wyden said on Monday night before the vote. “But not enough.”

Lawmakers in the Republican-led House have proposed a far narrower online safety measure that would impose strict safeguard requirements only on platforms that generate more than $2.5 billion in annual revenue, or video games that have more than 150 million global users in a three-month period.

In a floor speech before the vote, Mr. Blumenthal said he and Ms. Blackburn have met with House leaders “several times” and believe there is “broad support” for the bill among representatives, giving it a “clear path forward” to passage.

But while Speaker Mike Johnson, Republican of Louisiana, said last week that he was “committed to working to find consensus in the House,” he did not commit to bringing the Senate legislation to the floor.

“Parents should have greater control and the necessary tools to protect their kids online,” Mr. Johnson said in a statement. “I am looking forward to reviewing the details of the legislation that comes out of the Senate.”

Parent advocates for the measure are now turning their lobbying attention to the House, where proponents in the Senate say they are the most effective advocates for it.

“I am in awe of their courage and strength, their grit and grace, which has been so powerful,” Mr. Blumenthal said. “I’ll never forget the first time I sat with them and we went around the room, and I felt as though time stopped. Because every one of them had a story of loss and grief that they were reliving.”

Ms. Arnold described her daughter Coco as “a fighter for the underdog” and someone who wanted to make the world better. She said her daughter would be proud of the work that she and the other parents have done to try to keep other children and teens safe online.

“I think she would have been like: ‘Mom, good job. Thank you. Thank you, you’re doing the right thing. I’m here with you, and we’re going to fight this, and we’re going to make a difference,’” Ms. Arnold said.

Parents say leaders of the House Committee on Energy and Commerce have promised to quickly take up the bills once they return to Washington in September. Until then, they plan to knock on as many doors and make as many calls as possible to spread the word.

“You know what this bill is doing?” Ms. Bogard said. “It’s honoring Mason, and every child who has passed or is suffering from an online harm. I truly believe that our kids are smiling — and that just makes me feel a little at peace.”
