Edward Caban Will Become First Latino Commissioner of New York Police

Mayor Eric Adams will name Mr. Caban as the 46th commissioner of the New York Police Department. He will follow Keechant Sewell, who resigned after 18 months.

Maria Cramer, Hurubie Meko and William K. Rashbaum

July 17, 2023, 9:33 a.m. ET

Edward Caban, who grew up in the Bronx as the son of a Puerto Rican transit police detective, will become the first Latino officer to lead the New York Police Department in its 177-year history.

Mayor Eric Adams will hold a 10 a.m. news conference on Monday at a precinct in the Bronx to name Mr. Caban, who had been serving as acting commissioner, according to three people with direct knowledge of the decision.

The move comes just over a month after Commissioner Keechant Sewell, the first woman to serve in the role, resigned after only 18 months, frustrated in her attempts to act with autonomy.

Mr. Caban, who previously served as first deputy commissioner, had remained close to the mayor through Ms. Sewell’s tenure. He will oversee roughly 36,000 officers and 19,000 civilian employees.

A father of two young children, Mr. Caban began his career in 1991 in the Bronx.

When Mr. Adams pushed for Mr. Caban to become deputy commissioner, he bypassed the department’s ranks of chiefs to make the promotion.

Mr. Caban, along with Chief of Department Jeffrey Maddrey, another close associate of Mr. Adams, frequently called him directly about department business, circumventing Ms. Sewell, according to several officials with knowledge of the relationships.

Ms. Sewell had taken over the nation’s largest police department at a critical juncture. Police morale was especially low and public faith in the department shaky in the wake of protests decrying police brutality.

The rates of murders and shootings were higher than before the pandemic. The relationship between the department and the city’s police unions was strained over unresolved contract negotiations and tensions over how to resolve calls for changes in tactics and disciplinary procedures.

During Ms. Sewell’s tenure, the rate of shootings and homicides fell and the city and the Police Benevolent Association negotiated a new contract.

Publicly, Mr. Caban gave the impression he had a warm relationship with the commissioner. On June 16, three days after Ms. Sewell announced that she was leaving, he posted a photo of the two of them smiling outside Gracie Mansion, where they had gone for a Juneteenth celebration.

In an interview with News 12 in October, he showed off a framed photo of him standing next to Ms. Sewell on the day of his promotion to first deputy commissioner.

“It signifies pride not only for myself but standing next to the first African American female who shattered the glass ceiling — 177 years,” Mr. Caban said. “Those two next to each other, that’s pride to me.”
