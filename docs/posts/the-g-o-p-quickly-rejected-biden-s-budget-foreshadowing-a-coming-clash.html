March 9, 2023, 5:14 p.m. ET

President Biden released a $6.8 trillion budget proposal on Thursday that would reduce the deficit, raise taxes on the rich, bolster military spending and ramp up competition with China.

The plan is widely considered to be dead on arrival with Republicans, who control the House. But it serves as an opening bid as lawmakers in Congress grapple over raising the debt limit as well as a policy blueprint for Mr. Biden’s widely anticipated re-election campaign.

In a speech in Philadelphia on Thursday, Mr. Biden said that his budget was designed to “lift the burden on hard working Americans” and drew sharp contrasts with the proposals that Republicans have offered, which the president argued would threaten the nation’s social safety net programs and benefit the rich.

The budget pitches a return to a progressive tax policy.

During his first two years in office, many of Mr. Biden’s most progressive campaign proposals were discarded from the legislation that he signed. But in his 2024 budget, the president suggested he would campaign again on those policies.

The budget calls for a new 25 percent minimum tax on billionaires, an increase in the corporate tax rate to 28 percent from 21 percent and quadrupling a tax on stock buybacks to 4 percent from 1 percent. It would also eliminate the tax treatment of “carried interest” that allows private equity and hedge fund executives to pay lower tax rates than many of their salaried employees and would block billionaires from using retirement savings accounts as tax shelters.

In all, the budget would usher in around $5 trillion of tax increases over the next decade, with most of those increases aimed at the wealthiest Americans and large corporations.

Democrats want to reduce the deficit too.

As lawmakers fight over raising the debt limit, Republicans have insisted they will allow the government to borrow more money to pay its bills only if there is an accompanying plan to reduce the federal deficit. Their proposals tend to hinge on deep spending cuts, but Mr. Biden’s plan makes clear that he and the Democrats want to use tax increases, enforcement of the tax code and the closing of tax loopholes to reduce America’s reliance on borrowed money.

Beyond tax increases, Mr. Biden is also proposing new savings for the government stemming from more aggressive negotiation over prescription drug prices. The White House estimates that those changes and other tweaks to the drug negotiation provision would save the government an additional $200 billion over 10 years, which it seeks to direct to the Medicare trust fund.

The plan would reduce federal budget deficits by nearly $3 trillion over the next 10 years, according to White House estimates.

Biden leans into military spending, with a focus on countering Russia and China.

The White House’s plans for reducing the deficit have drawn scoffs from Republicans, but Mr. Biden’s budget does include some spending plans that they might find palatable.

The budget goes big on military spending, requesting $842 billion for the Defense Department, a 3.2 percent increase from 2023. The funds would be used to modernize America’s nuclear deterrent capabilities and would allocate more than $6 billion to support Ukraine. They would also allocate $753 million to counter “Russian malign influence” and related needs like cybersecurity.

Mr. Biden also takes a hard line when it comes to China, which is one of the few subjects that tends to garner bipartisan agreement in Washington.

The budget says explicitly that the Biden administration wants to invest in new says to “out-compete” China. It proposes $9.1 billion in investments next year through the Pentagon’s “Pacific Deterrence Initiative” which includes expenditures on new weapons systems that can be used to protect allies and defend U.S. interests in the region.

Republicans find little to love in the president’s plan.

Moments after Mr. Biden released his budget, Republicans were ready to pounce.

Senator Chuck Grassley of Iowa, the top Republican on the budget committee, said, “President Biden’s FY2024 budget proposal is a roadmap to fiscal ruin" and asserted that the president “doesn’t seem to give a rip about keeping his promises or securing the fiscal health of our nation.”

Speaker Kevin McCarthy was equally unimpressed, arguing that the budget was “completely unserious.” He made clear that Republicans would not support reducing the deficit through tax increases.

“Mr. President: Washington has a spending problem, NOT a revenue problem,” Mr. McCarthy said on Twitter.

President Biden said the third budget proposal of his presidency would “lift the burden off families in America,” during a speech in Philadelphia meant to contrast his economic vision with that of Republicans who have called for spending cuts.

“My budget is about investing in America and all of America,” Mr. Biden said in a roughly 50-minute speech to scores of union workers, Biden supporters and local Pennsylvania politicians. “Too many people have been left behind and treated like they’re invisible. Not anymore. I promise I see you.”

In his $6.8 trillion spending plan — which serves as the opening bid in budget talks with Republicans controlling the House — Mr. Biden proposed $5 trillion in tax increases, mostly to offset new spending programs intended to help the middle class and low-income Americans.

“No billionaire should be paying a lower tax than somebody working as a schoolteacher, a firefighter or any of you in this room,” Mr. Biden said.

Mr. Biden took a different approach in rolling out his budget this year, choosing to deliver a speech in a union hall rather than give modest remarks at the White House.

Olivia Dalton, the White House deputy press secretary, said the choice of venue — which just happened to be in Pennsylvania, a pivotal swing state — was part of an effort to highlight those who would benefit from Mr. Biden’s economic plan: the middle class and union workers.

But the remarks appeared to double as a blueprint for the economic messaging of Mr. Biden’s potential re-election campaign.

The president spoke in front of a backdrop of a union workers and a collage of posters that said, “USA 46” and “Investing in America,” plus a rendering of the White House. He once again emphasized a message of bolstering the manufacturing sector, an effort many of his allies believe can sway blue-collar workers who in recent years lost faith in the Democratic Party.

He vowed to bring back jobs that were shipped overseas by companies that wanted cheaper labor.

“Where is it written America can’t lead the world in manufacturing?” he said. “Never underestimate what America can do.”

Mr. Biden sought to contrast his proposals, which would increase spending on a wide range of social programs, with those of Republicans who have accused him of spending recklessly.

He ticked through major agenda items from his budget plan — including increasing Pell grants for college students, lowering prescription drug prices, bolstering manufacturing and infrastructure and hiring police officers — while challenging Republicans to detail spending cuts Democrats believe voters will reject.

“This is not your father’s Republican Party,” said Mr. Biden.

At one point, he flipped one of the main attack lines from Republicans back at them. He accused them of wanting to defund the police, citing some members of Congress who called for cutting funding to the F.B.I. after the agency searched former President Donald J. Congressional Republicans on Thursday quickly condemned and dismissed President Biden’s budget outline, foreshadowing the difficult fiscal struggle in the months ahead as the two sides clash over funding the federal government and raising the debt limit.

Top Republicans in the House and Senate were unsparing in their criticism of the administration’s plan, saying that it maintained what they see as an unsustainable level of spending and debt that they attribute to an emphasis on progressive political policies.

“His new budget contains the highest sustained levels of taxes, spending and deficits in American history,” said Representative Jodey Arrington, Republican of Texas and the new chairman of the House Budget Committee.

In the Senate, Senator Charles E. Grassley of Iowa, the top Republican on the Budget Committee, called Mr. Biden’s spending blueprint “an unserious proposal, and will be treated as such by both parties in Congress.”

Mr. Biden’s budget proposal “is a road map for fiscal ruin,” Mr. Grassley said.

Lawmakers have in recent years given the budgets submitted by presidents of both parties short shrift, preferring to use their own authority to map out budget and spending policies. But with the Republicans who control the House eyeing steep spending cuts and Democrats holding the Senate and White House, settling on common ground is going to be difficult.

Speaker Kevin McCarthy and his lieutenants made it clear that they intend to insist on significant reductions in spending before they will consider allowing the federal debt limit to be raised — even though a stalemate over the debt limit could shake the world economy and endanger the retirement savings of millions of Americans.

“We must cut wasteful government spending,” Mr. McCarthy and the other members of his leadership team said in a joint statement issued after the Biden budget was released. “Our debt is one of the greatest threats to America, and the time to address this crisis is now.”

The next step is for lawmakers to forge their own budgets. House Republicans have begun laying the groundwork for a conservative alternative to the president’s proposal, meeting among themselves to explore deep spending cuts. With the huge Social Security and Medicare programs off the table, Republicans are focused on Medicaid, food stamps, foreign aid and other elements of domestic spending that will be defended by Democrats and the White House.

Mr. Arrington blamed Biden’s policies for inflation, higher interest rates and the threat of a recession. “Unfortunately, Biden’s latest budget is more of the same bloated bureaucracy at the expense of working families, while sticking our grandchildren with the bill,” he said.

There was a sliver of bipartisan optimism.

The top two lawmakers on the Senate Appropriations Committee, Senator Patty Murray of Washington, the Democratic chair, and Senator Susan Collins of Maine, the senior Republican, said the budget clears the way for them to begin writing the spending bills necessary to fund the government for the fiscal year beginning Oct. 1.

“We have a real opportunity — and an important responsibility — to work together to make our country safer, more competitive, and do some good for the people we all represent back home,” they said in a joint statement. “The power of the purse rests with Congress and we take that responsibility seriously.”

Republicans who are demanding deep cuts to federal spending have already greeted President Biden’s budget plan with a familiar refrain: Mr. Biden and his party are to blame for causing the debt to balloon.

But an analysis of House and Senate voting records, and of fiscal estimates of legislation prepared by the nonpartisan Congressional Budget Office, shows that Republicans bear at least equal blame for the biggest drivers of federal debt growth that passed Congress over the last two presidential administrations.

The national debt has grown to $31.4 trillion from just under $6 trillion in 2000. That increase, which spanned the presidential administrations of two Republicans and two Democrats, has been fueled by tax cuts, wars, economic stimulus and the growing costs of retirement and health programs.

The analysis is based on the forecasts that the C.B.O. regularly issues for the federal budget, which include descriptions of newly passed legislation that affects spending, revenues and deficits, tallying the costs of those new laws over the course of a decade.

A significant portion of that debt has accumulated recently. Going back to the start of former President Donald J. Trump’s tenure in 2017, those reports highlight 13 new laws that, by the C.B.O.’s projections, will combine to add more than $11.5 trillion to the debt.

Nearly three-quarters of that new debt was approved in bills that gained the support of a majority of Republicans in at least one chamber of Congress. Three-fifths of it was signed into law by Mr. Trump.

Many of the votes were roundly bipartisan, such as a series of Covid-19 relief measures. Others passed entirely along party lines — and in those cases, on net, Republicans added slightly more to the debt than Democrats.

That’s because of the sweeping corporate and individual tax cuts that Mr. Trump signed into law at the end of 2017, which cost $2 trillion. Despite Republican claims that the tax cuts paid for themselves, the C.B.O. estimated last month that Mr. Trump’s corporate tax cuts alone would cost the federal government hundreds of billions of dollars in revenue in the years to come.

Earlier C.B.O. analyses suggest that the full slate of tax cuts have already cost the government $1.2 trillion through the 2022 fiscal year.

President Biden’s budget has several health care-related proposals, including ones aimed at prolonging Medicare’s solvency by raising new dedicated tax money to finance the health program, and would impose policies to reduce prescription drug prices for various programs.

Here is a look at the proposals for each program:

Medicare

As the White House previewed earlier this week, the budget would increase taxes that fund Medicare, reduce spending on prescription drugs and redirect more funding streams into the Medicare hospital trust fund. Together, these policies would extend the life of that fund for 25 years, the White House said.

Congress recently gave Medicare the power to negotiate with drugmakers over the prices of a select number of prescription drugs. The budget proposal would apply the approach to a larger number of medicines, and begin negotiations sooner after they enter the market.

The budget would expand coverage for mental health services in Medicare, allowing coverage of more types of counselors and lowering the cost to beneficiaries who seek treatment.

The proposed tax increases would raise payroll taxes on high earners and subject more types of investment income to Medicare taxes.

Some of the proposed changes would improve the hospital trust fund’s balances without reducing spending or raising any more revenue, by changing the way the government accounts for existing tax revenues and the savings on pharmaceutical spending.

Only Medicare’s hospital program must be financed out of a special trust fund; the rest of the program is paid for with general funds and with premiums from beneficiaries.

Affordable Care Act

The budget proposes two major expansions of Obamacare. It would provide a federal government insurance option for poor childless adults in states that have not expanded their Medicaid programs. It would also make permanent the more generous health insurance subsidies that were first made law as part of the Covid stimulus package, and then extended in legislation last year.

Those enhanced subsidies make insurance premiums free for most Americans just above the poverty line, and they help higher income Americans lower the cost of insurance premiums. Since the subsidies were enacted, enrollment in Obamacare coverage has increased substantially, reaching record levels.

Medicaid

The budget would require state Medicaid programs to cover women who have recently had children for one year after giving birth. States currently can opt into coverage for this population, and most have. The latest state to pursue this option is Mississippi, whose legislature passed a bill this week that would extend postpartum coverage.

The budget sets aside $150 billion over the next decade to expand Medicaid’s funding for home health services for beneficiaries with long-term care needs, an effort to expand alternatives to nursing home care. While many states will pay for such care, funding is limited, leading to long waiting lists.

Proposals would also expand tools to lower the prices Medicaid programs pay to private insurance companies that administer their benefits and to pharmaceutical companies for prescription drugs.

Few of these proposals are likely to be met with enthusiasm by Republican lawmakers, who oppose raising taxes and have voted down similar expansions of Obamacare, Medicaid — and the drug-pricing policies — when Congress considered them last year. Republicans in Congress did vote for some changes to mental health coverage in Medicare as part of last year’s omnibus government funding bill.

That includes money devoted to the U.S. International Development Finance Corporation, a foreign aid agency that will finance international projects to counter Chinese programs like the Belt and Road Initiative.

The budget also references various domestic U.S. investments, which the administration argues are needed to make the U.S. economy more competitive with China. That includes funding domestic research into agriculture, an area where it says China has become the largest funder of research, as well as major investments in the manufacturing of semiconductors, clean energy products and other technologies in the United States.

President Biden’s new budget offered a glimpse into the White House’s modest economic expectations for 2023 — and the relatively glum outlook seemed to acknowledge that America is headed for a challenging period as the Federal Reserve raises interest rates to try to slow demand and combat rapid inflation.

Last year’s White House budget painted a rosy, and ultimately over-optimistic, picture of the American economy. The administration expected gross domestic product to grow 4.2 percent after adjusting for inflation, for instance, but it ultimately climbed by a more modest 2.1 percent.

But this year’s budget, released on Thursday, predicted a far more muted future. The White House thought the economy would grow by just 0.6 percent after adjusting for inflation in 2023, a weak pace that is in line with outside expectations. Analysts in a Bloomberg survey are expecting annual growth to be 0.8 percent, and economic projections recently released by the nonpartisan Congressional Budget Office suggested that annual growth could be 0.3 percent in 2023.

Even more notably, the White House predicted that the unemployment rate would increase. Joblessness was forecast to rise to 4.3 percent, up from 3.4 percent in January. All else equal, a bounce that big in joblessness would leave roughly 1.5 million more people looking for work but unable to find it.

The White House finalized its projections in November, and economic data has been stronger than expected in the time since. Administration economists said in a post on the White House website that unemployment “would likely be lower” than the official forecast in light of that, but they did not specify how much.

The White House also expected inflation to moderate as the economy slowed and the job market cooled. Administration officials projected that the Consumer Price Index would climb 4.3 percent on average this year, down from about 8 percent over the course of 2022.

Taken together, the forecasts suggested that the White House was accepting something that its last budget estimates rejected: The economy could take a beating as the Fed tries to wrangle rapid inflation.

While last year’s budget economic projections set out a hopeful scenario in which price increases gradually moderated as growth cooled gently and unemployment remained very low, this year’s outlook matches up with what the Fed and other forecasters are expecting. Bringing inflation back down after two years of rapid price jumps is likely to take a period of below-normal economic growth that might put a dent in the labor market.

Still, the White House projections suggest that an outright recession could be avoided — also in line with the Fed’s latest economic forecasts, released in December. Many government officials are hoping that the unusual economic situation that has taken hold after the pandemic, one with plentiful job openings and resilient demand, will mean that growth can slow substantially without leading to mass layoffs and a painful downturn.

“Other business cycles had quite different back stories than this one,” Jerome H. Powell, the Fed chair, said during congressional testimony this week. “We’re going to have to find out whether that matters or not.”

But while the White House called resilience in the labor market and economy “welcome news” in its blog, the Fed has been watching signs of continued strength apprehensively, unsure whether the economy is moderating enough to cool down inflation to a more sustainable pace.

The Fed has raised interest rates from near zero to above 4.5 percent over the past year, and it is expected to lift them again at its meeting on March 22. How much — a quarter point or a more aggressive half-point — will likely hinge on the strength of labor and inflation data over the next week.
