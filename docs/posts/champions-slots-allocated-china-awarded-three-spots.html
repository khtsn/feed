🟨⬜🟩⬜⬜ EARLY

Today’s rating is based solely on my agonizing performance, and should prove that there is no insider trading within New York Times Games.

EARLY has been my starting word for just over three months. I used to change it up frequently, sometimes getting as greedy as RAZOR or JUICE. Nowadays, I like knowing early on (heh) if the word ends in Y, for instance, or contains both E and R.

It’s nice to see both a green and a yellow square, but this isn’t very helpful. I can feel the flop sweat already: If I, a New York Times puzzle editor, take more than the standard four guesses, I’ll never hear the end of it. One of the first words that comes to mind for my second guess: HORDE. But … nah, I can’t get cute, busting that one out so early. Not in front of our Gameplay readership. Wasn’t it HORDE once already, anyway? Or am I thinking of HORSE?

I need to do something safer. An S, a T. Let’s try STREW.

No progress. I know it has only been two guesses, but I’m already getting nervous. I channel my “What would Tracy do?” mind-set. Really, it’s just a sorry excuse to get cute again.

I bypass two much more common vowels in favor of CURVE. It reveals the place of the E — progress! But most likely not enough. My brain jumps to diabolical traps like MERGE and GORGE, yet I shudder at the thought of embarrassing myself further, reaching so brazenly for a double letter.

FORGE. It has to be FORGE. A perfectly cromulent, face-saving guess.

It was not FORGE. And I knew exactly what it was immediately thereafter. BORNE was technically possible, but at that point I just knew. What a twisted joke.

I type in the correct five-letter word, and followed this up by saying several four-letter words I use much more commonly.

Today’s word is HORDE. According to Webster’s New World College Dictionary, it’s a noun meaning “a large, moving crowd.”
