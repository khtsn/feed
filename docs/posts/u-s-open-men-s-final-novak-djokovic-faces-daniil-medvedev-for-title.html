Reporting from Flushing Meadows, Queens

Here’s how Djokovic and Medvedev reached the final.

The roads to the U.S. Open final have been different for Novak Djokovic and Daniil Medvedev.

Djokovic has made it look easy, having dropped just two sets in his six matches. Those two sets came in the third round against Laslo Djere, a fellow Serbian.

Djokovic faced two young Americans along the way, beating Taylor Fritz in a quarterfinal and Ben Shelton in a semifinal.

For Medvedev, the path has been slightly more challenging. Three of his matches have gone to four sets: in the second round against Christopher O’Connell, in the fourth round against Alex de Minaur and, perhaps the hardest of all, against Carlos Alcaraz in the semifinals on Friday.

Medvedev has spent slightly more time on court, at 15 hours and 45 minutes through the semifinals. Djokovic has spent 15 hours and 2 minutes on court so far. That comparison is much more even than it was in 2021, when Medvedev reached the final having played about 12 hours through his six matches, while Djokovic had spent more than 17 hours on court.

Sara Ziegler

The other winners at the U.S. Open.

The men’s singles championship will be the 19th — and final — title to be awarded at the 2023 U.S. Open. We assume you know all about Coco Gauff’s big win in women’s singles on Saturday (and if you weren’t following along with our blog, you can catch up here). But what about the other freshly crowned champions?

Doubles gave us a new piece of U.S. Open history. Rajeev Ram of the United States and Joe Salisbury of Britain are the first pair to claim three straight men’s doubles titles in New York in the Open era. David Waldstein also wrote about an impressive display of sportsmanship on the part of the losing team, Rohan Bopanna and Matthew Ebden.

In women’s doubles, Gabriela Dabrowski of Canada and Erin Routliffe of New Zealand beat Laura Siegemund and Vera Zvonareva. And in mixed doubles, Anna Danilina of Kazakhstan and Harri Heliovaara of Finland toppled the No. 1-seeded American team of Jessica Pegula and Austin Krajicek.

The juniors championship saw another American teenager win it all, with the 18-year-old Katherine Hui beating Tereza Valentova for the girls’ singles title. Joao Fonseca of Brazil won the boys’ singles by defeating the American Learner Tien, who also played in the men’s tournament, losing to Frances Tiafoe in the first round. In junior doubles, Mara Gae of Romania and Anastasiia Gureva of Russia beat Sara Saito and Nanaka Sato for the girls title, and Max Dahlin of Sweden and Oliver Ojakaar of Estonia defeated Federico Bondioli and Joel Schwaerzler for the boys crown.

In the wheelchair championship, the dominant force of women’s tennis completed her third straight calendar Slam. Diede de Groot of the Netherlands beat Yui Kamiji in straight sets and has now swept all four major tournaments each year since 2021. On the men’s side, Alfie Hewett beat his fellow Brit and doubles partner, Gordon Reid. And in the quad final, doubles partners Niels Vink and Sam Schroder of the Netherlands were facing off for the title in Louis Armstrong Stadium after they won their third straight doubles title at the Open on Saturday, over Andy Lapthorne and Donald Ramphadi.

Stephane Houdet of France and Takashi Sanada of Japan beat Takuya Miki and Tokito Oda for the men’s wheelchair doubles title. And in women’s doubles, the team of Diede de Groot and Jiske Griffioen had to withdraw because of an injury to Griffioen, so Yui Kamiji of Japan and Kgothatso Montjane of South Africa were awarded the title.

In the junior division of the wheelchair championship, Dahnon Ward of Britain beat the top-seeded Francesco Felici for the boys’ singles title, and he also won the doubles crown, teaming up with fellow Brit Joshua Johns to defeat the American team of Charlie Cooper and Tomas Majetic. The girls’ side also saw a two-time winner, with Ksenia Chasteau of France beating the American Maylee Phelps in singles, then pairing with Phelps for a doubles win over Sabina Czauz and Yuma Takamuro.

Plan to see some famous faces in the crowd.

Fans will be watching with bated breath on Sunday to see if Novak Djokovic can tie Margaret Court’s record of 24 titles. Many of those fans will be famous.

A number of celebrities were in the crowd on Saturday to watch Coco Gauff beat Aryna Sabalenka for her first Grand Slam title, including Shonda Rhimes, Mindy Kaling and Nicole Kidman.

Celebrities in the music industry have also made appearances during the tournament, including the rappers Meek Mill and Lil Uzi Vert and the singers J Balvin and Seal.

Matthew Futterman

Reporting from Flushing Meadows, Queens

Some players lost their cool in the stifling heat.

In most years, there is a very specific climate pattern at the U.S. Open.

The tournament starts at the end of the dog days of August, in the lingering heat and humidity of a New York summer. By the final matches, at the end of the first full week of September, it’s a good idea to bring a light sweater or a windbreaker to the Billie Jean King National Tennis Center.

Not this year. Not even close.

A first week filled with cool, breezy afternoons and crisp nights gave way to some of the hottest days — and nights — of the summer, with conditions that brought some of the fittest athletes in the world nearly to their knees, even when they were playing in twilight and after sunset.

It was the first thing that Daniil Medvedev thought of when taking the court for his warm-ups in the second week of the tournament, sessions that took place hours before his matches.

“I was like, ‘Oh, my God,’” Medvedev said as he prepared for his fourth-round match. Medvedev is from Russia and, like many of the Eastern European players, can become awfully cranky in extreme heat.

In a quarterfinal match on Wednesday, he struggled to see the ball and relied on instinct to survive a grinding battle with his countryman and close friend, Andrey Rublev. For the second consecutive day, organizers used a new measure to bring relief — partially closing the roof of Arthur Ashe Stadium to shade the court.

“One player gonna die, and they gonna see,” Medvedev muttered in the middle of the match.

Matthew Futterman

Reporting from Flushing Meadows, Queens

Novak Djokovic, loving New York as never before.

For two years, Novak Djokovic has been dreaming about New York.

He has had plenty of success here, winning the U.S. Open three times. It’s where he made one of his most famous shots, returning Roger Federer’s serve with a walloping forehand when he was down double match point in their semifinal in 2011.

His mind, though, has been stuck on one of his lowest moments, just before the end of his disappointing loss in the 2021 U.S. Open singles final against Daniil Medvedev.

Djokovic was one win away from just about the only thing he has not accomplished in his career — becoming the first man since Rod Laver in 1969 to win all four Grand Slams in a single year. He sat in his chair on the sideline before the final game listening to the crowd of 23,000 in Arthur Ashe Stadium, who had long mostly cheered for his beloved opponents, roaring for him instead. He sobbed into a towel.

He knew that New York crowds appreciated seeing greatness and history. He had felt and heard them pulling for him as soon as he walked onto the court, and they were still there for him as he sat on the edge of defeat.

“I fell in love with the New Yorkers and New York in a completely different way that day,” Djokovic said during an interview before this year’s tournament in the player garden outside the stadium.

After missing the tournament last year because of his refusal to be vaccinated against Covid-19, Djokovic is finally back at the U.S. Open, and back in the final. Like his collection of Grand Slam singles titles, now numbering 23 and the most of any man, the love he felt that Sunday two years ago seems only to have grown, on both sides.

Oskar Garcia

Reporting from Flushing Meadows, Queens

The U.S. Open winners get $3 million each. So $1.5 million is on the line in the final.

Of all the ways to think about this U.S. Open men’s final — its shaping of legacy, rivalry and strategy — perhaps no framing is easier to understand than cold, hard cash. Consider that when Novak Djokovic and Daniil Medvedev take the court, their matchup will have $1.5 million on the line.

That’s the difference between first and second place in the men’s and women’s singles tournaments at the U.S. Open, with the winner getting $3 million and the runner-up getting $1.5 million.

Coco Gauff accepted that winner’s payday on Saturday and immediately turned to Billie Jean King, who was standing on the stage for the trophy ceremony, and said: “Thank you, Billie, for fighting for this.” Her comment was a nod to King’s role in pushing for equal pay for men and women at the Open in 1973, which has been celebrated as a central theme during this tournament 50 years later.

Back then, the paydays for the singles winners were $25,000.

The $3 million winner’s check this year is up 15 percent from the $2.6 million awarded in last year’s tournament. The total prize pool for each of the U.S. Open’s top singles tournaments is nearly $22.4 million.

Each entrant in the main singles draws earns $81,500, and the payouts escalate in each round. That means that each of the first-round matches had $41,500 on the line — the difference in the payday for tournament entrants and those who made the second round, who earned a guaranteed $123,000.

The structure of the tournament provides higher stakes for every subsequent match. The four semifinalists in the men’s and women’s tournaments each earned $775,000, and those who reached the final earned an extra $725,000. Plus, of course, the chance at that top prize.

How to watch the men’s final.

Novak Djokovic, winner of 23 Grand Slam titles, will take on Daniil Medvedev, who won the 2021 U.S. Open, on Sunday in the men’s final.

Here’s what you need to know about how to watch:

When is the final?

The men’s final will be at 4 p.m. Eastern time on Sunday.

How can I watch on television or online in the United States?

ESPN has been carrying live coverage of the tournament, but a dispute between Disney and the cable giant Charter Communications has resulted in a blackout of the channel for customers of Charter’s cable television service, Spectrum, since Labor Day weekend.

The dispute has led to some sports fans signing up for other web-based services that carry ESPN, including Sling and YouTubeTV, a move that ESPN itself has encouraged in its battle for public sentiment, with Disney and Charter each blaming the other for the stalemate.

As our media reporter Ben Mullin reported previously, these kinds of disagreements happen regularly, with channels going dark for some customers on different providers for days or weeks at a time. But this one is particularly contentious because Charter has positioned the fight as a key one for the future of the cable business, and Disney has said it has made its best offer to Charter.

What about in other countries?

Fans around the world can watch the final on various networks. TSN will carry it in Canada, Sky Sports will televise the match in the United Kingdom and Ireland, SupperTennis in Italy, Telefonica/Movistar Plus in Spain, Sportdeutschland.TV in Germany and Migu in China.

Reporting from Flushing Meadows, Queens

Djokovic and Medvedev are meeting in a U.S. Open final rematch.

The last time Novak Djokovic and Daniil Medvedev met in a U.S. Open final, Medvedev ruined Djokovic’s ambitious plans for 2021.

Djokovic was one title away from completing the calendar Grand Slam, having already won the Australian Open, the French Open and Wimbledon that year. But Medvedev defeated Djokovic for the U.S. Open title in straight sets.

Now they meet again at the same tournament, also with a championship on the line. There’s no calendar Grand Slam on the line this time, after Carlos Alcaraz defeated Djokovic over the summer at Wimbledon. But if Djokovic wins on Sunday, it will be his third Grand Slam of the year, having won the Australian Open and the French Open, and it would be his 24th Grand Slam title.

Medvedev said Friday that defeating Djokovic this time around will be harder than it was in 2021, adding that Djokovic tends to find ways to improve after losing.

“When he loses, he’s never the same after,” Medvedev said. “He’s going to be 10 times better than he was that day, and I have to be, if I want to still beat him, 10 times better than I was that day.”

But a 24th Grand Slam title won’t come easy for Djokovic, either. Medvedev reached the final after upsetting Alcaraz in the semifinals, and Medvedev defeated Djokovic the last time they played, in March at a tournament in Dubai.

Djokovic said on Friday that he would approach the final the way he does any other match, “with intention to win.”

“I’ll play my opponent knowing that it’s going to be the toughest challenge, without a doubt,” Djokovic said.
