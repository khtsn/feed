The last time Novak Djokovic and Daniil Medvedev met in a U.S. Open final, Medvedev ruined Djokovic’s ambitious plans for 2021.

Djokovic was one title away from completing the calendar Grand Slam, having already won the Australian Open, the French Open and Wimbledon that year. But Medvedev defeated Djokovic for the U.S. Open title in straight sets.

Now they meet again at the same tournament, also with a championship on the line. There’s no calendar Grand Slam on the line this time, after Carlos Alcaraz defeated Djokovic over the summer at Wimbledon. But if Djokovic wins on Sunday, it will be his third Grand Slam of the year, having won the Australian Open and the French Open, and it would be his 24th Grand Slam title.

Medvedev said Friday that defeating Djokovic this time around will be harder than it was in 2021, adding that Djokovic tends to find ways to improve after losing.

“When he loses, he’s never the same after,” Medvedev said. “He’s going to be 10 times better than he was that day, and I have to be, if I want to still beat him, 10 times better than I was that day.”

But a 24th Grand Slam title won’t come easy for Djokovic, either. Medvedev reached the final after upsetting Alcaraz in the semifinals, and Medvedev defeated Djokovic the last time they played, in March at a tournament in Dubai.

Djokovic said on Friday that he would approach the final the way he does any other match, “with intention to win.”

“I’ll play my opponent knowing that it’s going to be the toughest challenge, without a doubt,” Djokovic said.

Of all the ways to think about this U.S. Open men’s final — its shaping of legacy, rivalry and strategy — perhaps no framing is easier to understand than cold, hard cash. Consider that when Novak Djokovic and Daniil Medvedev take the court, their matchup will have $1.5 million on the line.

That’s the difference between first and second place in the men’s and women’s singles tournaments at the U.S. Open, with the winner getting $3 million and the runner-up getting $1.5 million.

Coco Gauff accepted that winner’s payday on Saturday and immediately turned to Billie Jean King, who was standing on the stage for the trophy ceremony, and said: “Thank you, Billie, for fighting for this.” Her comment was a nod to King’s role in pushing for equal pay for men and women at the Open in 1973, which has been celebrated as a central theme during this tournament 50 years later.

Back then, the paydays for the singles winners were $25,000.

The $3 million winner’s check this year is up 15 percent from the $2.6 million awarded in last year’s tournament. The total prize pool for each of the U.S. Open’s top singles tournaments is nearly $22.4 million.

Each entrant in the main singles draws earns $81,500, and the payouts escalate in each round. That means that each of the first-round matches had $41,500 on the line — the difference in the payday for tournament entrants and those who made the second round, who earned a guaranteed $123,000.

The structure of the tournament provides higher stakes for every subsequent match. The four semifinalists in the men’s and women’s tournaments each earned $775,000, and those who reached the final earned an extra $725,000. Plus, of course, the chance at that top prize.

Novak Djokovic, winner of 23 Grand Slam titles, will take on Daniil Medvedev, who won the 2021 U.S. Open, on Sunday in the men’s final.

Here’s what you need to know about how to watch:

When is the final?

The men’s final will be at 4 p.m. Eastern time on Sunday.

How can I watch on television or online in the United States?

ESPN has been carrying live coverage of the tournament, but a dispute between Disney and the cable giant Charter Communications has resulted in a blackout of the channel for customers of Charter’s cable television service, Spectrum, since Labor Day weekend.

The dispute has led to some sports fans signing up for other web-based services that carry ESPN, including Sling and YouTubeTV, a move that ESPN itself has encouraged in its battle for public sentiment, with Disney and Charter each blaming the other for the stalemate.

As our media reporter Ben Mullin reported previously, these kinds of disagreements happen regularly, with channels going dark for some customers on different providers for days or weeks at a time. But this one is particularly contentious because Charter has positioned the fight as a key one for the future of the cable business, and Disney has said it has made its best offer to Charter.

What about in other countries?

Fans around the world can watch the final on various networks. TSN will carry it in Canada, Sky Sports will televise the match in the United Kingdom and Ireland, SupperTennis in Italy, Telefonica/Movistar Plus in Spain, Sportdeutschland.TV in Germany and Migu in China.
