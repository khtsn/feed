Beryl weakened to a tropical storm as it traveled across the Yucatán Peninsula of Mexico on Friday, after making landfall there as a Category 2 hurricane that brought destructive winds and storm surge to the region. Beryl, which had swept through the eastern Caribbean and flattened islands there over the past week, is expected to emerge in the Gulf of Mexico by the end of the day and strengthen again this weekend.

In Quintana Roo, where power outages were reported, state and federal authorities were removing sand, debris and downed trees from the streets and helping drivers whose cars were stranded on flooded roads.

Texas officials were already beginning to prepare for possible impacts from the storm in the coming days as a hurricane watch went into effect for the southern part of the state on Friday afternoon.

Here are key things to know about the storm:

  • Power outages were reported in the state of Yucatán as Beryl moved there from Quintana Roo, the governor there said. A tropical storm warning was in effect for part of the peninsula’s coast, from west of Cabo Catoche to Campeche, on Friday.

  • Storm surge could raise water levels by as much as two to five feet.

  • Maximum sustained winds decreased to near 65 m.p.h. with higher gusts, according to the National Weather Service.

  • Although Beryl weakened slightly as it crossed the Yucatán Peninsula, the storm is expected to re-intensify over the southwestern Gulf of Mexico late Friday. Then it will probably move toward northeastern Mexico and southern Texas.

  • The Weather Service on Friday afternoon issued a hurricane watch and a storm surge watch for the southern Texas coast, from the mouth of the Rio Grande to Sargent, a community roughly 65 miles southwest of Houston.

  • Mexico’s meteorological service issued a hurricane watch for the northeastern coast of Mexico from Barra el Mezquital to the mouth of the Rio Grande, The Weather Service said.

The storm has left destruction in its wake.

Forecasters were predicting that Mexico would be hit twice by the hurricane. It was on track to cross the Yucatán Peninsula on Friday, and then, after traversing the Gulf of Mexico, reach the coast of the northern state of Tamaulipas.

Officials in Texas were already preparing for the storm’s possible landfall on Monday morning as a hurricane watch was issued for the state’s southern coast and part of northeastern Mexico on Friday afternoon.

Dan Patrick, the lieutenant governor of Texas, who is serving as acting governor while Gov. Greg Abbott travels out of the country, said that emergency response teams were paying close attention to areas at greater risk, starting from Brownsville, Texas all the way to the Louisiana border.

“Everyone along the coast should be paying attention to this storm,” Mr. Patrick said. Likely flooding and storm surge remained major concerns, state officials said. State officials are preparing for a possible landfall on Monday morning.

In Mexico, no injuries or deaths had been reported so far, Laura Velázquez Alzúa, Mexico’s coordinator of civil protection, said at an afternoon news conference. More than 2,100 people who took refuge in shelters were already returning to their homes.

Thousands of electricians in Quintana Roo were working to restore power outages, which affected about 20 percent of the population, Ms. Velázquez Alzúa said. No major flooding was reported and the storm did not damage the state’s water system.

“Normality is being reestablished,” Ms. Velázquez Alzúa said. “The damages are minor.”

Early Friday, Mexico’s meteorological agency said the storm had dumped between six and 10 inches of rain in Campeche, Quintana Roo and Yucatán. It reported wind gusts as high 135 m.p.h. and a storm surge of more than 10 feet along the coasts of Quintana Roo and Yucatán.

Earlier this week, at least nine people were killed as the storm lashed parts of Grenada, then Jamaica and the Cayman Islands. The Jamaican prime minister’s office released a statement on Friday offering condolences to the families of the two people killed there.

Beryl made landfall on Monday in Grenada, where officials said about 98 percent of the buildings on Carriacou and Petite Martinique, home to 9,000 to 10,000 people in total, had been damaged or destroyed, including Carriacou’s main health facility.

“We have to rebuild from the ground up,” said Dickon Mitchell, the prime minister of Grenada.

Aid was just starting to arrive by boat on Carriacou on Friday, after rough seas and destroyed roads complicated response efforts.

The storm then churned along toward Jamaica, where on Wednesday, heavy rain and destructive winds left their mark. Beryl was the strongest storm to approach the island in over a decade. Over half of the customers of the country’s main power provider, remained without electricity on Friday, the company said.

The storm also ripped away a section of the jet bridge roof of Jamaica’s main airport.

Residents in the Cayman Islands were largely breathing a sigh of relief after Hurricane Beryl passed by at Category 3 strength on Thursday morning without making landfall. There were no reports of significant damage, injuries or fatalities.

This hurricane season is expected to be busy.

Forecasters have warned that the 2024 Atlantic hurricane season could be much more active than usual.

In late May, the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration predicted 17 to 25 named storms this year, an “above-normal” number and a prediction in line with more than a dozen forecasts earlier in the year from experts at universities, private companies and government agencies. Hurricane seasons produce 14 named storms, on average.
