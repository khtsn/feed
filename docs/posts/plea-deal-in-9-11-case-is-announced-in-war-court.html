Plea Deal in 9/11 Case Is Announced in War Court

The accused mastermind of the plot, Khalid Shaikh Mohammed, appeared at a session as the agreement was disclosed. A sentencing hearing could begin next year.

Aug. 1, 2024Updated 7:44 p.m. ET

The man accused of plotting the attacks of Sept. 11, 2001, appeared in court on Thursday, watching silently as the prosecutor who had pursued his capital case since the beginning formally announced that a plea agreement had been reached that would remove the possibility of the death penalty.

The prosecutor, Clayton G. Trivett Jr., also gave the court the sealed and signed agreements between the prisoner, Khalid Shaikh Mohammed, and the Pentagon’s overseer of the war court cases.

Disclosure of the agreement in the court at Guantánamo Bay is the first step toward a sentencing hearing before a military panel, which could begin next summer.

Prosecutors who had negotiated the agreements with Mr. Mohammed and two accomplices, Walid bin Attash and Mustafa al-Hawsawi, disclosed the deal on Wednesday to family members of the nearly 3,000 people who were killed in the attacks.

The notices provided some details of the agreement that were not included in a brief Defense Department announcement, including an opportunity for Sept. 11 family members to submit questions to be posed to Mr. Mohammed and his co-defendants.

Mr. bin Attash and Mr. Hawsawi, who also reached plea agreements with prosecutors, did not come to court on Thursday. They each signed a document saying their absence was voluntary.

But Mr. Mohammed, who had the coronavirus earlier this month, arrived in court in his typical no-nonsense fashion, sat with his defense team at the lead defendant’s table and chatted extensively with his lawyers. He wore a medical mask that covered his bright orange hennaed beard, as well as a red-and-white check headdress, a kaffiyeh and an olive drab tunic and trousers.

The next step is for the judge, Col. Matthew N. McCall, to hold a question-and-answer session with the three defendants to establish whether they understood the agreement and had signed it voluntarily.

The case originally had five defendants, including the three who agreed to plead guilty. A fourth, Ramzi bin al-Shibh, was found mentally unfit to assist his defense counsel, and his case was severed from the others.

Ammar al Baluchi, Mr. Mohammed’s nephew, is the fifth defendant. He declined to join the others in pleading guilty, Mr. Trivett announced, and will have his own capital trial.

Mr. Trivett came to the war court in the early 2000s as a Navy lieutenant and remained on the case as a civilian. As other, more senior members of the prosecution team resigned or retired, he rose to the position of managing trial counsel and had a key role in both open court hearings and secret negotiation sessions.

On Thursday, he asked the judge to seal the plea agreements until a jury was empaneled in the case, meaning the soonest the public could learn the details would be next summer. Colonel McCall agreed but said that Mr. Baluchi’s team could also obtain a copy.

Gary D. Sowards, Mr. Mohammed’s lawyer, endorsed the idea. He said that perhaps it would “facilitate disposition globally of the case.”

News of the deal was greeted with mixed reaction in the political arena.

Senator Mitch McConnell, the longtime Republican leader, called the plea agreement “a revolting abdication of the government’s responsibility to defend America and provide justice” and blamed the Biden administration for permitting it to happen.

“The only thing worse than negotiating with terrorists is negotiating with them after they are in custody,” he said. “The families of their victims and the American people deserve real justice.”

The White House said in a statement that it was not a party to the agreement and learned about it after it was signed by the Pentagon official, Susan K. Escallier, a retired Army brigadier general. The Biden administration had earlier declined to sign off on conditions sought by the defendants and returned the issue to the Defense Department.

“The president and the White House played no role in this process,” the White House said on Wednesday evening. “The President has directed his team to consult as appropriate with officials and lawyers at the Department of Defense on this matter.”

Senator Richard J. Durbin, Democrat of Illinois and the chairman of the Judiciary Committee, hailed the plea agreement on social media.

“I commend the military prosecutors for finally delivering a small measure of justice and finality to the victims and their loved ones,” he said. “The Bush administration’s disastrous decision to torture detainees and set up untested military commissions made a fair trial impossible.”

On Thursday, Theodore B. Olson, who was solicitor general under President George W. Bush and whose wife, Barbara, was killed aboard the flight that crashed into the Pentagon on Sept. 11, declined to comment on the deal.

He gained attention earlier this year for a column published in The Wall Street Journal that urged a negotiated resolution to the Guantánamo cases. He said the U.S. government erred in creating the war court and then deciding to pursue capital cases there.

“Death penalty cases are the most hotly contested legal proceedings, given their irreversible nature,” he wrote. “We doomed these newly created commissions to collapse under their own weight.”
