Why Trump Gets Away With All Those Horrible Things

Contributing Opinion Writer

An Oscar is seldom just an Oscar. It’s a signal and a symbol. It’s the movie industry romanticizing itself or reinventing itself or taking a stand or maybe just taking leave of its senses. It’s a mirror no matter what, and rarely has that been truer than in this year’s best actress race, less a discrete contest than a whole cultural conversation.

Can it be an accident that the neck-and-neck front-runners — Lily Gladstone, for “Killers of the Flower Moon,” and Emma Stone, for “Poor Things” — play characters who are emblems, respectively, of white men’s exploitation of darker-skinned people and of all men’s exploitation of women? It cannot. When Oscar voters aren’t blissfully ignoring the social tensions beyond Hollywood, they’re shouting their awareness of injustice, and the best actress face-off is a shout that asks those voters to pick their form of bigotry, choose their manner of cruelty.

I suspect that on Sunday night, when the gold statuettes are handed out, it will be Gladstone and not Stone who’s clutching one, in somewhat of a repeat of last year, when Michelle Yeoh triumphed for her performance in “Everything Everywhere All at Once” and became the first Asian woman to win best actress. Gladstone would be the first Native American woman.

That milestone has been mentioned constantly in Oscars coverage, and that’s not all: Subtract Gladstone’s character from “Killers” and you have yet another epic of oppression that focuses on its white agents rather than its nonwhite victims. Reward Gladstone and you’re striking a blow against that longtime lopsidedness.

That would seem to seal her victory, but there’s a hitch. While Gladstone’s work is exquisitely poignant, her role is close to a supporting one, and it traffics in a subtlety that isn’t typically the best Oscar bait.

Stone? She dominates “Poor Things” with a flamboyant, daredevil performance that requires her to pantomime a full-grown woman with a toddler’s brain, then to mature into a less impulsive adult. There is none of the glimmer or glamour of her role in “La La Land,” for which she won best actress seven years ago. In many scenes she seems not to be wearing any makeup. Often her face isn’t all that’s naked.

By Oscar’s arithmetic, that adds up to a bravery that can’t be ignored. But with Gladstone in the mix? The best actress category is an embarrassment of virtue in an age that sorely needs it.

When the Golden Raspberry Awards, or Razzies, were established in 1981, they must have seemed like a necessary antidote to the self-serious pomp of Hollywood’s awards season. Who doesn’t think it’s funny to point out that “Can’t Stop the Music,” the first Razzie recipient for worst picture, is bad?

Over the ensuing decades, in which both the fortunes of the industry and the context of film fandom have changed, the Razzies march on, undeterred, with the 44th Razzie Awards set to be announced on Saturday. Great. Fun. There should never be a 45th.

In the pre-internet era, it was useful to have dissenting outsider voices that could playfully puncture the illusion of Hollywood infallibility. (I speak from some experience.) In the internet era, there’s no lack of such voices; in fact, it’s a cacophony of dissent. When “Madame Web” memes swamp social media long before opening weekend, an annual roast of the films that failed seems so quaint as to be obsolete.

And the Razzies have long since curdled into a meanspirited ritual, with a litany of embarrassing missteps. In 2022 the Razzies rescinded an award it created to highlight bad Bruce Willis performances when it was revealed the actor had been diagnosed with aphasia. In 2023 the Razzies nominated a 12-year-old for worst performance and, in the face of a justified backlash, announced it would no longer target kids under 18. When your jokey awards ceremony has to self-police in order not to traumatize children, maybe the world doesn’t need your jokey awards ceremony anymore.

Every affectionate film lover knows that it’s useful to dissect both successes and failures. But the Razzies feel like a misguided prank from a different era, oddly out of sync with the times. This month, no less an arbiter of film excellence than the Criterion Channel curated a streaming collection called And the Razzie Goes To, celebrating some of the outrageously outré classics that the Razzies have mocked.

On Saturday, let the last Golden Raspberry for lifetime achievement go to the Razzies themselves. Then they can razzie off into the sunset.

Those of us in the anti-Trump camp love to play the Horribleness Game.

Donald Trump does something horrible. We are righteously appalled. We send emails to our friends saying, “Did you hear about this horrible thing Trump said?” We click on articles that fulminate against the horrible thing. We watch TV shows in which the pundits emote shock and dismay at the horrible thing. We feel very morally superior about ourselves and very morally appalled by that horrible man. It’s very satisfying. In fact, it’s addictive.

But somehow the horrible thing — or even the sum total of all the horrible things — never seems to end Trump’s career. He’s still leading in the polls, especially in swing states. So then we switch to the Wait Until They Know Game. Wait until voters know the latest thing Trump said about immigrants. Wait until voters know what’s really in the indictments.

The ultimate dream of these games is that the Trump era will end with some massive moral disqualification. We’ll be morally vindicated. They will be morally disgraced. The people who play these games dream of that killer conviction. They dreamed that 14th Amendment clause would disqualify Trump from even being on the ballot.

The problem with these games is they don’t work. Trump is in his strongest political position ever. According to the latest New York Times/Siena poll, 97 percent of the people who voted for Trump would do so again, while only 83 percent of Biden supporters say the same. Biden is losing ground among his most loyal constituency groups. In 2020 Biden won young adults by 24 points; now his lead is 12 points. He won Hispanics by 21 points, but now trails by six. There used to be a lot more Democrats in this country than Republicans, but now the two parties are tied.

That’s because many voters don’t see politics as a place to go for moral affirmation. They want to know who will create a good environment in which they can lead their lives, and right now they trust Republicans more than Democrats to handle the economy, to handle immigration, to be generally competent. It’s about policies.

There was a lot to like about President Biden’s State of the Union address, but the most important thing was this: He understands that if you want to beat Trump, you have to show you have better policies that will improve people’s lives. It’s less emotionally satisfying, but it’s what people actually vote on.

In late October, I began working with Kamal Almashharawi, a Gazan alumnus of Seeds of Peace, a summer camp in Maine that brings together children of global conflict zones in order to create leaders with conflict-resolution skills.

For weeks, Kamal sent us voice memos from Gaza that we in Times Opinion helped him stitch into an essay. He was with 35 extended members of his family, all of whom were looking for food, water, safety. He still believed in the promise of peace and coexistence, he told us, even as his family fled their home in Gaza City. He described scenes of destruction from the Israeli bombing campaign on that journey, and sometimes he went dark for days.

In November, Kamal and his immediate family managed to flee Gaza, and he and I were able to share personal stories of loss and of war.

In journalism we often create distance between our subject and ourselves, both as a protective mechanism and as a practical one. As the war drags on, I have thought back often on moments of shared humanity like that one.

This week, for the first time in person, I met Kamal and his parents, Ismail and Futna Almashharawi, in Washington. His father was an engineer in Gaza, his mother a lawyer. Abroad, they cannot work. They told me they are safe and they feel fortunate but the future is unclear. Kamal can work in Saudi Arabia, and his parents are on a three-month visa while they apply for humanitarian parole in the United States. It was not clear where they would go next.

Kamal seemed hopeful, his mother terribly sad, his father resolute. The war, Ismail said, needs to end, and a real rebuilding needs to begin.

Kamal said he had met with an Israeli who lost both of his parents on Oct. 7. Kamal was impressed by the man’s continued commitment to peace. “Billions should be invested in peace building,” Kamal said. “I decided that I need to be more committed to making peace and making more impact, because I lived hardship now.”

The Almashharawis continue to carry with them many possessions. Among them: Futna’s spices. Just in case she has the chance to cook for her family.

Earlier this week, The Times’s Mark Landler described the gossip around Catherine, Princess of Wales, and her medical condition as “often hysterical.”

It’s been nearly two months since Kensington Palace released a statement that said Catherine would be undergoing a “planned abdominal surgery” that required her to be in the hospital for 10 to 14 days, and that she was “unlikely to return to public duties until after Easter.” The palace knew the speculation about Kate’s medical condition would be intense, but requested that the public respect “her wish that her personal medical information remains private.”

The vagueness of “planned abdominal surgery” and her complete absence from public view have sent the internet into an absolute tizzy, and it just underscores how out of control the culture around celebrities has become. The royal family, funded by British taxpayers, are Britain’s stars. And star-watchers with bad boundaries and unhealthy parasocial relationships feel they are entitled to every bit of personal information about the objects of their fixation.

Kate, despite choosing a very public life, has never been a “warts and all” kind of princess. In her book “The Palace Papers,” Tina Brown describes the princess as having a “Mona Lisa quality,” with a charm that is “less about dazzle, and more about sheen.”

In her 20 years in Prince William’s orbit, Kate’s sheen has not faded. When she paraded her newly postpartum body in front of the world three times, she did so consummately made up and in high heels. When she suffered from hyperemesis gravidarum, or severe morning sickness, she had to announce her pregnancies in her first trimester, when the risk of miscarriage is high. After all, there had to be some explanation for why she was missing royal engagements.

Must a princess’s body be a public commodity?

Kate has a right to keep her health issues private. It’s not like she’s got her finger on the nuclear button.

I am certainly not above a bit of good-natured speculation in my group chats. (Who among us is?) But there’s a difference between some rude private discussions among friends and tremendous public pressure to talk about something that truly isn’t anyone’s business but Kate’s.

Don’t be fooled by the bigger-than-expected February jobs growth that the Bureau of Labor Statistics reported today. True, the economy added 275,000 payroll jobs, which was well above expectations of around 200,000.

But in other respects, the jobs report was disappointing. “Today’s U.S. payrolls report was undeniably soft,” Matthew Ryan, the head of market strategy at Ebury, a financial services company, said in a statement.

The unemployment rate rose from 3.7 percent in January to 3.9 percent. That’s still low by historical standards, but it’s the highest since January 2022.

The reason the unemployment rate can go up while the economy is adding jobs is that the unemployment rate is based on a survey of households, which is separate from the survey of business establishments that’s used to calculate the change in payroll jobs.

According to the household survey, the number of people with jobs fell by 184,000 in February, continuing a downward trend. And on the survey of business establishments, the Bureau of Labor Statistics now says job growth was slower in December and January than it previously estimated. It lowered job growth in December by 43,000 and job growth in January by 124,000.

In another sign of underlying weakness in the labor market, average hourly earnings rose just 0.1 percent in February, although economists blamed some of the slowdown on seasonal effects.

“Higher interest rates and tighter credit conditions are forcing small firms — which employ about half the work force — to tighten their belts, and labor is their biggest variable cost,” Ian Shepherdson, the chairman and chief economist of Pantheon Macroeconomics, wrote in a client note. He added that there is “a real risk of an outright drop in private jobs by midyear.”

On the bright side, “there is less reason now to be concerned that renewed labor market strength will drive inflation higher again,” Andrew Hunter, the deputy U.S. chief economist at Capital Economics, wrote in a client note.

The weak jobs report could help give the Federal Reserve the confidence to start cutting interest rates, although that probably won’t happen before May or June, at the earliest.

President Biden reportedly will announce that American troops will build a temporary port to deliver aid to Gaza by sea. That’s welcome, but utterly inadequate. Like the airdrops of food that Biden previously announced, it looks to me more like a photo op than an all-out effort to address a humanitarian crisis that the United States has helped to create.

Biden has strongly backed what he acknowledged is an “over the top” war by Israel in Gaza, supplying weapons for Israel and protecting it diplomatically at the United Nations. So we’re for the war, but we’re also now mounting a relief effort to ease its consequences.

The port can indeed be helpful, but The Times quotes officials as saying that setting it up may take more than a month or two. And 20 people in Gaza have already died from malnutrition and dehydration, with hunger at “catastrophic levels,” according to the U.N.

When children are acutely malnourished, deaths can accelerate quickly, and the port may come too late for many of them. Likewise, airdrops are better than nothing, but they are small. The first airdrop involved 38,000 meals, or a single meal for less than 2 percent of Gaza’s population.

The advantage of a naval delivery is that ships can deliver much more assistance than airdrops. But aid workers say that the real problem is that Israel has an inspection process that impedes delivery into Gaza by truck and then targets civilian police officers (because they are affiliated with Hamas) so they are unwilling to protect aid deliveries. The U.N. reports that Israel has sometimes attacked and blocked its aid convoys, even if it had previously cleared the delivery with Israeli authorities. Airdrops and a seaport don’t solve those distribution problems. It’s not entirely clear how aid coming into the port will be trucked across Gaza to the places where hunger is greatest, or who will protect convoys, or how final distribution will be managed. Israel will apparently continue to inspect assistance coming in by sea, which does not inspire confidence.

It’s true that Hamas has stockpiled food that it could share and that Hamas could end the war by giving up. But the United States doesn’t have influence over Hamas. It does have leverage over Israel, as its arms supplier and diplomatic protector.

Will Biden be willing to use his leverage to pressure Israel into expediting food assistance into Gaza? That means no longer vetoing U.N. resolutions, slowing down military aid and speaking directly to the Israeli people. That’s the best hope to avert starvation.

On Wednesday, Gov. Kathy Hochul announced that she will deploy 1,000 members of New York State’s police force and National Guard to help patrol the city subways — the sort of heavy-handed, quasi-militaristic bigfoot response that used to be reserved for spasms of genuine crisis.

Here is the counterargument, in short: Crime is not up in New York City, but down over the last year, as is violent crime and transit crime. Though assaults on the subway have grown slightly (there were 570 felony assaults in 2023, compared to 539 in 2022), that small increase reflects higher ridership levels.

Felony assaults in the subway remain higher than they were before the pandemic, and assaults on transit workers have been on the rise, as well, but the latest crime data from the N.Y.P.D. shows that major crimes committed in the subway are down more than 15 percent compared to last year.

In the slightly longer view, the data looks even better. In fact, counting by the total number of major felonies, the subway system was safer at the worst point in the pandemic than it had been in all but two of the last 25 years.

Ridership is down, which means that, on a per-ride basis, the picture looks a bit darker, though only a little bit — by that metric, the subway system looks more dangerous than it was in 2019, but only about as dangerous as it was in 2015.

Subways do feel emptier in 2024 than they did before the pandemic, and they feel that way because they are: Ridership has rebounded since 2020, but only to about 70 percent of the level in 2019. But as I wrote a few weeks ago about the broader narrative of the “doom loop,” in taking post-pandemic stock of American cities we are too often seeing places become less crowded and mistaking that emptiness for other forms of social disorder and disarray. It is a bit eerie to walk down a street and see three people where you once might’ve seen 10, and perhaps less comfortable to take a seat on a subway car with five other passengers rather than seven.

It would be better, of course, if the trains were fuller and safer still. But the city isn’t in the midst of a major generational crime surge, and the subways aren’t, either.

There was once a proverbial 3-6-3 rule in banking. It described a hypothetical practice after World War II in which bankers gave 3 percent interest on deposits, offered 6 percent on loans, and then got to the golf course by 3 p.m. We’ve strayed so far from this fantasy.

Today, the average deposit rate for bank savings accounts hovers at around half a percentage point. Banks lend their 30-year fixed rate mortgages at around 7 percent. And while bankers often still play golf, they have graciously extended their business hours long enough for me to rush over after work.

Meanwhile, the Federal Reserve, the bank for bankers, is giving them a deposit rate of 5.4 percent. The Fed has raised its rates as a part of its monetary policy — they raise the cost of borrowing so that eventually, Americans spend less money and inflation abates. A higher deposit rate would incentivize us to save more and consume less, which is what the Fed currently wants. Instead, banks are earning 10 times as much on interest as we are. A 2013 study from the Federal Reserve estimated that depositors are missing out on as much as $100 billion a year because of the high rates that banks enjoy but we cannot.

So, Chairman Jerome Powell, how about I just hand you my savings directly? I will happily do my part to help fight America’s inflation if you offer me 5.4 percent interest. Sure, I could get a higher deposit rate myself by shopping around boutique savings accounts, but that wouldn’t be particularly patriotic.

Central banking can actually take a greater role in Americans’ lives. In 2018, three lawyers, including two former Treasury Department officials, wrote a proposal for “FedAccounts,” in which the Fed would open up its services to the broader public. Consumers could enjoy higher deposit rates, instantaneous money transfers, no transaction fees and deposit security. That meant no Silicon Valley Bank-style default could tank an individual’s savings. The Federal Reserve could also actually fine-tune interest rates and conduct monetary policy in a way that is not blunted by private banks.

The New York Federal Reserve Bank, my local branch, already has a defunct teller’s window in the building. Dust it off and I’ll be sure to swing by with my deposits before tee time.

Barring extraordinary developments, we will see a rematch of Donald Trump versus Joe Biden in 2024. But Biden’s theory of this election has me alarmed. In a lengthy profile in The New Yorker this week, Biden and his top strategists indicated that they want to campaign on democracy — or the “soul of the nation” — which they say will work just as it did in 2020.

Mike Donilon, a longtime adviser to Biden, predicted that by November “the focus will become overwhelming on democracy. I think the biggest images in people’s minds are going to be of Jan. 6.”

I’m not so sure.

First of all, Biden’s 2020 electoral strategy had little margin for error. If about 30,000 people in the right states had voted differently, Trump would have won. And that was when Trump’s behavior was fresh in people’s minds — he was still president — and Biden had not been through all the ups and downs of a presidential term.

Second, running on democracy alone rarely works. It didn’t work in Turkey recently, when the opposition ran on the theory that concerns about democracy and the economy would result in President Recep Tayyip Erdogan being voted out, despite an opposition candidate many called uncharismatic and unpopular. Instead, Erdogan sailed to his third term with almost 50 percent of the vote. In Hungary, Viktor Orban is in his fourth consecutive term. Many countries experienced similar outcomes.

Third, it’s hard to change voters’ minds about more abstract concerns like democracy in such a fractured media environment. Even if the major newspapers ran nothing but headlines on democracy, the rule of law and corruption until November, large sections of the electorate would be informed elsewhere. Biden staff members say they want to imitate the 2004 Bush strategy, focusing on national security, but that was two decades of the internet ago.

David Axelrod, a Democratic strategist, told The New Yorker that Trump’s entire campaign was that a strongman is the solution for an out-of-control world. “I think you have to be thinking about how you counter that,” he said, “and how you deal with fears about Biden’s condition.”

That’s probably correct for significant segments of the electorate still up for grabs. It will take Biden more than Jan. 6 to attract voters. He will have to convince them that their personal lives under Trump would truly be worse than they are now.
