What Might Replace 421a, the Law That Got NYC Housing Built?

The tax exemption known as 421a expired in 2022. A fight over its replacement, called 485x, could have major implications for the city’s future.

Jan. 28, 2024, 3:01 a.m. ET

Nearly every debate about New York’s housing crisis involves a state program called “421a.”

But what is it?

On one level, it’s a simple idea: Give developers a property tax break to build housing in New York City that might otherwise be a drag on their bottom line. The program was first put in place 50 years ago, and its proponents — including developers and politicians across the political spectrum — say it is one of the main reasons that any rental properties are built in a city with some of the most expensive housing in the world.

It’s also extremely controversial.

In 2022, the law expired, and the governor, city officials and many state lawmakers have been trying to bring it back ever since — including by introducing a new version this legislative session. But left-leaning lawmakers have fought the program’s resurrection because they say it is a giveaway for developers.

Mayor Eric Adams and many housing experts believe a new tax incentive is necessary to address the housing shortage at the root of the city’s affordability problems. Without more supply, they argue, demand for housing will continue to drive costs ever upward.

Even some of the program’s critics now acknowledge there needs to be a tax incentive that also mandates that rents on some new apartments be kept affordable. But any new law faces major hurdles, including criticism that the old one did far too little to address the city’s worsening housing crisis.

What did 421a have to do with affordability?

To receive the 421a tax break under the program, also known as Affordable New York, developers had to make a certain percentage of units “affordable” to people and families at different levels of income. Affordable in this case meant that rent should not exceed 30 percent of a household’s income.

Developers could do this in several complicated ways that varied based on the size of the property they were building and where it was being built. Each option required the building to contain a different combination of mostly market-rate apartments and apartments affordable to renters at different income tiers. Last year, for example, one option allowed developers to offer some one-bedroom apartments for about $1,000, with slightly higher prices for another subset of units.

But the most common option, according to the New York University Furman Center, was to keep about a third of apartments in a building affordable for people making 130 percent of something called the “area median income” — a complex measure calculated by the federal government and used by housing officials.

In 2023, those apartments would have cost $4,130 for a two-bedroom. For the median household in New York City, according to data from the U.S. Census Bureau, an affordable rent would be closer to $1,900.

Did 421a help the housing crisis?

Not as much as it should have.

When 421a was first put in place, typical New Yorkers spent about 20 percent of their income on rent. When it expired, that number was closer to 35 percent.

High housing costs continue to drive people out of New York City. One-third of all tenants spend more than half of their income on rent. And the city has lost some 500,000 cheap apartments over the last 30 years.

Groups working on behalf of tenants say that 421a was inefficient, costing the city an increasing amount of tax revenue while not doing enough to help lower-income New Yorkers who face the biggest housing problems.

But many developers and officials say that without some sort of tax exemption or subsidy to entice developers to set aside affordable housing in their buildings, few will bother, especially with construction costs soaring because of inflation. They also note that the program was extremely popular among developers: More than two-thirds of the 117,042 rental units built between 2000 and 2010 benefited from 421a, according to research from the Furman Center.

Without it, construction appears to have tapered off, though at least some of that can be explained by high interest rates that are making borrowing expensive right now. In 2023, developers planned to build 9,090 units, compared with 45,593 in 2022, according to a December analysis of New York City Buildings Department data by the Real Estate Board of New York, the industry’s lobbying arm. (The 2022 number was buoyed in part by developers anticipating the end of the tax break.)

Still, REBNY has also acknowledged that the old 421a program needed to be retooled to help reinvigorate apartment construction.

What would a replacement look like?

Gov. Kathy Hochul is trying to get a new program off the ground, which she is calling “485x.”

Previously, she attempted to tweak and resurrect the original tax incentive program through a 2022 proposal that tied tax breaks to deeper affordability and reduced the amount of tax revenue lost under 421a. It is possible that 485x would follow a similar pattern.

But she is leaving the details of the new program up to the real estate industry, the city and labor unions, and it appears increasingly unlikely that they will come to an agreement. Many lawmakers want to pair a new tax break with new tenant protections, called “good cause eviction,” which would give tenants a defense in court against evictions tied to rent spikes and prevent landlords from kicking renters out after their lease expires.

But the real estate industry has fought fiercely against these protections.

A new tax break will also need buy-in from labor groups, who are seeking to tie any tax incentive to certain wage standards for construction projects.

And some left-leaning lawmakers say landlords and developers did not always follow the rules under 421a. They say a new tax break needs to come with tighter enforcement, including better audits of developers receiving the benefits.

A new law may not fix the root of the problem

Any new law will likely not address a deeper problem — that the city’s unique and antiquated property tax system places a higher burden on rental buildings than on homeowners.

In the city, 30 percent of the income a building generates from rent goes toward property taxes, compared with 13 percent elsewhere in the country, according to the Citizens Budget Commission, a nonprofit fiscal watchdog.

As a result, the group said in a 2022 report that “nearly all new multifamily rental development requires some form of subsidy to be financially feasible.”

Some officials say that instead of reviving some version of 421a, the whole system needs an overhaul. They argue that homeowners, including condo and co-op apartment owners, should pay much larger amounts. Brad Lander, the New York City comptroller, said in a 2022 report that rentals are typically taxed at twice the rate of condos and co-ops.

But experts generally agree that a major legislative change would be an even greater political challenge than creating a new tax exemption.
