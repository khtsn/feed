Kristi Got Her Gun

Carlos Lozada

Kristi Noem did herself no favors with “No Going Back,” her new book featuring a fake story about a dictator and a true story about a dog. But she did the rest of us a big one.

I’m truly grateful to Noem for writing — or at least publishing — this book. Political memoirs usually feature wide margins, large fonts and low stakes, so it is a rarity to witness someone so willfully violating what Politico’s Michael Schaffer calls the first law of the genre: Do no harm. (If Noem’s mistake was a solar eclipse, she achieved totality.)

But the book by the South Dakota governor and presumably erstwhile contender to be Donald Trump’s running mate is worth reading in full for two additional reasons. It shows how the demands of political fealty can lead to career self-immolation; and it proves, yet again, how moments that are supposedly “taken out of context” are often worsened, not mitigated, when viewed in their full splendor.

Only Trump can decide whether Noem’s slaying of a dog named Cricket also killed off the governor’s vice-presidential hopes. But that anecdote, which has generated an enormous amount of attention, takes up only five pages in a 260-page book. Reading the rest of it makes clear not only why Noem killed the dog, but why she chose to share the story — and why she belongs nowhere near the vice presidency.

The short version of the tale, for those oblivious or enlightened enough to have ignored it, is that many years ago, Noem killed an unruly 14-month-old wirehair pointer named Cricket, after the dog wrecked a hunting party the future governor was hosting. Cricket compounded the offense by leaping from Noem’s pickup truck on the way home and murdering several chickens belonging to a neighbor. In return, Noem shot the dog dead in a gravel pit.

The facts are unsettling enough, but most telling is what Noem discloses about her motives and mind-set. The visiting hunters were longtime friends of hers, people who traveled from Georgia to South Dakota annually for the chance to bag some pheasants at her hunting lodge. This was the kind of gentlemanly pursuit in which birds are strategically guided to a convenient area for ease of slaughter. “I wanted these guys to have an amazing amount of success on their final morning of hunting,” Noem explains. But when an over-excited Cricket dashed ahead of her better-trained canine comrades and riled up the pheasants before the visitors were in range, “the hunt was ruined,” Noem writes. “I was livid.”

In short, Cricket’s antics embarrassed Noem in front of her friends, and that made her angry.

It also made her reckless. The chicken massacre was possible only because of a foolish decision Noem made right after the hunt. “As I loaded the dogs and supplies, I realized I was one kennel short,” she writes. “No matter. I would just let Cricket ride loose in the back end of the truck on the way home. If she was dumb enough to jump out, then good riddance.” In other words, the fowl play was as much Noem’s fault as the dog’s. After Noem reimbursed the distraught chicken owners and returned to the truck — where Cricket was waiting in the passenger seat like a good doggy — she was seething. “I hated that dog,” Noem writes. Curtains for Cricket.

Speaking on CBS News’s “Face the Nation,” Noem explained that she included this story in her book because she wanted to show how she makes tough choices. “People need to understand who I am, and some of those difficult decisions,” she told Margaret Brennan, the show’s moderator. Now we all know how Kristi Noem made this difficult decision: out of embarrassment, carelessness and anger.

Fresh off killing Cricket, Noem spotted the family’s cantankerous billy goat. “The goat had been a problem for years,” she notes. So Noem dragged it to the same gravel pit, tied it to a post, and shot it dead. “While taking care of unpleasant business,” the governor explains, “I decided now was as good a time as any to dispose of this problem, too.”

Now is also as good a time as any to note that this entire saga appears in the middle of a chapter devoted to Noem’s expertise and beliefs on foreign policy and national security. Immediately after describing the animal shootings, the governor outlines the Noem Doctrine, which includes principles like “peace through strength,” “fight to win,” and “our enemies should fear us, and we must give them reason to.” Much has been made of the meeting Noem recounts with North Korean dictator Kim Jong-un — an encounter that never happened — but it is the tale of the shootings that should give readers pause concerning Noem’s instincts and leadership on foreign policy.

“As soon as this was brought to my attention, I certainly made some changes,” Noem has said of the North Korea farce, promising to excise the relevant lines from future editions. You can edit the fabrications out of a book, but you can’t take back a gunshot. Noem invokes the memory of Ronald Reagan in this chapter, but if the story of Cricket and the billy goat is meant to showcase her decision making, then I’d say her impulses are less Reagan than George W. Bush, who followed up his invasion of Afghanistan by going after Iraq. After all, it was right there. And Saddam Hussein had been a problem for years.

Noem gave people plenty of reason to fear her. Construction workers at her house witnessed her going back and forth to the pit with the animals and heard the shots, and Noem recalls the “looks of shocked amazement on their faces.” Noem’s uncle, who doubled as her general contractor, questioned her that evening. “What got into you today?” He told her that the crew had “hurried back to work before you decided they were next!”

Noem displays enough self-awareness in the book to acknowledge that “I guess, if I were a better politician I wouldn’t tell the story here.” I guess so, too. So why did she?

The charitable answer is that Noem hoped to project decisiveness — to prove, as she puts it, that she’s a “doer” and not just a talker. In another damage-control interview, she told “CBS Mornings” that “the purpose of telling the story was so that people would know I don’t pass my responsibilities on to anybody else.”

Noem certainly shot Cricket on her own, but other passages suggest some pettier motivations. For example, Noem uses the Cricket story to needle President Biden about his own dog, Commander, who was removed from the White House after biting Secret Service agents. “A dog who bites is dangerous and unpredictable (are you listening, Joe Biden?),” Noem writes. She reiterates the point in her final chapter when, in a burst of optimism, Noem lists her Day 1 priorities should she become president in 2025. “The first thing I’d do is make sure Joe Biden’s dog was nowhere on the grounds (‘Commander, say hello to Cricket for me’).”

The latter line doesn’t even really work as a burn (an outgoing president is unlikely to leave his pet behind on the South Lawn), but such simplistic trolling has become an essential part of the faux-tough-guy, vice-signaling discourse of the Trump era. It’s a tone Noem valiantly tries to emulate. “To me, Donald Trump talks like normal people talk, like my friends and neighbors talk — not like people in D.C. talk,” Noem gushes. Even while she lapses into political clichés — “our best days are ahead” and such — she mimics Trump’s attitude, mocking the “bastards” and “haters” and lamenting the Washington “swamp,” all while extolling the former president’s “renegade spirit.”

There are moments in “No Going Back” when Noem sounds like a reasonable leader, even offering some of the generic bipartisanship Washington pretends to long for. She notes that Tom Daschle, the South Dakota Democrat and former Senate majority leader, once urged her to seek public office, and that her early negative opinions of Democrats were lessened once she worked with them. “Healthy debate happened, education happened, and respect was forged.” (It’s passive voice, but it’s something.) And the brief passages recalling her loneliness upon moving to Washington as a member of Congress in 2011 are among the more humanizing moments in the memoir. “I felt completely alone, because nobody there really knew me or cared about me,” she writes. “No one there grew up with me or had shared memories.”

But Noem also revels in smallness and contradiction. She describes how she got back at Minnesota’s governor, Tim Walz, for criticizing her Covid policies. Noem directed “significant portions of our business recruitment ad budget” to erecting billboards in the neighboring state, including near the governor’s mansion where Walz would see them. (“Move to South Dakota for freedom,” they read.) “Just the thought of it still makes me chuckle,” Noem writes, because spending taxpayer funds to trigger the libs is hilarious.

And shortly after arguing that female politicians should support each other (“I find it frustrating — no, disgusting — when insecure women compete with each other rather than help each other”), Noem complains, rather dubiously, that Nikki Haley, who served in the Trump administration as U.N. ambassador but later challenged him in the Republican primary, once threatened her during a phone call. Noem highlights moments when Haley had condemned, and then supported, Trump, concluding that “you never know who she’s going to be tomorrow.”

There is an audience-of-one feel to “No Going Back.” Noem is not just channeling Trump but auditioning for him, proving her devotion. “Trump always was, and still is, a fighter,” she writes. “And someone fighting for you inspires loyalty.” In the aftermath of the Cricket controversy, Noem has likened herself to the former president, accusing the news media of turning on her and spinning her story. “They’re doing the same thing to me that they do to Donald Trump every day,” she told Brennan.

It seems unlikely now, but maybe Trump will decide he wants Noem as his No. 2 despite the Cricket story, or even because of it. (This is the same guy who crowed that the ISIS leader, Abu Bakr al-Bahgdadi, “died like a dog” in a 2019 U.S. raid.) But if not, Noem has only herself to blame for her fate. “So many of the problems we face as conservatives are self-inflicted,” she writes. “Let’s stop shooting our mouths off and stop shooting ourselves in the foot. Sure, we can blame the media or liberals, but the truth is we need to be smarter, speak clearly and tell the truth.”

It’s good advice. Someone should bring it to Noem’s attention.
