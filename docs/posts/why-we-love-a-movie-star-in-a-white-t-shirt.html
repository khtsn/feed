Ellen Mirojnick

Ms. Mirojnick, the costume designer for “Oppenheimer” and “Bridgerton,” has outfitted Keanu Reeves, Glenn Close and others in white T-shirts.

  • Aug. 23, 2024

This is the first installment of Visual Studies, a series that explores how images move through and shape culture.

I consider the white T-shirt to be a star maker.

Whether it’s on Marlon Brando or James Dean, Glenn Close or Glen Powell, we’ve seen how a white T-shirt at just the right moment can burn its way into an audience’s memory — as happened this summer when a showstopping scene featuring Mr. Powell in a wet white T-shirt in “Twisters” became a sensation.

What’s more, the white T-shirt onscreen and on celebrities has become a symbol of how Americans want to imagine ourselves: unfussy, hardworking, laid-back and egalitarian.

As a costume designer, I see the white T-shirt as the ultimate blank canvas. I’ve cut up white tees to define a character’s unique style; I’ve used their simplicity to accentuate the silhouette of a suit. I always start by thinking: Will I find a way to create an iconic moment, as so many white T-shirts from cinema’s past have done?

It’s the perfect garment for the heroic figure who bears our dreams and aspirations. Its simplicity and adaptability make it a powerful tool, communicating understated sexiness and absolute coolness, a combination that’s birthed many a cinematic legend.

From a humble undergarment to a cultural staple, the white tee and its meanings continue to evolve.

In 1951, Marlon Brando in “A Streetcar Named Desire” wore a tight white T-shirt that symbolized his raw masculinity and physical strength, adding a layer of sexual tension and power to the character​​.

In “Rebel Without a Cause” in 1955, James Dean’s white T identifies his character as an outsider and a nonconformist. The white T-shirt became a symbol for defiance and a desire for change.

What started as a basic undergarment favored by laborers and sailors dating back to before World War I evolved on film into a powerful symbol with a rich history and a potent cultural significance …

… and by the mid-1950s and into the 1960s, the white T-shirt, seen here on Paul Newman at an acting class in 1955, had become a cultural phenomenon.

It could accommodate different ideologies and ascendant philosophies, whether on the Beat poet Gregory Corso in 1959 …

… or on Harry Belafonte in 1957. Countercultural movements embraced the white T-shirt as a symbol of change, freedom and equality.

Onscreen, a white T could elevate simplicity to a seductive tool, as on Brigitte Bardot in “A Very Private Affair” in 1962.

Offscreen, it was a symbol of American leisure and the approachable Everyman, from John F. Kennedy in 1953 …

… to Ronald Reagan in 1945.

As a costume designer, I’ve depended on this versatile garment in large part because of its adaptability. Over my career I’ve used the white T to telegraph a mood …

… Glenn Close in 1987’s “Fatal Attraction,” as she sinks into darkness …

… and Keanu Reeves in 1994’s “Speed,” to show a man on his day off who’s pulled in to an action-packed adventure.

A white tee is the rare garment that allows you to outfit a character in a way that’s not distracting but which can still define the narrative.

Is the character aggressive and edgy, like Angelina Jolie in “Girl, Interrupted” in 1999?

Or understated and cool, like Denzel Washington in “The Book of Eli” in 2010?

In 1999’s “Fight Club,” Brad Pitt and Edward Norton demonstrated how the garment can be read as either rebellious or conformist, depending on the character who’s wearing it.

If the wearer is intimidating, a white T-shirt can communicate power.

But it’s also a perfect garment to signal nonchalance, as on Jeff Bridges in “The Big Lebowski” in 1998.

One element is always consistent with the white tee: It’s a statement piece, well suited to the all-around hero.

Which is why it continues to be a star-making item — for Jeremy Allen White in “The Bear” …

… and for Glen Powell in “Twisters,” in a scene that was almost cut from the movie, before people in the editing room fought to save it.

What started as a signal of cultural rebellion has become a cinematic symbol of seduction — and a means to instantly communicate to any audience a brand of classic American cool, as with Tom Cruise in “Top Gun: Maverick.”

The white T-shirt is the garment that’s most like the blank screen of a cinema: We can project on it whatever our dreams might be.

After decades, it still tells stories that everyone wants to live.

Ellen Mirojnick is a costume designer who worked on “Oppenheimer,” “Bridgerton,” “Wall Street” and “Fatal Attraction” and other films.
