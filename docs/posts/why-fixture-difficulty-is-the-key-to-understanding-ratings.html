Why fixture difficulty is the key to understanding ratings

Every sports fan has seen or heard a player get discredited for performing against "plumbers" or "farmers."

That is as true in Counter-Strike as it is anywhere else, as statistics are used as ammunition by fans of one player against another. Metrics like rating are a key part of this, but on its own, it is more of a conversation starter than a be-all and end-all.

Rating 3.0 added plenty of context to the conversation; the average rating by role and playstyle is far closer, and eco frags are punished.

But there are always going to be additional factors, the most important of which is the level of their own team and that of their opposition. A player who is a permanent underdog at Big Events cannot be fairly compared to a third star on a top team with far higher round win percentages.

btrams has already looked at ratings in close matches, while as3455 has put emphasis on your 'win share' within a team and beat me to the punch by posting his own version of adjusted rating, but there's still plenty more to explore. To that purpose, we collected a heap of map-by-map data just before BLAST Rotterdam of players in all of CS2.

We'll start by simply looking at how players behave as the favorite. The vast majority of players, intuitively, are worse when they are the underdog.

There are exception. Mathieu "⁠ZywOo⁠" Herbaut averages a 1.41 rating when Vitality are the lower-ranked team in a series. Mind you, that's only 25 maps in the entirety of CS2 on LAN, but it still speaks to something that he was able to put up numbers like that against teams that were usually ranked number one.

With our forum hats on, we know what you want to see now: Who the biggest 'farmers' of lower-ranked opposition are, and who then fail to deliver as the underdog themselves.

Enter the deltas of players' ratings against higher and lower-ranked opposition. Blue is good, orange is bad. We've shown the top 24 players of 2025 (if they had enough maps) as well as the three best and three worst in this metric, but you can see a full list here.

To understand how to interpret this, let's go on a bit of a detour into how a player's image is created. Fans have busy lives. From viewership figures, we know that most of the games the average person watches are either in the playoffs or between two top teams. So when we look at these figures, we can start to understand why there is so much venom for players who drop off in these scenarios.

If the only time you watch Ádám "⁠torzsi⁠" Torzsás is when he's playing Vitality, no wonder you think he's a bad AWPer. Even elite players like Lotan "⁠Spinx⁠" Giladi and Ilya "⁠m0NESY⁠" Osipov show large drop-offs as underdogs, which explains why the forums can have a field day on those two as well. Even the mighty Danil "⁠donk⁠" Kryshkovets falls 0.08 rating points to a paltry 1.32.

But before you run to the comments to say that donk/ZywOo/this article is a fraud, hold on to your hats. We know that winning games leads to higher ratings, and for that, you need a good team. This data provides a jumping-off point, and the names at the bottom are the ones you expect to see, but we can go much further. A favorite might be a favorite by 25 places, or one; this metric does not account for that.

When we pivot to looking at a player's rating against similarly ranked opposition (within five ranking places, in this case), a player like Mareks "⁠YEKINDAR⁠" Gaļinskis goes from the bottom of the last chart right to the top of this one. Ihor "⁠w0nderful⁠" Zhdanov drops the most, only having a 1.01 rating against opponents ranked similarly to Natus Vincere. Clearly, there is more nuance to this than simply looking at ratings as a favorite or an underdog.

Only looking at evenly matched games has its own flaws, of course. The difference in quality within ten ranking places can vary wildly over the full 200+ team ranking. We could use ranking points instead, but there's a better way to reach our destination.

With data from over 200 players on LAN in CS2, we have tracked the featured ratings section on every player's profile. This is the easy place to check for 'frauds' as a fan: If they have a 1.20 rating overall, but under 1.00 against top 50 teams, you know they are benefitting from weak opponents.

In our sample, we clocked the average rating versus each new level of opposition as follows:

Average featured ratings

As is intuitive, tougher opposition means lower ratings, to the point that a 0.95 rating against the top five teams is above the average. That's probably even higher than it should be, because survivor bias means we are only looking at players who were good enough to play a lot of maps against top teams without getting kicked.

Even without survivor bias though, we need to take an extra step. One player's sample in the same bucket can be very different to another's; 253 of Robin "⁠ropz⁠" Kool's 436 maps against top 50 opposition were against top 10 teams (58%). Even other tier one players like Jonathan "⁠EliGE⁠" Jablonowski (45%) are miles below that fixture difficulty. Felipe "⁠insani⁠" Yuji always impresses statistically, but his result here is just 20%.

So it is clearly not fair to compare ropz's rating vs all top 50 opponents against insani's. Therefore, we must separate them out, and look at a player's rating against top 5 opponents, then against top 6-10, then 11-20, and so on.

Under this approach, the average ratings in our sample now look a bit different, as we circumvent the ropz-insani issue.

Average ratings when buckets are split up

In the 18 maps ropz had in our sample against teams ranked between 31 and 50, he averaged a 1.37 rating, making the most of his opportunity against lower-ranked opposition but only moving his rating against top 50 opponents 0.01 higher (1.14) than his one against top 30 (1.13).

Using these now separated ratings, we can make a weighted rating that sees how players do for their level of opposition. This is the result:

Players at the top of the chart have very difficult opposition. Our adjusted rating is then shown by how far right a player goes. The likes of Santino "⁠try⁠" Rigal, Benjamin "⁠blameF⁠" Bremer, and Kacper "⁠xKacpersky⁠" Gabara are very impressive for their tier, but their adjusted figures are much lower compared to the 1.20+ ratings they had before.

If we look at players that improve the most relative to their un-adjusted rating over all of CS2 on LAN, it's the likes of Helvijs "⁠broky⁠" Saukants (who jumps from the 52nd percentile to the 79th), René "⁠TeSeS⁠" Madsen (36th to 60th), and torzsi (58th to 83rd) who stand out. All three struggle as underdogs, but play top teams so much that their average ratings are unfairly low.

Notable fallers are predictably younger talents, who haven't quite taken the next step yet. Anarbileg "⁠cobrazera⁠" Uuganbayar falls from the 74th percentile to the 47th. Dmytro "⁠jambo⁠" Semera goes from the 83rd to the 63rd. This shows us how easy it is for overall ratings to favour flashy, young talent over the familiar faces we are used to seeing event after event.

We are getting there already, but there is an obvious issue if we want to go further. When we bucket together opposition, we lose a lot of the detail. Playing against a 1000-point top 1 team is clearly harder than against a fifth-placed team who got there off the back of a few decent results. So for our next metric, we're diving deeper.

For this part of the article, we collected data from all of our notable players on a map-by-map basis to see how performance changed at a granular level. From that, we can see that the average rating against the number one team is just 0.90, with it rising to 1.01 against the number ten team, a big enough jump that we are clearly losing a bit of context we could use keeping.

Nikita "⁠HeavyGod⁠" Martynenko, for example, has a decent 1.02 rating vs top 5 teams per map, but 1.17 versus the world number five, which is helping mask some of his poorer performances versus the top four.

To create a real opposition-adjusted rating (OAR) then, we need the exact quality of each opponent. This then gives us an adjusted rating for each map, based on the opponent's ranking points. A 1.75 for ZywOo against world number one, 921 ranking point, Spirit in March 2025 thus becomes a 2.03. We then repeat this process for every map.

Already, you might spot an issue. Vitality were the number two team at that point, so does ZywOo deserve that big of a buff? We thought not, so we've added another factor to the adjustment: 'relative difficulty.' So in this series, ZywOo's OAR for that map comes out as 1.88, an average of the 2.03 from opponent quality and 1.76 from relative ranking difference.

Over bigger samples, that means players on top teams have slightly lower relative-difference ratings, since they are rarely heavy underdogs against the best teams. It also means players in lower tiers are able to stand out, if they are excellent against teams of their own quality.

When we look at the all-time CS2 OAR leaderboard then, blameF jumps into the top ten at a 1.13. That's 0.08 below his LAN rating of 1.21, but recognition of the fact that his ratings against top teams tend to hold up (1.10 vs top 10, 1.14 vs top 20) even when more than 80% of his sample came playing in teams outside the top 20.

Like we alluded to at the top of this article, any advanced metric that seeks to compress a lot of context into one number has its blind spots. Maksim "⁠kyousuke⁠" Lukin is sixth here, but has a 1.07 in playoffs. Opposition quality is a large part of the picture, but it is still just one colour in a full rainbow of factors.

Evaluating players, whether it's for Prospects, the Top 20, or as a team scout, needs to take as many of those as possible into account. But any of that is impossible before you account for the quality of opposition correctly. We hope, with OAR, that we are now further along that path.
