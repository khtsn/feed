Ben Protess, Jonah E. Bromwich and Maggie Haberman

May 13, 2024

Michael D. Cohen, the do-anything fixer who once boasted of burying Donald J. Trump’s secrets and spreading his lies, took the stand at the former president’s criminal trial in Manhattan on Monday and exposed those machinations to the jury and the world.

Narrating the prosecution’s case in tell-all detail, Mr. Cohen testified that Mr. Trump in 2016 had personally directed him to pay off a porn star and had approved a dubious reimbursement plan.

“Just do it,” the former fixer recalled Mr. Trump saying about the hush-money payment to the porn star, Stormy Daniels.

After Mr. Trump had won the White House, Mr. Cohen demanded his money back, he said, and met with Mr. Trump, who approved monthly reimbursements. Then, the president-elect changed the subject to his new job, saying “This is going to be one heck of a ride in D.C.”

Over nearly five hours of testimony, Mr. Cohen painted a damning portrait of his relationship with Mr. Trump, decoded their shady vernacular and spotlighted the conduct at the center of the first criminal trial of an American president: the silencing of women who had stories of sex with Mr. Trump to tell and to sell.

The Links Between Trump and 3 Hush-Money Deals

Mr. Cohen’s star turn marked the climax of the prosecutors’ case as they formally introduced the jury to a witness who has loomed over the trial and could trigger the former president’s downfall. He brought the case’s architecture into focus, offering firsthand corroboration of what jurors have heard from other witnesses. He also wove a sprawling cast of characters into a single narrative, saying that Mr. Trump, bent on winning the presidency, had orchestrated the coverup of scandalous stories.

Mr. Cohen recalled playing an intimate role in concealing or spinning away the stories that could have torpedoed Mr. Trump’s 2016 presidential campaign. None was bigger than when Ms. Daniels shopped her account of a sexual liaison with Mr. Trump, which Mr. Cohen said posed a “catastrophic” threat.

“Total disaster,” Mr. Cohen quoted Mr. Trump saying. “Women will hate me.”

As jurors jotted copious notes, Mr. Cohen said that Mr. Trump did not confirm if Ms. Daniels’s story was true, but acknowledged knowing her and called her “a beautiful woman.” And when Mr. Cohen was unable to quash the story promptly, Mr. Trump blamed him.

“I thought you had this under control,” Mr. Cohen recalled Mr. Trump saying, drawing a scoff and a laugh from the defendant.

In a crucial passage of testimony, Mr. Cohen directly tied Mr. Trump to the hush-money deal that kept Ms. Daniels quiet. Mr. Trump, he said, initially conveyed a general instruction: “Just take care of it.”

Mr. Cohen ultimately did — paying $130,000 to Ms. Daniels, silencing her in the campaign’s critical waning days. Once Mr. Trump was in the White House, he reimbursed Mr. Cohen, and, according to prosecutors, adopted a plan to falsify records that disguised the repayment as ordinary legal expenses. The prosecutors, who are expected to conclude their case this week, charged the former president with 34 felony counts of falsifying business records: 11 checks, 11 invoices and 12 entries in Mr. Trump’s ledger, none of which reflected the true nature of the reimbursement.

On Tuesday, Mr. Cohen will return to the stand and is likely to testify about an encounter with Mr. Trump in the Oval Office, where prosecutors say the two men confirmed the plan to disguise the payments. Soon afterward, Mr. Cohen began to receive the checks.

On the stand Monday, Mr. Cohen recounted how he had conspired with Mr. Trump and the longtime publisher of The National Enquirer, David Pecker, to protect the campaign.

They plotted, Mr. Cohen said, to promote flattering stories and suppress damaging ones through so-called catch-and-kill deals, the tabloid practice of buying the rights to a story and withholding it. One woman, Karen McDougal, a former Playboy model, accepted $150,000 from The Enquirer to keep quiet about her story of an affair with Mr. Trump, a deal Mr. Cohen helped arrange.

Mr. Cohen’s loyalty disintegrated in 2018 as federal authorities scrutinized his involvement in the hush-money deals, and Mr. Trump shunned him. But until then, Mr. Cohen told jurors, he adopted a take-no-prisoners approach to Mr. Trump’s dirty work, bullying enemies with single-minded servitude.

“The only thing that was on my mind was to accomplish the task, to make him happy,” he recalled.

He explained that he had reported his moves to Mr. Trump directly, seeking approval from the man he called “boss.”

Mr. Cohen’s testimony brought the men face to face again on Monday, an only-in-New York collision of spotlight-seeking showmen, bombastic personalities expert in operating the twin engines that propel the city: media and money. Though hailing from different worlds — Mr. Trump a real estate scion, Mr. Cohen the child of a Holocaust survivor — they were creatures of the same ecosystem, sharing a gritty argot and tough-guy personas that belied fragile egos.

Mr. Trump bestowed a sense of importance on Mr. Cohen, allowing his hangdog fixer to bask in reflected glory. In return, Mr. Cohen has said, he would bully and cajole Mr. Trump’s enemies, both real and perceived, throwing tantrums and deploying profanity as punctuation.

He also became something of an alter ego to his fame-hungry boss. Mr. Cohen learned from him, imitated him and even had Mr. Trump’s voluminous list of contacts synced into his phone for ease, he explained from the stand Monday.

But Mr. Trump steered clear of email, Mr. Cohen noted, out of fear he would create a paper trail for prosecutors. Mr. Cohen said that the boss knew “too many people who have gone down as a direct result of having emails.”

For weeks leading up to the testimony, prosecutors had inured the jury to Mr. Cohen’s outsize personality, as they elicited testimony from witnesses who had little love for the former fixer.

That move was designed to neutralize the defense’s attack on Mr. Cohen as a liar motivated by a deep-seated desire for revenge. He is also, as the defense is fond of noting, a felon: In 2018, Mr. Cohen pleaded guilty to federal crimes, some related to the hush-money payment.

Prosecutors are offering Mr. Cohen nothing. Unlike a traditional cooperating witness who trades testimony for leniency, he has already spent more than a year in federal prison. Since then, he has cast himself as an anti-Trump crusader, dedicating two books, a podcast and numerous television appearances to seeking what he once called a “way to right some of the many wrongs I committed at his behest.”

On Monday, Mr. Cohen seized the moment, delivering a steady performance that could offer him a measure of public redemption and personal revenge. But next, the defense will cross-examine him, and it could be brutal. Mr. Trump’s lawyers are expected to seize on his credibility, or, as they see it, his lack of it.

Mr. Trump, who faces probation or as long as four years in prison, has cast himself as a victim of prosecutorial overreach. He has attacked the district attorney who brought the case, Alvin L. Bragg, who watched Mr. Cohen’s testimony from the courtroom on Monday. And even as the judge has imposed a gag order that prevents Mr. Trump from attacking Mr. Cohen and other witnesses, the former president has summoned political allies to the courthouse to do his bidding for him.

On Monday, he was traveling with an even deeper entourage of Republican officials than normal, including two United States senators (J.D. Vance and Tommy Tuberville) a congresswoman (Nicole Malliotakis of Staten Island) and two state attorneys general. He also had his son, Eric Trump, who sat two rows behind his father, glaring at Mr. Cohen and posting social media attacks.

Mr. Cohen’s story on the stand included no heroes, and the prosecution did not sugarcoat it. Even Mr. Cohen appeared upset at one point: He shook his head and darted his eyes as prosecutors played a surreptitious recording he had made of a conversation with Mr. Trump about the hush-money deal with Ms. McDougal.

Mr. Cohen hammered home the recording’s significance, translating veiled messages to an apparently rapt jury. On the recording, Mr. Cohen and Mr. Trump discussed how to repay their “friend David,” a reference to Mr. Pecker.

Mr. Cohen also revealed more private conversations, recalling that Mr. Trump had remarked how “really beautiful” Ms. McDougal was. When Mr. Cohen warned that she was poised to go public, he said, Mr. Trump told him to “make sure it doesn’t get released.”

The Donald Trump Indictment, Annotated

The indictment unveiled in April 2023 centers on a hush-money deal with a porn star, but a related document alleges a broader scheme to protect Donald J. Trump’s 2016 campaign.

The bargain with Ms. Daniels, hashed out in the campaign’s final month, was less smooth. Mr. Pecker refused to pay her, putting the onus on Mr. Trump, and therefore Mr. Cohen. When Mr. Trump was slow to decide, hoping the threat would subside after Election Day, Mr. Cohen said that he used every imaginable excuse to stall Ms. Daniels’s lawyer, even the holiest of Jewish holidays — Yom Kippur, the day of atonement.

“I was following directions,” Mr. Cohen explained.

But Ms. Daniels became impatient and threatened to walk away, prompting Mr. Cohen to pay out of his own pocket. Mr. Cohen said he told Mr. Trump “immediately” once he closed the deal, an assertion backed up by phone records.

“I was doing everything that I could and more in order to protect my boss,” Mr. Cohen told the jury, “which was something I had done for a long time.”

It paid off. Mr. Trump won the election a few days later.

But Mr. Cohen lost his patience when Mr. Trump did not award him a job in Washington, and gave him what he felt was an insultingly small bonus. He conveyed his anger to the Trump Organization’s chief financial officer, Allen H. Weisselberg, who promised that Mr. Trump would boost the bonus and reimburse him for the hush money.

Mr. Weisselberg drafted a plan, memorialized in notes that prosecutors displayed in the courtroom, and then, Mr. Cohen testified, Mr. Trump blessed it.

“Did he show this document to Mr. Trump?” a prosecutor, Susan Hoffinger, asked.

“He did,” Mr. Cohen said. “He approved it.”
