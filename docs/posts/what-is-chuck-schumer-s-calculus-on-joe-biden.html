What Is Chuck Schumer’s Calculus on Joe Biden?

Michelle Cottle

July 11, 2024, 5:03 a.m. ET

Michelle Cottle

Ms. Cottle writes about national politics for Opinion and is a host of the podcast “Matter of Opinion.”

No one likes to be the bearer of bad news. And when the news is as bad as telling the president of the United States that he should rethink his re-election campaign, because the bulk of voters think he is too old for the job, vanishingly few people have the stature or spine for such a heart-to-heart.

There’s one leader who would, though, if he felt moved to intervene in the Democratic Party’s current pickle concerning President Biden. And he is the rare leader Mr. Biden might just listen to: Chuck Schumer.

We know Mr. Schumer is feeling a bit twitchy about the situation. He has privately been “signaling to donors” that he is open to the idea of an alternative nominee, according to a Wednesday evening report by Axios. And while allies of Mr. Schumer say he would never tell Mr. Biden flat out that he had to quit the race, given his respect for both the president and the enormity of such a decision, the voice of the Senate majority leader would carry disproportionate weight if he wanted to shape the president’s thinking. Mr. Schumer speaks not just for himself, but for the chamber’s Democratic caucus, to which Mr. Biden proudly belonged for so many years. The president may thumb his nose at Democrats in the perennially twitchy House. But his former Senate colleagues? They are harder to dismiss. Some of Mr. Biden’s senior advisers are headed to the Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee headquarters Thursday to huddle with members at a special caucus lunch.

So why hasn’t Mr. Schumer interceded (as far as we know)? I’ve been talking to people who know him well, rereading his 2007 book on politics, going through old interviews and profiles. My sense is that he is not averse to weighing in — the man is hardly a fragile flower. But Mr. Schumer’s political career suggests he moves strategically and with an endgame in mind. He is not one to rush ahead, certainly not without a plan and a mandate from his caucus. His leadership style relies on consensus building, and as yet there is nothing approaching consensus among Democrats about whether Mr. Biden should be pressured to leave the race.

Mr. Schumer knows the political landscape. He has chaired the Senate Democrats’ campaign committee and understands the electorate in battleground states. He keeps close tabs on his troops’ political needs and vulnerabilities. (He has put some minutes on that old flip phone.) And unless Mr. Schumer is pretty darn confident that a ticket led by Mr. Biden would be more disastrous than one led by Kamala Harris, or possibly a nominee to be named later, he is unlikely to change his public stance. “I’m with Joe,” he stressed repeatedly at a news conference Tuesday.

Mr. Schumer has worked with Mr. Biden for three decades. In the mid-1990s, while still in the House, Mr. Schumer joined forces with Mr. Biden to pass the Brady gun control bill and the assault weapons ban. “That’s how we really got to know each other, bonded over those kinds of bills,” Mr. Schumer told a reporter in 2020.

Elected to the Senate in 1998, Mr. Schumer served about 10 years with Mr. Biden, including working together on the Judiciary Committee. As vice president, Mr. Biden was the Obama administration’s point man for tricky negotiations with the Senate. As the Democratic leader, Mr. Schumer has been a key to helping Mr. Biden get his legislative agenda enacted and his judicial picks approved. At a 2013 luncheon at the White House, Mr. Biden toasted his “pal” Mr. Schumer, noting they were “cut from the same cloth.”

Obviously, the Senate leader is not guided primarily by personal affection or preferences. “Chuck is a pragmatist,” Howard Wolfson, a longtime Democratic strategist who worked on Mr. Schumer’s first Senate campaign, told me. “He operates in an evidence-based world that relies on data and facts rather than hope and intention.”

Like much of the party, Mr. Schumer is assessing whether Mr. Biden’s troubled candidacy is likely to cost Democrats control of the Senate in November. He is looking through the polls and listening to the caucus. If his members, especially those in tough re-election fights, start telling him that voter doubts about the president will spill over to tank their campaigns, the calculus will shift for Mr. Schumer. Ditto if polling indicates that Mr. Biden will be a much bigger drag on the ticket than, say, Ms. Harris.

“He’s in a job as caucus chair where he has to lead without being too far out in front,” said Mr. Wolfson. “He has done a really good job of keeping the Senate Democrats together, and that’s because he finds the center of gravity in the caucus and leads from there.”

“Obviously he’s shaping the opinions of his members. I think they trust him and his judgment,” continued Mr. Wolfson. But he’s also “reflecting” their views. “So it’s a balancing act.”

So far, the Senate has stayed quieter than the House about Mr. Biden’s viability, at least publicly. But the president has done little to settle nerves, and the situation is highly fluid. On Tuesday, Michael Bennet of Colorado became the first Senate Democrat to publicly warn that Mr. Biden, and possibly the entire party, was headed for a thumping. “Donald Trump is on track, I think, to win this election, and maybe win it by a landslide, and take with him the Senate and the House,” he told CNN. On Wednesday, Vermont’s Peter Welch went a step further, becoming the first in the caucus to call explicitly for Mr. Biden to withdraw from the race.

Mr. Schumer recognizes that to push aside a president from one’s own party is no small matter. In modern American politics, two incumbents struggled badly enough to forego another run: Harry Truman in 1952 and Lyndon Johnson in 1968. As it happens, Mr. Schumer’s introduction to politics came in that 1968 race, when he “caught the bug” as a Harvard undergraduate working on the presidential primary campaign of Senator Eugene McCarthy, who challenged L.B.J. with an anti-Vietnam War message. “It was the most exhilarating feeling I had ever experienced,” to be part of a scrappy movement working to stop an unjust war and defeat “the most powerful man on earth,” he wrote in his book, “Positively American.”

The venture was not wholly successful, of course. The McCarthy campaign did help dissuade Mr. Johnson from running again. But Mr. McCarthy lost the Democratic nomination to Vice President Hubert Humphrey, who in turn lost the election to Richard Nixon.

Generally speaking, the Senate is not known as a hotbed of risk taking. Few institutions fetishize tradition and protocol more than the hidebound upper chamber. And Mr. Schumer’s party tends to be cautious about disrupting the regular course of things — about upending structures and processes or overthrowing the old guard. Democratic lawmakers still put a premium on seniority and on paying one’s dues. More broadly, the party has not experienced the equivalent of the Tea Party or the MAGA revolutions that swept through the Republican Party in recent years — or, going back farther, the Gingrich revolution of 1994. Democrats don’t much like to break stuff. That’s not how they roll.

This impulse has its upsides. But the warning lights are flashing red that too many voters have lost faith in Mr. Biden, for whatever mix of reasons. And if Democrats truly think another Trump term poses a mortal threat to American democracy, it sure seems time for someone to step up and break that emergency glass. “It’s not a question about politics. It’s a moral question about the future of our country,” said Mr. Bennet of the current mess.

Whether Mr. Schumer can bring himself to pressure test that moral question with his old pal the president may prove to be his biggest leadership test yet.
