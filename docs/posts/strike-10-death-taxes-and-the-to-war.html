Strike #10: Death, taxes and the TO war

Strike is a (not so) regular column written by HLTV Editor-in-chief Milan "Striker" Švejda, which mainly focuses o new realities of a Valve-regulated circuit.

Disclaimer: I've been hired by PGL for talent work in the past and for two future tournaments. The tournament organizer will feature heavily in this column, and although nothing in our agreement prevents me from speaking my mind freely, it's my ethical duty to share this for transparency.

The tournament organizer war is inevitable. No matter how many times it goes on hold, it always ends up quietly bubbling in the background until it starts anew, and on and on it goes.

Most recently, the war went dormant after ESL fended off Flashpoint (shoutout that one time) at the beginning of COVID to bring about the BLAST-ESL duopoly, but that has once again changed after Valve put a stop to the partnership era.

PGL's re-entry into the tier one circuit in 2025 threw a third player into the ring for the first time in over four years. Although its first punches were light and often aimed in the wrong direction (occasionally at its own face), the Romanian TO bided its time until it was ready to deliver one massive knockout blow.

Until now, it had been firmly third on the pecking order, well behind ESL and BLAST, who had been quick to give the biggest organizations in the world what they wanted in the wake of the partnership era's demise — their share of the pie.

Resistant to give into what it no doubt considered strong-arming, even if I could just as well call it good business, PGL stood its ground at first to its detriment. Ultimately, though, the company and its resolute-bordering-on-stubborn CEO, Silviu Stroie, had to accept the game for what it is if it was to improve its position. And give in it did.

That decision had already done the company a world of good, as you can see from the improving team lists compared to the beginning of 2025 up to the likes of PGL Cluj-Napoca and PGL Astana this year, which outmatched even those of competing events from ESL in the Pro League and IEM Atlanta.

However, it was not enough to set it firmly at the top, as those two events just happened to be better positioned on the calendar and geographically. To do that, PGL needed to provide the big teams a better overall package and choose a better target than ESL, who were always going to be less likely to fall out of the teams' good graces thanks to their legacy and connection to the lucrative Esports World Cup incentives.

So it turned its attention to BLAST from 2027 and struck hard, removing any chance of peaceful co-existence as it created an overlap between four out of their six events scheduled for the year.

And with the new battleground set, it was time to bring out the big guns: a package unlike anything PGL had invested into the scene ever before, an $11 million per year guaranteed payout that not only rivals BLAST and ESL's programs, it outmatches them both.

ESL's has essentially remained the same from the previous year at a $10.45 million guarantee (plus the potential Grand Slams for $1 million each), split over one additional event compared to the others, and BLAST mustered an increase to $10 million from $8.5.

It was a distinct change of tune from a PGL that had been so resistant to the teams' demands in the past, with the addition of both a year-long rev share program, all too similar to that of ESL, and flat acceptance fees, which BLAST had in its program in 2025 and reintroduced for 2027.

Although some in the cabal were hesitant and were hoping to remain with the tried and tested BLAST over the notoriously rogue PGL, I hear, it convinced enough of the teams involved for the big conglomerate to prioritize PGL events in the future over BLAST, reversing their positions compared to the past two years.

Judging by BLAST's circuit announcement, it has accepted its fate for now. No year-long incentive is a clear sign that it won't be able to hold the best teams down for enough of their events, and that it doesn't want to waste that money on teams that need no persuading to come, instead utilizing it to maximize the potential of each individual event on its own merit.

From the same position, PGL picked up the stragglers in past tournaments and occasionally took away a top team or two when attending a competing event wasn't in their best interest, and an offering like that will help BLAST do the same, even if it'll lose out for the year as a whole.

So, the battle for 2027 is over.

However, like PGL in these last two years, I expect BLAST to now bide its time as it gets a taste of what it's like to be third in line for a year or two and awaits the opportune moment for a counter-strike, in the next phase of the tournament organizer war.
