The Divorce Crusader

She didn’t have all the details yet, but Adina Sash saw her chance to pounce.

Sash, an Orthodox Jew who goes by Flatbush Girl on social media, received one Instagram message after another that day in March. A man calling himself Nathan Gettisberg had appeared on a popular podcast to discuss Jewish divorce. Several viewers had recognized him and knew he was using a pseudonym.

His real name, they told Sash, was Raphi Stein, and he was involved in an acrimonious separation. He had not granted his wife a get, they believed — the religious divorce that many Orthodox Jews consider more significant than a civil one.

Sash, 38, is an outspoken advocate on behalf of agunot (literally, “chained women”). Here was a man — by Sash’s reckoning a get refuser — politely chatting about Jewish divorce in front of one of the biggest audiences imaginable for this community.

It was “sadistic and hubristic,” Sash remembered thinking.

So she sprang into action. She didn’t try to contact Stein’s wife, Adeena Kohn. Nor did she reach out to Stein. Instead, she began to post condemnations of the podcast network, Living L’Chaim, and to probe her 100,000 followers for information about the case.

“I didn’t even know her name was Adeena,” Sash said. “I only found it out a day after I went public.” Soon, Sash heard from a close friend of Kohn’s, who explained that the woman wanted to remain private.

For Sash, this was no problem. For years, she had created campaigns on behalf of agunot, sometimes getting paid for her work. But in this case, with attention already focused on Stein and with Kohn not participating, Sash decided to crowdsource funds for a fight. (Through a representative, Kohn declined to comment for this article.)

At first, she tried her usual tactics: shaming social media posts, education about get refusal. But after the podcast host apologized in mid-April for hosting Stein, people started to tune out.

“The views started to tank,” Sash said.

She knew she had to do something shocking. In a call-out on Instagram, she asked Jewish women to show their “erva,” their nakedness, to make a point about the way Jewish divorce could be abused to control women’s bodies.

In early May, the forbidden flesh began to appear on Sash’s Instagram page. There were breasts and hips; navels and buttocks; shoulders and thighs; an elbow and even a pinkie — all prohibited by Jewish religious laws pertaining to physical modesty.

The images, of about 200 women, became part of her Instagram campaign, “Gett Naked.” The pictures raced from Sash’s Instagram page through the WhatsApp groups where Orthodox Jews gather and gossip online, igniting countless debates over whether Sash and the women — many of whom were ultra-Orthodox, according to Sash — had gone too far.

“Attention is not the same thing as justice,” the Orthodox educator Talia Avrahami wrote in The Times of Israel. “A tactic does not become Orthodox simply because the cause behind it is sympathetic.”

Sash is a hypermodern figure produced by a traditional world, who has figured out how to turn an ancient religious legal concept into an online cause célèbre. She combines the passionate intensity of an activist with the attention economy savvy of an influencer. And she brings that combination of stridency and digital spectacle to bear on an ultra-Orthodox world that uses shame as a powerful tool of coercion, and in which women have very little power.

The clamor Sash generates makes many people uncomfortable — not least because the secular world pays attention to the ultra-Orthodox mostly when something goes wrong. And some in Sash’s community criticize her as a kind of moral entrepreneur, who turns a painful issue for an insular community into an online spectacle for her own benefit.

“Sometimes really challenging situations require really bold actions,” said Jennifer Lankin, the chief executive of the Organization for the Resolution of Agunot, a New York nonprofit started in 2002, which also advocates for agunot. “But the approach also matters.”

The question raised by Adina Sash, then, is an ancient one: When do the ends justify the means?

Sash was raised in Flatbush, in an Orthodox community, where she received mixed messages about how deferential she should be to tradition. Her parents each had a foot placed strongly in the secular world: Her mother is a clinical psychologist at Brooklyn College who studies Jews who leave ultra-Orthodoxy, and her father teaches the ultra-Orthodox work force at B&H Photo how to acclimate to a modern work environment.

They sent her to a school for ultra-Orthodox girls, where she was expected to learn how to be, as she put it, “refined, demure, compliant and domesticated.” At the same time, Sash’s parents told her to filter out the things at school she didn’t like, and Sash had a rebellious streak.

“I am very noncompliant,” she said.

The internet made it impossible to keep Sash away from the outside world. At 10, she discovered an online message board dedicated to the science-fiction series “Animorphs” and developed friendships with non-Jews all over the country.

In 2005, shortly after her high school graduation, Sash married her husband, Chaim, because she “really just wanted to have sex,” she said.

She attended Brooklyn College and got a master’s in medieval literature before going on to work as a saleswoman at a high-end bridal boutique for Orthodox women. It was a demanding environment that required her to learn how to make the hard sell to customers with sometimes extreme or contradictory expectations. She excelled, but she didn’t like what it stood for — an unthinking embrace of what Sash called “feminine superficiality.”

In 2015, she quit and started making comedic social media content, gravitating toward women’s issues. She protested the prohibition in Orthodox media against showing women’s faces, and the rules that forbade women to sing in front of men. In 2021, a friend told Sash about an aguna named Chava Sharabani.

Sash was barely familiar with the concept of get refusal at the time, and at first she felt ambivalent about embracing it as a feminist cause. She also wasn’t sure how widespread the problem really was.

Indeed, it’s not an easy problem to measure. The Organization for the Resolution of Agunot works on about 75 active cases of long-term get refusal at any given time, and claims to have obtained gets for some 550 people. In recent years, the advent of religious prenuptial agreements has eased the issue, but the threat of get refusal hangs over any traditional marriage without such an arrangement.

As Sash thought about her own relationship, she came to the painful realization that she, too, was subject to the whims of the rabbinate and her husband.

“It’s almost like you start to feel like a prisoner in your own marriage, even if you’re happily married,” she said.

So she started to post about Sharabani. Quickly, she found that she had tapped into something people were far more eager to talk about than any of her previous campaigns.

“I had never seen those metrics before of high levels of engagement,” she said. “And I was blown away by it from a marketing psychological perspective and about women’s rights.”

Sash threw herself into the advocacy, and her husband helped her write some of her early scripts.

Chaim Sash, a marketing executive, said that some of his wife’s campaigns had made him uneasy but that his wife’s work mostly inspires him.

“I’m not that type of controlling male,” he said. “I’m so happy with how she gets to express herself and do what she loves. Even when I’m uncomfortable.”

Sash’s breakthrough moment came in March 2024, when she persuaded a group of Orthodox women to go on “mikvah strike” — to refrain from the holy bath that’s required after the end of a menstrual cycle and before sexual intercourse — until a woman named Malky Berkowitz received a get. A kind of modern “Lysistrata,” the strike attracted national media attention, as well as admonishment from Hershel Schacter, a prominent Orthodox rabbi and professor at Yeshiva University, who called it “a recipe for disaster.”

But in September, Berkowitz received her get. Today, Sash estimates that she has served as a primary advocate for about 50 agunot and has been involved with campaigns on behalf of another 50.

“I would have been screwed without Adina,” said Molly Bires, a Chicago woman who hired Sash in 2024 to help her obtain a get from her husband. “I probably still wouldn’t have a get.”

By the time of the podcast, Stein and Kohn had been separated for years. According to court records, in the summer of 2020, Adeena Kohn was admitted to the hospital to be treated for postpartum psychosis. When she was discharged, she wanted to spend some time with her mother, and Kohn, Stein, and their three young children traveled from their home in Montreal to Monsey, N.Y.

After several months, Stein — who believed the arrangement in Monsey would be temporary — began to agitate to return to Montreal, and Kohn refused. The couple’s relationship deteriorated, and they began to fight over their children’s passports, according to court documents. In October 2021, Kohn filed for divorce.

A custody dispute ensued, and the proceedings got nasty. Stein sued Kohn in federal court to have the children returned to Canada under the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction. (The judge ruled against Stein on the basis that the children were effectively settled in Monsey, though he was “quite sympathetic to Mr. Stein’s situation.”) Kohn engaged a prominent rabbinical court to summon Stein to appear and work out a get. Stein refused, saying he would not engage in a Jewish divorce until the civil case was finalized, and found a rabbi in Monsey to rule against the rabbinical court. The pair had reached an impasse.

This was still the state of affairs on a sultry night in May, as Sash arrived in an ultra-Orthodox enclave in Monsey to protest the local rabbi she said was protecting Stein from the consequences of get refusal.

“This network is what emboldens him and makes him feel immune, that there’s no consequences for what he’s doing,” she said.

As Sash unpacked her car, dozens of ultra-Orthodox children gathered across the street to gawk, prayer garments peeking out from under their polo shirts.

Parked in front of the rabbi’s house was a truck Sash had paid for through her “Free Adeena” crowdfunding campaign, equipped with large LED panels and loudspeakers. It displayed a graphic of an Egyptian pharaoh. “Raphi Stein,” it read. “Let Adeena go!” The Ramapo police, whom Sash had alerted, idled nearby.

As it began to rain, Sash, wearing a knee-length dress decorated with small colorful unicorns, marched up and down the street, screaming. Sash’s natural hair, which she leaves partially uncovered by her wig, had puffed up in the humidity. Men and women peered from the second-floor windows of their homes, silently watching her pace the sidewalk below.

“We’re standing up for a victim of religious abuse! Shame! Shame!”

More protesters arrived, as did a group of Orthodox teenagers on mopeds who were there in the hope — raised by the Gett Naked campaign — that they might see nude women.

A great din followed. There were honks from a school bus blocked by the LED truck, and honks from a convoy of S.U.V.s that appeared to be trying to block the LED truck. This was mixed with the slogans of the protesters and a prerecorded refrain from a boombox the teenagers had brought along: “Free the nipple!” And then, above it all, rose a deep male voice from the speakers on the truck.

“Five years of tears, five years of waiting for a get, enough is enough!” the recorded voice blared, before switching into Yiddish. “Achtung, Achtung!”

Sash’s advocacy doesn’t come cheap. Though she said she works on a sliding scale, her publicly listed rates include $750 for an initial consultation and $7,500 to be put on retainer. (O.R.A., by contrast, does not take a fee.)

One of the reasons Sash says she needs to charge is because she gets sued so much. In April, Raphi Stein, representing himself, sued Sash in State Supreme Court in Rockland County, accusing Sash of defaming him, doxxing him and causing him to lose his job.

Another major difference between Sash’s work and that of the legacy advocacy groups, like O.R.A., is that she sometimes works on get refusal cases without the direct involvement of the aguna herself. For the traditional advocates, this is the biggest of Sash’s no-nos.

In the case of Adeena Kohn, it’s squishy. Sash is coy about her contacts with Kohn — “I’m not in touch with her, and if I was, I wouldn’t tell you,” she said. But Stein’s suit contends that Kohn and her family provided Sash with the “the factual scaffolding of her campaign,” including personal information and photographs.

Though Sash is strategic, she also admits to making some decisions impulsively. The idea for the Gett Naked campaign, for instance, came to Sash in February, she said, when she began to take risqué photos of herself to send to her husband — a way of spicing up a 20-year marriage.

Then one morning, Sash connected the dots, just like that: The way to make more people pay attention to the Kohn case was hundreds of revealing photos.

“It was just a jolt of inspiration, and from conception to posting it publicly was very impulsive and a matter of minutes,” she said.

It was clear from the messages accompanying the photos that Sash had unleashed a torrent of repressed emotions. There were former agunot, wives of rabbis who were furious at their husbands for ignoring the problem, and threats to expose ultra-Orthodox men as they walked out of strip clubs. In some ways, the women seemed to be angry not just at the get refusers, but at the entire gendered organization of Orthodox Judaism itself, in which men and women sit separately at prayer.

Back in Monsey, the sky had darkened and the rain was coming down harder. The Orthodox teenagers had moved to confront some of the protesters, arguing about Sash’s motives, whether the entire campaign was just being done for attention, whether Adeena Kohn was to blame too — after all, there were two sides to every story.

Between chants, Sash distributed her signs to some of the leering teens. If they were there for ignoble reasons, she could at least make it look in her Instagram stories as if they were down for her cause. (As of this writing, Sash’s Instagram account is down, which she says is the result of the mass reporting of her content by opponents in the ultra-Orthodox community.)

Nearby, a young man in a yarmulke and a green T-shirt that depicted George Washington wearing modern military armor and holding a rifle, with a misogynistic slogan about the American Revolution, was debating a series of women. Dozens of spectators were filming it. Exasperated, one protester broached a sexual nuclear option: Maybe Jewish women, oppressed by get refusal, would marry non-Jewish men, she suggested. After all, their children would be Jewish, according to religious law.

“Why would I not protect myself?” she said.

The spectacle felt powerful and slightly dangerous, as if Sash had set in motion forces that she couldn’t quite control.

Then, in early June, word came that the civil divorce between Adeena Kohn and Raphi Stein was about to be settled. And Stein had given Kohn her get.

In an email, Stein insisted that Flatbush Girl’s campaign against him had nothing to do with the settlement.

“Contrary to her claims of helping ‘agunas,’ her activities helped strengthen my negotiating position,” he said. He was going to continue going after Sash in court.

But soon after the get, Sash received an Instagram message from Ahuva Kohn, Adeena’s sister.

“Your methods were unorthodox to say the least but you made your point because you got her her get,” she wrote. “Thank you for rescuing my sister.”
