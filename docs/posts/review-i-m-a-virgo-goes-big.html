Review: ‘I’m a Virgo’ Goes Big

Critic’s Pick

Boots Riley’s surreal satire about a teenage giant and an authoritarian superhero is a larger-than-life achievement.

June 22, 2023, 10:00 a.m. ET

I’m a Virgo
NYT Critic's Pick

Boots Riley doesn’t aim small. In his 2018 directorial debut film, “Sorry to Bother You,” the frontman of the revolution-minded hip-hop group the Coup created a chaotic fantasia of corporate exploitation that strained at the seams of its indie-movie clothing.

In his magnificently batty TV series, “I’m a Virgo,” which arrives on Amazon Prime Video on Friday, the screen may be smaller. But Riley’s satirical ambitions are 13 feet tall.

That’s also the height of his protagonist, Cootie (Jharrel Jerome), an Oakland teenager whose aunt and uncle (Carmen Ejogo and Mike Epps) have kept him hidden to protect him from a world that will see him as a monster. “People are always afraid,” his aunt says, “and you are a 13-foot-tall Black man.”

As he approaches adulthood, the walls and door frames of the house can’t take the arrangement much longer. Neither can Cootie, who longs to see a world that he’s experienced only through TV commercials and comic books. He sneaks out, makes friends with some young neighbors — they take him on a joyride, Cootie squatting on the car as if it were a skateboard — and meets the world.

As in “Sorry to Bother You,” that world is a surreal, capitalist-dystopian version of our own, equal parts magic realism and B-movie sci-fi. The airwaves are filled with disturbing fast-food ads and a cult-hit cartoon, a forbidden episode of which puts viewers into a catatonic state of existential despair. Characters are matter-of-factly given supernatural powers, including Cootie’s crush, Flora (Olivia Washington), who has super-speed abilities. Like Cootie, she has to modulate her physicality, forcing herself to move at a painstakingly slow pace in order to engage with the regular-speed world.

Riley makes Cootie’s situation feel claustrophobically real using practical effects, including forced perspective, which give the series a more handmade, approachable feel compared with today’s sleek, cold C.G.I. showcases. Cootie hunches into buildings, drinks beer from pitchers, holds envelopes and phones with his fingers like they were doll accessories. (There’s also a sweet, comic scene exploring the logistics of losing one’s virginity with a partner less than half one’s size.)

Cootie’s towering mass is also an idea: It’s a larger-than-life manifestation of the way society perceives Black youths as dangers. Black boys, for instance, are routinely seen as being older than their actual age.

Jerome, who played one of the wrongfully convicted Central Park Five in “When They See Us,” makes Cootie’s effort to manage his body touchingly real. He has a formal, shy, sincere manner, polite and cautious, as if to efface his size and menace. He also has a sense of wonder, as when a friend turns up his stereo and Cootie experiences bass for the first time in his sheltered life: “It sinks to your bones and you can feel the ground and the sky at the same time,” he says. In his neighborhood, he’s received less as a freak than as a friendly giant.

But he eventually draws the scrutiny of the Hero (a perfectly unbalanced Walton Goggins), a billionaire comic-book creator who has taken on the world-weary vigilante persona of one of his own characters. Wearing a goofy white supersuit and a propeller backpack, he flies around the Bay Area nabbing minor offenders and quelling protests like a one-man surveillance state. (As Riley put it in the Coup’s “The Guillotine”: “We got hella people/They got helicopters.”)

You could connect the Hero to comic-book capitalists like Batman and Iron Man — and just maybe to the real-life mogul, with a predilection for spaceflight and whirlybird drones, whose company bankrolls this series. “Virgo,” in its spirit of biff-bam-pow anticapitalism, is broadly concerned with co-optation, with selling out, with whether the master’s gadget-toolbelt can ever dismantle the master’s secret lair.

The ability to make us root for the overdog, after all, is one of the super-est superpowers that pop culture has. (This is also the relentless theme of the series’s Amazon authoritarian-superhero sibling, “The Boys.”) Comic books have taught Cootie to believe in justice the way his aunt taught him to believe in astrology, and he holds onto this childlike faith even while the Hero paints him as a supervillain.

Their rising conflict provides the minimal frame for the first season of “Virgo,” which at seven episodes is more about world building than plot. There’s a bit of “Invisible Man” picaresque to Cootie’s journey, which leads him to a cult that sees him as a prophesied savior and a streetwear company that hires him as a model in a racially stereotyped ad campaign. There’s also a Jonathan Swift streak of social caricature in its story of a noble giant among the little people.

But the anticapitalist fervor is 100 percent Riley. In “Sorry to Bother You,” a rising star in a telemarketing center discovers a corporate plot to create a work force of horse-human hybrids. In “Virgo,” when Cootie gets involved with an Occupy-style protest led by his activist friend Jones (Kara Young), the Hero and the media demonize him. In both cases, when humans prove inconvenient, capitalism creates monsters.

Riley is not afraid to put his agenda in boldface, triple-underlined. As the Hero puts it, describing the law-and-order themes of his comic books, “All art is propaganda.” It’s an argument that Riley has made himself in interviews: “Every movie is a message movie.”

But it speaks to Riley’s willingness to complicate his storytelling that he puts his deeply held belief in the mouth of a megalomaniac vigilante. Indeed, one of the series’s strangest but most insightful episodes, written by the playwright Michael R. Jackson (“A Strange Loop”), is a character study of the Hero that shows how righteousness can be both armor and blinder.

Riotous and urgent, “I’m a Virgo” is hardly the most subtle show of the year. It is, by its own definition, propaganda. But it is absolutely art.

James Poniewozik is The Times’s chief television critic. He writes reviews and essays with an emphasis on television as it reflects a changing culture and politics. He is also the author of “Audience of One: Donald Trump, Television and the Fracturing of America.”
