May 3, 2023Updated 12:56 p.m. ET

On the sixth trial day of the writer E. Jean Carroll’s lawsuit accusing former President Donald J. Trump of rape, her lawyers are calling witnesses to bolster her account of the attack and the aftermath.

Dr. Leslie Lebowitz, a clinical psychologist and trauma specialist who spent about 20 hours interviewing Ms. Carroll, continued on the stand Wednesday after saying the day before that Ms. Carroll has suffered from years of painful, intrusive memories and a “diminishment in how she thought and felt about herself.”

“Perhaps most prominently,” Dr. Lebowitz said, “she manifests very notable avoidance symptoms, which have curtailed her romantic and intimate life and caused profound loss.”

Among the other witnesses her team plans to call on Wednesday are Natasha Stoynoff, another woman who has accused Mr. Trump of sexual assault, and Carol Martin, a friend of Ms. Carroll’s who she says she told about the attack shortly after it happened. It was Ms. Martin who advised her not to go to the police, Ms. Carroll testified.

Ms. Carroll’s lawyer, Roberta A. Kaplan, has said that Ms. Carroll’s sister will likely testify.

Ms. Kaplan said she planned to finish presenting Ms. Carroll’s case perhaps by midday on Thursday. Mr. Trump’s lawyer, Joseph Tacopina, on Tuesday told the judge, Lewis A. Kaplan of Manhattan’s Federal District Court, that his client would not be coming to court to testify, suggesting that the trial could go to the jury early next week.

The Accusation

Ms. Carroll said the attack took place during a visit to Bergdorf Goodman one evening in the mid-1990s. As she was leaving through a revolving door, Mr. Trump entered and recognized her, she testified, and persuaded her to help him shop for a gift for a female friend. She said the former president went on to attack her in a dressing room in the lingerie department.

In the Courtroom

On Wednesday, Ms. Kaplan tried to elicit testimony from Dr. Lebowitz that would put Ms. Carroll’s behavior in the context of common responses to traumatic events.

When Mr. Tacopina cross-examined Ms. Carroll, he highlighted what appeared to be her unusual behavior during the alleged attack and her inability to remember some key details afterward.

Dr. Lebowitz testified that when a person’s brain is inundated with stress hormones during a traumatic event, the prefrontal cortex — part of the brain that is associated with logic and reasoning — shuts down and a person may “do things that appear to be illogical and irrational.”

She offered an example of a person being raped in the stacks of a public library who might not scream even though “screaming might be helpful.” The fact that Ms. Carroll did not scream during the encounter has come up several times throughout the trial.

Dr. Lebowitz also explained why people might have memory lapses about an attack; Ms. Carroll has testified that she did not remember the date of the encounter she said happened, but she remembered how she felt in that moment.

“Our brain doesn’t hold onto things that at the time didn’t seem important,” Dr. Lebowitz testified.

Ms. Kaplan also questioned Dr. Lebowitz about why Ms. Carroll refused to use the words “rape” or “victim,” and how she avoided men.

“She blamed herself for it — she felt like she was stupid in a way that was hard to shake,” Dr. Lebowitz said. But perhaps even more fundamentally than that, she said, “it made her feel like she was worth less than she had been before.”

During cross-examination, Chad Seigel, one Mr. Trump’s lawyers,

asked Dr. Lebowitz whether it would be fair to say Ms. Carroll had presented her symptoms in a way that would benefit her case. “I don’t believe she did that,” Dr. Lebowitz said.

He asked whether Ms. Carroll had a “vested interest” in the case’s result.

“She certainly cares very much about the outcome of this case,” Dr. Lebowitz replied.
