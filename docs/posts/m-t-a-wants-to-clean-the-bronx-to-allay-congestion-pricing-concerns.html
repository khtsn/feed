M.T.A. Wants to Clean the Bronx To Allay Congestion Pricing Concerns

New York|M.T.A. Plans to Use Congestion Pricing Funds to Address Bronx Pollution

https://www.nytimes.com/2023/03/28/nyregion/mta-congestion-pricing-pollution-bronx.html

Transit officials are thinking of spending millions of dollars to fight pollution in some of New York’s poorest and most contaminated neighborhoods.

March 28, 2023, 6:39 p.m. ET

Ever since the Metropolitan Transportation Authority unveiled an ambitious vehicle tolling program last year, critics have been dismayed by evidence that some of New York City’s poorest neighborhoods could end up with dirtier air from all the diverted traffic.

Now, the M.T.A. is considering spending at least $130 million it raises from tolling to help address the potential impact to those neighborhoods. The authority has presented the plan to federal transportation officials, who are also deciding whether to let it implement the new tolls.

The proposal, first reported by Streetsblog and confirmed by people familiar with the matter, includes plans to install air filtration units in some schools near highways, and to reduce overnight toll rates to encourage commercial vehicles to drive during off-peak hours.

The tolling program, known as congestion pricing, is the first of its kind in the nation and is intended to reshape the crushing flow of traffic in New York City. It would place tolls on drivers entering the busiest parts of Manhattan.

But for congestion pricing to become a reality, the authority must balance the program’s intended effect — to relieve pollution and gridlock in the city’s central business district — against federal regulations meant to ensure that the diverted traffic will not harm people from vulnerable communities.

To address those regulations, the proposed investment appears to be concentrated in the Bronx, which has some of the city’s poorest and most contaminated neighborhoods. Lawmakers and community leaders there have expressed concern over an environmental assessment released by the M.T.A. in August which showed that motorists avoiding the new tolls could add traffic and soot to parts of the Bronx, Staten Island, Nassau County on Long Island and Bergen County in New Jersey.

A spokesman for the authority did not confirm the $130 million plan but said that “we continue to work for environmental justice, while reducing pollution and traffic in the most congested area of the country.”

Through a staff member, Mayor Eric Adams said he is “taking a hard look at the historical and disproportionate impacts traffic and pollution have had on the residents of the Bronx.” The staff member said Mr. Adams has been working with the M.T.A., the New York State Department of Transportation and Ritchie Torres, a Democratic congressman from the Bronx, to “right these historic wrongs,” referring to harmful public policies that have pushed truck traffic to the Bronx and contributed to asthma at rates that have vastly outpaced those in the city and state.

One busy highway which could get more traffic as a result of the congestion pricing program is the Cross Bronx Expressway, a key artery for commercial traffic that is flanked by residential buildings holding about 220,000 Bronx residents, most of whom are people of color.

The M.T.A. has yet to decide how much to charge car and truck drivers; generally, the higher the tolls, the more vehicles are expected to avoid them. Among the options that were studied, the one with the greatest impact on the Cross Bronx would send 704 more trucks per day along it. The busy artery currently logs more than 27,000. It would increase the soot from truck traffic by about 5 percent, and overall soot — which also comes from factories, building boilers and other sources — by 1 percent, according to a New York Times analysis of M.T.A. data.

The M.T.A.’s new funding plan is intended to alleviate pollution for residents by spending $25 million on an asthma treatment program in the Bronx. It will also spend $5 million to plant vegetation along roads to improve the air quality around them. Other initiatives include renovating parks and green spaces in communities that might be impacted by congestion pricing, as well as replacing refrigeration trucks at a market in the Bronx.

The authority is awaiting approval from federal transportation officials before a panel comes up with recommendations for toll rates, including discounts, exemptions and other allowances. After that final approval will come from the M.T.A. board.

In a prepared statement, the Federal Highway Administration said that it is reviewing public input collected after the release of last year’s environmental assessment and is “diligently working toward a decision,” though no timetable was given.
