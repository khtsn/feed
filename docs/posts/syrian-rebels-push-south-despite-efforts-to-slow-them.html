Syrian Rebels Push South, Despite Efforts to Slow Them

Government forces are said to be sending more troops to reinforce their most crucial strongholds, including Damascus, the capital, and the major city of Homs.

Dec. 6, 2024Updated 10:29 a.m. ET

Syrian rebels on Friday pushed south toward the major city of Homs, capturing towns along the way, the rebels and a war monitor said, as the military scrambled to slow the rapid advance that has appeared to have taken the government by surprise.

Military forces erected earthen berms and carried out airstrikes on a major highway as the rebels moved south from Hama, a city they had taken the day before as government forces retreated.

The sudden rebel advance, launched last week by a coalition of opposition groups headed by the Islamist group Hayat Tahrir al-Sham, has abruptly changed the landscape of Syria’s 13-year-old civil war after a long stalemate.

The forces of Syria’s president, Bashar al-Assad, were reassigning from other parts of the country, according to the monitor, the Syrian Observatory for Human Rights, to reinforce the most crucial government strongholds, including Damascus, the capital, and Homs, the loss of which would be a significant blow for Mr. Assad’s government and its allies.

The rebels’ advances come at a moment of weakness for Mr. Assad’s biggest backers. The Syrian regime has relied on Iran and its ally in Lebanon, Hezbollah — in addition to Russia — to fight the rebels over the course of the civil war. Russian missile strikes to try to slow the recent rebel advance have been relatively sparse, in what analysts see as a sign of Russia’s limited ability to aid Mr. al-Assad amid its war in Ukraine.

Homs is key because it lies at the heart of a critical land corridor that runs from Lebanon to Iran, which serves as a pipeline used by Tehran to send weapons, supplies and people to Hezbollah, the armed Lebanese group that is Syria’s most important regional ally.

On Friday, the Syrian military struck rebels and their vehicles both north and south of the city of Hama with artillery, missiles and airstrikes, assisted by Russia. Dozens of opposition fighters were killed and wounded, according to Syrian state media.

Last weekend, the rebels took control of the northern city Aleppo, once a commercial hub and one of the largest cities in the country. The loss of Aleppo and Hama were major defeats for Mr. al-Assad. After withdrawing from Hama on Thursday following several days of fighting, the Syrian military issued an unusual statement explaining its pullback, saying that it was seeking to avoid battles inside the city that would endanger civilians.

But sacrificing Hama also enables the Assad government to shift its limited military resources and forces to the areas it regards as more important.

That includes Homs Province and its eponymous provincial capital.In a sign of the heightened tensions, the Russian Embassy in Damascus issued a statement warning Russians of “the difficult military and political situation in Syria.” The Embassy added that it was reminding Russians “of the possibility of leaving the country on commercial flights through operating airports.”

Israel, meanwhile, said it was reinforcing its troops in the Israeli-occupied Golan Heights, which it captured from Syria in 1967, in the wake of the rebel advance. In a statement, the military said it was “prepared for all scenarios, offensive and defensive.”

Israel has long been at odds with Mr. Assad and Iran. The potential ramifications for Israel should the conflict spill over into Israel are not clear.

Aaron Boxerman in Jerusalem and Anton Troianovski in Berlin contributed reporting.
