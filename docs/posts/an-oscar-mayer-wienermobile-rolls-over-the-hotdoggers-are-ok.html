An Oscar Mayer Wienermobile Rolls Over. The ‘Hotdoggers’ Are OK.

U.S.|An Oscar Mayer Wienermobile Rolls Over. The ‘Hotdoggers’ Are OK.

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/07/23/us/oscar-mayer-wienermobile-crash-illinois.html

No one was injured when the promotional car struck another vehicle and rolled over on a highway in Illinois, the police said.

July 23, 2024, 5:12 p.m. ET

You might expect to see litter, trash or even roadkill on the side of a highway. But a 3,600-pound hot dog? Just after 11 a.m. Monday, employees of Oscar Mayer were driving one of the company’s promotional Wienermobiles on an Illinois highway when the driver hit another car, lost control, overcorrected and rolled the vehicle on its side, the Illinois State Police said.

No one was injured in the crash, but the right northbound lane of Interstate 294 near the suburb of Oak Brook was closed for about an hour, the police said. Social media users were soon sharing images of the overturned vehicle with relish. “A wurst-case scenario,” one observed.

The company’s promotional literature describes the Wienermobile as “a grilled fiberglass hot dog that is resting on a lightly toasted fiberglass bun.” After the collision, the base of the vehicle, a yellow sedan, rested on its driver-side door in the middle of the highway, still attached to the hot-dog-shaped roof. The front fender of the car appeared to be missing and the headlights were smashed.

Video taken after the collision showed two people — “hotdoggers,” as the company calls them — climbing out of the passenger-side door and hopping down to safety. Later, a tow truck crane arrived, scooped up the Wienermobile and carried it away.

“We’re grateful that everybody involved is safe and there were no injuries,” Oscar Mayer, a Chicago-based packaged meat producer, said in a statement. “We’re offering ongoing support for our Hotdoggers, and we appreciate the support we’ve received from local authorities.”

In 1936, Carl Mayer, the nephew of the company’s founder, “came up with the idea of a 13-foot metal hot dog on wheels” as an advertising gimmick, according to the company.

Since then, the hot-dog-shaped cars have increased in size and number. The regular Wienermobile now has a 23-foot fiberglass hot dog roof. The one that crashed on Monday was the smaller version. It is 15 feet long and 8 feet tall, and called a Mini, in part because its body is a modified Mini Cooper.

Oscar Mayer hires Hotdoggers to drive Wienermobiles around, pose for pictures and hand out swag.

Dave Ihlenfeld, a comedy writer who has worked on Family Guy and Malcolm in the Middle, wrote a memoir called “Dog Days: A Year in the Oscar Mayer Wienermobile” about the year after college when he worked as a Hotdogger. He was an aimless post-grad when he signed up.

“It turned out to be the best job I’ve ever had,” Mr. Ihlenfeld said in an interview. “It’s been all downhill since the Wienermobile.”

In his book, Mr. Ihlenfeld recalls traveling the United States in the car with three other Hotdoggers in 1999, learning real-world skills and meeting all kinds of people. Oscar Mayer even sent him and his hot dog overseas to U.S. military bases in Europe, where the vehicle was called the Wurstwagon.

“Every so often, it’s like ‘Oh yeah! I learned that doing the Wienermobile,’” Mr. Ihlenfeld said. “It’s interesting to think what a different person I would be if I didn’t do this for a year.”

In 1988, a speaker system was installed in Wienermobiles to play 21 versions of the Oscar Mayer Wiener jingle — another cornerstone of the company’s branding. Mr. Ihlenfeld said Hotdoggers taught fans how to play the jingle on branded whistles so they could go home singing, “Oh, I wish I were an Oscar Mayer wiener.”
