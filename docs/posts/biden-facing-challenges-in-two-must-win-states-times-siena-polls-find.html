Adam Nagourney and Ruth Igielnik

July 15, 2024Updated 5:01 p.m. ET

President Biden is locked in a tight race with former President Donald J. Trump in Pennsylvania, a state that Mr. Biden barely won in 2020 and that is critical to his re-election hopes, and in Virginia, a state where Mr. Biden defeated Mr. Trump by 10 percentage points in 2020, according to the latest New York Times/Siena College polls.

The polls, which were completed before the assassination attempt on Mr. Trump while he was campaigning in Pennsylvania on Saturday, found that Mr. Trump was leading the president by 48 percent to 45 percent among likely voters in Pennsylvania. The results are almost unchanged from a New York Times/Siena College poll taken in May and within the margin of error.

By almost any Electoral College map calculation, it would be nearly impossible for Mr. Biden to win re-election without Pennsylvania.

Of potential greater concern for Mr. Biden and Democrats in the polls, which were conducted from July 9 through July 12, is the prospect that Virginia, a state that few strategists from either party thought would be in contention this year, appears to have turned competitive in a Trump-Biden rematch. In that state, Mr. Biden has a lead of 3 percentage points over Mr. Trump among likely voters, which is within the margin of error.

The last time a Republican won Virginia in a presidential race was 2004, when President George W. Bush beat his Democratic challenger, John F. Kerry, on his way to winning a second term in the White House.

“Joe Biden doesn’t really stand for traditional American values,” said Kendall Wood, 31, a truck driver who is an independent voter from Richmond, Va. He voted for Mr. Biden in 2020 and said he would support Mr. Trump — if he voted at all — this year. “Trump is more appealing to me simply because, again, I feel like his policies would empower me as opposed to hinder me,” he said.

“Was I better off under Trump? It feels like it,” Mr. Wood added.

The polls also found Vice President Kamala Harris, who has been mentioned by Democrats as a potential successor should Mr. Biden leave the race, in a slightly stronger position against Mr. Trump than the president. In Virginia, she is 5 percentage points ahead of Mr. Trump among likely voters, and in Pennsylvania she trails Mr. Trump by a single point. In both cases, the difference is within the margin of error.

The Times/Siena College polls, taken after Mr. Biden’s halting debate performance in June, found that, among registered voters, 58 percent of Democrats in Virginia, and 46 percent of Democrats in Pennsylvania, wanted Mr. Biden to step aside and make way for a different Democratic candidate. (By contrast, just 15 percent of Republicans in Pennsylvania, and 18 percent in Virginia, said they wanted Mr. Trump to step aside.)

Democrats in the survey who said Mr. Biden should step down were generally younger and more educated than Democrats who want the president to remain their party’s nominee.

Roughly 60 percent of Democrats in both Pennsylvania and Virginia said that Mr. Biden, who is 81, was too old to run for president. By contrast, 15 percent of Republicans in Virginia, and 20 percent of Republicans in Pennsylvania, said that Mr. Trump, who is 78, was too old to run for president. Mr. Biden’s favorable rating in Pennsylvania has dipped slightly since May, while Mr. Trump has seen a slight uptick in the percentage of voters who have a favorable view of him.

A majority of voters in both states said they thought that Mr. Trump, who was convicted in New York in May of falsifying business records to cover up a sex scandal, and who is facing trials on various charges in two other jurisdictions, had “committed serious federal crimes.”

The results of the Times/Siena polls are the latest indication of Mr. Biden’s weakened political standing, particularly after a debate with Mr. Trump in which at times he seemed to lose his train of thought, speaking in a barely audible voice as he yielded the stage to Mr. Trump again and again.

“I had to turn it off,” said Christine Kramlich, 53, an information technology specialist from Reading, Pa., who voted for Mr. Trump in 2016 and Mr. Biden in 2020. Ms. Kramlich said in a follow-up interview that she would most likely leave the top part of her ballot blank this November.

“I don’t believe he is cognitively fit to hold the office,” Ms. Kramlich, who considers herself an independent, said of Mr. Biden.

Richard Fink, 73, a retired candy factory worker who lives in Hummelstown, Pa., and voted for Mr. Biden in 2020, said the president should leave the race. “He should have done it earlier,” Mr. Fink said. “If you watch him get down off Air Force One, he walks like he’s 81 years old.”

Mr. Biden has defied pressure to drop out of the race, presenting himself as the Democrats’ strongest candidate to defeat Mr. Trump. That said, even before the debate, a series of polls raised questions about that contention. And a national Times/Siena poll taken after the debate showed Mr. Trump with a six-point lead over Mr. Biden among likely voters, 49 percent to 43 percent. Polls by CNN and The Wall Street Journal have found similar leads.

That polling has stirred concern among many Democrats, and it has prompted open discussion of who might succeed Mr. Biden if he bowed to pressure and stepped aside.

Ms. Harris is often named as the candidate who might step in, and the Times/Siena polling in these two states suggests that she might be a stronger candidate than the president in a hypothetical matchup against Mr. Trump.

In both states, Ms. Harris was viewed more favorably than Mr. Biden overall, and she was slightly stronger with Black voters, younger voters and women — key constituencies that Democrats need in order to be successful in the fall. At the same time, much of the vice president’s increased strength comes from infrequent voters, who may not be able to be counted on to cast a ballot.

Matthew Kirkley, 46, a social worker from Lancaster, Pa., said that he thought Mr. Biden should “100 percent” step aside, and that he would gladly vote for Ms. Harris over Mr. Trump. “We need somebody besides an old white guy in the White House,” he said. “I would definitely feel more inclined to vote for her.”

Other potential Democratic contenders for the nomination were not included in the surveys.

The polls underlined a trend — voter discontent with Mr. Biden — that has appeared throughout the country: The Democratic candidates for Senate in both Virginia and Pennsylvania have sizable leads over their Republican challengers.

These types of ticket-splitters have become an increasingly rare phenomenon in the era of polarization, but are becoming more common as voters grapple with their feelings about the presidential candidates.

In Pennsylvania, voters who plan to vote for Mr. Trump as well as for Senator Bob Casey Jr., a Democrat, are more likely to be white and less likely to have a college education than voters who will cast their ballots only for Democrats.

The polls did find some encouraging news for Mr. Biden. Since May, he has made up some ground in Pennsylvania with crucial voting blocs, including Black voters. At the same time, he has lost ground in suburban areas, which are essential swing communities in the state.

Voters who dislike Mr. Biden and Mr. Trump — often known as the “double haters” — have drawn increasing attention by both campaigns. At 20 percent of registered voters in Virginia and 18 percent in Pennsylvania, the group is growing in size, and leans toward voters who supported Mr. Biden in 2020.

“We got a choice between two people that nobody wants and everybody thinks is too old,” Mr. Fink, the retired factory worker, said. “What’s the matter with that system?”

In Pennsylvania, Mr. Biden wins these disenchanted voters by more than 20 percentage points, but in Virginia he is winning the group by just 5 percentage points. Republicans hope they can win over some of these disheartened Biden supporters.

Camille Baker and Christine Zhang contributed reporting.

Here are the key things to know about these Times/Siena polls:

  • We spoke with 872 registered voters in Pennsylvania from July 9 to 11, and 661 registered voters in Virginia from July 9 to 12, 2024.

  • Our polls are conducted by telephone, using live interviewers, in both English and Spanish. About 91 percent of respondents were contacted on a cellphone for these polls.

  • Voters are selected from a list of registered voters. The list contains information on the demographic characteristics of every registered voter, allowing us to make sure we reach the right number of voters of each party, race and region. For this poll, we placed more than 160,000 calls to nearly 100,000 voters.

  • To further ensure that the results reflect the entire voting population, not just those willing to take a poll, we give more weight to respondents from demographic groups that are underrepresented among survey respondents, like people without a college degree. You can see more information about the characteristics of our respondents and the weighted sample on the methodology pages for Pennsylvania and Virginia, under “Composition of the Sample.”

  • The margin of sampling error among registered voters in Pennsylvania is plus or minus 3.7 percentage points and plus or minus 4.2 percentage points in Virginia. In theory, this means that the results should reflect the views of the overall population most of the time, though many other challenges create additional sources of error. When computing the difference between two values — such as a candidate’s lead in a race — the margin of error is twice as large.
