With missiles and ship attacks, Yemen’s Houthis gain regional clout.

The world’s largest shipping companies have started to reroute vessels away from the Red Sea, creating delays and extra costs that could be felt around the world through higher prices for oil and other imported goods.

A militia backed by Iran has disrupted shipping in the crucial shipping lane that connects the Indian Ocean with the Suez Canal, through which about 12 percent of world trade passes each year. The United States has promised to lead a group to help defend the area against the militia, the Houthis, who have targeted commercial vessels with missiles and drones in the past few weeks.

On Tuesday, the shipping giant Maersk said that it would reroute vessels scheduled to pass through the Red Sea to instead go around Africa via the Cape of Good Hope, “to ensure the safety of our crew, vessels and customers’ cargo.” Any decision to send ships back into the Red Sea would be made on a “case by case” basis, the company said.

“We have faith that a solution enabling a return to using the Suez Canal and transiting through the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden will be introduced in the near future, but at this time it remains difficult to determine exactly when this will be,” Maersk said.

The attacks pose a particular threat to the oil industry. On Monday, BP said it had stopped sending tankers through the Red Sea because of the “deteriorating security situation,” raising fears of disruptions to the flow of oil from major producer countries like Saudi Arabia and Iraq, where BP operates an oil field.

The price of Brent crude, the international oil benchmark, has risen about 5 percent since early December, partly reversing a monthslong slide in energy prices. Analysts at Goldman Sachs estimate that a prolonged redirection of all crude away from the Red Sea could raise oil prices by up to $4 per barrel, or another 5 percent from current levels, because more barrels would be stuck at sea on longer transit routes on their way to buyers.

Most of the drones and missiles aimed at commercial ships have fallen into the sea or been intercepted by U.S. ships and, recently, a French ship. The most successful Red Sea attack thus far was more than a week ago, when the Houthis struck a Norwegian tanker with a missile, causing a fire. No one was injured.

The U.S. Navy has moved more vessels to the area to protect ships, defend U.S. personnel in the Middle East, and aid Israel. The attacks have fueled concern of a broader conflict in the region as Israel’s bombardment of the Gaza Strip has gone on for more than two months, killing about 20,000 people.

Militias backed by Iran have vowed since Oct. 7, when Hamas killed about 1,200 people in southern Israel, that they would support the group in Gaza. That promise has not translated to much beyond rockets and missiles fired at Israel from Lebanon by Hezbollah and the attacks in the Red Sea by the Houthis, who control northern Yemen. But it has been the focus of discussions among Arab leaders, who want to avoid a broader war, and the United States and its allies, who are pushing for at least a pause in fighting and more humanitarian aid.

Defense Secretary Lloyd J. Austin III said the attacks threaten commerce, endanger mariners and violate international law. He was in Israel on Monday, where he displayed a change in tone as he met with leaders. He reassured them about U.S. support against Hamas but urged them to progress to a smaller, targeted war that would put civilians at less risk. Criticism has grown inside Israel and among its allies of the death toll in Gaza and since the Israeli military said it had accidentally killed three hostages last week.

Defense Secretary Lloyd J. Austin III on Tuesday promoted a new U.S.-led maritime security initiative in the Red Sea, but analysts said it was not likely to immediately stem a spate of drone and missile attacks by Iran-backed Houthi rebels in Yemen against commercial shipping in the pivotal waterway.

Speaking to fellow defense ministers virtually from Bahrain, Mr. Austin said that as a result of the attacks, freight and insurance rates have already increased, and oil prices are going up.

“Many of the largest shipping companies in the world have elected to avoid the Red Sea entirely,” Mr. Austin said.

More than a dozen countries and growing have agreed to join the security operation within an 18-month-old U.S.-led maritime task force, the United States says. But independent analysts said the initiative, called Prosperity Guardian, was mostly a symbolic move that allows the White House to say more countries are supporting Red Sea security and to increase the confidence of shipping companies.

There’s little if any change yet in the number of ships committed to patrolling the lower third of Red Sea or, on a case-by-case basis, to escorting certain commercial vessels with specific cargoes. At any given time, there are about 1,000 commercial vessels of all types transiting the Red Sea, a senior U.S. military officer said, and about 400 of those are in the lower Red Sea, the area most vulnerable to Houthi missiles and drones.

The multinational mission will include contributions from the United Kingdom, Bahrain, Canada, France, Italy, the Netherlands, Norway, Seychelles and Spain. The United States, Britain and France will continue to provide most of the warships to protect the waterway but only in a defensive capacity. About half a dozen ships from those countries that had started operating in the Red Sea soon after Hamas’s Oct. 7 attacks on Israel will now fall under the formal initiative.

The expectation is that more countries will join and send ships or surveillance aircraft, though Mr. Austin and his aides did not give projected numbers.

The Houthis have defended the attacks as an effort to force Israel to allow food and medicine to enter Gaza and to halt its military assault on the territory. A senior Western intelligence official said the new initiative was unlikely to deter future Houthi attacks. The official, speaking on condition of anonymity to discuss operational matters, said that at the same time, any retaliatory strike by the United States could result in an escalation, which the Houthis sought. The Houthis’ aim, the official said, was to highlight Western support to Israel and any retaliation scenario would do that.

A top Houthi negotiator, Mohammed Abdulsalam, said on Tuesday that the militia would not stop its “legitimate operations,” calling the U.S.-led coalition “a militarization of the Red Sea without any justification.”

Current and former U.S. military officers with experience in the region said the announcement aims at the least to give shipping companies greater confidence to resume routes through the Red Sea, easing rising fuel and insurance costs, and addressing potential supply chain issues.

“Economically, the hope is such a task force would give international investors and companies the support they need to potentially reopen maritime transit at some point in the future when conditions improve,” said Adam Clements, a former U.S. Army attaché for Yemen.

Shooting missiles toward Israel and attacking ships sailing through the Red Sea, Yemen’s Houthi militia has been gaining popularity across the Middle East and building regional clout that could help expand its power at home, analysts say.

Across the Middle East, where the war in Gaza has left citizens seething with anger at Israel and the United States — and in some cases, at their own American-backed governments — people have hailed the Houthis as one of the only regional forces willing to challenge Israel with more than harsh words.

“What they did has given us dignity, because they did this in a time when everyone was watching idly,” said Khalid Nujaim, who works at a medical supply company in Sana, the Yemeni capital, which is controlled by the Houthis.

A once-scrappy tribal group, the Houthis have taken over much of northern Yemen since they stormed Sana in 2014, gradually increasing their military capabilities and effectively winning a war against a Saudi-led coalition that spent years trying to rout them. Now that the most intense fighting in Yemen’s civil war has largely died down, the armed group is increasingly functioning as a de facto government.

They have described their recent attacks as a campaign in solidarity with the 2.2 million Palestinians living under Israel’s siege and bombardment of Gaza, which was launched in response to the Oct. 7 attacks by Hamas.

That campaign has transformed the Houthis from a local and regional force into one with a global impact, said Yoel Guzansky, a senior research fellow at Tel Aviv University’s Institute for National Security Studies.

“At the end of the day, what they really want is a bigger stake in Yemen, and perhaps they want to do that through becoming a global problem,” said Mr. Guzansky, a former Israeli official.

Particularly now, when the Houthis are on the verge of a peace deal with Saudi Arabia that would potentially recognize their control over northern Yemen, the war in Gaza is “a massive opportunity for them to get legitimacy in region,” said Farea al-Muslimi, a Yemeni research fellow at the Middle East and North Africa program at Chatham House, the London-based research group. “Right now everyone who is in the region is confusing the Yemenis with the Houthis, and for the Houthis, that’s the best thing that can happen.”

In statements announcing their attacks, the Houthis call themselves the “Yemen armed forces” — brushing aside the presence of an internationally recognized government and other armed groups based in the country’s south. On Tuesday, Mohammed Ali al-Houthi, a senior member of the Houthi movement, posted a warning on social media outlining the risks of traveling in the Red Sea, telling ships not to travel to “occupied ports in Palestine” and to be prepared to respond to orders from “the Yemeni navy.”

These days, wherever he goes in the region, Ahmed Nagi, a senior Yemen analyst at the International Crisis Group, finds that people are thrilled to learn that he is from Yemen, and quickly begin “talking about the Houthis and how brave they are,” he said.

“This is a very deep reflection of public opinions across the Arab countries at this moment,” Mr. Nagi said. He expressed concern that people might increasingly believe that they cannot trust their state actors, and that nonstate actors like the Houthis are their only hope to challenge what they see as Western hegemony.

Support for the Palestinian cause and hostility toward Israel has long been a pillar of the Houthi narrative; “Death to America, death to Israel” is in the group’s slogan. Part of the way they frame themselves is in opposition to American-backed Arab leaders, whom they view as “just mercenaries for the West,” Mr. Nagi explained.

Arab governments that once went to war with Israel and led an oil embargo to punish its Western backers have mostly reacted to the war in Gaza with public condemnations, aid campaigns and diplomatic efforts to push for a cease-fire, reinforcing a sense of impotence among some of their citizens who would prefer to see them cut ties with Israel or take other, more forceful actions.

At a news briefing on Tuesday, Eylon Levy, an Israeli government spokesman, described the Houthis as Iranian proxies “with the self-awareness of cartoon villains,” calling their attacks “a clear threat not only to Israel, but also to international peace and security.”

Using military force against Israel also helps the Houthis evade challenges on the domestic front, Mr. Nagi said. As Yemen’s civil war moves to a new phase, they are facing pressure from people asking for basic public services or for their long-delayed salaries as civil servants to be paid, he said.

While it is not the only reason behind their attacks, “this is a way out from that dilemma,” Mr. Nagi said. Now the message is essentially: “Don’t speak about anything, because we are in a war,” he said.
