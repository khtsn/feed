Westminster Dog Show 2024: Photos From Behind the Scenes

The tennis balls had been stashed away safely by the time 2,500 dogs and their obedient human entourages overtook the U.S.T.A. Billie Jean King National Tennis Center in Queens this weekend for the Westminster Kennel Club Dog Show.

The event began on Saturday with agility, obedience and diving competitions before transitioning on Monday into the contests that are more familiar to the audience at home: best in breed, best in group and best in show.

The human-to-dog ratio at the tennis center might be 2 to 1, but the power balance favors the dogs. There were pugs being wheeled around in strollers, Dalmatians posing like statues on artificial turf, and basset hounds whose ears were strapped to their chins so they didn’t sweep the asphalt. In a tent reserved for grooming, a Lhasa apso was carefully flat-ironed while several miniature poodles were sculpted into sudsy topiaries.

The event, which was first held in 1877, called Madison Square Garden home for a century but relocated to the Lyndhurst, an estate just outside the city, during the early pandemic. This is the second year of the show’s being held in the Flushing section of Queens, with Arthur Ashe Stadium offering deluxe accommodations for the show’s main events.

Whether they win or lose, the dogs have remained exceedingly well behaved. The humans, who have a selection of beer and gin and tonics at several concession stands, have had more varied results.

For ease of use, it is best to have one French bulldog to walk and another, placed on something tall, to pet.

There is a universal look of relief when you check inside the pile of gray dreadlocks and discover that your beloved komondor is still in there.

At the weekend’s agility championship, dogs competed in height classifications from eight inches to 24 inches. No one was brave enough to approach this Great Dane with a ruler.

There are no bad places to wear your giant gold dog pendant, but it’s an especially good choice at Westminster.

The tennis center, which is also home to the U.S. Open, has been the site of meticulous grooming in the past. (We’re looking at you, Roger Federer.) Still, the number of hair brushes per capita is far higher when the dog show is in town.

To go inside the tents is to see things you can’t unsee. Like the actual size of a terrier under its fur.

While not as flashy as a gold dog pendant, a “Take the Lead” brooch makes it clear you’re no casual observer.

Even the leashes of Westminster offer something you don’t see every day, with these ones appearing to come directly out of the dogs’ heads.

More than 2,500 dogs compete at Westminster, and the sad reality is that only one can take home best in show (and all the free dog food that comes with such a win). But there are plenty of other ribbons a dog can win.

That … that is not a dog. There has been some kind of mistake.

Every dog at the show is already a champion in some regard, and some do a better of job of carrying themselves as such (even if their skin is so loose it nearly hits the ground when they walk).

The dogs, win or lose, keep their cool throughout the event. The humans, on the other hand, run the gamut, with excitement, fear, happiness and devastation all on the table.
