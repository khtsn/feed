The Cold Truth at the Heart of ‘Succession,’ Lying on an Airplane Floor

Michelle Goldberg

One dark pleasure of HBO’s “Succession” is that, in a plutocratic age, it makes having an obscene fortune look like a curse. This worldview is reflected in the relentless corporate blandness of its aesthetic, one that mostly eschews wealth porn for a soulless series of S.U.V. back seats, badly lit conference rooms, private airport terminals and generic lofts. (The show’s cinematographer once described it as “the anti-‘Billions,’” referring to the Showtime series that regularly dwells on the rarefied indulgences of its hedge fund antiheroes.) But the perspective is reflected even more in the show’s emotional austerity, its vision of a world in which love is almost always a weakness. Despite the extreme affluence, there is not a single person in “Succession” worth envying.

As the most recent episode of the final season makes clear, this very much applies to Logan Roy, the titan at the show’s center. (Stop reading now if you want to avoid spoilers.) It’s not just that Logan dies — it was pretty obvious that was coming; eventually, a show called “Succession” was going to have a succession. And Logan’s mortality was very much on his mind in the season’s first episode, when he stalked out of his joyless birthday party to go to a diner with his bodyguard, whom he called, pitiably, his “best pal.” “You think there’s anything after all this?” asked Logan, before saying: “I don’t think so. I think this is it.”

The shock, then, wasn’t the fact of Logan’s death but how quick and ignoble it was. It came not at the culmination of the season but in Episode 3, the result of some sort of medical disaster in an airplane bathroom. Logan, played by the tremendous Shakespearean actor Brian Cox, has often been compared to King Lear, but he had no grandiose final words. Instead, his death happened mostly offscreen, and, as relayed by speakerphone to his children, it was confusing and indeterminate.

As he’s either dead or dying on the airplane floor, a cellphone was held to his ear so three of his kids, Kendall, Roman and Shiv — who over the course of the series have tried, alternately, to break free from their dad and supplant him — could say their pained goodbyes. There was great pathos in those farewells, since Logan’s children suffered for the love that kept them unwillingly tethered to their manipulative patriarch.

But their final words to their dad were also impromptu epitaphs to a misspent life. “You’re going to be OK. Because you’re a monster, and you’re going to win,” said Roman, who managed only stumbling banalities when he tried to speak more personally, later realizing he forgot to say, “I love you.”

Kendall did tell his father he loved him, but also said, “I can’t forgive you.” Shiv, too, expressed her love, but left hanging the phrase, “There’s no excuses.” On the plane, Logan’s young girlfriend was stunned but also almost giddy. “It looks like she caught a foul ball at Yankee Stadium,” Shiv’s soon-to-be ex-husband Tom remarked. He himself seemed crushed that the bet he made in betraying Shiv for her father was now unlikely to pay off.

There is a karmic justice in the smallness of Logan’s death, especially given the speech he gave to his Fox News-like cable network, ATN, in the previous episode, declaring his intention, after the expected sale of his multibillion-dollar company Waystar Royco, to push ATN in even more outré directions. “This is not the end,” he said. “I’m going to build something better. Something faster, lighter, meaner, wilder.” But it was the end, at least for Logan. “Succession” is a show at once obsessed with money, and with hammering away at the message that money cannot buy what matters.

For those of us who aren’t grotesquely rich, this vision of the lives (and deaths) of members of the ruling class is bleakly comforting. Their privilege may be undeserved, but at least they aren’t enjoying it.

Consider the relationship of Logan’s neglected eldest son, Connor, and his fiancée, a former sex worker and failed playwright named Willa. The episode in which Logan died is titled “Connor’s Wedding,” and much of it takes place on a boat to Ellis Island where Connor and Willa plan to get married, with Connor hoping, sadly, that his father would “pop by.”

In “Succession,” Willa has a surprising amount of power; she left her rehearsal dinner early, clearly having second thoughts, and later all but admitted to Connor that she’s marrying him for his money. Yet within the world of the show, everyone acts as if he’s lucky to have her. Connor’s vulnerability and Willa’s forthright melancholy about the compromise she’s making turn what could be a clichéd gold-digger dynamic into something much more interesting, even if I doubt that sad-sack billionaires in the real world have much trouble finding partners willing to pretend to love them.

Humiliation is a consistent theme in “Succession,” providing viewers the satisfaction of psychic comeuppance, and in the end, even Logan can’t escape it. In the first episode this season, musing about how all of life is a market, Logan sneered: “I’m 100 feet tall. These people are pygmies.” His death, though, makes an existence measured in market value seem pathetic. At one point Roman points to a graph on his cellphone showing the family company’s stock price plunging on news of Logan’s demise. “There he is,” he said. “That is Dad.”
