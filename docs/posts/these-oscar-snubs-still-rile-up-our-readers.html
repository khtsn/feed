You can’t forgive the Academy for passing up “Brokeback Mountain” or omitting Amy Adams in “Arrival,” among other oversights that still sting.

Stephanie Goodman

March 9, 2024, 5:02 a.m. ET

Some things will always stick in your craw. When I asked readers, ahead of the Academy Awards on Sunday, if they were still mad about an Oscar snub, boy, did I get an earful.

Technically a snub involves a film or an artist (or a song or any other possible contender) that was overlooked altogether at the awards. But a nominee losing to an unworthy rival was also fair game, and readers took both slights to heart.

I received hundreds of responses. Readers felt strongly about the lack of nominations for “Paddington 2,” Danny Elfman’s score for the 1989 “Batman,” Will Ferrell in “Elf” and Abby Ryder Fortson in “Are You There God? It’s Me, Margaret.” But these weren’t the most notable omissions and oversights. Here are the lightly edited responses:

‘Saving Private Ryan’

Steven Spielberg’s World War II drama lost best picture to the period romance “Shakespeare in Love” in 1999.

“Saving Private Ryan” is unforgettable. The opening beach scene was jaw-dropping. “Shakespeare in Love” is entirely forgettable. Harvey Weinstein campaigned to get that Oscar. Shame on the academy. MATT DENTON, Old Bridge, N.J.

Probably the best war movie of all time versus a lightweight rom-com about Shakespeare’s love life. Need I say more? SCOTT PARKIN, Reston, Va.

Who has ever watched “Shakespeare in Love” more than once? BART DEWING, Mount Vernon, N.Y.

Fernanda Montenegro in ‘Central Station’

More “Shakespeare in Love” ire: Gwyneth Paltrow won for best actress in that film over the star of the Brazilian road trip tale.

Fernanda Montenegro is the Meryl Streep of Brazil. She lost the best actress Oscar to Gwyneth Paltrow over 20 years ago, but Brazil has not forgotten. There are memes and jokes about it to this day. YOHANA de ANDRADE, New York

Amy Adams in ‘Arrival’

Though up for the Oscar six times over her career, Adams wasn’t nominated for her role in Denis Villeneuve’s sci-fi tale in 2017; Emma Stone won best actress for “La La Land” that year.

It was mind-blowing that Amy Adams was snubbed. It becomes more galling with every time I rewatch that film. She is extraordinary. She brings me to tears every time. KATE PETRUCCELLI, Norwood, Mass.

“Arrival” was nominated for eight Oscars, including best picture and director. How do you nominate a film for that many awards but not the actress at its beating heart? It still gets me riled up to this day. LIBBY SPURGEON, Chicago

‘Brokeback Mountain’

In 2006, Ang Lee’s western romance about two gay ranchers lost best picture to “Crash,” a morality tale about race and class in Los Angeles.

The fact that the trite “Crash” won best picture over “Brokeback Mountain” still enrages me. That win was the ultimate in Hollywood patting itself on the back. DAMIAN FALLON, Philadelphia

This was unconscionable homophobia on the part of academy voters. BRIAN LOGAN, Mountain View, Calif.

Judy Garland in ‘A Star Is Born’

The star of the showbiz drama lost best actress to Grace Kelly in another showbiz drama, “The Country Girl,” in 1955.

Don’t get me wrong. Grace Kelly was fine. But in my view her performance was limited in range and demanded little of her compared to Judy’s virtuoso command of the screen in “A Star Is Born.” GLORIA FOWLER, Los Angeles

Danielle Deadwyler in ‘Till’

Last year, she wasn’t nominated for the best actress award, which went to Michelle Yeoh for “Everything Everywhere All at Once.”

Glaring omission. Deadwyler’s portrayal of Mamie Till, at a time when African American history is being erased, brought truth to the story of Emmett Till with force and grace. — DOMINIQUE CIERI, Branchville, N.J.

Whoopi Goldberg

“The Color Purple” star lost best actress to Geraldine Page for “The Trip to Bountiful” in 1986.

Whoopi WAS Celie! Her performance was flawless and powerful! The 1986 Academy Awards, when Oscars were “so white”! LYNDA COSTAGLIOLA

Five years later, though, Goldberg won best supporting actress for “Ghost” over Lorraine Bracco for “Goodfellas.”

“Ghost” was a terrible movie, and Whoopi’s performance was nothing special. Lorraine Bracco’s performance had amazing depth in “Goodfellas,” a great movie. LORI CALLAN, San Mateo, Calif.

‘Goodfellas’

Martin Scorsese’s mob classic itself lost best picture to the Kevin Costner western “Dances With Wolves” in 1991.

I have never gotten over this. Arguably, Scorsese’s last truly great movie was an exhilarating ride. “Goodfellas” is endlessly watchable while “Wolves” — then and now — is a slog. MICHAEL CHAMBERLIN, Washington, D.C.

“Goodfellas” is top-of-his-game Scorsese, seared into your memory on first viewing. All I can remember from Costner’s directorial debut is his naked butt prancing in the prairie. ALEXANDER WHITE, New York

‘Do the Right Thing’

The Spike Lee drama lost for best screenplay, and wasn’t nominated for best picture or director in 1990. “Driving Miss Daisy” won the top prize that year.

“Do the Right Thing” is an absolutely flawless movie that so brilliantly explored issues of race. Perhaps it was ahead of its time. Perhaps it was too honest, too explosive for the academy. KEN HARVEY, Toronto

Toni Collette in ‘Hereditary’

In 2019, she wasn’t nominated for best actress, which Olivia Colman took for “The Favourite."

The Oscars are notorious for snubbing the horror genre, sure, but this one really made me lose my head. “Hereditary” is hands down the scariest film I’ve ever seen. And the scariest thing about it, by far, was Toni Collette. ZACH de STEFAN, New York

‘Pulp Fiction’

Quentin Tarantino’s philosophizing hit men movie lost best picture to the feel-good “Forrest Gump” in 1995.

Not only was “Pulp Fiction” a groundbreaking, amazingly creative film, but to make it even worse, “Forrest Gump” was one of the dumbest, most idiotic films I’ve ever seen. TIM McFADDEN, Encinitas, Calif.

Shivani Gonzalez contributed reporting.
