Tommy Robinson, Colorful Arkansas Sheriff and Congressman, Dies at 82

Tommy Robinson, who over two terms as a county sheriff in Arkansas in the 1980s made repeated national headlines with stunts like chaining inmates to a prison tower to protest jail overcrowding, and who then used that reputation to win three terms in Congress, died on July 10 in Forrest City, Ark., east of Little Rock. He was 82.

His daughter Fran Moseley confirmed the death, in a hospital. She said that while he had been ill recently, a cause of death had not been determined.

Mr. Robinson was once among Arkansas’s best-known political figures, referred to universally by his first name or simply “T.R.” His unconventional, often outlandish behavior won him hordes of admirers, but just as many enemies.

“People either loved or hated my dad,” his daughter said. “There was no in between.”

In 1981, when the jail in Pulaski County, the home of Little Rock, became dangerously overcrowded with state prisoners, Mr. Robinson ordered his deputies to take 19 of them to a nearby state prison; when the warden refused to take them, he had the men chained to a guard tower.

And when the state police threatened to return them, Mr. Robinson ordered his men to form an armed cordon around the county jail.

“Let’s have a standoff,” he told reporters, adding that if things got hot, he would put shotguns in their hands and make them deputies.

Soon after, when a judge-appointed special master arrived to inspect his jail, he refused to let him in. When the judge and the county comptroller in response blocked funds for the sheriff’s department, Mr. Robinson had them arrested.

He drew national attention again in 1982 with his response to a rash of deadly convenience-store robberies: Off-duty deputies would be assigned to hide inside randomly selected stores, armed with shotguns. The news media called it “Robinson roulette.”

“When I jump out of here, and I’m going to be in some of them, when I come down on Mr. Armed Robber,” he told a television crew, “I’m going to give you one chance, I’m going to say, ‘Drop it,’ and if you don’t drop it, you’re going to be dropped.”

He claimed that as a result of his strategy, convenience-store robberies declined by 96 percent over the next year.

His critics accused him of bending the law to accommodate his version of public order. He was accused of physically abusing a murder suspect, Barry Lee Fairchild, who was executed in 1995 despite his lawyers’ contention that his confession had been coerced. Mr. Robinson said that he had treated Mr. Fairchild “with kid gloves” and that the evidence was ironclad.

“The reason I’m perceived as being controversial is simply because I will take a stand,” he told United Press International in 1983. “I don’t worry about the criticism. I have to live with myself.”

Mr. Robinson served as sheriff from 1981 to 1985, followed by three terms in the U.S. House of Representatives.

In Congress he joined the dwindling caucus of conservative Southern Democrats known as the Boll Weevils. He consistently supported President Ronald Reagan’s agenda and opposed what he saw as the leftward drift of the national Democratic Party.

He switched to the Republican Party in 1989, a move that once more drew widespread attention after President George Bush invited him to give a statement during a White House news conference.

“My conscience is clear,” Mr. Robinson told reporters. “I have made my decision.”

He otherwise led a quiet career in Congress, though he gained notoriety for topping a list of Congress members who frequently bounced checks at the House bank — in his case, nearly 1,000.

He strongly disliked Washington, living out of a hotel and coming back to Arkansas as often as possible. When the opportunity arose in 1989 to run for governor against Bill Clinton, he jumped.

Mr. Robinson was considered a favorite to win the Republican nomination, but he narrowly lost in an upset to Sheffield Nelson, a businessman. Arkansas allowed anyone to vote in primaries, regardless of party, and political observers claimed that Democrats had flooded the polls to back Mr. Nelson, widely considered the weaker candidate. Mr. Clinton, the incumbent, won re-election.

Thomas Franklin Robinson was born on March 7, 1942, in Little Rock, the only child of Hoover Robinson, a firefighter, and Esther (Lingo) Robinson, who worked for the state government.

He grew up in North Little Rock; among his friends was Jerry Jones, who would become a wealthy businessman and the owner of the Dallas Cowboys. Mr. Jones financed Mr. Robinson’s early campaigns, but their friendship ended when Mr. Jones backed Mr. Nelson in 1990.

Mr. Robinson served in the Navy from 1959 to 1963, then became a police officer in North Little Rock. He took night and weekend college classes and earned a bachelor’s degree in criminal justice from the University of Arkansas at Little Rock.

He married Carolyn Barber Hughes in 1967. Along with their daughter Fran, she survives him, as do their other daughters, Debbie Holt and Leslie Corbett; their sons, Bill, Greg and Jeff Robinson; 13 grandchildren; and 12 great-grandchildren.

Mr. Robinson steadily climbed the ranks of law enforcement. He was a U.S. marshal during the Watergate hearings, providing protection to several witnesses, and was appointed police chief in Jacksonville, Ark., a suburb of Little Rock, in 1976.

He immediately reorganized the department around countering the city’s drug problem. After a local judge criticized his changes, Mr. Robinson publicly called him “a real bastard”; when he refused to apologize, the judge cited him for contempt.

Governor Clinton, during his first term, appointed Mr. Robinson to run the Arkansas Department of Public Safety in 1979.

After losing the 1990 governor’s race, Mr. Robinson owned a liquor store and a soybean farm, ran a political consulting firm and served on several state commissions.

In 2006 he had his own run-in with the law. After a creditor forced him into bankruptcy, Mr. Robinson and his son Jeff attacked the man at a barbecue restaurant. Both served several days in jail for misdemeanor assault.

“Tommy’s career was colorful, but it was colorful in service of the people of Arkansas,” Gov. Sarah Huckabee Sanders said in a statement after his death. “He was never afraid of a fight.”
