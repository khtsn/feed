Mayor Adams Vetoes Police Transparency and Solitary Confinement Bills

New York|Mayor Adams Vetoes Police Transparency and Solitary Confinement Bills

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/01/19/nyregion/mayor-adams-veto.html

The New York City Council is likely to override the mayor’s vetoes despite his objections and fervent campaign to have the bills rescinded.

Jan. 19, 2024, 3:13 p.m. ET

After a failed and unusually protracted effort to convince the New York City Council to rescind a bill requiring the police to document more of their interactions with the public, Mayor Eric Adams vetoed the legislation Friday, arguing that it would harm public safety.

“We cannot handcuff the police,” Mr. Adams said at a news conference at City Hall, where he was surrounded by community supporters and police officials. “We want to handcuff bad people for violence.”

Hours later, the mayor also vetoed a bill that would ban solitary confinement in the city’s jails.

That Mr. Adams, a former police captain who ran for mayor on a platform of public safety, would oppose the bills is not surprising. But it was unusual for the mayor to be so fervently opposed — he commissioned a public-relations campaign that included an animated video — to the bills, considering they were approved by the Council with veto-proof majorities.

The mayor said on Friday that he had conversations with numerous Council members about the bills, suggesting that he may have persuaded some to oppose the policing legislation, in particular.

In spite of the mayor’s late push, the City Council is “prepared to override” both the mayor’s vetoes, according to Adrienne Adams, the Council speaker. It would be the second and third time that the Council has overridden the mayor, following a decision in July to reject his veto of a housing voucher program.

The mayor’s stance — buttressed by the deputy mayor for public safety, the police commissioner and the chief of department, all of whom stood alongside Mr. Adams on Friday — is that the police bill would jeopardize safety by forcing officers to perform unnecessary administrative tasks following interactions with the public.

An “everyday engagement” becomes “transactional record keeping,” said Edward Caban, the city’s first Latino police commissioner.

The bill, a piece of the so-called How Many Stops Act, would require police officers to log basic information about investigative stops with the public. Officers would be asked to record details such as the race, age and gender of the people they encounter and the factors that led to the interaction.

Police officers were already required to document certain interactions with the public, such as when they stop and question someone they believe may have committed a crime. The City Council wants the police to record other kinds of interactions, such as questioning a potential suspect or requesting information from someone.

The bill is designed to document what legislators believe are discriminatory and unreported stops by the police. In 2013, a federal judge in Manhattan ruled that the police were using stop-and-frisk tactics to racially profile Black and Latino men after use of the practice soared under Mayor Michael R. Bloomberg.

The use of stop-and-frisk declined precipitously under Mayor Bill de Blasio, but it has begun to rise again under Mr. Adams. A federal monitor appointed after the lawsuit found that the Police Department was underreporting stops and performing too many illegal stops.

Jeffrey Maddrey, the chief of department, said that under the new policing bill, he would be forced to document an interaction he had during the New York City Marathon with a runner when he checked on the person’s condition and offered Gatorade. The bill’s sponsors said that Chief Maddrey’s interpretation of the legislation was incorrect.

“The term investigative encounter means an interaction between a member of the department and a member of the public for a law enforcement or investigative purpose,” said Jumaane Williams, the city’s public advocate, who introduced the bill with the City Council. “The term does not include a casual conversation or interaction.”

Mr. Williams accused the mayor of spouting “Trumpian lies” about the legislation for political purposes. At 28 percent, Mr. Adams has the lowest approval rating in a Quinnipiac University poll since it began surveying the city in 1996. The mayor also faces a federal investigation into his campaign fund-raising, though he has not been accused of wrongdoing.

The mayor has also been criticized for his handling of an influx of more than 160,000 asylum seekers, which has stretched the city’s shelter system and budget.

“The mayor’s in a slump,” Mr. Williams said. “He’s flailing all over so he wants to tell New Yorkers he’s the hero that can save them and is using this basic reporting bill to try to show that.”

Mr. Adams has not limited his criticism of the bill to social media. The mayor used a recent appearance at a bar mitzvah to ask Jewish New Yorkers to support his effort to rescind the legislation.

“This is our line in the sand,” the mayor said at the event.

At a gala on Thursday night, Mr. Adams also criticized a close ally, the Real Estate Board of New York, the industry’s lobbying organization, for not doing enough to fight the bill.

The group’s president, James Whelan, was perplexed by the admonishment.

“At last night’s event,” Mr. Whelan said in a statement, “we appreciated the opportunity to hear from the mayor and his team for the first time regarding the How Many Stops Act.”

Dana Rubinstein contributed reporting.
