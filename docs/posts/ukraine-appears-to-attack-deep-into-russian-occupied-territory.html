President Vladimir V. Putin of Russia met with Wang Yi, China’s top foreign policy official, at the Kremlin on Wednesday, working to keep China in Russia’s corner amid a flurry of diplomacy across Europe by Beijing.

Mr. Putin told Mr. Wang that he was looking forward to welcoming “my friend” Xi Jinping, the Chinese leader, to Russia soon, but indicated that the meeting had yet to be confirmed. Mr. Wang responded that deepening the relationship with Russia remained a priority for China.

“Our relations have never been directed against third countries,” Mr. Wang told Mr. Putin, according to a Russian translation of his remarks. “Our relations have withstood pressure from the international community and are developing very stably.”

Mr. Putin is looking to shore up alliances as the Ukraine war approaches its first anniversary and the early stages of Russia’s new offensive to swallow up territory appear to be sputtering. As Mr. Putin and Mr. Wang met, President Biden was gathering with NATO members from the alliance’s eastern flank in Warsaw in a display of unity.

Mr. Wang arrived in Moscow this week after a tour of Western Europe, where he sought to persuade European leaders that Beijing is not supporting Mr. Putin’s war and wants to encourage a peaceful exit from the fighting.

“Currently, the international situation is certainly grim and complex,” Mr. Wang told Mr. Putin, according to brief footage from the meeting that was shared by the Chinese news media. “But Chinese-Russian relations have withstood the test of international turbulence, and are mature and durable — as steadfast as Mount Tai,” he said, referring to a famed Chinese mountain.

On Tuesday, Mr. Wang met with Nikolai P. Patrushev, Mr. Putin’s top security adviser. The Chinese government statement on the meeting said the two had “exchanged views” on Ukraine.

U.S. officials have warned that China could seek to support Russia with weapons. But there was no mention of Ukraine or weapons in the seven-minute, televised portion of Mr. Wang’s meeting with Mr. Putin on Wednesday, where Mr. Patrushev and Sergey V. Lavrov, the Russian foreign minister, joined Mr. Putin around a white, oval table.

In his opening remarks, Mr. Putin highlighted the economic aspect of the China-Russia relationship, predicting that the countries’ annual trade volume could reach $200 billion as early as this year, compared with $185 billion last year.

“Everything is moving forward, developing, we are reaching new frontiers,” Mr. Putin said. “And above all, we are talking, of course, about economic issues.”

Throughout his visit to Europe and Russia, Mr. Wang’s public comments have reflected China’s fraught efforts to maintain Beijing’s close strategic alignment with Moscow.

“On the Ukraine issue, China has always stood on the side of peace and dialogue, and has always insisted on pressing for peace and negotiation,” Mr. Wang said in a meeting with Ukraine’s foreign minister, Dmytro Kuleba, on Saturday, when they were both in Munich for an annual security conference, according to China’s official summary of the meeting.

“We don’t want to see the Ukraine crisis perpetuate and expand,” Mr. Wang said.

But even in that meeting, Mr. Wang’s published comments did not use the words war or invasion to describe the fighting in Ukraine. And while in Moscow, Mr. Wang may be even more reluctant to expose any deep differences between China and Russia.

Wang Yi, China’s top diplomat, met with Russia’s foreign minister, Sergey V. Lavrov, on Wednesday in Moscow as concerns in the West grow about their countries’ ties and the position Beijing has taken on the war in Ukraine.

The Kremlin said that Mr. Wang would also meet with President Vladimir V. Putin later in the day. The Chinese diplomat’s trip is expected to help lay the groundwork for a state visit by his country’s top leader, Xi Jinping, to Moscow in the spring that could inflame tensions between China and the West.

In the diplomats’ opening remarks, they did not mention the war, with Mr. Lavrov stressing that despite “turbulence in the international arena,” Russia and China “demonstrate unity and readiness to defend each other’s interests.”

Echoing Mr. Lavrov’s comments and calling him his “dear friend,” Mr. Wang said that even though the international situation was changing, Russia and China had “always maintained their strategic determination and are firmly and confidently moving toward the formation of a multipolar world.”

“We are always committed to multipolarity and firmly oppose unilateral actions and hegemonies,” Mr. Wang said, according to the Russian translation of his remarks.

China has been trying to promote itself as a peace broker and is expected to reveal a proposal soon. Skepticism abounds about such an effort because of Russia’s strategic value to Beijing. The United States has accused China of considering supplying Russia with weapons and ammunition — a decision that could seriously widen the conflict.

China needs Russia’s long-term support to counter American global dominance. That has undermined Beijing’s recent efforts to court Western European powers to help reignite its sluggish economy.

On Tuesday, Mr. Wang met with one of Mr. Putin’s closest advisers. He told the adviser, Nikolai P. Patrushev, the head of the Kremlin’s Security Council, that relations between Russia and China were “rock solid,” according to the Russian state news media.

Mr. Patrushev expressed similar support, telling Mr. Wang that Russia backed China on some of its most contentious issues.

“I want to reaffirm our invariable support for Beijing on the Taiwan, Xinjiang, Tibet and Hong Kong issues, which the West is exploiting to discredit China,” Mr. Patrushev said, according to a video of the event.

An official Chinese readout of the meeting said only that the two had “exchanged views on the Ukraine issue.”

KHARKIV, Ukraine — Several explosions rang out on Wednesday in the northeastern Ukrainian city of Kharkiv amid a nationwide air alert and warnings from Ukrainian officials that Russia was planning a large-scale missile barrage timed to Friday’s anniversary of its invasion.

The volley of missiles that hit Kharkiv could be a prelude to a larger strike or to a continuation of smaller-scale attacks on the city, which lies near the Russian border and is often struck by shorter-range missiles that cannot reach more distant cities such as Kyiv, the capital. In past barrages, the Russian military has fired both shorter-range missiles at areas near the border and longer-ranged weapons at targets deeper inside Ukraine.

On Wednesday, a half-dozen thunderous booms echoed through the city shortly before 11 a.m. It was not immediately clear what was hit. A few minutes after the explosions, the air alert was canceled.

Russia has launched volleys of missiles and exploding drones every week or two since October, mostly aimed at Ukraine’s electrical plants, transmission lines and transformer substations, with an intention of causing blackouts and widespread suffering among the population during the cold winter months.

Frequent flights by Russian planes capable of launching missiles and, in recent days, the detection by radar of balloons floated over Ukraine — possibly as decoys to confuse air defenses — have raised alarms that Russia is planning a major missile strike.

Ukrainian officials and analysts have suggested that Russia could fire a larger than typical barrage around the date of the anniversary of its invasion to overshadow the military setbacks it has suffered during the year of ground warfare.

A barrage would also serve as an answer to high-profile pledges of Western support for Ukraine a year into the war, including a visit by President Biden to Kyiv on Monday.

KYIV, Ukraine — Ukraine appears to have stepped up its assaults on positions deep into Russian-held territory, with nearly a dozen explosions reported overnight into Wednesday in the southern port city of Mariupol and explosions in other occupied parts of Ukraine, according to Ukrainian officials, local residents and video.

The areas are outside the known range of the American-made HIMARS missile systems that Ukraine has in its arsenal.

In occupied areas of the Donetsk, Luhansk and Kherson regions, there were explosions throughout the night, and air defenses were at work, according to reports and video.

In Mariupol, which has been under the control of the Russians since they laid siege to the city in the spring, the exiled City Council reported at least 11 explosions. Russian forces have turned the city into a major garrison, because it was thought to be out of the range of HIMARS from the nearest Ukrainian stronghold around the ruined mining town of Vuhledar.

The number of strikes, their targets and the impact on Russian forces were not immediately known, although the Mariupol City Council said on Wednesday morning that one of the explosions had destroyed a Russian ammunition warehouse in the central district near the airport.

The Ukrainian General Staff said only that Ukraine’s air force had launched eight attacks on the temporary bases of Russian troops and two strikes on the positions of Russia’s anti-aircraft missile systems. It offered no details on the locations of the strikes.

Kyiv has been begging its Western allies for longer-range weapons to attack concentrations of Russian troops, supply lines, command centers and ammunition depots. While Ukrainian officials have expressed confidence that such weapons would soon arrive, military analysts said it was likely that they would be used on the battlefield before being announced to catch Russian forces by surprise.

Ukraine has used long-range attack drones to target Russian positions outside the known range of HIMARS.

WARSAW — If Russian President Vladimir V. Putin were to order tanks into other European countries, the nine nations along NATO’s eastern flank would be the likeliest targets.

So on Wednesday, President Biden will meet with the leaders of those countries, offering reassurance that the United States is prepared to defend the most vulnerable members of the alliance from the kind of assault now taking place in Ukraine.

The meeting, slated to take place just hours before Mr. Biden concludes his three-day visit to Ukraine and Poland, is meant to underscore his administration’s determination to confront Mr. Putin and prevent him from intimidating some of the world’s more fragile democracies.

“These are largely the group of eastern flank NATO allies who are basically and, quite frankly, literally on the front lines of our collective defense right now,” John Kirby, a senior spokesman for the National Security Council, told reporters before the president’s trip.

Mr. Kirby said the president would meet with leaders of the nine countries — Bulgaria, the Czech Republic, Estonia, Hungary, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland, Romania and Slovakia — to “reaffirm the United States’ unwavering support for the security of that alliance and trans-Atlantic unity.”

In his speech from the royal castle in Warsaw on Tuesday, Mr. Biden was resolute about the American commitment to the defense of all of NATO, declaring that the guarantee to come to the aid of any threatened ally “is rock solid. And every member of NATO knows it. And Russia knows it as well.”

But the threat of direct military intervention is less theoretical for Poland, Hungary and the other countries whose borders lie not far from Russian territory than for Britain, France or Spain.

For those countries, Mr. Putin’s invasion of Ukraine almost a year ago is not only a threat to the principle of democracy, but also a road map for an invasion that could just as quickly plunge their own cities into the darkness of war.

Recognizing that special threat, the leaders of the nine countries came together in 2015 to discuss the steps they could take to reduce the risk. The gathering on Wednesday with the American president will be the most high-profile, and urgent, meeting since the group was formed eight years ago.

For Mr. Biden, it will be another opportunity to praise NATO’s unity in the face of Mr. Putin’s actions. In his briefing last week, Mr. Kirby said that the group would focus on maintaining a unity of purpose even as the war in Ukraine drags on.

White House officials said the United States and its allies would soon announce tougher sanctions on Russia aimed at increasing the cost of Mr. Putin’s war.

“The leaders will discuss our efforts over the past year to strengthen NATO, which is stronger and now more united than it was — than it has ever been, and how each of our nations will continue to work together,” he said.

After the meeting, Mr. Biden is expected to depart Poland and return to Washington, concluding a foreign trip that began with his top-secret visit to Ukraine’s capital.

LUXEMBOURG — Luxembourg’s military consists of fewer than 1,000 troops, one cargo plane, two helicopters shared with police forces and fewer than 200 trucks, ranging from Humvees to about 10 state-of-the-art Dingo combat reconnaissance vehicles.

There are no tanks, warplanes or Patriot air defense missiles to contribute to the Western push to arm Ukraine. The 102 anti-tank missiles and 20,000 rounds of machine gun ammunition Luxembourg has sent from its arsenal were as many weapons as it could afford to give without putting its own military readiness at risk.

So Luxembourg, a nation with a population of 640,000, decided to use its considerable wealth to try to buy weapons for Ukraine on the open market, and signed a multimillion-dollar deal last spring for 6,000 Soviet-era rockets. In the end, though, the government delivered only 600 and was left scrambling for ways to spend the money.

At a time when Western stocks of Soviet-era weapons and ammunition are running low, Luxembourg’s travails provide a window on the vexing problem of supplying Ukraine with the arms it needs to hold off Russia until the arrival of sophisticated Western rockets, missiles and tanks later this year.

Ukraine has been burning through ammunition at a prodigious rate since the start of the war, relying on allies to replace its stocks. But there are no weapons producers in Luxembourg, and the government had already given all it judged it could afford from its own limited arsenal.

But determined to make a greater contribution to the war effort, Luxembourg set up a two-man team of in-house arms dealers soon after the Russian invasion. They set out to scour commercial weapons markets in Europe and the United States, and to demonstrate that their country’s commitment to defeating Russia was every bit as big as that of its much larger NATO partners.

“We are so small, and we have no large army, and therefore limited stock, and we wanted from the beginning to help Ukraine,” Luxembourg’s defense minister, Francois Bausch, who also doubles as the country’s transportation minister and deputy prime minister, said in a recent interview. “But we are flexible, and so we can go and buy on the market what they need and deliver it directly to them.”

Kristalina Georgieva, the managing director of the International Monetary Fund, expressed optimism on Tuesday that Ukraine could soon secure a substantial loan package to support its economy as it continues to fend off Russia’s invasion after a year.

Ukraine faces a budget deficit of about $40 billion this year and has been working to secure more economic support from Western allies as the war is about to enter its second year. The United States and the European Union have provided robust humanitarian and military aid, but Ukraine has been seeking additional financial support so that it can continue to operate its government.

Ms. Georgieva met with top Ukrainian officials this week, including President Volodymyr Zelensky, and saw the destruction firsthand in Kyiv, the capital, and Irpin, a suburb.

“In my meetings, I reiterated the I.M.F.’s unwavering commitment to continue supporting Ukraine,” Ms. Georgieva said in a statement.

Ms. Georgieva added that constructive talks between Ukrainian officials and the I.M.F. have paved the way for “discussions on a full-fledged I.M.F. financing program.”

Ukraine, which saw its economy contract by 30 percent last year, has been seeking a multiyear loan package valued at around $15 billion. The I.M.F. has made several recommendations for Ukraine to solidify its economy in preparation for what will be a protracted rebuilding process.

Following a meeting with Mr. Zelensky, Ms. Georgieva said that she would tell the I.M.F.’s board that the country is “functioning extremely well and efficiently” despite the attack.

Mr. Zelensky expressed hope that assistance from the I.M.F. would help attract international investment to the country.

“Your support creates an opportunity for Ukraine to remain strong,” he said. “It is also a weapon — an economic weapon that allows us, our society, and our business to develop.”

During the Cold War, Finland maintained its independence by staying in the shadow of the Soviet Union, remaining neutral and shaping its foreign policy to accommodate the larger power’s interests. But Russia’s aggression in Ukraine has pushed Finland, a small Nordic nation on Russia’s western border, to shift away from that strategy, known in diplomatic circles as Finlandization.

President Biden drew attention to the shift in his speech about the Ukraine war in Warsaw on Tuesday, referring to the “NATO-ization” of Finland. The comment evoked Finland’s pending bid for membership in the North Atlantic Treaty Organization, which it filed last May, along with its neighbor Sweden, a few months after Russia invaded Ukraine.

Mr. Biden’s turn of phrase was especially resonant because of its timing: He delivered the speech a day after his daring visit to Kyiv, the Ukrainian capital, and just hours after President Vladimir V. Putin of Russia gave a state-of-the-nation speech in which he said he would suspend participation in a nuclear arms control treaty with the United States.

Here’s what to know about Finland’s and Sweden’s bids to join NATO, a 30-nation alliance.

Why do Sweden and Finland want to join NATO?

Security concerns, mainly. Finland and Sweden jointly submitted their applications in May, in the wake of Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine. If accepted, they will have protection under NATO’s mutual defense agreement.

Were Finland and Sweden in an alliance before NATO?

No. While they held strategic partnerships, they abandoned decades of nonalignment to apply.

What are the obstacles to their joining?

Recep Tayyip Erdogan, Turkey’s president, has objected to Sweden’s bid. Although both countries have strong backing from most member states, NATO requires unanimous consent to approve new members. Mr. Erdogan has indicated he is willing to ratify Finland’s NATO membership (after much negotiation), but has demanded that Sweden crack down on Kurdish refugee groups — members of which he considers to be terrorists — and extradite a number of Kurds living in Sweden, some of whom are political refugees. He is still holding out even though Sweden has promised to take a harder line.

Where do things stand now?

Earlier this month, Jens Stoltenberg, NATO’s secretary general, raised the possibility that the countries’ applications might be considered separately. This appeared to open the door to a split decision on membership.

Mr. Stoltenberg said that he was “confident that both will be full members and are working hard to get both ratified as soon as possible.” But his suggestion that the applications be separated raised concerns among NATO member states that doing so could lessen the alliance’s leverage on Mr. Erdogan.

Mr. Stoltenberg has held three-way talks with Sweden, Finland and Turkey to work out differences and satisfy Mr. Erdogan, who is expected to face strong opposition in elections scheduled for May.
