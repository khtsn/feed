Adams, Under Pressure, Drops Some of His School Cuts in Latest Reversal

Mayor Eric Adams said he would restore previously announced reductions in financing for free summer programs and services for low-income students.

Jan. 12, 2024Updated 5:27 p.m. ET

Mayor Eric Adams said on Friday that he would reverse a portion of previously announced cuts to New York City’s education budget that would have slashed money for high-needs schools and reduced the hours of free summer programs.

The cuts being restored are part of a nearly $550 million reduction in school spending that Mr. Adams announced last fall amid a series of budget cuts across city agencies. The move caused an uproar among parents and prompted the teachers’ union to sue the city, arguing that the administration was breaking state law by reducing school spending in the middle of the year for political reasons.

The reversal was the third such about-face by the mayor this week. In announcing the broader budget cuts in November, he cited the financial strain caused by the arrival of tens of thousands of migrants from the southern border in recent months.

But Mr. Adams said this week that the city would restore some funding to the police, fire, parks and sanitation departments because of “better-than-anticipated tax revenue” and reduced costs for migrant care. Officials had previously estimated that housing and feeding migrants would cost $12 billion over three years, but they now say they expect to spend about 20 percent less than that, or about $9.6 billion.

The city will still require the Education Department to make more than $500 million of the planned cuts, including to programs like free prekindergarten services. The total education budget for the current school year is about $37 billion.

“We do not want it to be taken as a signal that this city’s out of the woods,” Mr. Adams said at a news conference. “We still have a $7 billion budget gap.”

The teachers’ union, the United Federation of Teachers, argued in its suit that the announced cuts would have a “devastating impact” on public schools. The union’s president, Michael Mulgrew, had said previously that teachers could not afford a loss of resources in the wake of the pandemic, when many students fell behind. The results of national exams show that many children are still struggling to catch up.

Educators are already being forced to contend with overcrowded classrooms, a lack of supplies and gaps in therapy services for children with disabilities, Mr. Mulgrew said.

“Suddenly, the budget projections aren’t so bad, just as we’ve been saying from the start,” the union said in a statement posted online. “Now, he needs to keep going and cancel the rest of his school budget cuts.”

The union did not say whether its suit would proceed.

When the mayor first ordered the cuts across agencies, union officials and some officials questioned whether he had overstated migrant-related costs in an effort to push President Biden to offer the city more help to deal with the crisis.

“The law does not permit school funding to be used as a political bargaining chip,” the union said in its suit.

This week, the mayor said that the Police Department could proceed with adding 600 recruits in April, after warning of a hiring freeze. He reversed plans to eliminate a firefighter from each of the city’s 20 five-member engine companies. And he canceled plans to remove 9,000 of roughly 23,000 curbside trash baskets.

Justin Brannan, a Democratic City Council member from Brooklyn who leads the finance committee, wrote in an opinion column on Friday that the administration was taking a “doom and gloom approach” to the budget process, making “overly pessimistic” projections about the city’s economic health.

“There’s no question that our city faces fiscal challenges,” Mr. Brannan and Nathan Gusdorf, the executive director of the Fiscal Policy Institute, wrote. “But overstating their severity, and making unnecessary broad budget cuts, inflicts real economic harm on working New Yorkers.”

Mr. Adams said on Friday that arguments that “we’re manufacturing a crisis” were “sending the wrong message to New Yorkers.”

“And it’s sending the wrong message to Washington, D.C.,” he added.

Mr. Adams’s announcement came less than 24 hours after a contentious public hearing about the future of mayoral control over the school system, where dozens of parents and educators argued that state lawmakers should remove or limit such authority. Mr. Adams said on Friday that he was meeting families’ needs.

“Why would we mess with what is working?” he said.

The cuts to school spending would have forced a reduction in hours for the city’s summer program, which is so in demand that about 45,000 families who applied last year did not get seats. The cuts would have meant the program ended at 4 p.m. this summer, instead of 6 p.m., and that Friday service would have been eliminated.

The administration will now put $80 million in new city funds into the program, which has been financed partly with federal aid that will expire soon.

Community schools, which offer counseling, clinics and adults classes for disadvantaged families, were also set to lose funding. Four of the schools, including two in Brooklyn — Public School 335 in Crown Heights and Intermediate School 96 in Bensonhurst — would have lost nearly $2 million combined, the teachers’ union said, forcing cuts to staff and specialized programs.

The mayor said on Friday that about 170 schools would have a total of about $10 million in funding restored for those services.

The Education Department said separately that it would not require schools for children with the most advanced disabilities to reduce their budgets. There are about five dozen such schools in the city, and the principals at some of those schools said earlier in the year that they had been asked to cut more than $1 million each, according to reporting by the news outlet Chalkbeat.

The rollbacks would have pushed the schools “even further out of compliance with special education requirements,” the union said in its suit.

Other deep cuts are still expected across the school system and at other city agencies. After the city’s three public library systems were forced to close on Sundays at many branches starting last month, library leaders have expressed concern that they could also be forced to end Saturday service.

Emma G. Fitzsimmons contributed reporting.
