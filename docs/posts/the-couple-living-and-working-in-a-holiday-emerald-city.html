The Couple Living and Working in a Holiday ‘Emerald City’

A decade ago, Emily Eerdmans and her husband, Andrew McKeon, were living in a rented Brooklyn Heights garden apartment with low ceilings. They both worked from home: His office was very small, with a door. Her office was the ivory damask sofa in the living room — a perch she described as the “ideal backdrop for my needlepoint pillow collection.”

Just before her 40th birthday, Ms. Eerdmans, a design historian, decided she needed her own space “to make money.”

She saw a New York Times real estate advertisement for a windowed basement office at 14 East 10th Street, an 1839 townhouse in Greenwich Village. For the next five years, she commuted to her gallery, Eerdmans Fine Art, from the apartment in Brooklyn. Customers ducked under the stoop to enter, the bathroom was freshly renovated and the landlady lived like a modernist on the patrician parlor floor right upstairs.

The landlady died and a year later, in the summer of 2020, Ms. Eerdmans began to envision a live-work duplex.

Fortunately, Mr. McKeon, too, could look past the many sloppy coats of paint and see the potential of the sublime neoclassical architecture. “Even in that glopity-glop state, you couldn’t miss the beauty,” he said. He agreed to give up their Brooklyn apartment to afford the extra rent on 10th Street.

Today, Ms. Eerdmans, 49, turns the duplex into a Christmas wonderland, taking liberties with the holiday decorations.

“Outdoors is Kansas, and you come into the Emerald City,” she said.

Indeed, the upstairs parlor gallery at Eerdmans is lacquered Chic Lime, a Benjamin Moore green originally recommended by her mentor in decorating, Mario Buatta. “Some people want environments to soothe, but mine need to energize,” she said.

Hanging on nails spiked into the shiny walls, an array of framed giclée prints by Tug Rice, a digital illustrator, is Eerdmans merchandise.

Private rooms in the apartment are off-limits to customers, but the public areas do double duty to connect them after business hours. If Ms. Eerdmans or Mr. McKeon, 66, wake up wanting a late-night snack, they walk down the hall, past the office, up the stairs from the basement, through the green parlor and into the adjoining kitchen.

Ms. Eerdmans has a background in design, including a master’s degree from Sotheby’s Institute of Art and work as a researcher and seller of English brown furniture at Hyde Park Antiques. But the duplex was her public debut as a decorator.

Also an author, Ms. Eerdmans took lessons from décor doyens she has written about. The walls of a moody parlor that the couple uses as a private living room have a multilayered purple paint finish that was a favorite of Mr. Buatta. Jaguar-print polypropylene carpet she installed on the stairs recalls woven leopard rugs that Madeleine Castaing, the Paris antiques dealer and designer, installed to camouflage dirt. A coat of Kelly green paint hides “terrible” architectural flaws in the stairwell.

“I just went crazy decorating,” Ms. Eerdmans said.

Perhaps surprisingly, she calls herself not precisely “a Christmas person.” Ms. Eerdmans’s great-grandfather was a Christian publisher in Grand Rapids, Mich. Her mother is an artist who moved to Ohio, followed a macrobiotic diet and eked out a living as a potter. Her mother, who was single, always put presents under the tree, but holidays “were kind of a downer,” Ms. Eerdmans said. “It would break my mother’s heart.”

When the family gained a stepfather who was Jewish, they also lit a menorah, for seven years, but she calls Christmas “the only holiday for us maximalists.”

When it comes to decorating, Ms. Eerdmans said, “Humor is really important to me.”

That’s evident in this year’s theme for the holiday bazaar, Disco Christmas. She asked the interior designer Harry Heissmann, who is based in Newburgh, N.Y., for “jazz hands and pizazz.” Ms. Eerdmans originally hired him in 2022, he said, because “she knew I was a Christmas nut.” Last year, he dressed her big tree to resemble an enormous pineapple. Aloha, SpongeBob.

At the recent opening night of this year’s bazaar, the eighth, offering novelties and seasonal décor through Dec. 22, Ms. Eerdmans baffled her husband by hiring a gender illusionist. “Why do you need a Liza Minnelli impersonator?” asked Mr. McKeon, the executive director of the Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative. Most memorable parties transport guests to another dimension, Ms. Eerdmans said, and “it’s fun to go over the top.”

This year, the 13-foot fresh Christmas tree in the parlor is “for Andrew,” Mr. Heissmann said. Mr. McKeon grew up in the South Bronx, the youngest of eight children living in their grandmother’s Victorian mansion. The Christmas trees were very tall, the ceilings even taller.

“It’s a sentimental time,” he said.

Mr. Heissmann decorated the parlor tree with disco balls and tinsel icicles “to the max,” under a cloud of metallic balloons that Mr. McKeon taped to the ceiling. It’s a riff on Andy Warhol’s 1966 “Silver Clouds” installation, floating balloons puffed with helium and air. The print of a 1970 photo showing the back of Mr. Warhol’s head by Dustin Pittman, the nightlife photographer, hangs in the basement and sells for $2,500 on the store website.

The former basement office is now a gallery where small tinsel trees are hung with blown-glass pigeons and poop emoji ornaments, all for sale. The basement gallery walls sport mirrored mylar, and Mr. Heissmann hung a fringed tinsel miniskirt at the ceiling since he has had bad luck with fresh pine garlands indoors. “It’s not pretty getting out the vacuum every day to suck needles off the floor,” he said.

After the bazaar ends, the couple will load up their golden British Shorthair Cat, Babs, and pug, Pompey, and drive eight hours to Castine, Maine, where they have a seven-bedroom villa. A 1920s Spanish colonial revival by William Lawrence Bottomley, the New York society architect, the villa was known locally as “the pink palace,” even after a former owner whitewashed its stucco exterior.

Mr. McKeon plans to cut yet another tree in the woods and set the library mantel at the villa with a century-old, tabletop ceramic crèche from Vatican City. (It was a gift to his parents from a favorite nun.) Ms. Eerdmans moved her mother into the old chauffeur apartment above the former garage, and a crew has already repainted half the stucco in a shell pink. Ms. Eerdmans shared plans to have white shutters on the outside, with her characteristic enthusiasm, but she reported that her husband “said he would divorce me if I got a pet white peacock.”

For now, the couple is staying in the bedroom of the duplex with a door that opens out to the backyard. The walls are papered with a print from the 2017 Vogue Living collection that Schumacher developed with Hamish Bowles — just one more very personal restoration of the sort usually reserved for apartments people own.

“Life is too short. Why waste a single day living in a dingy white box?” Ms. Eerdmans said.
