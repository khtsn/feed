Trump Said He Was ‘Not Happy’ With Putin

President Trump said today that he was “very disappointed” that Russia had continued to drop bombs on Ukraine while the U.S. was trying to quickly broker a deal to end the war. However, Trump declined to suggest any potential consequences for the Kremlin.

Instead, the president said that he would wait to see the results of negotiations over the next two weeks to establish whether President Vladimir Putin of Russia was stringing him along. Some of Trump’s allies in Congress have urged him to impose new sanctions on Russia. But he said that he hadn’t done so because he felt he was close to a deal to end the war in Ukraine.

Trump also argued that his recent criticism of Putin — who he said had “gone absolutely CRAZY” over the weekend — spoke “pretty loud.”

In the Oval Office today, Trump issued a warning to another foreign leader he has spoken approvingly of: He said he had told Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu of Israel that it would be inappropriate to take action against Iran right now, during negotiations for a nuclear deal.

The U.N. condemned Israel’s new aid program in Gaza

A senior U.N. official denounced Israel’s new aid system in Gaza as “an assault” on the dignity of Palestinians there. The U.N. and many other humanitarian groups have boycotted the initiative, which got off to a chaotic start yesterday.

The U.N. World Food Program said that crowds “of hungry people broke into” its warehouse in central Gaza today, and that at least two people had died.

“Israel says the new system makes it harder for Hamas to steal and hoard food,” Patrick Kingsley, our Jerusalem bureau chief, said. “The U.N. warns it will put civilians at risk by forcing them to walk for miles to a handful of sites in Israeli-controlled areas.”

Haiti hired an American contractor to fight gangs

Erik Prince, a private military contractor and prominent ally of Trump, is working with Haiti’s government to conduct lethal operations against gangs that are terrorizing the nation.

Prince’s team has deployed attack drones in Haiti that are meant to kill gang members, and he recently shipped a large cache of weapons to the country. Security experts also said that he had been scouting Haitian American military veterans and was expected to send up to 150 mercenaries to Haiti over the summer.

A Missouri town backed Trump. Then Carol was detained.

For 20 years, Carol Hui served waffles, raised her children and embraced the conservative farming hub of Kennett, Mo. She hugged customers and got to know everyone. Then, on April 30, she was arrested and told that she would be deported.

Her detention forced Kennett to face the fallout of Trump’s immigration crackdown, which most locals had supported in theory. Many residents rallied to support her, and the diner she worked at held a “Carol Day” fund-raiser. But others were glad to see Hui detained. “We’ve got to get rid of all the illegals,” one man said. “This is just a start.”

In other immigration news, A judge said the government should release a Russian scientist because the U.S. had “no factual or legal basis” for revoking her visa.

Also: Federal agents are showing up unannounced to interview minors for what they call “wellness checks.” Critics see these visits as part of the immigration crackdown.

The world in 1,726 wondrous objects

A renovated wing of the Metropolitan Museum of Art, with works from Africa, the Americas and Oceania, opens on Saturday after four years.

The 1,726 objects on display — majestic wood figures from Africa, mythical beings cast in gold from Mexico, a Sistine-worthy ceiling of the South Seas from New Guinea — aren’t just beautiful. They represent the spiritual, political and emotional lives of people spread over five continents and eight millenniums. Take a look.

‘Duck Dynasty’ is coming back for a second term

Just two days after Trump returned to the White House for a second term, A&E announced that it had ordered 20 new episodes of its hit reality comedy “Duck Dynasty.” The network didn’t officially link the two developments. But as our television critic James Poniewozik points out, they didn’t have to.

The show, about a family making a fortune selling duck calls, was also a precursor of the conservative identity politics that swept in after it. It was filled with cultural signifiers — beards, Bibles and buckshots — that spoke to the authenticity of rural life in a way that much pop culture did not. The reboot, which focuses on the family’s media empire, captures a new era.

The ballet kids bring the magic

The New York City Ballet’s staging of George Balanchine’s “A Midsummer Night’s Dream” is both grand and carefree, irresistible for its sweetness. A group of exuberant young performers are responsible for that.

They are the two dozen children who play the Fairies and the Butterflies scurrying across the forest stage. The City Ballet informally calls them “the Bugs” — a small but mighty squad of fleet-footed girls, ages roughly 10 to 12. They are the real stars of “Midsummer.”
