By Euan WardLiam StackAric Toler and Michael Levenson

Euan Ward reported from Beirut, Lebanon, and Liam Stack from Tel Aviv.

Oct. 10, 2024Updated 6:26 p.m. ET

Israeli airstrikes hit two buildings in a densely populated area of central Beirut on Thursday, Lebanon said, in what appeared to be the deadliest attack in the Lebanese capital in more than a year of fighting between Israel and Hezbollah.

The strikes killed at least at least 22 people and wounded at least 117 others, Lebanese officials said.

Lebanon’s state-run news agency reported that one of the strikes had targeted the middle of an eight-story building in the Ras el-Nabaa neighborhood, while the other had leveled a four-story building in the Basta neighborhood. Videos verified by The New York Times showed that the building in Basta had been destroyed and adjacent buildings had been damaged. Two plumes of thick, acrid smoke could be seen rising above the city skyline.

The Israeli military did not immediately respond to a request for comment on the strikes. But it has been systematically targeting Hezbollah leaders and sites in a heavy bombing campaign in areas in and around Beirut and in a ground invasion in southern Lebanon.

The strikes came hours after United Nations officials said that Israeli forces had fired on U.N. peacekeepers in southern Lebanon, wounding two and touching off international criticism of the Israeli military’s offensive against Hezbollah, the Iranian-backed militant group.

The U.N. peacekeeping force in Lebanon, known by its acronym, UNIFIL, said the two soldiers, from Indonesia, had been injured when an Israeli tank fired toward and directly hit an observation tower at the force’s headquarters in Naqoura, Lebanon.

At a separate base nearby, the U.N. force said, Israeli soldiers fired at the entrance of a bunker where peacekeepers were sheltering, damaging vehicles and communications equipment.

The U.N. mission condemned the strikes, saying in a statement that “any deliberate attack on peacekeepers is a grave violation of international humanitarian law.”

The Israeli military said on Thursday that Israeli troops operating in the Naqoura area, next to a UNIFIL base, had instructed the U.N. forces in the area to move to “protected spaces,” and then “opened fire in the area.” The military said that Hezbollah “operates from within and near civilian areas in southern Lebanon, including areas near UNIFIL posts.”

Hezbollah has used southern Lebanon as a staging ground for rocket and drone attacks on Israel for more than a year, beginning right after its ally Hamas led an assault on southern Israel on Oct. 7, 2023.

Last month, Israel stepped up its campaign against Hezbollah, blowing up pagers and walkie-talkies used by the group’s members and launching airstrikes that killed its leader and other top commanders, as well as many civilians. Israeli ground troops have since invaded southern Lebanon and have battled Hezbollah’s fighters in deadly, close-range combat.

Since 1978, UNIFIL, the United Nations Interim Force in Lebanon, which has more than 10,000 civilian and military personnel from 50 countries, has had the task of maintaining peace along a volatile 75-mile stretch of territory called the Blue Line, the de facto border to which Israel withdrew after ending its occupation of southern Lebanon in 2000.

Although the peacekeepers are armed, their mission is largely observational, and they are generally restricted to using force only when their safety or the safety of civilians is immediately threatened.

The United States and Israel have argued that the peacekeepers have been largely ineffective at preventing Hezbollah from stockpiling weapons and firing into Israel. On Thursday, Hezbollah launched 190 more rockets and other projectiles into Israel, the Israeli military said.

The U.N. peacekeepers stationed in southern Lebanon have faced increasing attacks since the fighting between Israel and Hezbollah has escalated, U.N. officials say, including Israeli machine gun fire and shelling from tanks.

On Thursday, several countries that contribute troops to the U.N. force in Lebanon, including France, Italy and Spain, criticized the Israeli military’s firing on peacekeepers.

The Italian defense minister, Guido Crosetto, called the actions “very serious violations of the norms of international law, not justified by any military reason.” Italy has one of the largest contingents of soldiers in UNIFIL.

France also expressed “its deep concern,” the foreign ministry said, adding that it was “awaiting explanations from the Israeli authorities.”

“The protection of peacekeepers is an obligation imposed on all parties to a conflict,” the ministry added in a statement. “France calls on the parties to respect this obligation.”

Israel’s ambassador to the United Nations, Danny Danon, issued a statement calling on U.N. peacekeepers to move five kilometers north “to avoid danger as fighting intensifies” while “the situation along the Blue Line remains volatile as a result of Hezbollah’s aggression.”

He added that “Israel has no desire to be in Lebanon, but it will do what is necessary” to force Hezbollah away from the border so that more than 60,000 residents could return to their homes in northern Israel.

Asked about the request to move, Farhan Haq, a spokesman for the United Nations secretary general, António Guterres, said: “While we continue to assess the safety and security of our peacekeeping forces, it’s important that they also fulfill their mandate, and they are, at present, staying in their positions.”

Israel’s offensive in Lebanon has expanded at the same time as Israeli military operations in the Gaza Strip have appeared to intensify, prompting alarm from U.N. officials about the worsening humanitarian situation in the enclave and the danger to civilians. The death toll in Gaza has passed 42,000, the Gazan health ministry says.

On Thursday, the United Nations agency for children, UNICEF, said that an airstrike on a school in central Gaza killed women and children who were lining up for malnutrition treatment.

“This attack serves as yet another stark reminder of the immense toll being inflicted on children and mothers in Gaza,” Adele Khodr, UNICEF’s director for the Middle East and North Africa, said on social media, noting that two health workers from a partner agency had also been killed in the strike.

The Israeli military said on Thursday that its troops and Air Force had struck militants operating a command center in a former school compound in Gaza and that “numerous steps were taken to mitigate the risk to civilians.”

In a separate attack, Iran’s Red Crescent Society said that Israel had bombed its field hospital on the border between Lebanon and Syria. Iranian state television footage showed the structure had been destroyed and firefighters were hosing down the flames. The hospital had 56 beds, state television said, adding that Iranian Red Crescent workers were safe.

Amid anticipation of an Israeli response to an Iranian barrage of nearly 200 missiles last week, Israel’s security cabinet met on Thursday night, officials said. The cabinet was expected to authorize Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu and the defense minister, Yoav Gallant, to initiate the response at their discretion, the officials said.

Mr. Gallant posted a video on Wednesday saying that Israel’s attack against Iran would be “deadly, precise and, above all, surprising.” He added: “They will not understand what happened and how it happened. They will see the results.”

President Biden has urged Mr. Netanyahu not to hit Iran’s nuclear or energy sites, which the White House fears could lead to an escalating cycle of Iranian missile strikes and Israeli reprisals.
