    Book Bans and What to Read in May

Jennifer Szalai discusses recent books about natural history, and Jeff Goodell talks about his new climate change book, “The Heat Will Kill You First.”

Subscribe: Apple Podcasts | Spotify | Stitcher | How to Listen

It’s been hard to escape the feeling this summer that, after years of warnings, climate change is starting to make itself felt in everyday life: Floods, wildfires and deadly heat waves have all made headlines for months, and it looks as if July will be the hottest month ever recorded. In that sense Jeff Goodell’s book “The Heat Will Kill You First” — about the real-world costs and consequences of a warming planet — feels particularly urgent at the moment. On this week’s podcast, Goodell talks with Gilbert Cruz about air conditioning, urban heat traps and the effects of extreme heat on the human body, among other things.

“I’ve been working on this book for four years,” Goodell says, “and for it to be out now. at this moment when we have these searing heat waves around the world, is at once a kind of vindication of the kind of direction that I was going with the book, but also just plain spooky. It sometimes feels like I’m living in my own Stephen King novel. On the other hand, I often feel also that this is a privileged moment, because people are paying attention and talking about these things in a way that I think focuses the mind more than other times.”

Also on this week’s episode, our nonfiction critic Jennifer Szalai discusses a handful of natural history books she has reviewed recently, Goodell’s among them.

Here are the books discussed on this week’s episode:

  • “The Heat Will Kill You First,” by Jeff Goodell

  • “What an Owl Knows,” by Jennifer Ackerman

  • “Blight: Fungi and the Coming Pandemic,” by Emily Monosson
