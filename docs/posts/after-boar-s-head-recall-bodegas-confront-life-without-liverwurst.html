After Boar’s Head Recall, Bodegas Confront Life Without Liverwurst

Cold cuts linked to a nationwide listeria outbreak have vanished from bodega and deli counters across New York City. For some shops, business is down, too.

At delis and bodegas across New York City and New Jersey this week, the same scene has repeated itself: A Boar’s Head delivery truck pulls up outside, but instead of delivering meat, uniformed workers descend upon the store’s deli counter, hauling away dozens of pounds of cold cuts.

For Jimmy Estevez, the owner of Jimmy’s Deli on Staten Island, the truck came twice — once last week and again on Tuesday, when Boar’s Head expanded a list of recalled meats. The men from the truck looked in his meat case, scanning bar codes and examining labels. They carted away ham, salami and bologna. Then they asked for his liverwurst.

“Liverwurst?” he said. “I don’t sell it.”

The recall — initially issued on July 25 over concerns of tainted liverwurst and now affecting seven million pounds of meat — was felt intimately in the city, where bodegas are ubiquitous, and Boar’s Head is the cold cut of choice.

The company issued its recall after some of its products were linked to a nationwide listeria outbreak, which has killed two people and sickened almost 40 more. Twelve people have become ill in New York, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, more than any other state, and one of the two deaths has occurred in New Jersey.

At least 61 Boar’s Head products have been recalled, including liverwurst, bologna, ham bacon, bratwurst and pâté.

Listeria bacteria is naturally found in soil and can contaminate a range of foods, including fruits and vegetables as well as meat. Most people can consume listeria without getting sick, but some people, including pregnant women, older adults and those who are immunocompromised, can become seriously ill and, in some cases, die.

Mr. Estevez, who opened his deli in 2015, said this was the first time he had ever had to deal with a recall. Jose Collado, who owns bodegas in the Bronx and on the Lower East Side of Manhattan, said he was worried that news of the listeria outbreak might scare customers away.

But Fernando Mateo, a spokesman for the United Bodegas of America, a trade group representing thousands of bodegas, delis and convenience stores in the city, said that a majority of owners were not overly concerned.

Boar’s Head has pledged to give the owners credit for the recalled meat, Mr. Mateo said, but some small-business owners may lose out on a few hundred dollars of inventory in the interim. A manager at D&F Italian Deli in Astoria, Queens, said that he had lost 40 pounds of meat in the recall, but that the bigger problem was that cold cut sales were down about 50 percent.

Ralph Jawad began working at his father’s deli, Ralph’s, in Fort Greene, Brooklyn, in 1977. For more than a quarter-century, he said, he has sold Boar’s Head products at the store. Mr. Jawad said he had heard from a number of customers who were concerned about the recall.

“People are still scared,” he said. “You can’t blame them.”

Mr. Jawad said business had dropped about 30 percent in the past week. Some would-be customers had come in and, after seeing the Boar’s Head logo on the menu, asked for recommendations for other places to get food, he said.

Annie Capellan, who works at Jerannie Deli Grocery in the Bedford-Stuyvesant neighborhood in Brooklyn, also said that business was down.

“The people see the news,” she said.

Still, not all bodegas have suffered.

On the west side of the Bronx, business largely went on normally, as many delis did not carry the recalled products. And in Hoboken, N.J., Bassem Gabrielle, who runs Delight Deli and Grocery, said that representatives from Boar’s Head had stopped by on Tuesday, but that none of his products had been recalled. Mr. Gabrielle said he felt lucky to have been spared — he mostly sells turkey.

Daniel Munoz, an owner of Cherry Valley Deli and Grill, a popular sandwich spot in Whitestone, Queens, also said that he felt fortunate, since most of his business centers on chicken cutlets. Even so, a Boar’s Head truck driver recently took away between 30 and 50 pounds of cold cuts — mostly bologna and, yes, liverwurst.

“Matter of fact, an elderly guy came in who wanted liverwurst on rye,” Mr. Munoz said. “He says, ‘What do you mean you don’t have any?’”

The man was disappointed when Mr. Munoz explained the recall to him. He then asked for a bologna sandwich, which led to more disappointment. Eventually, the customer walked away with a ham sandwich. It wasn’t Boar’s Head.

Nate Schweber and Liset Cruz contributed reporting.
