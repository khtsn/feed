Ron DeSantis and Gavin Newsom’s Fox News Debate Was a Contest With No Prize

Critic’s Notebook

Ron DeSantis, Gavin Newsom and a not-exactly-neutral Sean Hannity staged a campaign debate from an alternative timeline.

Dec. 1, 2023, 10:22 a.m. ET

Thursday’s made-for-TV exhibition match between Gov. Ron DeSantis of Florida and Gov. Gavin Newsom of California was not, despite its name, a debate. Oh, there was “debating,” or the cable-news version: Sniping, insults, cross-talk and the delivery of packaged attacks between the red and blue leaders of states known for their oranges.

But “The Great Red vs. Blue State Debate” on Fox News was really a work of speculative fiction. Like “Watchmen” or “The Plot Against America,” it was a glimpse into an alternative timeline in which the country somehow took a different path.

In this reality, maybe Joseph R. Biden Jr., president of America-2, decided to pass the baton rather than dig his fingernails into it. Maybe the former president Donald J. Trump gracefully conceded defeat in 2020 and retired from politics to spend more time with his lawyers. Maybe the gerontocracy was yielding, and America-2 was turning a new page.

In our timeline, Mr. Newsom is not running for president, though his media ubiquity has spurred speculation that, were an opening to come available, he could fit it into his calendar. Mr. DeSantis is running for president, but he has been losing ground in the polls and supporter momentum.

So the two of them found themselves in a studio in Georgia with Fox’s Sean Hannity, two not-quite-opponents in a not-quite-presidential debate designed to elevate their profiles, if not the level of political discussion.

The host kicked off the debate saying that while he was a conservative, he planned to moderate the debate evenhandedly. This was the first statement of the night that would not survive a fact check. The list of topics skewed toward conservative targets: blue-to-red-state migration, taxes, immigration and the border, crime, late-term abortions, gas prices and the question of whether President Biden was experiencing “cognitive decline.”

Essentially, Mr. Hannity — despite his friendliness with Mr. Newsom, a past guest — was hosting a Republican primary debate that happened to have a Democrat in it. He played hardball with Mr. Newsom and T-ball with Mr. DeSantis.

But both governors seemed to enjoy the chance to mix it up. Their styles are aggressive but distinct. Mr. DeSantis charges and snaps like a bulldog; Mr. Newsom pivots and glides like a fox. Mr. Newsom called Mr. DeSantis a “bully”; Mr. DeSantis called Mr. Newsom “slick.”

And I don’t know if, deep down, each minds his label. Mr. DeSantis wants to show Republican voters a fighter, in hope of weaning them off Donald Trump (but somehow without attacking Mr. Trump too much). Mr. Newsom has aimed to be the Democrats’ explainer in chief, fluid on TV and perhaps a more effective defender of President Biden than President Biden (but somehow without suggesting himself as a replacement).

Their different goals made for a weird misalignment of incentives. Mr. DeSantis, who is running for president, was in a gubernatorial debate, touting his version of Florida over California. Mr. Newsom, who is not running for president, was in a presidential debate, focusing on national policy.

Mr. DeSantis came with explicit visual aids, including a page from the often-banned graphic memoir “Gender Queer” and a map of feces on San Francisco streets; if he were a book, I’m not sure he would be allowed in a Florida school library. Mr. Newsom repeatedly needled Mr. DeSantis for his fading in the primary polls. Each spent a lot of time barking over the other, while Mr. Hannity pleaded for them not to make him be “a hall monitor.”

Yet as strange and meaningless as the debate was, I will at least give it points for taking a different approach to a moribund TV routine. (Also for not having a studio audience to feed the chaos by whooping and booing.)

Was this a manufactured spectacle, an exercise in hot air far removed from the real high-stakes contest for the presidency? Sure. But then what do you call the actual Republican primary debates, which the prohibitive front-runner hasn’t shown up for?

Still, it’s hard to invest in a contest with no prize. This is the point in a debate-analysis piece where we might assess which candidate won. But what does it even mean to “win” this pseudo debate? Mr. DeSantis and Mr. Newsom were not running for governor of each other’s states. Americans will still gladly visit Disney parks in both Orlando and Anaheim.

But they each got to score points with the sort of partisans who will tune in to a novelty cable debate on a weeknight to see their guy beat up on the other guy. Fox got its 90-minutes-plus of politico-tainment. (Mr. Hannity, mercilessly, let the debate run long and tried to push it to a full two hours.)

And everyone else who watched? We got to spend a little bit of time in America-2 — whether or not we would actually want to live there.
