Aulon "⁠Krabeni⁠" Fazlija has climbed a place in the latest HLTV Prospects ranking, leading to half of the top six spots being occupied by FUT players. Džiugas "⁠dziugss⁠" Steponavičius has kept hold of number one, and Dmytro "⁠dem0n⁠" Myroshnychenko sits third after an impressive series against Vitality in Texas.

The old NAVI Junior team has consolidated its place in tier one after their win at PGL Bucharest, and sit at 4th on the VRS despite needing to play BLAST Rivals with their coach. As a reminder, this month's ranking goes up to May 6, so PGL Astana and IEM Atlanta will not be included until next month.

Krabeni's high placing is helped by his impressive 1.06 rating against top 20 opponents in the last six months, the second-best among the eight prospects who have more than 40 maps against those ranks. He also has 101.3 ADR in round wins in our sample, a seriously impressive figure for a player who also has in-game leading responsibilities.

Krabeni's aforementioned teammates, dem0n and dziugss, were introduced to the game by their brothers, but in classic Kosovan fashion, FUT's in-game leader first cut his chops in the country's internet cafes.

At first, he would go and play any game available before focusing more on Counter-Strike. In 2018, like many young teenagers, he put his focus on the game of the moment, Fortnite, until an update came out in late 2019 that he didn't like, prompting him to return to the king of shooters.

Krabeni didn't look back and started to take Counter-Strike more and more seriously. During the pandemic, with the internet cafes closed, he even rented a computer to keep grinding from home, spending over ten hours a day playing. Eventually, once the lockdowns subsided and he could return to long hours at the internet cafes, his father gave in and bought him a computer.

"I think it was because he couldn't really stop me from going to the internet cafe and spending money there," Krabeni says of his father's gift. "So he just decided to buy it and better play from home because I was going out to the internet cafe until like 4 or 5 AM. He would get mad at me sometimes, and after a few years, he decided to buy it for me."

Grinding online and on FPL-C for years, Krabeni joined a mix-team, ESK, and went to his first LAN in his home city of Pristina, XP Game Fest 2022, where they lost to BLINK. He was the highest-rated player on his team with a 1.18 in the series, and one month later would be offered a spot on BLINK, where he played until April of 2023. That LAN in Pristina was when Krabeni started to see that there was a real opportunity for him to make a real career in Counter-Strike.

In the first two or three days, Kairi already wrote to me saying that he wanted me on the team, but I didn't know that I would be the IGL

Aulon "⁠Krabeni⁠" Fazlija on trying out for NAVI Junior

At this time, Bad News Eagles were in their prime, qualifying for three Majors, starting with PGL Antwerp 2022, then IEM Rio 2022, and BLAST.tv Paris 2023, and by then, Krabeni was already in grind mode, which is why they may not have really had an outsized impact on him. "I'm good friends with all of them," he says. "We play FACEIT, and just talk. I have a good relationship with all of them.

"But when they were in their prime, I was already playing in BLINK, and I knew that CS could become very profitable, so I was grinding anyway."

Krabeni (left) with BLINK at the Esports Adria Championship Season 6 Finals in July 2022

Profits weren't something Krabeni was focused on particularly, but it wasn't anything to scoff at, either. "I wasn't thinking about the money, but I was thinking that if I just play, play, play, and get better, the money would come by itself," he says. "In Kosovo, salaries are very low, so obviously, with the money you make at a higher tier of CS, it makes your life easier."

Krabeni joined another mix-team after his time in BLINK, named KS, where he teamed up with Drin "⁠makazze⁠" Shaqiri for the first time. "It didn't end well," Krabeni recalls, but he formed a bond with the future Natus Vincere star.

"We had some invites from academy teams, but we wanted to go together," Krabeni recalls. "Whatever academy we wanted to go to, we wanted to go together. We decided to go for NAVI because it was huge at the time. It has always been a huge organization, but it was also at that moment a top-three team."

After passing the tryouts alongside makazze, Krabeni was made the team's in-game leader, which he was skeptical about at the time. "It was very random, I would say, because when I had the tryouts, it was Kairi who was coaching at the time," he recalls.

"I was always active when it came to speaking, and we had like two weeks of trials with NAVI Junior," he adds. "In the first two or three days, Kairi already wrote to me saying that he wanted me on the team, but I didn't know that I would be the IGL."

Kairi, during his time as NAVI Junior's coach, set Krabeni on the path to leadership

It was, at first, a sneaking suspicion. "After the last day of tryouts, when I got to know who was going to be on the team, like makazze, I knew he wasn't going to be the IGL," Krabeni says. "Magic, the AWPer, I knew that he wasn't going to be the IGL. Then it was fnl and dem0n, and I realized that Kairi was probably going to make me the IGL.

"I remember telling him that if I didn't like it or if I thought it wasn't fitting me after two weeks, I would want to leave or replace someone on the team as IGL, because maybe it wouldn't fit.

"The first week maybe didn't go well, but after that, I realized that I really liked the role because, also in real life, I like people to listen to me," Krabeni adds, laughing. "Since then, I've never wanted to change. I will always commit to being IGL because I like it."

The times were good in NAVI Junior, and Krabeni enjoyed playing with his countryman. The atmosphere they had was excellent, and many times they'd sit on TeamSpeak after the officials to just talk about the games, or practice, or anything that would come up.

I'm trying to grow in every term as a leader, inside and outside of the game, and I think that slowly, with the experience from playing in good LANs, it's getting better and better

Aulon "⁠Krabeni⁠" Fazlija

For Krabeni, losing makazze to the main team was bittersweet. It was sad to lose a teammate he had such a good rapport with, but at the same time, he was waiting for the moment when one of them would get the call-up and could show the world how good the players on that academy squad really were.

Soon, it became clear that it wasn't only makazze who was ready for a call-up, and that the team had outgrown their status as an academy squad. Unable to play at the highest level because they were blocked by Natus Vincere's main team, the juniors still made it to the top 20 in the world ranking. A sale was imminent and FUT came knocking.

Finally, after some time to adjust and find the perfect fit for the team as makazze's successor in Laurențiu "⁠lauNX⁠" Țârlea, the former Juniors were rewarded. "It's like a dream for all of us to be playing these tier one tournaments because before that we were grinding," Krabeni recalls.

"Some days we had like three best-of-threes a day, or four best-of-ones and a best-of-three, playing online, online, just to get the chance to play at the level we're at now," he adds. "What's good about our team is that we have been together for two or more years. What's new is only coming from lauNX, because the other five of us know each other very well."

Being 21 can be hard for an in-game leader, as it is a position that often falls on older, more experienced players, who can also take on leadership aspects outside of the server. "If you're playing for a lot of years in tier one, as a star player, or anchor, or whatever, and then you decide to be IGL, you have the experience," Krabeni says.

"But for me, I'm trying to grow in every term as a leader inside and outside of the game, and I think that slowly, with the experience from playing in good LANs, it's getting better and better," he adds. "Of course, I'm not like FalleN, the leadership, but I'm just trying to get better and better."

Krabeni holding the hardware at PGL Bucharest, FUT's first notable event trophy

At first, Krabeni himself struggled when his teammates were struggling, and didn't know how to react. "I was always trying to watch not only CS, but also leaders in other sports, trying to learn some words or some things to say in a certain situation," Krabeni says. "Now, at the moment, I'm trying to help my teammate if there's something wrong.

"I'm always trying to speak, like in-game or outside of the game, I'm a bit maybe blaming my team, but sometimes I know that I was wrong and I'll write to them. It's not that they reply to me in a bad way, but in-game, and also outside of the game, I'm a bit of an emotional guy, and I want to win always, and sometimes maybe I go over the limit."

One of the things that sets Krabeni apart from many leaders is his high fragging output. The Kosovan player has a 1.05 rating since joining FUT, which is nothing to scoff at for an in-game leader given the team's youth and the competition they now play against (98% fixture difficulty in our prospect ranking). In FUT's title run at PGL Bucharest, he posted a 1.10 rating, he has 101 ADR over 129 maps, and he does not drop off against top opposition, averaging a 1.06 against top 20 teams.

Krabeni, as a fragging in-game leader, is of course matched up with the likes of Garidmagnai "⁠bLitz⁠" Byambasuren, a player who is not afraid of playing roles and will not just put himself in the worst positions for the team's sake. That doesn't mean he won't take some of the dirtier jobs, such as making space or being a grenadier, as can be seen in the table above.

"I know that I can have an impact also going second and killing," Krabeni says. "In our team, we're not saying that I should go first and die for the team, we always try to mix it up, a few times it's me, a few times it's someone else, because everyone on this team knows that they can go second and trade, or go first and kill.

"It's not like the IGL should just die for the team, and I won't say names, but I don't really like that in big teams, the IGLs sacrifice a lot by dying. Of course, I sacrifice in other terms, like maybe utility or taking space or something like this, but it's not that every time I should just die for the team. For me, at least, I don't see it as enjoyable to go die and just spectate."

FUT are by now ready to graduate our prospect ranking after becoming one of the best teams in Counter-Strike. As they head into their first Major, which was a dream for Krabeni, he says that the next step is winning a premier event in front of a roaring crowd. "We won PGL Bucharest, and there were The MongolZ, PARIVISION, and Astralis — top 10 teams —, but I want to win some good tournaments with big crowds," he says. "My goal is to win an S-tier tournament with every big team (in attendance)."
