A New York Official Is Filling Community Boards With Pro-Housing Members

New York City community boards are known for rejecting development. In Manhattan, one politician is revamping them with appointees who say they are committed to easing the housing crisis.

July 15, 2024, 3:00 a.m. ET

Solutions to the housing crisis often face a small but mighty roadblock: Local residents tend to resist more development in their neighborhoods, and they typically have clout.

This has often been true in New York City, where groups of activists and civic leaders — sitting on 59 community boards — have earned a reputation for saying no to new housing.

Now, Mark Levine, the borough president of Manhattan, is trying to flip that narrative. He is using his power to appoint board members who are amenable to change — and to replace those who would stand in the way. Of the more than 100 people he has appointed or reappointed, he characterized more than 80 percent as “pro-housing,” as determined by a questionnaire he gives them before they are chosen.

His efforts have been contentious, with opponents accusing him of capitulating to the real estate industry. But his practices may already be having an effect.

“Community boards have an extremely important voice,” Mr. Levine said. “And my office, this is an area where I have some direct levers to pull.”

Mr. Levine’s push is yet another indicator of how a crushing housing crisis is reshaping New York City politics. A recent survey indicated that the city’s shortage is the worst it has been in half a century, fueling a rise in the cost of living that could push rents to break records again this summer.

Mayor Eric Adams and Gov. Kathy Hochul have both backed major proposals to make it easier to build homes, the sort of ideas that have drawn opposition from community boards in the past. But on Monday, the chairs of Manhattan’s community boards voted to support some key parts of the mayor’s efforts.

“What we’re seeing is a rapidly shifting consensus toward pro-housing policies,” said Annemarie Gray, the executive director of Open New York, a nonprofit that supports more development and has encouraged its members to apply to sit on community boards.

Community boards do not have any explicit power to reject development proposals. But they usually must be consulted. Their opinions — seen as a proxy for how voters might feel about a project — can influence the City Council members who do have the power to approve or reject the proposals.

Critics of the boards say members are not actually representative of the public. They do not always reflect their neighborhood’s racial and ethnic diversity, and homeowners and older people tend to be overrepresented.

“Part of the challenge is diversifying our community boards — ensuring renters and public housing residents, for example, are adequately represented,” said Antonio Reynoso, the Brooklyn borough president.

Mr. Reynoso said he has also been appointing pro-development people to community boards. “But the other part of this work, shifting mindsets within community boards and across neighborhoods, is just as important,” he said.

Mr. Levine appointed Tibita Kaneene, a banker, to a community board representing parts of the Upper West Side in 2022. To get the position, Mr. Kaneene said he had to write an essay and sit for an interview, in which he proved his pro-housing bona fides.

Mr. Kaneene, who had worked on financing affordable housing projects in the past, said his main motivation for joining the board was to help fix the housing crisis in the city, which he mentioned during his interview.

“I wanted to build more housing, in a smart way, that helps lower the cost of housing and provides more housing for people,” he said.

Now, he chairs the board’s land use and housing committee.

The efforts to flip community boards have not come without controversy, particularly in Midtown, where development projects around Penn Station and at a Chelsea public housing complex are drawing public scrutiny.

Several members of the local community board resigned, criticizing the influence of “special interests” because some newer board members are affiliated with Open New York. (Ms. Gray said that she knew of about 25 Open New York members who had been appointed to community boards.) After they resigned, an Open New York employee became the board’s chair.

When another longtime member of the board sought to unseat that person and become chair, Mr. Levine did not reappoint him to the board.

Mr. Levine has not publicly said why he was not reappointed. The board member also sat on a parent leadership council for the Education Department, and was under fire for supporting an effort to review whether transgender girls should be allowed to participate in school sports.

Mr. Levine, who opposed that effort, said he does not comment on specific appointment decisions. But he said “reappointment is a privilege.”

His efforts have drawn other opponents.

John Mudd, the president of the Midtown South Community Council, a nonprofit group that supports tenants and works on other civic issues, said that “stacking” community boards with people who support development meant that the boards would prioritize profits. On Thursday, Mr. Mudd wrote a letter to state and city officials about the influence of developers and pro-housing groups.

“They’re circumventing what the community board was meant to be,” he said, adding, “They’re going to destroy the city.”

Mihir Zaveri covers housing in the New York City region for The Times.
