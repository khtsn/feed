Missouri Attorney General Fights to Keep Exonerated Prisoners Behind Bars

In Missouri, Attorney General Andrew Bailey is in a tough primary fight against a Trump lawyer who says Bailey is soft on crime.

Shaila Dewan

Aug. 5, 2024Updated 1:10 p.m. ET

Over the decades he spent in prison, sentenced to life for a fatal shooting in 1990, Christopher Dunn faced one obstacle after another as he sought to clear his name.

Last month, he ran into yet another. A Missouri judge had exonerated Mr. Dunn and ordered his release from prison. Dressed in a jacket and tie, Mr. Dunn was signing the last bit of paperwork when he overheard the warden taking a call, saying, “We were just about to release him — what do you want me to do?”

The state attorney general, Andrew Bailey, had intervened to keep Mr. Dunn, 52, behind bars. He was ordered to change back into his prison uniform and returned to his cell, leaving the St. Louis prosecutor, who had asked the court to clear Mr. Dunn, scrambling to free him.

It was not the first time that Mr. Bailey had sought to upend an exoneration, and his intervention in the Dunn case came at a critical point in his tenure, with the Missouri primary election scheduled for Tuesday. Mr. Bailey, who took office 17 months ago to fill out his predecessor’s term, is trying to fend off a formidable challenger as he seeks a full four-year term as the state’s top law enforcement officer.

Weeks before trying to block Mr. Dunn’s release, Mr. Bailey moved to keep Sandra Hemme locked up after a judge ruled that she was innocent. Ms. Hemme, who had been in prison for 43 years, spent another month behind bars before the state’s highest court stepped in and ordered her release.

And in June, Mr. Bailey tried unsuccessfully to quash a motion by the St. Louis County prosecutor to exonerate Marcellus Williams, who was convicted of the 1998 murder of a local journalist and faces execution in September.

So far, Mr. Bailey’s efforts have managed to delay but not derail the release of exonerees. But his actions have generated news coverage of him facing off against Democratic prosecutors, at a time when his primary opponent, Will Scharf, a lawyer for former President Donald J. Trump, was attacking Mr. Bailey as soft on crime.

Mr. Trump has endorsed both men, which left Mr. Bailey needing to distinguish himself from Mr. Scharf.

“It’s certainly to his political advantage, going into the primary, not to give any hint of weakness on crime,” even if that means keeping innocent people imprisoned, said Peverill Squire, a political science professor at the University of Missouri.

Voters in many big cities have elected district attorneys who pledge to curb injustices and reduce incarceration. Republican governors and lawmakers in some states have pushed back. Legislatures have tried to expand oversight of district attorneys and wrest control of certain cases. Prosecutors have faced impeachment efforts and even been suspended from office.

Now those tensions have entangled prisoners like Mr. Dunn who have managed to persuade prosecutors that they have a strong innocence claim. In states like Alabama, Illinois, Pennsylvania, Tennessee and Texas, such exoneration cases have raised new questions about what should happen when the justice system makes a mistake, and how much weight a prosecutor’s reversal should carry.

It has been more than three decades since DNA evidence first began to prove that innocent people were serving time for serious crimes. Many of those early innocence claims faced pointed opposition from prosecutors. But as exonerations became more common, the factors that contribute to wrongful convictions, including coerced confessions, withheld evidence and flawed identification procedures, have become more widely understood.

With that in mind, more than 100 prosecutors’ offices across the country have established conviction review units to take a new look at old cases, according to the National Registry of Exonerations.

The jurisdictions include Memphis, where a unit was established in 2022 by the new Shelby County district attorney, Steve Mulroy. A Democrat and former law professor, Mr. Mulroy has been willing to review both questionable convictions and questionable punishments. He supported a successful appeal to move a prisoner off death row because the man was intellectually disabled, a stance that angered Republican lawmakers, even though, as the Memphis Commercial Appeal pointed out, his Republican predecessor had done the same thing in a different case.

Following that, the Republican-dominated Tennessee legislature stripped district attorneys of the power to contest old capital convictions, saying that only the attorney general can represent the state after sentencing.

In other instances, it is judges who are objecting to reconsidering cases in which prosecutors and defense lawyers agree that a conviction should not stand. In a Nashville hearing, a prosecutor presented seven medical experts who disputed the guilt of Russell Maze, who is serving life in prison for killing his child. Judge Steve Dozier declined to overturn the conviction, saying that the experts had not been cross-examined, as they would have been in a trial.

In Austin, Texas, Areli Escobar was convicted in the rape and fatal stabbing of a teenager in 2009, based on forensic evidence from a scandal-plagued crime lab that has since been shut down. After a judge recommended a new trial, the Austin district attorney, José Garza, a Democrat, agreed, saying that the state had used “false, misleading and unreliable DNA evidence” to convict Mr. Escobar. But the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals, made up of nine elected judges, all Republicans, declined to overturn the conviction.

The U.S. Supreme Court ordered the appeals court to reconsider its stance “in light of” the prosecutor’s support for Mr. Escobar. But the appeals court once again declined to vacate the conviction.

Many such conflicts break along party lines, but not all do. This fall the U.S. Supreme Court is scheduled to hear the case of Richard Glossip, a death row prisoner in Oklahoma whose case has led some Republican lawmakers to call for a moratorium on executions. Mr. Glossip, a hotel manager, was convicted of paying a maintenance worker to kill the hotel’s owner in 1997.

After an independent review of the evidence by a Republican former prosecutor, the state attorney general, Gentner Drummond, also a Republican, conceded last year that the case against Mr. Glossip, which relied on the testimony of the actual killer, was deeply flawed. Mr. Drummond asked the state Court of Criminal Appeals to vacate the conviction; it refused.

Mr. Drummond has stopped short of declaring that Mr. Glossip is innocent, but he has asked the U.S. Supreme Court to decide whether a prisoner can be executed when the state’s chief law enforcement officer has acknowledged major constitutional violations in the case.

In St. Louis, Mr. Dunn was convicted of murder in 1991, had when he was 19. The prosecution’s principal witnesses were 12 and 14 at the time of the shooting. Their accounts of the shooting were not consistent, and both eventually recanted.

Mr. Dunn and his lawyers first persuaded a judge of his innocence in 2020. But the judge said that his hands were tied because Missouri allowed only defendants facing execution to make an innocence claim.

The following year, the legislature passed a law allowing prosecutors to file a motion to vacate a conviction “if he or she has information that the convicted person may be innocent or may have been erroneously convicted.”

The St. Louis circuit attorney, Kim Gardner, moved to vacate Mr. Dunn’s conviction in May 2023, just days before she stepped down. Her successor, Gabe Gore, withdrew that motion to conduct his own investigation, and then filed a new motion to vacate in January, noting that Mr. Dunn had a strong alibi and that the state had presented no physical evidence and no motive.

When a judge agreed, and ordered the conviction overturned, Mr. Bailey filed an appeal.

Mr. Gore says that the new law allows Mr. Bailey to participate in exoneration hearings, but not to appeal the outcome.

Mr. Bailey also directed the prison not to release Mr. Dunn, despite the judge’s order to do so.

Asked for comment about his opposition to innocence claims, Mr. Bailey said in a statement that a jury’s decision should be respected: “Too often, people forget about all of the evidence that was used to convict the defendant — the evidence that jury relied on — and the victims. And I want to make sure that we always honor the victims’ voices, because they get forgotten.”

Mr. Bailey’s opponent in the primary, Mr. Scharf, said in an interview that he would not contest a court order to release a prisoner. “You will not find someone harder on crime than me,” Mr. Scharf said. “That having been said, actual innocence claims should be taken very seriously.”

The Missouri Supreme Court cleared the way on Tuesday for Christopher Dunn to be released, more than a week after he was exonerated. He put on his jacket and tie and walked out, a free man at last.

Shaila Dewan covers criminal justice — policing, courts and prisons — across the country. She has been a journalist for 25 years.
