Senator Robert Menendez of New Jersey could appeal the ruling, which could end up delaying his trial for months.

March 14, 2024Updated 7:25 p.m. ET

A Manhattan judge refused on Thursday to dismiss bribery and other charges against Senator Robert Menendez of New Jersey, rejecting an argument that they violated constitutional protections afforded to members of Congress.

Mr. Menendez could appeal the judge’s decision, which might end up delaying his trial for months. It currently is scheduled to begin on May 6.

The ruling does not address other grounds on which Mr. Menendez, a Democrat, is seeking dismissal of the charges.

The senator’s lawyers had asked the judge, Sidney H. Stein of Federal District Court, to throw out the charges, arguing that overzealous prosecutors were criminalizing the normal activity of legislators and flouting the protections given to members of Congress under what is known as the Constitution’s “speech or debate” clause.

Judge Stein, in his 17-page decision, rejected each of Mr. Menendez’s arguments that the conduct cited by prosecutors as criminal was in fact protected legislative activity.

The ruling applied to two charges central to the indictment: that Mr. Menendez attempted to install a U.S. attorney in New Jersey who might be inclined to go easy on a friend accused of federal crimes, and that he steered weapons and aid to Egypt in return for bribes.

“The court finds that none of the allegations at issue concerning the U.S. attorney scheme or the Egyptian aid scheme are protected by the speech or debate clause,” Judge Stein wrote.

Adam Fee, a lawyer for Mr. Menendez, did not indicate whether the senator planned to appeal the decision, saying only that the senator and his lawyers were reviewing the ruling and considering their “legal options.”

He called the indictment “a gross distortion of reality.”

“We continue to have full confidence that a jury will see the truth: that Senator Menendez did nothing wrong,” Mr. Fee said. “We look forward to proceeding to trial, where we intend to clear the name of this devoted lifelong public servant.”

In October, Mr. Menendez, the former chairman of the powerful Senate Foreign Relations Committee, became the first senator in U.S. history to be accused of plotting to be an agent of a foreign government — a charge he strenuously rejects and asked the judge to dismiss.

Judge Stein refused, ruling that the behavior outlined in the indictment against Mr. Menendez — including the sharing of sensitive information about Egyptian embassy staff members — was not exempt from prosecution.

“Surely meetings with, and the provision of information to, Egyptian officials in relation to a corrupt bribery scheme must be viewed as occurring outside of the legislative process,” Judge Stein wrote.

Similarly, regarding Mr. Menendez’s effort to secure the nomination of a friendly U.S. attorney, the judge concluded that the “speech or debate” protection would apply to a senator’s duty to offer advice and consent about a president’s choice for federal prosecutor only after a person was nominated — not before.

Nicholas Biase, a spokesman for the U.S. attorney’s office in Manhattan, declined to comment on the ruling.

The ruling is one of several recent setbacks for Mr. Menendez, who has pleaded not guilty to the charges.

On March 1, one of his co-defendants, Jose Uribe, a former New Jersey insurance broker, pleaded guilty to attempting to bribe the senator with a $60,000 Mercedes-Benz and agreed to cooperate with prosecutors leading the bribery investigation. Within days, Mr. Menendez was charged in an updated indictment in which some of the new counts appeared related to information provided by Mr. Uribe.

Mr. Menendez’s remaining co-defendants — his wife, Nadine Menendez, and two New Jersey businessmen, Wael Hana and Fred Daibes — have also pleaded not guilty.

Judge Stein also recently denied Mr. Menendez’s motion to exclude evidence that his lawyers said was obtained through unconstitutional searches.

Lawyers for Mr. Menendez, in a recent court filing, raised the issue of a possible appeal — perhaps on an expedited basis — if the judge ruled against the senator on the “speech or debate” clause issue. It remains unclear, if an appeal were pursued, how quickly it might be resolved and for how long that could delay the trial.

Judge Stein acknowledged there are broad protections offered by the Constitution to shield members of Congress “from liability for their actions within the ‘legislative sphere,’ even though their conduct, if performed in other than legislative contexts, would in itself be unconstitutional or otherwise contrary to criminal or civil statutes.”

Still, he said that Mr. Menendez had “failed to meet his burden” of establishing that he was immune from prosecution.

If Mr. Menendez does go to trial, the case would be his second federal corruption trial in less than seven years. In 2017, Mr. Menendez avoided conviction in a federal corruption trial in New Jersey, which ended in a hung jury and dismissal of charges against him.
