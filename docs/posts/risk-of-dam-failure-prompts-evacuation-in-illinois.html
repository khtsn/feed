Risk of Dam Failure Prompts Evacuation in Illinois

U.S.|Risk of Dam Failure Prompts Evacuation in Illinois

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/07/16/us/nashville-illinois-dam-failure.html

Residents of about 200 homes in Nashville, Ill., were briefly told to evacuate on Tuesday amid the risk of a dam failure.

July 16, 2024Updated 6:53 p.m. ET

Residents of about 200 homes in Washington County, Ill., who were ordered to evacuate on Tuesday morning because of the threat of a dam failure as strong storms moved through the area were told by late afternoon that they could return to their homes.

“At this time, without further rain, the immediate safety concern has passed,” the Washington County Emergency Management Agency said in a Facebook post after the Army Corps of Engineers had assessed the dam. The county agency said that the assessment had found that a secondary dam had “reacted as designed,” preventing a full dam failure.

Earlier in the day, the officials told residents in Nashville, Ill., about 55 miles southeast of St. Louis, to evacuate, warning that “the failure of the Nashville dam is imminent.”

St. Louis and surrounding areas had also been warned of the potential of flash flooding as rain continued on Tuesday afternoon.

Alex Haglund, a spokesman for the agency, said in an interview earlier Tuesday that water had reached the top of the dam, and that some water was spilling over causing flooding downstream and toward homes nearby.

Some residents said water was waist deep inside their homes, Mr. Haglund said in a statement.

It was unclear how many peopled lived in the evacuation area. The emergency management agency had initially said that about 300 homes needed to be evacuated. About 3,200 people live in Nashville, where rain was expected to continue into the afternoon.

By Tuesday afternoon, the immediate threat to those downstream from the dam had been managed, Mr. Haglund said, adding that many roads remained closed.

One woman needed to be rescued because of flooding in her home, but no injuries had been reported, Mr. Haglund said.

State Senator Jason Plummer said in an interview that local officials were making arrangements to send engineers to the dam to assess the situation.

“Their bigger concern isn’t the water topping it,” he said. “They’re a little concerned about the base of the dam, and if it’s potentially eroding.”

The Nashville Community Center was open for residents to take shelter outside of the evacuation area, according to the emergency management agency.

Portions of south-central Illinois, including Washington County, were under a flash flood warning on Tuesday, as strong storms brought heavy rainfall. More than six inches of rain had been recorded as of 1 p.m. local time in Washington County, Mr. Haglund said.

The National Weather Service in St. Louis said that 2 to 6 inches of rain had fallen in some areas as of Tuesday afternoon, with an additional 1 to 3 inches expected.

“Complicating the dam situation are multiple road closures,” Mr. Haglund said in a video update. “This is an ongoing issue.”

Portions of multiple highways and roadways in the area were closed because of flooding, according to the Illinois Department of Transportation. Images that were circulating on social media showed flooded roadways across the area as vehicles appeared to struggle to move through floodwaters.

The storms on Tuesday came after severe weather on Monday night in the Chicago area, where there were reports of tornadoes and damaging winds.

The heavy rainfall in south-central Illinois, the Weather Service said, could bring “life-threatening flash flooding” to creeks, highways and streets across the area.

“Move immediately to higher ground,” the Weather Service said.

Flooding was also a concern across part of the region as storms dumped several inches of rain on Tuesday. St. Louis and surrounding areas in Missouri were under a flash flood warning on Tuesday afternoon as thunderstorms brought the risk of “life-threatening” flash flooding, the Weather Service said. About 1 to 6 inches of rain had fallen in the St. Louis area, and an additional 1 to 3 inches was expected later on Tuesday, forecasters said.

The St. Louis Fire Department said it had responded to reports of vehicles stuck in floodwaters on Tuesday. The Fire Department shared a video on social media of flooding inside a fire station.

In Washington County, Mr. Plummer said that there had been concerns about the Nashville dam, but not as serious as declaring it “being at the point of failure.”

“It’s an old dam,” he said. “It’s been there a long time, and there’s been limited amount of improvements.”

Annie Correal contributed reporting.
