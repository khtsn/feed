The Stakes Behind the Fight Over Lina Khan’s Future at the F.T.C.

A question about the future of antitrust

News that Reid Hoffman, the billionaire LinkedIn co-founder and major Democratic donor, wants Vice President Kamala Harris, if she wins in November, to replace Lina Khan as chair of the F.T.C. provoked outrage on the left. Senator Bernie Sanders, independent of Vermont, called it “unacceptable,” and Tim Wu, the Columbia Law School professor and former Biden antitrust official, demanded that the Harris campaign return Hoffman’s donations.

Khan is perhaps the most consequential regulator for business in the Biden administration, and her fate as F.T.C. chair underscores anxiety among Democrats about how Harris would approach antitrust policy should she become president.

A president can’t fire an F.T.C. chair at will. Per a 1935 Supreme Court case, Humphrey’s Executor v. United States, a president can only remove an F.T.C. commissioner for good cause, which excludes policy agreements.

That said, the president has the power to designate the chair among existing members. So Khan could be stripped of that role and replaced with a colleague, though that could be awkward.

The political calculus of replacing Khan is complicated. Khan’s term expires in September, though she could serve until any replacement is confirmed by the Senate. But nomination processes take time and if Republicans retake the Senate in November, that could make it difficult for a Harris administration to get a different nominee confirmed.

One wrinkle: Donald Trump’s own running mate, Senator JD Vance of Ohio, has praised Khan as part of his anti-Big Tech stance.

Khan has given no indication that she plans to step down. She has shown a desire to permanently reshape U.S. antitrust law, dating to her law school days. When DealBook asked an F.T.C. spokesman if Khan would consider serving as chair in a Harris administration, he said, “Yes.”

The debate underscores Democrats’ anxiety over how Harris would govern. Khan’s appointment arose in part out of Biden’s effort to shore up progressive support in 2020 by giving Senator Elizabeth Warren of Massachusetts significant sway over economic regulatory agencies. “Warren, to an extraordinary degree, was the gatekeeper for the administration about appointments,” William Kovacic, a former F.T.C. chair, told DealBook.

But corporate donors have backed Harris in part because they believe she would be more moderate when it comes to regulating business.

It’s unclear what Harris’s own antitrust views are, or whether she would seek to continue the Biden administration’s approach. “I don’t know if that commitment that Biden made to Warren is going to bind Harris,” Kovacic said. “The crucial question is what is her relationship going to be with the strong intervention-minded members of the Democratic Party who extracted that promise.”

  • In other election news: Barack Obama endorsed Harris, after the former president had sought to stay impartial on the matter. A new Times/Siena poll shows Harris in a virtual tie with Trump among likely voters. And the Black Economic Alliance PAC recently convened a call with more than 250 business leaders — including Charles Phillips, formerly of Infor; Roz Brewer, formerly of Walgreens Boots Alliance; and Ken Frazier, formerly of Merck — asking them to back Harris for president, DealBook is first to report. The B.E.A. PAC has raised more than $3 million for the Democratic presidential ticket.

HERE’S WHAT’S HAPPENING

Train attacks mar the opening day of the Paris Olympics. Service on three high-speed lines was disrupted because of arson attacks overnight, France’s national railway operator said, adding that travelers should postpone trips if possible. The attacks took place amid a drastic tightening of security around Paris ahead of the Games, with thousands of police officers and military personnel deployed to guard venues, tourist sites and more.

U.S. economic growth exceeds expectations. Preliminary data released on Thursday showed that G.D.P., adjusted for inflation, rose 2.8 percent in the second quarter, double the rate in the first three months of the year. That suggests the country remains on track for a soft landing as the Fed prepares rate cuts, and that whoever wins in November would take over a solid economy.

Uber and Lyft score a major legal win in California. The state’s Supreme Court upheld a four-year-old ballot measure classifying the ride-hailing services’ drivers as contractors, not full-time employees. The decision staves off a significant overhaul of the gig economy, though labor activists condemned the ruling.

How China is catching up in A.I.

It’s been a tough week for technology stocks, as investors grow increasingly skeptical about the costs to advance artificial intelligence.

But the race between the U.S. and China to dominate A.I. isn’t slowing down — and the gap between them is narrowing.

Chinese companies have been releasing new products and services that rival American counterparts, The Times’s Meaghan Tobin and Cade Metz write.

A chatbot from 01.AI, a start-up founded by the former Google and Microsoft executive Kai-Fu Lee, performs nearly as well as leading U.S. platforms on common benchmarks. And new software from Alibaba, the Chinese e-commerce giant, topped the rankings for open-source A.I. systems.

Chinese progress comes despite a raft of U.S. export restrictions. The Biden administration has tried to prevent Chinese companies from getting the most powerful chips that power A.I. systems. Lawmakers have also moved to make it easier to restrict the export of top-flight American software.

A key to that is open-source software. Chinese researchers told The Times that the approach was a core factor in their industry’s leaps in innovation. Chinese companies are more willing to share the source code underlying their innovations so that other businesses and developers can build on top of it.

Open source is also at the heart of a major philosophical split among Western developers. Mark Zuckerberg of Meta doubled down on the approach this week, while rivals including Google, Microsoft and OpenAI warned that A.I. software should be more tightly controlled. Officials in Washington are increasingly concerned that A.I. presents a national security risk.

China’s embrace of open source could flip the A.I. narrative. Some of the tech used to build 01.AI’s system came from LLaMA, Meta’s A.I. model. But while American technology has long been considered the gold standard, Stanford University researchers recently introduced a new large language model this year that outperformed others — and is based on an open-source system originally from China.

Some tech executives fear that American efforts to limit open-source development at home could backfire. “If the trend continues, it becomes more and more of a challenge for the U.S.,” Clément Delangue, the C.E.O. of Hugging Face, a prominent open-source A.I. start-up, told The Times.

  • In other A.I. news: OpenAI unveiled a prototype search engine that could be integrated into ChatGPT, causing shares in Alphabet to fall. And Elon Musk said he would ask Tesla’s board to invest $5 billion in xAI, his A.I. start-up.

Building a women’s soccer empire

A burgeoning force in men’s soccer for the past decade has been the investors assembling collections of clubs around the world. Among them are City Football Group, the owner of Manchester City and New York City F.C., and Fenway Sports Group, the parent of Liverpool F.C.

Now Michele Kang — the American who owns Olympique Lyonnais Féminin in France, Washington Spirit in the U.S. and London City Lionesses in Britain — wants to do the same for women’s soccer.

Kang will announce the creation of Kynisca Sports International, DealBook is first to report. The name is inspired by Cynisca of Sparta, the first woman to win at an Olympic Games, and Kynisca intends to assemble teams globally and invest in growing their fan bases and facilities while providing shared resources like training equipment and research, data analytics and scouting support.

For now, Kynisca is backed solely by Kang, who founded Cognosante, a digital health care company that she sold to Accenture this spring. (Kang is also a part-owner of the Baltimore Orioles.)

It’s a bet on continued growth in women’s soccer, which is drawing ever higher valuations. Consider the $250 million valuation that Angel City F.C. commanded in its sale to Bob Iger and Willow Bay, or the $240 million media deal that the National Women’s Soccer League signed last year.

That said, it remains to be seen how interest in women’s soccer translates into financial success, at least in the near term. Revenue at women’s soccer clubs in Europe is growing, for instance, but profit is still missing, according to a report by Deloitte.

Still, Kang suggested that the multi-team approach would help compound success at the clubs Kynisca eventually assembles. “We will provide opportunities for girls and women worldwide to access high-level sport, reach their potential under the best conditions, and play football in the clubs of the global elite,” she said.

LVMH shows off its Olympic credentials

All eyes are focused on the Olympics opening ceremony this evening, but what was arguably the Games’s unofficial launch event took place last night.

LVMH, the world’s largest luxury group and a top Olympics partner, co-hosted with Comcast a star-studded gala in Paris, underscoring how closely entwined business, money and celebrity are at the Games.

The bash was held at the Louis Vuitton Foundation, the Frank Gehry-designed art museum on the edge of the city. Called Prelude to the Olympics, it was another sign that this is Bernard Arnault’s Olympics as much as it is France’s: LVMH has spent €150 million, or about $163 million, to be one of the main sponsors and its brands are omnipresent, having designed the medals, dressed athletes and officials, and more.

The gala drew power players from business, sports and media. Besides Arnault, its co-hosts were Anna Wintour, the longtime Vogue editor; Brian Roberts, the C.E.O. of Comcast; and Pharrell Williams, the musician and men’s creative director for Louis Vuitton.

Guests included the actors Zendaya and Elizabeth Banks; the pro athletes LeBron James and Serena Williams; and the musicians Mick Jagger and Snoop Dogg, who will carry the Olympic torch through part of Paris before the opening ceremony.

It was a display of branding, star power and commercialism. As Wintour told The Times’s Sarah Lyall, “Sports stars are the new superstars.”
