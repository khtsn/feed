karrigan on Astana run: "I'd give it an 8 or 9 out of 10"

Falcons arrive in Shanghai looking to build on a runner-up finish at PGL Astana, Finn "⁠karrigan⁠" Andersen's debut event with the team. The 3-0 loss to Spirit in the grand final didn't take away from the team's positive run, headlined by the quarter-final win against FURIA in a mouth-watering series.

When asked to rate his team's performance in Astana, karrigan gave it "an 8 or 9 out of 10."

"A 10 out of 10 obviously would be winning, but for the preparation we had, for all the big changes we did on the T side, I think we can be satisfied," he said.

"But everybody in the team wants to win, of course."

As the highest-ranked side at the CS Asia Championships, Falcons are among the favorites to lift the trophy in Shanghai. karrigan stressed that he doesn't see this event as a must-win — just as he hadn't seen Astana — but admitted that winning the tournament would be a confidence boost ahead of the Major."

"I think for me it is a journey and a process we're going through," he said. "We need to be as ready as possible for Cologne…

"With a trophy in hand, that would be very nice, but also we need to understand that now we haven't practiced for 10, 11 days, and we go straight into an official setting again."

This is your second event since you joined the team. Your first one, PGL Astana, was quite successful, I would say, even though you fell short in the final. Give me your main takeaways from Astana.

My main takeaway is that obviously with the limited time and practice, I think we did alright. Obviously, we would have liked to make the final more competitive, but I think we had a great chance in the first round of overtime on Dust2, but we didn't win the one-on-three. I think if we had won that round, maybe the final could have looked a little bit different, but in the end we lost 3-0 and that's how it is.

It was to be expected that we could be challenged in our map pool [against] a strong team like Spirit, who played fantastic at that event. It is what it is, we reset and try to do it consistently again. We are looking for consistency, and for that to happen, we need to repeat the same success in China.

The way we managed to come back was fantastic, and what I saw from the team was really good

Are you satisfied with the way that the team played in the final? It was obviously going to be very difficult. You didn't have a lot of time to prepare and a best-of-five against a team like Spirit is always difficult. Are you satisfied with the way that the team played there?

Yeah, I think I'm pretty satisfied. I think with the limited time and practice we had, we went beyond our playbook. Obviously we played like seven Dust2 games that event and ended up looking like a very different team every map and made it competitive.

I think I could have done a different approach on Mirage, so that's a learning curve for me. I'm still trying to find the balance between giving freedom and playing with more structure as a team, and that's a learning curve that we need to learn as a team. Right now I learned something from that final, so next time we get into the final, maybe I will approach things differently in terms of the team.

How would you rate the tournament from 1-10? I guess the highlight would be the match against FURIA, right?

I think the FURIA game I would definitely give a 10 out of 10. The mental resilience we had, I think we had to defend seven match points or something during that game, and as a new team, that's probably the hardest thing, where the pressure is the highest. The way we managed to come back was fantastic, and what I saw from the team was really good.

But we need to try to replicate that. Maybe the game gives us that, but we could maybe have replicated it again in the final against Spirit. So there's definitely some data we need to look at, how we performed, but overall I'd give it an 8 or 9 out of 10. A 10 out of 10 obviously would be winning, but for the preparation we had, for all the big changes we did on the T side, I think we can be satisfied. But everybody in the team wants to win, of course.

This event has a slightly weaker field than Astana, but at the same time you didn't have any rest, you came straight from an event to this one. Considering all this, what kind of expectations do you have for this tournament?

For me, I think I didn't look at these events and say we need to win one of these. It would be very nice, but I think for me it is a journey and a process we're going through. We need to be as ready as possible for Cologne… With a trophy in hand, that would be very nice, but also we need to understand that now we haven't practiced for 10, 11 days, and we go straight into an official setting again.

Other teams are having the same, going from the US or Astana as well, but they obviously have had more practice time as a team. So yeah, maybe the field is weaker, but we're in a different country. In my opinion, we will do our best and perform as well as we can, and obviously a final would be nice again. Hopefully we can win it this time.

While you were in Astana, Atlanta was going on. You probably don't want to put too much stock into Vitality's loss in the quarterfinals, but I imagine you were still somewhat happy to see them lose two matches in that tournament.

I think it's not a surprise that Vitality lost. You're basically not practising for a week, not playing CS for a week, and going to an official setting. I don't think the public knows how hard it is not playing the game for seven days and going to an official game. I knew that they would probably be losing a match or two, and if they lost to NAVI, I understood why they lost to NAVI. So I'm not really happy about that. I think we will see a very strong Vitality at the Major that's really hungry, because that's the tournament they are looking at as well.

You're now going up against BCG here in your first game against s1mple, who was briefly a teammate of yours in FaZe. A best-of-one game is always tricky, right, especially given that you've had almost no time to prepare for this one. So what kind of expectations do you have for this game?

My expectation is, of course, to win. But it's a best-of-one: anything can happen. We have all played the Majors, so we know what can happen in best-of-ones, and it's just part of the game, it's the way the system is structured here. I would have loved a BO3, of course, but we just have to make sure we are locked in for that best-of-one.

Last time I was in China, I think we were up 11-4 in a BO1, and then ended up losing 13-11 to FUT with FaZe, and that changed the whole outcome of the tournament. We had to win three BO3s in a row, so we need to make sure we are locked in. We still have to be able to beat them in best-of-one with where we are.

And finally, what plan do you have for the Major? Because last year, before you joined the team, Falcons decided to rest and not bootcamp for the Major after a short period in Asia. Obviously, the circumstances are different now with the new IGL. Are you going to bootcamp before the Major? What sort of plan have you come up with?

I think we are bootcamping for five full days, go home for a few days, and then we go into the tournament. So it's not bootcamp straight into Cologne. I think it's a smart way to up the intensity in the team, and then having two or three days at home to prepare and practice at home. So bootcamp with the guys, and of course using all the data we are getting from Astana and China to kind of see where we need to improve on.

But we did well. Sometimes you're always trying to improve your weakness, but with this short time span and with the Major around the corner, I think it's very important we focus on the positives and actually keep pushing the positives, because that's how you do really well short term.
