Can Kamala Harris Step Up? Asking for a Friend.

The Conversation

Bret Stephens: Gail, these have been some very difficult days. I’ve been thinking of you a lot but maybe we should go to politics first. Biden is out. Where is Howard Cosell when you need him?

Gail: Bret, ever since we started conversing about this we agreed that President Biden should announce that he wasn’t running for re-election. Right away. So talented, qualified Democrats would have time to launch their candidacies.

Looks like we got the one but not the other.

Bret: What’s past is past. Democrats could still have an opportunity to reset the whole race, bring the excitement over to their side and at least have a chance of avoiding what looked like certain doom in November. At least if they don’t make the mistake of simply accepting a Harris nomination as a fait accompli. Or do you think that Biden’s endorsement of her was the right thing to do?

Gail: Be practical. Harris is his vice-president who’s served him well and loyally. Not to endorse her would be a real diss. And given the fact that we’re only talking about weeks until the nomination, it’s hard to imagine a rebellion. None of those other talented, qualified Democrats is a standout star who’d be an obvious alternative.

Bret: You may be right, but if that’s true, I think we can say hello to President Trump right now. Harris is an even weaker candidate than Biden. Not that I’ll vote for Trump, but I don’t think I can vote for her.

Gail: No! Can’t believe you’d go there. No matter how bad the nominees are, one is always less bad than the other.

Bret: Sorry. I’m happy to vote for a middle-of-the-road, seasoned Democrat, even when I disagree on many issues — that’s why I voted for Hillary in 2016 and for Joe in 2020. I could gladly vote for Gretchen Whitmer, Josh Shapiro, Andy Beshear or Wes Moore, among other promising Democratic possibilities. But I won’t just vote for any Democrat at all on the theory that definitionally they’re all better than Trump. I don’t believe that. And I don’t believe that Trump means the end of democracy or civilization or life on Earth. We lived through four bumpy Trump years before and I’m pretty sure we can survive another four.

I know a lot of our readers will pillory me for saying this, so let me add one thing: I am where a lot of independent voters are. Democrats ought to think carefully about which nominee might appeal to voters like us.

Gail: Accepting your crankiness but not the threat to not exercise your most important right as a citizen. And if Trump wins, I’m gonna blame you for everything that happens after that.

Bret: Fair.

Gail: We’re probably gonna be talking about nothing but the Democratic ticket for a while. So let’s at least take a minute to go back to the Republicans. What’s your opinion of JD Vance? Trump’s only a few years younger than Biden and hardly in great physical shape.

Bret: My initial reaction to Vance joining the ticket was that Trump had made a mistake — that he should have chosen someone like Marco Rubio to win over Hispanic votes or Glenn Youngkin to turn Virginia red again, or Nikki Haley to reassure moderate Republicans.

But Vance also has strengths: a remarkable life story, abundant intelligence, a quality of relatability — and youth. He’s half Trump’s age. That will help pull younger voters into Trump’s camp. What do you think of him?

Gail: Gotta give him the youth, and it’s hard to spend much time criticizing a career that basically involves an up-from-terrible-childhood story plus about six minutes in elective office.

Bret: Seven minutes, Gail.

Gail: His acceptance speech was really boring, but it’s possible he was just being careful not to outshine the boss. Which would have been pretty easy to do.

And when you peel down from his story you’ve got a guy who’s more conservative than Trump.

Bret: Vance’s brand of conservatism is also the polar opposite of mine: He’s socially conservative, anti-free trade, pro-union and isolationist. But given that he was just about the opposite of that a few years ago, I’ll keep some hope that he’s sufficiently opportunistic to … evolve further.

Gail: You know, things were looking terribly dim for the Democrats — Trump’s dramatic survival of an assassination attempt made his candidacy look so strong for … a minute. But once he got into his acceptance speech he zipped past the call-for-unity theme and got right back into his usual harangues. And the speech was not just a thematic disappointment — it was wildly, stupendously boring. Even the true believers were checking their watches.

Bret: In its length, the speech was worthy of Fidel Castro. In its depth, of Justin Bieber.

It was a good reminder, as our colleague Patrick Healy wrote, that “this guy is still beatable.” Trump could have emerged from the assassination attempt by showcasing a more humble, gracious, transformed and improved version of himself — one that could appeal to the wavering voters he still needs to win. But that version simply doesn’t exist, and he couldn’t fake it for very long.

Gail: Yep, Patrick hit that exactly.

Bret: That said, I don’t think the speech damaged him all that much, if only because it was, as you said, so boring and so typically Trumpian. He’s got his party solidly behind him and a clear sense of momentum. And I think that, of all the probable Democratic contenders, Harris is by far the weakest. She’s a blue-state Democrat who’s hardly ever run a truly competitive campaign. She comes with all of the Biden policy baggage, particularly the rise in prices and the immigration crisis. Her reputation as a manager is abysmal and she doesn’t seem especially well briefed. Her notes of sympathy for the campus anti-Israel protesters don’t sit well with a lot of voters — emphatically including me.

But maybe you’re right, and Democrats should just make the best of it. Who would you like to see as her veep pick?

Gail: Well, as much as I love the idea of a two-woman ticket, it’s not gonna happen. Looks like a guy governor, right? Do I remember you dropping some compliments about Josh Shapiro of Pennsylvania?

Bret: Shapiro is terrific and could help bring Pennsylvania into Harris’s corner. But as a purely political matter, I think Harris will want a vice president who conveys a little more experience and solidity than Shapiro, who’s only been in office for 18 months.

My first choice would be retired Adm. Jim Stavridis, the former commander of NATO. He was on Hillary Clinton’s short list in 2016 and is the sort of guy who could handle any conceivable 3 a.m. call, up to and including invasion by space aliens. Second choice: Senator Mark Kelly of Arizona, a former Navy captain and astronaut who is also Gabby Giffords’s husband. He also might give Harris a better chance of winning the state. Third choice would be an anti-Trump Republican like Adam Kinzinger, the former Illinois congressman: It would be a good way of trolling Trump with one of his most effective critics while illustrating that a Harris presidency would not be super-progressive. Anyone else?

Gail: Gotta say that the regular folks I’ve talked to in passing today mentioned Mark Kelly a lot. He’s got an aura of glamour you definitely do not see a whole lot in national politics these days.

Bret: Kelly has an identical twin, Scott, who’s also an astronaut. They could switch out from time to time and nobody would be the wiser.

Gail: This all feels like a very good moment for the country, Bret. Can’t predict what’ll happen over the next weeks, but people are going to enjoy talking about new options, new names.

Bret: I really hope you’re right. The country really felt stuck in a kind of nightmarish Groundhog Day while it was Biden versus Trump. For all of my misgivings about Harris, at least there’s a little more excitement.

Gail: And I hope there’ll be a celebration of Joe Biden’s service. Looking forward to arguing with you about which parts were the best.

Bret: Although it took him much too long, Biden’s greatest service was what he did on Sunday: It must have been an agonizing decision to make. But it was the right decision, and I salute him and his family for it.

Gail, I often wrap up our conversations by pointing to a piece in The Times that I especially admired. This week, the admiration comes wrapped in heartbreak. Your wonderful husband, Dan Collins, passed away earlier this month — much, much too soon. Our friend Sam Roberts wrote a moving obituary for him, which I thought captured him beautifully. He was a brilliant journalist who loved a contrarian take, an indefatigable scoop-getter, a hilarious dinner companion, a man who put everyone at ease with wit and kindness and a glass of really good wine. Also: twinkly-eyed handsome like only the Irish can be. Your partner of more than a half-century. May his memory be for a blessing.

Bret Stephens is an Opinion columnist for The Times, writing about foreign policy, domestic politics and cultural issues. Facebook
