anthony williams

Every election cycle, campaigns from city council all the way up to President spend untold millions of dollars reaching out to and trying to understand the perspectives of those who vote on a regular basis. And so you end up getting the same opinions reinforced over and over and over again. And those opinions dominate the political conversation.

So our question in this study was, what does everybody else think when you consider that everybody else is about 100 million eligible voters?

anthony williams

So we define non-voters as going back over six election cycles. So we took three presidential elections and three midterm elections. If somebody voted in one of those six elections, they were still eligible to participate in the survey. Because depending on the issue, depending on the candidate, even a consistent non-voter might occasionally show up. If they voted in two or more elections, they were out.

So that’s how we define non-voters — people who had voted in no more than one out of the last six major elections and/or those people who were eligible to vote but weren’t even registered.

How did those myths line up to reality of the hundred million eligible voters who don’t participate that you all study?

anthony williams

Well, in terms of some of the rationale for why they don’t participate, many of those sentiments were genuinely reflected in the research that we did. I guess the one big myth that we were able to dispel is there was a notion that this group was somehow monolithic — all low-income, all supporting one particular party or the other. And that just wasn’t the case. This is not a monolithic group by any stretch of the imagination.

And in fact, when you look at it from a partisan lens, it’s roughly a third, a third, and a third between Democrats, Republicans, and independents.

astead herndon

So on the plugged-in end of the perspective, you have one subgroup that’s established progressives — tend to lean a little bit more toward Democratic candidates and policies, a traditional conservative cohort, which tends to be older, male, and more Republican-leaning, and then a modern moderate, which is sort of in between.

Absolutely. So one of the things that we did as part of the survey is we wanted to have a specific comparison frame among young people. And so we did what’s called an oversample. For other groups, as they get older and take on responsibilities and have kids, and you buy a house and you do those things, those behaviors then tend to push you towards participation in the political system. This is a group of people that I’m not 100 percent sure it’s going to be as easy. Either you’re going to have a particularly good candidate that turns people on, or you’re going to have a particularly scary candidate that turns people off. But what we’re trying to focus on in this particular study, and we hope that elected officials and activists in the political space will take seriously, are these fundamental chips in the strength of American democracy. It’s hard to convince people to vote if they don’t believe fundamentally that their vote is going to matter or change anything.
