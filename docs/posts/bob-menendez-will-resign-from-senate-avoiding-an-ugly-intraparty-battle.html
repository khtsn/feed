Nicholas Fandos and Tracey Tully

July 23, 2024Updated 6:36 p.m. ET

Senator Robert Menendez of New Jersey announced on Tuesday that he would resign from Congress effective in late August, bowing to intense pressure from Democratic colleagues who had pushed him to step down or face an expulsion vote after his conviction in a vast international bribery scheme.

Mr. Menendez has maintained his innocence and vowed to appeal a guilty verdict returned last week by a federal jury in Manhattan. But with the Senate Ethics Committee fast-tracking a vote to expel him, he opted to quit his term months early, rather than force an ugly intraparty fight that threatened to make him the first senator ousted since the Civil War.

Mr. Menendez conveyed his resignation in a letter to Gov. Philip D. Murphy of New Jersey, shortly after he shared the news with what remained of his staff. Mr. Murphy, a Democrat, said he would promptly appoint a replacement who would serve until January.

In the letter, Mr. Menendez, 70, said he had concluded that his presence in the Senate would only complicate his efforts to clear his name and salvage his legacy. However, he notably left open the possibility that he might remain a long-shot candidate for re-election this fall as a political independent; he has until Aug. 16 to take his name off the ballot.

“While I fully intend to appeal the jury’s verdict, all the way and including to the Supreme Court, I do not want the Senate to be involved in a lengthy process that will detract from its important work,” the senator wrote.

By delaying his departure until August, Mr. Menendez will allow his staff members time to find new jobs but also ensure that he receives another month’s salary and health insurance at a time when his finances are crumbling and his wife, Nadine Menendez, is undergoing cancer treatment. His resignation will not immediately affect his federal pension, though the senator could still stand to lose it under an anticorruption law if his conviction is upheld.

And yet, there was no avoiding the magnitude of the collapse of his storied five-decade career — one that saw Mr. Menendez, the son of Cuban immigrants, rise from mayor of his hometown, Union City, N.J., to become the powerful chairman of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee and one of the most senior Latinos in Washington.

For much of that time, the senator had looked invincible, no more so than after he avoided a conviction on earlier federal bribery charges at a lengthy trial in New Jersey in 2017.

Now, Mr. Menendez has been branded a felon, convicted of selling his office for bars of gold and making himself an agent of a foreign power. He faces decades in prison when Judge Sidney H. Stein of Federal District Court sentences him on Oct. 29.

Mr. Menendez had faced calls for his resignation since he, his wife and three New Jersey businessmen were first charged last fall with taking part in a yearslong bribery conspiracy. But demands that he step aside intensified after the jury found him guilty of 16 federal crimes on July 16.

The counts included helping to disrupt criminal cases for friends, locking in a lucrative Egyptian halal certification monopoly for an ally and using his political influence to cement a business deal with an investment fund run by a Qatari sheikh.

In exchange, the government said that he and his wife were rewarded with gold, cash and a $60,000 Mercedes-Benz convertible. F.B.I. agents discovered bars of gold bullion worth more than $100,000 and roughly $480,000 in cash stuffed into envelopes, a shopping bag, boots and a closet safe.

There is nothing in the Constitution or in the Senate’s rules that required Mr. Menendez to give up his seat upon conviction. But his colleagues appeared intent on denying him the choice to stay, even if it meant using a rare expulsion vote. Senator Cory Booker of New Jersey, Mr. Menendez’s political protégé, volunteered to lead the effort, and the bipartisan Ethics Committee had indicated on Monday that it was moving “expeditiously” to complete the procedural work needed to tee up a vote.

The political context had also changed, upended by President Biden’s decision to bow out of his race for re-election and the possibility of former President Donald J. Trump’s return to power. Mr. Menendez’s continued presence in Washington would have been a distraction for a party trying to refocus its energy on retaining the White House and its slim Senate majority.

Still, the relief for Democrats on Tuesday was not complete. The party was left to wonder whether Mr. Menendez would follow through on his independent campaign for re-election that could shake Democrats’ decades-long hold on the New Jersey Senate seat.

Representative Andy Kim won the Democratic nomination to run for Senate last month after a bruising contest with Tammy Murphy, the wife of the governor. Mr. Kim said on Tuesday that he would try to bring New Jersey into “a new era of politics built on integrity, service and delivering for all families.”

Polls suggest there is no path for Mr. Menendez to win, but some in the party fear he might have enough of a nostalgic following, particularly among Latino voters, to siphon off Democratic votes and create an opening for the Republican nominee, Curtis Bashaw.

Even a modest level of support for Mr. Menendez “could be consequential,” according to Mike DuHaime, a Republican political consultant advising Mr. Bashaw.

“The D.C. folks are definitely paying attention,” he said.

Mr. Murphy, the governor, was already winnowing a list of potential successors to temporarily fill Mr. Menendez’s seat. According to several current and former aides, the list included Lt. Gov. Tahesha Way; Representative Bonnie Watson Coleman; Nina Mitchell Wells, a former New Jersey secretary of state; and George Helmy, Mr. Murphy’s former chief of staff.

The governor’s wife quickly removed herself from consideration Tuesday afternoon. “While I’m grateful for the humbling support and outreach I’ve received since last year, I want to reiterate that I will not accept an appointment to the U.S. Senate,” she wrote on X.

The aides familiar with his thinking said that Mr. Murphy was opposed to appointing Mr. Kim, despite pressure from Washington to give him the title of senator and the advantages of incumbency ahead of November’s race. Mr. Kim has indicated he would accept an appointment if one was offered.

Though the biggest question was answered by Mr. Menendez’s planned resignation, his Senate colleagues were left to adjudicate smaller ones in the interim. For example, Senator Chuck Schumer of New York, the majority leader, said he would leave it to the Ethics Committee to determine whether Mr. Menendez should continue receiving classified intelligence briefings or serving on the Foreign Relations Committee given the nature of his conviction.

As others turned their attention to the future, though, many of Mr. Menendez’s former aides and longtime friends expressed melancholy at the end to an often-distinguished career and the specter of what could amount to a lifetime prison sentence.

One noted ruefully that Mr. Menendez’s sentencing date coincided with the 12th anniversary of Superstorm Sandy and the disaster recovery that he helped to lead. Another cited the senator’s unwavering, decades-long support on behalf of New Jersey’s large and varied immigrant communities.

Tasos D. Zambas, a close friend of Mr. Menendez who attended parts of his two-month trial, said the man prosecutors described in court was unrecognizable.

“Agent of a foreign government? You’ve got to be kidding me,” said Mr. Zambas, who grew up in Union City after fleeing Cyprus as a child in 1975. “He’s an amazing individual. This is not who he is.”

But last weekend, more than two dozen of the senator’s onetime political foes gathered in Lavallette, on the Jersey Shore, for what an organizer described as a “Menendez conviction party.” Guests wore T-shirts depicting hands gripping a jail cell above the words “BYE BYE BOBBY.”

Maya C. Miller contributed reporting.
