Dozens of Engaging Ways to Make Media Literacy Meaningful to Teenagers

Teaching resource

This collection of interactive exercises uses Times resources to help young people find the news that matters to them, understand how it’s made, connect it to their lives — and make media of their own.

March 27, 2025

The Learning Network has been publishing since 1998, and, in a sense, everything we do is related to media literacy.

Every weekday during the school year, we choose pieces from across sections of The New York Times and turn them into free, interactive activities that ask students to think critically about what they are reading, watching or listening to. We then invite teens to talk back — to tell us what they think about what they’re learning, and to explore how it connects to their lives and communities.

But now, at a time when mis- and disinformation, generative artificial intelligence and distrust in traditional media are all on the rise; when young people are getting most of their news from social media; and when the president is demonizing the press, it is more crucial than ever to help students understand how media is made and why that matters.

In this resource, you’ll find links to dozens of activities and opportunities on The Learning Network and in The Times, categorized under four headings. Please let us know which of them are most useful for you and your students. You can post a comment or write to us at LNFeedback@nytimes.com.

Consider the importance of a free press.

What is the purpose of journalism? What role does it play in our society? Does a democracy need an independent free press to function? Why or why not?

These are some of the questions we pose in our 2017 lesson plan, Analyzing the Relationship Between the Press and the President. How would your students answer?

In the lesson, we ask students to look closely at a front page of The New York Times. You can find a PDF of today’s print edition here, by navigating to “Today’s Front Pages” in the right column and clicking to enlarge the one you choose (New York, National or International).

Which of the headlines on that page might not have run if the U.S. government were in control of the press? Why? Students can use this handout (PDF) to write their responses.

That lesson also provides teaching activities to help students explore the relationship between the president and the press throughout history. It touches on President John F. Kennedy after the failed Bay of Pigs invasion, President Richard Nixon during the Watergate scandal and more.

To put those questions into a current context, your students might listen to — or read the transcript of — a recent episode of “The Daily” podcast, “Nixon Dreamed of Breaking the Media. Trump Is Doing It.” Among other things, it analyzes the new and “completely uncharted media environment” of podcasts and social media that many teens will recognize.

To go with it, we are also hosting a student conversation forum. We’re asking: At a time when the Trump administration is attacking the news media in ways that are markedly different from those of previous presidents, is our press freedom under threat?

We hope you’ll encourage your students to read more about the issue and have their say.

Understand how journalism works.

Talk to Times reporters.

Our new Conversations With Journalists series is an open invitation to teens to ask questions, post comments and suggest ideas to Times reporters across sections.

Every few weeks, we post a teen-friendly article and invite students to read it and then share their thoughts in our comments section. Then, a Times reporter writes back.

So far, we have featured articles about how TikTok has changed America; the rise of Asian grocery stores; the gender gap among Gen Z voters; the safety of automated vehicles; what rats can teach us about social media; how a culture reporter creates music playlists; the cultural resonance of the Harlem Renaissance; how Times journalists cover love and marriage; what it’s like to report on the Trump White House; and what it’s like to be a professional opinion writer.

Here is a sample exchange, this one with the pop music reporter Lindsay Zoladz:

Alex R, Missouri, Jan. 15

Hi, I’m a 9th grader from Harrisonville High School and I was wondering how you get people’s attention from your writing. Is it better to use more complex and eye-catching vocabulary, or is it better to write like I’m having a casual conversation?

Lindsay Zoladz, Pop Music Critic, Jan. 20

@Alex R Hi Alex. That’s a *great* question. I think the best writing feels like a conversation. Sometimes people can load up their sentences with too many complex vocabulary words when a much simpler word would have been a better or more direct choice. I always strive to write in a way that is inviting and that everyone can understand, and I personally think that the best writing is usually the clearest and most direct.

Discuss “What’s going on in this picture?”

What’s going on in this picture? What do you see that makes you say that? What more can you find?

Those are the questions that power our popular weekly “What’s Going On in This Picture?” discussions about Times photojournalism. Facilitated by our collaborating organization, Visual Thinking Strategies, the live-moderated conversations have been running since 2012. Each week, hundreds of students from around the world join us.

Here’s how it works: On Monday mornings during the school year, you’ll find an intriguing photograph like the one above, stripped of its original caption. We ask students to think critically about what they see and post their thoughts. As students contribute ideas, our facilitators respond. Then, on Thursday afternoons, we reveal the caption and some of the back story about the photo and what it depicts.

Teachers across subject areas and levels have told us the activity is a fun way to help their students strengthen their visual and news literacy skills, support opinions with evidence, practice critical thinking, learn about other cultures and collaborate with students across the globe.

So generally useful are the skills this exercise strengthens, in fact, that, for many teachers, the feature serves as a warm-up for other academic units — whether for writing lab reports in science class, reading Shakespeare in English or completing an inquiry unit in history.

You can find collections of past images by scrolling the column, or by looking through this bank.

Read graphs and understand statistics about issues that touch teen lives.

Can your students read and understand information displayed in graphs and charts, whether they relate to climate change, college admissions, the world youth population, LeBron James, Taylor Swift, swing voters, the economy, the amount of time Americans spend at home or Uncrustables?

“What’s Going On in This Graph?” — like its cousin, “What’s Going On in This Picture?” — is a weekly interactive exercise that is live-moderated by math educators and features three simple, open-ended questions:

What do you notice? What do you wonder? What’s going on in this graph?

On Wednesdays during the school year, students wrestle with these questions, considering what impact the data might have on them and their communities. As students post their thoughts, our partners from the American Statistical Association respond in real time. On Thursdays, we add a “reveal” to the post, which includes additional background about these graphs, shout-outs for great student headlines and relevant statistical concepts.

To find graphs relevant to your students, scroll through these two collections, which are organized by topic and graph type: 69 graphs from 2017-20, and 79 graphs from 2020-23. You can find the latest editions in this column, which is updated continually.

React to the news that gets your attention.

Thinking critically about what you read and watch, asking questions, forming opinions and making connections to your own life — all of these are habits of good readers and news consumers.

How do we encourage those habits on our site?

First, each school day since 2009, we have asked students a question about an issue, idea or event in the news. Each day’s prompt draws from and links to a free Times article and is open to comments from teenagers around the world. Because we use materials from across sections of The Times, there is something for everyone, whether your students are interested in thinking about fashion, friendship, A.I., sports gambling, cursing, favorite childhood toys, social media, governmental checks and balances, dream travel destinations, mental health, physical health, annoying popular phrases or hundreds of other topics.

In a recent guest post, Peggy Dimock, a high school teacher in Minnesota, explains why and how she uses the feature:

In my English classroom I turn to The Learning Network’s daily writing prompts because they pose thought-provoking questions and surface student-friendly articles from across The New York Times. I value the range of topics (school life, politics, fun) they address. And I know whatever I find in The Times will be well written, thoroughly researched and balanced. For the writing exercises below, that means students will have models of excellent writing to follow. And for the discussion exercises, it means that, even in today’s polarized world, students can have evidence-based conversations that are timely and relevant without being contentious.

Be intentional about the information you’re consuming.

Try our News Diet Challenge.

Back in 2017, we introduced our student News Diet Challenge by writing, “News and information comes at us constantly, whether we choose it or not. And not only is the pace relentless, but fewer and fewer of us seem able to even agree on what’s true and what’s fabricated.”

For teens in 2025, of course, that describes the only information environment they have ever known. But, as we wrote then, change can’t happen unless we think about information consumption not just as a school assignment, but in the context of our real lives.

Take a look at the steps of our News Diet Challenge, as well as the related lesson plan that guides teens and teachers through the steps with details and examples, to see if it might be right for your classes.

The basic sequence is simple: Students analyze their daily information diets, then experiment with changing them to make them better meet their needs and goals, then reflect on the results. Built into the exercise is encouragement to broaden one’s sources of news, as well as to consider their relative strengths, weaknesses and biases. Our hope is that the exercise will help anyone who participates become more intentional about what they consume, why and how.

As a 16-year-old named Max Wilson reflected after he completed it:

Examining, logging, and changing my media diet has been an incredibly eye-opening process. Just like the products at your average fast food joint, my media diet was composed of four low quality ingredients in different combinations — bias, selective reporting, out-of-context quotes, and irrelevant purposes.

Practice good media literacy habits across sources and platforms.

Last fall, we again asked teenagers about their relationship with news and information, and we were heartened to learn that many seemed hyper-aware of the attention economy and how it works — and how often it serves them false or misleading information. A notable number also said they regularly employ healthy media literacy strategies like lateral reading that they have learned from their teachers.

Still, as the writer Emily Dreyfuss recently pointed out in her Opinion essay, “Our Kids Are Living in a Different Digital World,” vast and largely unregulated forces are influencing teenagers on social media, and, to most adults, those forces are invisible.

Our lesson plan, Teenagers and Misinformation, offers some starting points for classrooms via five ideas to help young people understand the problem, learn basic skills, share their experiences and have a say in how media literacy is taught.

To explore further, check out “Helping Digital Natives Overcome Digital Naïveté: Four Steps to Media Literacy,” a guest post from David Nurenberg, an English teacher in Massachusetts. He writes about how he works with his students to navigate information with skepticism rather than cynicism.

To find even more resources, strategies and perspectives, we suggest you scroll through this recent Learning Network collection that features both teacher and student voices on this topic.

Question the media — even the sources we’re telling you to read.

Part of being media literate is asking questions of everything you read, watch and hear, including the Times sources we’re suggesting throughout this resource. All media has bias, as charts like this one or this one show, and we regularly remind students to do as this lesson plan from PBS News Hour Classroom suggests and compare how different media treat the same news story. But, since the mission of The Learning Network is to help students learn with Times materials specifically, we are focusing here on our own journalism.

While The New York Times, founded in 1851, is widely recognized as the paper of record in the United States, no “first rough draft of history” is without error, and sometimes the consequences of those mistakes can be momentous. For example, this lesson plan focuses on how The Times and other media reported on the Holocaust in the 1930s and ’40s — coverage that Max Frankel, a former executive editor of The Times, called “the century’s bitterest journalistic failure.” This 2004 piece, written by a group of Times editors, analyzes problematic aspects of the paper’s coverage of news in the two years leading up to the U.S.-led invasion of Iraq. No matter what important 20th- or 21st-century world or national event your students are studying, they might use TimesMachine or the Times archives to analyze how the paper covered what unfolded, what the publication did well and where it may have stumbled.

But we also encourage students to think critically about Times coverage as they read today’s news. For example, as they follow reporting on the second Trump administration, students might want to read this Insider piece in which Times editors address common reader questions about how the paper covers the president. To go further, students might also consider pieces by media critics from outside The Times, such as Margaret Sullivan, who was once the public editor for The Times and now writes for The Guardian and has a Substack newsletter focused on the question “Can journalism save democracy?” Don’t forget, too, that, via our Conversations With Journalists activity, students have the chance to pose their own questions to reporters directly, as many did in this recent discussion with a White House correspondent.

Sometimes, however, the most interesting and accessible critiques come from Times readers. In its commenting standards, The Times explains that it “welcomes strong opinions and criticism of our work.” Students can read those by scanning the daily Letters to the Editor column, or by checking out the thousands of comments left on scores of articles and opinion pieces each day. What did they learn? What might they add if they were to join the conversation? Practicing these critical thinking skills as they read The Times will help students become more savvy consumers of all media — as well as more engaged citizens.

Understand journalistic standards — and apply them.

In his guest essay, “Helping Digital Natives Overcome Digital Naïveté: Four Steps to Media Literacy,” David Nurenberg, an English teacher in Massachusetts, lists four steps he takes to help his students become media literate. The first: Have students make some journalism of their own.

Why?

Distinguishing false news from responsible reporting is easier when you understand the rigorous process that journalists undertake. Learning about that process teaches you the right questions to ask of what you read and see.

To do this, his students rely on Times standards:

To create their own news podcasts and TikTok videos, they use The New York Times’s standards and guidelines on integrity as one of their models. The guidelines cover fundamentals like how journalists attribute and present quotations, confirm the veracity of their information and ensure they have reached out to all sides of a complex topic. They study how to conduct polls to assess the student body’s opinion and, through the experience, learn the limitations and pitfalls of such polling. They submit their articles to our school newspaper, and on occasions when it turns out they have gotten something wrong, they send corrections.

Later, when I have them critically analyze news items from their social media feeds, they do so with firsthand experience of the “back end” of journalism. Having now made their own media that follows journalistic best practices, the students see the gulf between the thoroughness of their work and what most other content producers create.

Unattributed quotations, polls without information about sample size, no evident attempts to present a balance of sources — these all now stand out as red flags to the students, not because I told them these were red flags, but because they now know from experience what legitimate journalism actually looks like.

When they actually take offense (“Hey, we had to do all this work — why didn’t they?”), I know the lessons have been successful.

The contests we offer on our site all weave in these standards, and, since every contest is judged, in part, by volunteers from the Times newsroom, your students’ work will be read by an “authentic audience” of people steeped in these values.
