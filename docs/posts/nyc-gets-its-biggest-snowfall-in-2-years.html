More than an inch of snow fell in Central Park, ending a lengthy dry spell. Then it turned to rain.

Jan. 16, 2024Updated 2:44 p.m. ET

New York City saw its biggest single-day snowfall in nearly two years on Tuesday, and while the total amount was less than two inches, it was enough to break the city’s longest recorded streak of no significant accumulation.

A total of 1.6 inches had fallen in Central Park by 1 p.m., according to the National Weather Service, but the winter weather was short-lived. By afternoon, the snowflakes that graced the morning commute had turned to rain.

The modest snowfall broke a streak of 701 days without meaningful accumulation, which in New York City is measured by at least one inch falling in Central Park on a given day. The snow began Monday evening, when 0.4 inches fell before midnight, followed by 1.2 inches on Tuesday morning.

No more snow was expected, and no neighborhood in the city saw more than two inches, according to the weather service.

Conditions for the evening commute could still be tricky, said Dominic Ramunni, a meteorologist for the weather service. “This afternoon is going to freeze pretty hard,” he said.

The recent snowless streak began almost two years ago, after 1.6 inches fell in Central Park on Feb. 13, 2022.

It smashed the city’s previous record of 400 days without significant snowfall, which ended on March 21, 1998, and had held for decades, according to James Tomasini, another meteorologist with the service.

The snow was brought by a low-pressure system that moved up from the South on Monday, Mr. Tomasini said. According to the weather service, that system moved off the coast of North Carolina and South Carolina and passed north and east of New York City by Tuesday afternoon.

Central Park typically has about 24 inches of snow over the course of the entire winter. But last year’s mild winter brought only 2.3 inches of snow in all, the smallest amount recorded there since record-keeping started in 1869.

Most of that snow in 2023 fell in a two-day span that began on Feb. 27. While nearly two inches fell in total during that storm, less than an inch fell each day, keeping the streak alive.

Storm systems typically have a warmer and a colder side. Last year, New York ended up on the warmer side of most storms, resulting in less snowfall.

Mr. Tomasini said that last year’s weather pattern had been unusual, and this year was already shaping up to be more snowy. “It looks unlikely that the type of winter we had last year would happen again,” he said.

“There’s already another chance of snow for the end of the week because we’ve been in this active pattern,” he added.
