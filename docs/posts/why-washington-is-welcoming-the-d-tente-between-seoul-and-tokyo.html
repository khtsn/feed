Why Washington is welcoming the détente between Seoul and Tokyo.

Aug. 18, 2023, 4:31 p.m. ET

President Biden and the leaders of Japan and South Korea agreed to expand security and economic cooperation on Friday, after a summit at Camp David that sought to bridge generations of friction between the two Asian powers and to forge a trilateral bulwark against North Korea and the growing influence of China.

Mr. Biden said the three leaders had agreed to hold annual military exercises and to hold trilateral meeting each year to deepen their alliance, “not just this year, not just next year, forever.”

The American president lauded “the political courage” of Prime Minister Fumio Kishida of Japan and President Yoon Suk Yeol of South Korea for overcoming longstanding resentments stemming from Japan’s occupation of the Korean Penninsula before World War II to create a new alliance.

“We have laid in place a long-term structure for a relationship that will last and will have a phenomenal impact not just in Asia but around the world,” Mr. Biden said. “This is a big deal.”

Here’s what else to know:

  • Mr. Biden has made China a central focus of his foreign policy, working to stitch together various partnerships. But he took pains to say at a news conference with the other heads of state that the summit was not about China.

  • U.S. officials say Japan and South Korea obviously have strong economic ties with China (as does the United States), and the two Asian nations have felt economic coercion from China, so all three nations are being careful about the framing of the summit. China has made clear its displeasure about the convergence between the three countries, seeing it as more proof that the United States is plotting to contain its rise.

  • Japan’s prime minister has a split image. At home, Mr. Kishida’s approval ratings hit a low last month, and he has not fulfilled promises to broaden and enrich the middle class. But in the view from abroad, he has brought vigor to Japan’s foreign and national security policy, where he has made changes that long seemed impossible.

  • Mr. Yoon was a star prosecutor before becoming South Korea’s president. He has faced heavy criticism at home over his efforts to bury the hatchet with Japan. Critics say his approach has given too much to Washington and Tokyo while antagonizing China and motivating North Korea to build new weapons.

As President Biden met with the leaders of Japan and South Korea at Camp David on Friday, no country loomed more prominently in the background than China.

The three-way talks on Friday focused on establishing more secure supply chains that bypass China and figuring out which emerging technologies to try to keep out of the hands of the Chinese military.

The leaders discussed security cooperation, including strengthening missile defense systems and carrying out joint military exercises. The greater military coordination is motivated by China’s growing assertiveness in Asia, particularly around Taiwan and the South China Sea.

China “sits behind” a lot of the issues that will be discussed at the summit, including technological innovations and supply chain resiliency, Sheila A. Smith, a senior fellow for Asia-Pacific Studies at the Council on Foreign Relations said in a panel discussion on the summit on Tuesday.

“When you read through the formal statements, China is going to be in the background,” she said.

During a news conference, Mr. Biden mentioned that China was part of the private discussions, but he did not elaborate on what was said. Mr. Biden emphasized that their summit was not “anti-China.”

China has proved to be a unifying force for the three countries. Japan, South Korea and the United States are increasingly united around the goal of curbing Beijing’s ability to use its economic clout to influence other countries politically and diplomatically. They are also increasingly in agreement on limiting China’s access to advanced technology that may have military uses.

Still, the deep economic ties between China and other East Asian countries present obstacles to those efforts. China is the largest trading partner for both Japan and South Korea and is America’s third largest trading partner.

What’s more, many Japanese and South Korea industries, from consumer electronics to electric vehicle batteries and building materials, are closely tied with suppliers and subsidiaries in China, making it difficult to sever economic ties.

Japanese and South Korean officials and companies have cooperated with U.S.-led efforts to clamp down on the sale of chips and chip-making technology to the Chinese semiconductor industry, but they have also expressed unease about the economic consequences of those moves.

“The semiconductor sanctions are really tough on Korea,” said Susan Shirk, a research professor at the 21st Century China Center at University of California San Diego and the author of “Overreach: How China Derailed Its Peaceful Rise.”

Along with the United States, Australia, Europe and other allied countries, Japan and South Korea have been moving more aggressively to treat China as a geopolitical and economic threat.

China’s growing power has brought Japan and South Korea closer together, despite historical grievances — Japan occupied Korea from 1910 to 1945 — and more recent conflicts, including fierce opposition in Korea to Japan’s plan to release nuclear waste water into the sea.

It remains to be seen how a new entente with Japan, no matter how much it makes sense from a foreign policy standpoint, will be received by the South Korean public, experts said.

“One big question is whether or not Korean business and the Korean public will continue to support this approach, which tilts much more than previously against China and for the United States, and the degree that they’re willing to overlook their historical disputes and resentments against Japan,” Ms. Shirk said.

Evidence has also mounted in recent months that China’s economy is slowing, introducing a new dynamic. The effects of China’s slowing growth are likely to ripple outward toward the country’s largest trading partners, resulting in fewer sales and some downward pressure on prices.

The tightening alliance between Japan, South Korea and the United States already has triggered a harsh response from officials in Beijing. “Those who bet on China’s defeat will surely regret it later,” China’s ambassador to South Korea warned in June.

Peter Baker covers the White House and has traveled with President Biden to both Japan and South Korea.

A stronger three-way alliance between Japan, South Korea and the United States adds to the latticework of partnerships that President Biden has been building in the Indo-Pacific region to counter aggressive efforts by China to dominate that part of the world.

While the White House insists that the stronger bonds the president is trying to build are not aimed at China per se, Mr. Biden has concentrated on enhancing economic, technological, educational and strategic ties with nations that feel pressured by Beijing.

He has signed a three-way security agreement with Australia and Britain; bolstered the so-called Quad grouping of the United States, India, Australia and Japan; increased the American military presence in the Philippines; and established the Indo-Pacific Economic Framework with 14 nations.

Three of Mr. Biden’s first four state dinners have honored or will honor leaders from the Indo-Pacific region — President Yoon Suk Yeol of South Korea in April, Prime Minister Narendra Modi of India in June and Prime Minister Anthony Albanese of Australia in October. Mr. Biden also plans next month to visit India, which is hosting the annual meeting of the Group of 20 nations.

A stronger collaboration with Japan and South Korea could be a significant pillar in Mr. Biden’s strategy. While the United States has long been close to each of them individually, Mr. Biden’s goal is to form a trio that can coordinate each members approach to the region, overcoming the generations of grievances that have complicated relations between Tokyo and Seoul.

Mr. Biden’s goal is to institutionalize the three-way collaboration in a way that lasts beyond his administration. In addition to increasing security cooperation, Mr. Biden, Mr. Yoon and Prime Minister Fumio Kishida of Japan will commit to annual meetings between the leaders of the three nations, much like the regular meetings American presidents have with their Mexican and Canadian counterparts.

President Yoon Suk Yeol has spent much of his career rocking the boat. Before becoming president, he was a star prosecutor under a progressive government who helped​ imprison two former conservative presidents on corruption charges.

Then he switched sides, running a successful campaign as a conservative party candidate in the last presidential election.

As president, Mr. Yoon, like all his predecessors, has vowed to strengthen South Korea’s alliance with the United States. But unlike presidents before him, he has also sought to win Washington’s trust by making bold compromises with Japan, South Korea’s former colonial ruler.

Japan and South Korea are Washington’s most important allies in East Asia. Mr. Yoon’s pivot to bury the hatchet with Japan has helped the Biden administration tighten the trilateral ranks necessary to counter both China and North Korea more effectively.

But as Mr. Yoon enjoys the beer drinking in Tokyo and singalongs in Washington, he has faced heavy criticism at home.

Despite sweltering summer heat in Seoul, hardly a weekend has passed without thousands of protesters gathering and marching downtown, shouting “Out with Yoon Suk Yeol!” and “No to the South Korea-United States-Japan alliance!” A majority of South Koreans hold negative views of Mr. Yoon’s government, according to recent national surveys.

Critics say that Mr. Yoon’s foreign policy approach has given too much to Washington and Tokyo while getting too little in return. And, they argue, his foreign policy steps have antagonized China, South Korea’s biggest trade partner, while motivating North Korea to build and test new weapons.

Mr. Yoon has insisted that bonding with “countries that share universal values of freedom, human rights and the rule of law” is the best way to secure a safe and prosperous future for South Korea.

When he and Mr. Biden met in April, they adopted the “Washington Declaration,” in which Mr. Biden vowed to repel any nuclear attack by the North against South Korea with “the full range of U.S. capabilities, including nuclear.”

According to South Korean officials, Mr. Yoon is expected to use the Camp David summit to broaden the partnership with Washington and Tokyo with at least two joint declarations on building trilateral ties, the “Camp David Principles” and “Spirit of Camp David.”

Fumio Kishida has a split image: Abroad, he is seen as a decisive leader ushering Japan into a new era of muscular international engagement. At home, though, he is seen as an indecisive and colorless technocrat with few answers for solving Japan’s biggest domestic challenges.

He has brought vigor, however, to Japan’s foreign and national security policy, where he has made changes that long seemed impossible. While Mr. Abe, Japan’s longest-serving prime minister, was viewed skeptically by many Japanese because of his right-wing views on history, the more moderate Mr. Kishida has succeeded in nudging Japan away from its longstanding pacifism and toward a more active role in international affairs.

During his two years in office, Mr. Kishida has set Japan on a course to double its military spending, improved its relationships with global friends and allies and, in a break with decades of precedent, supplied Ukraine with nonlethal aid.

Peter Baker

Peter Baker has covered the past five presidents, including some events at Camp David.

President Biden’s three-way meeting at Camp David on Friday with the leaders of Japan and South Korea is the latest in a long line of big moments at the storied presidential retreat in the Catoctin Mountains of Maryland — but the first in eight years.

The gathering with Prime Minister Fumio Kishida of Japan and President Yoon Suk Yeol of South Korea at the woodsy getaway will be the first time Mr. Biden has invited foreign leaders to join him there since taking office and the first time any foreign leaders have been there since 2015, when President Barack Obama hosted top Arab officials.

The sprawling, remote 148-acre retreat has been a favorite of most presidents since Franklin D. Roosevelt started going there in the 1930s, although not every president fancies its rustic ambience (Mr. Obama among them). Guests staying in one of the 11 stone-and-wood cabins get around in golf carts and can go bowling, swim, shoot skeet, and play pool, tennis, basketball, golf or horseshoes.

A short helicopter ride from the White House, Camp David offers open-air freedom for presidents who otherwise remain mostly cocooned in a tight security bubble.

For many of them, it has been a welcome venue to decompress on weekends or to forge more relaxed relationships with foreign counterparts. Roosevelt, who called the camp Shangri-La, brought Prime Minister Winston Churchill of Britain there during World War II. Dwight D. Eisenhower later renamed it after his grandson David.

Over the decades, Camp David has hosted many momentous events, probably none more famous than Jimmy Carter’s epic 13-day negotiations in 1978 that ultimately led to a landmark peace treaty between Israel and Egypt and transformed the politics of the Middle East. Others have sought to replicate the magic of that moment, only to fall short, as when Bill Clinton huddled with Israeli and Palestinian leaders there in 2000.

Emulating the Roosevelt and Churchill meeting, George W. Bush invited Prime Minister Tony Blair of Britain to Camp David in 2001 for a get-to-know-you overnight visit that included an evening showing of the movie “Meet the Parents.” Mr. Bush later shared with reporters that the two leaders used the same brand of toothpaste. (“They’re going to wonder how you know that, George,” Mr. Blair quipped.)

President Biden hosting President Yoon Suk Yeol and Prime Minister Fumio Kishida at Camp David is an American diplomatic dream come true: a growing partnership between South Korea and Japan.

For decades, South Korea and Japan have been the two most important allies of Washington in East Asia. The United States keeps a large military presence in both nations. But Seoul and Tokyo could never get along, their relations soured by historical disputes rooted in Japan’s onetime colonial rule of Korea.

This year, Mr. Yoon began ​chipping away at the logjam. He offered a solution in March to settle the dispute over forced labor during colonial rule. In return, Japan lifted trade restrictions on South Korea’s semiconductor industry.

Here is why it is crucial for Japan and South Korea to get along as part of Washington’s broader strategy in Asia and beyond.

  • In recent years, the United States has found it more pressing than ever to bring its allies and like-minded partners together to deal with common threats, such as the Russian invasion of Ukraine and China’s economic and military ambitions. The troubled relationship between Tokyo and Seoul has long been a weak link in Washington’s efforts in the Indo-Pacific.

  • North Korea’s growing nuclear and missile threat helped Seoul and Tokyo recognize the strategic value of building up trilateral cooperation with the United States. During a speech on Tuesday, Mr. Yoon stressed the importance of the three nations sharing reconnaissance assets and real-time information about North Korean missile threats. He also reminded his people that the American military bases in Japan also helped deter North Korea from attacking the South.

  • South Korea and Japan are key components in Washington’s plans to secure global supply chains, especially in semiconductors. South Korea is the world’s leading producer of memory chips, and Japan supplies tools and materials essential to chip-making.

  • Seoul, Tokyo and Washington share a strong common interest in cooperating over the Taiwan Strait. If there is a conflict involving Taiwan, the nuclear-armed North Korea might see that as an opportunity to invade the South. China’s assertive behavior in recent years has only deepened Washington’s desire for its allies to play a bigger role in regional defense.
