George Santos is charged with fraud

Representative George Santos, the New York Republican who campaigned on a life story littered with lies, has been charged by federal prosecutors with 13 counts including money laundering, stealing public money, wire fraud and making false statements to Congress.

Santos pleaded not guilty and was released on a $500,000 bond. He faces up to 20 years in prison if convicted.

During a chaotic scene outside a federal court on Long Island this afternoon, Santos said the charges were a product of a “witch hunt” and that he would not resign. “I have to keep fighting to defend my innocence,” Santos said, “and I’m going to do that.”

Prosecutors say Santos was involved in three separate schemes, but the bulk of the indictment focuses on allegations that Santos directed a political consultant to solicit contributions to a company that he falsely claimed was a political fund. Prosecutors also accuse Santos of fraudulently claiming more than $24,000 in unemployment payments and lying on financial disclosure forms.

Santos has been besieged by questions about his background since December, when The Times and other outlets began reporting on numerous lies about his biography.

It is unlikely that the federal charges will affect his immediate standing in Congress. A growing number of Republicans are calling for his resignation, but House Speaker Kevin McCarthy has signaled that Santos will be allowed to continue to serve.

Inflation slowed, again

The rise in U.S. consumer prices slowed for a 10th straight month in April — good news for American families and for Washington policymakers who have been working to tame inflation for more than a year.

Today’s report showed that the Consumer Price Index rose 4.9 percent in the year through last month, less than some economists expected, offering further evidence that inflation is slowing down. Still, many experts warn that the road back to normal inflation — around 2 percent a year — remains a long one.

“It’s great news that we’re down to 4.9 percent, but it’s bad news that we’re still up at 4.9 percent,” my colleague Jeanna Smialek told me. “There’s this big concern that we’ve already eaten all the low hanging fruit that helped get inflation down from 9 percent, where it was last summer.”

Costs for new cars and gas, which for many months drove inflation up, have moderated, helping to bring overall inflation down. Price increases for services, which are typically harder to tamp down, are now the driving force behind inflation.

F.D.A. advisers favor an over-the-counter birth control pill

A panel of health experts said that the benefits of making a birth control pill available without a prescription outweigh the risks. If the F.D.A. approves nonprescription sales of the medication, called Opill, this summer, it could significantly expand access to contraception.

The decades-long push to make an over-the-counter pill available has garnered support from specialists in reproductive and adolescent health. While some Catholic organizations have spoken out against nonprescription birth control, most anti-abortion groups have been quiet on the issue.

Ukraine says it gained ground in Bakhmut

Ukrainian commanders said that their troops had broken through Russian positions in Bakhmut, taking back roughly three square miles of the embattled eastern city. If confirmed, it would be the first significant gain for Ukraine in months in a city that has taken on a symbolic significance for both sides. However, it was far from clear that it was a turning point in the monthslong battle.

Was Cleopatra Black?

Soon after the trailer for the Netflix series “African Queens” appeared last month, the show was attacked by the Egyptian media and the government, which called its portrayal of Cleopatra a “falsification of Egyptian history.” The issue at hand: Cleopatra is played by a Black actress.

Evidence of Cleopatra’s ancestry and physical attributes are inconclusive. Netflix’s casting is a reflection of Western arguments over Black representation in Hollywood and whether history is too dominated by white narratives that revolve around European primacy.

But it has stirred up a very different debate in Egyptian society, which often prizes light skin and where many view identity and race through another lens. For many Egyptians, the question is whether Egyptians and their ancient ancestors — geographical location notwithstanding — are African.

Traveling for discomfort

Vacation is most often associated with pleasure, relaxation and comfort. But in this week’s T Magazine, Hanya Yanagihara, the editor in chief, encourages readers to seek discomfort while traveling.

Not the discomfort that comes from a narrow airplane seat or delayed flight, she writes, “but the sort that comes from finding yourself someplace unfamiliar, where you know no one and life feels unpredictable.”

Americans are smuggling Fruit Roll-Ups into Israel

TikTok videos showing influencers wrapping a scoop of ice cream in a Fruit Roll-Up, which gives the dessert a satisfying, crunchy texture, have caused the American-made fruit leather snack to sell out in many Israeli stores — and led some people to take extreme measures.

Hundreds of pounds of Fruit Roll-Ups have been confiscated from people visiting Israel in recent weeks. One couple brought in nearly 400 pounds, far exceeding the country’s legal import limit, which is around 11 pounds for a specific food product.

Last week, the Israeli authorities called for an end to the frenzy, labeling it a “madness” and recommending that people try cucumber rolls instead.
