Residents of central Vermont began cleaning up on Thursday afternoon after heavy rain battered roads and bridges and forced dozens of overnight rescues and evacuations. The flash flooding affected some of the same towns that were devastated by record rain and flooding exactly one year ago.

One death in the state is believed to have been caused by the extreme weather, Jennifer Morrison, the state’s commissioner of public safety, said at a news conference Thursday morning, though she added that the cause had not been confirmed. The death occurred when a vehicle was swept into floodwater in Peacham, east of Montpelier, she said.

The rainfall, attributed to the passing remnants of Tropical Storm Beryl, soaked some locations with more than 7 inches in a 24-hour period beginning Wednesday morning, and was expected to taper off to isolated showers.

Compared with the historic rainfall over two days last July, the overnight event was much more localized, said Matthew Clay, a meteorologist with the National Weather Service in Burlington. The highest totals were scattered among a few locations, rather than across wide swaths of the state.

Mr. Clay said the most extreme rainfall “just missed” Montpelier, the state capital, where floodwaters from the Winooski River inundated the city’s center last July. But nearby in Barre, a city of about 8,000 people that was devastated by last summer’s storm, flooding was once again extensive: Its downtown was underwater, and some homes were washed away.

“People have a sense of PTSD with what we went through a year ago,” said Chris Violette, the town manager in Barre Town, a separate municipality that surrounds the city. “And now we’re dealing with what seems like déjà vu.”

State officials at the news conference said that three bridges had been destroyed by floodwaters and three others damaged, in locations including Norton, Charleston, Morristown and Barnet. The officials urged residents to stay away from rivers, where flood-borne debris continued to pose hazards.

More than a dozen swift-water rescue teams, including one from New Hampshire and another from Connecticut, made 118 rescues from Wednesday into Thursday, officials said. Rescues continued in Lyndonville, northeast of Montpelier, on Thursday morning.

Mental health counselors were deployed in some communities on Thursday to assist residents experiencing trauma, said officials.

“I can’t assure people this won’t happen year after year,” Gov. Phil Scott said at the news briefing. “But what we need to do is build back better, and we will get through this, as we did last time around.”
