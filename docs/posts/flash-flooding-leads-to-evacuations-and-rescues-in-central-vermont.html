Flash Flooding Leads to Evacuations and Rescues in Central Vermont

The downpour took place exactly a year after a storm caused devastating flooding in some of the same towns.

July 11, 2024Updated 10:24 p.m. ET

In Plainfield, Vt., a town of 1,350, some residents spent the past year cleaning up their homes after catastrophic floods swept through last July.

On Thursday, many found themselves right back where they had started after flooding once again hit Plainfield and other parts of central Vermont, on the anniversary of the disaster that they had just started putting behind them.

“It’s something very difficult to witness — people who have spent a year cleaning their place up, and now it’s covered in four feet of mud,” said Bram Towbin, the town clerk and treasurer in Plainfield. “We’re very resilient people, but these are not people of means. This is something we’ll be living with for many years.”

The flooding battered roads and bridges in the state and forced more than 100 rescues and evacuations overnight.

Two deaths in the state are believed to have been caused by the extreme weather.

Dylan Kempton, 33, was found dead in South Peacham Brook, east of Montpelier, around midnight, state police confirmed. His utility terrain vehicle, similar to an ATV, was found nearby where a portion of adjacent roadway had collapsed.

Later on Thursday, the body of a 73-year-old man was recovered in Lyndonville after his car was swept off a flooded road.

Police Chief Jack Harris of Lyndon confirmed the victim as John Rice, 73, of Concord. His vehicle was swept away when he attempted to drive on a flooded road that morning, according to the police.

The rainfall, attributed to the passing remnants of Tropical Storm Beryl, soaked some places with more than seven inches in a 24-hour period beginning on Wednesday morning.

Compared with the record rainfall over two days last July, the overnight event was much more localized, said Matthew Clay, a meteorologist with the National Weather Service in Burlington. The highest totals were scattered among a few locations, rather than affecting wide swaths of the state.

Mr. Clay said the most extreme rainfall “just missed” Montpelier, the state capital, where floodwaters from the Winooski River inundated the downtown last July. But 10 miles away in Barre, a city of about 8,000 people that was devastated by the storm last summer, flooding was again extensive. The downtown was underwater and some homes in the area were washed away.

“People have a sense of PTSD with what we went through a year ago, and now we’re dealing with what seems like déjà vu,” said Chris Violette, the town manager in Barre Town, a separate municipality that surrounds the city. “The crazy part is, we came so close; 10 miles to the south, there was no rain at all.”

State officials at the news conference said that three bridges had been destroyed by floodwaters and three others damaged, including in Norton, Charleston, Morristown and Barnet. The officials urged residents to stay away from rivers, where flood-borne debris continued to pose hazards.

More than a dozen swift-water rescue teams, including one from New Hampshire and another from Connecticut, made 118 rescues from Wednesday into Thursday, officials said.

In Plainfield, Mr. Towbin said residents of one apartment building were evacuated just minutes before it collapsed into the raging Great Brook. Two other houses, a trailer and two bridges were also swept away, he said. Most of the main roads in and out of town are shut down, and dozens of people have been displaced from their homes.

He estimated the losses as a “multimillion-dollar event,” on top of the $320,000 the town has spent from its own modest $1.4 million annual budget to recover from last year’s floods. Reimbursement is said to be coming, he said, but no one is sure when.

In Barre and elsewhere, some residents were able to prepare better for the heavy rain and flooding because of their hard experience last year, Mr. Violette said. Many piled sandbags in advance and moved cars and other valuables to higher ground.

Harder to prepare for were the psychological effects. Mental health counselors were deployed in some communities on Thursday to assist residents experiencing trauma, officials said.

Gov. Phil Scott of Vermont, speaking at the news conference on Thursday, acknowledged the fear, more pervasive than ever, that severe weather would strike the state again and again.

“I can’t assure people this won’t happen year after year,” the governor said. “Climate change is real, and we have to deal with it and build back stronger, safer and smarter than we have before.”
