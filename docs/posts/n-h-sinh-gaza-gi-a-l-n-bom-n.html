Daniela Blum and Dr. Robert Flowers didn’t quite intend to be the first couple to be married at the Manhattan Marriage Bureau this year.

Daniela Llorente Blum and Dr. Robert Costigan Flowers, the first couple to marry at Manhattan Marriage Bureau in 2024, hustled downtown on the morning of Jan. 2 not to be first in line, but because they were finally ready to quit stalling: They had already called off their wedding six times.

Ms. Blum and Dr. Flowers, both 34, live in the Murray Hill neighborhood of Manhattan. When they met on the dating app Hinge in December 2018, both of their locations were set to South Florida. Ms. Blum, who moved to Key Biscayne from her native Bogotá, Colombia, in 2016, was working as a project manager at d music marketing, an agency that promotes American artists in Latin America. Dr. Flowers, then a resident in internal medicine at the University of Miami Hospital, had moved to Miami months earlier after receiving his M.D. at Tulane University.

Neither had a lot of energy for dating at the time. Ms. Blum often traveled to Latin America for work. When Dr. Flowers wasn’t in scrubs, he was usually exhausted. Dating apps were testing the fortitude of both: “I was kind of giving up hope,” Dr. Flowers said. (Multiple women he had struck up promising conversations with online seemed different in person.) Ms. Blum didn’t have much hope to begin with. “I always had this hate against apps, like they were so fake,” she said.

A friend convinced her to dip a toe in the dating pool a year after she and a long-term boyfriend had broken up. Her first time swiping through profiles left her cold. “It was a major letdown,” she said. Then, in December 2018, she came across Dr. Flowers’s profile, which seemed different.

For one thing, he wasn’t hoisting a dead fish. “I don’t know why that’s a thing for men” on dating apps, she said. His love of “Harry Potter,” her favorite book series, was also a positive sign.

Ms. Blum was first introduced to Harry Potter in Bogotá, where she grew up with her younger brother. Their parents divorced when she was 15. Her mother, Maricel Blum, an artist, splits her time between Denver and Sweden and travels often to Africa to do educational outreach, said Ms. Blum, who graduated with a bachelor’s degree in business and psychology from the University of the Andes in Bogotá. A sense of nostalgia brought her to South Florida, where she used to visit her grandmother’s apartment in Key Biscayne every summer with her family when she was a child.

Dr. Flowers spent his youth in and around Florida, taking family vacations in Destin and Fort Morgan, Ala. He grew up with five siblings in Ridgeland, Miss., where their parents, an infectious disease doctor and a dermatologist, still live. Although he and his siblings weren’t expected to follow in their parents’ — and both grandfathers’ — footsteps into medicine, four of the five are now physicians.

“My parents actually encouraged us to look elsewhere for careers,” Dr. Flowers said. At Emory University, he initially took classes in journalism but graduated with a bachelor’s in anthropology. He earned a master’s degree in public health from Tulane. But, he said, “ultimately I realized that my passion was interacting with people, and making a diagnosis through listening to their story.”

When he asked Ms. Blum out on a date toward the end of December 2018, he had already started learning her story. For three weeks, while they were visiting their families for the holidays, they texted daily, sometimes hourly. By the time they met on Jan. 5, 2019, at AMC Sunset Place 24 in Miami to watch the Harry Potter spinoff “Fantastic Beasts: The Crimes of Grindelwald,” he was relieved that they could skip the small talk.

Except he was late. “Blame it on Miami traffic,” he said. “Dani was waiting in the lobby for me. I remember feeling bad about it.” In their rush to find seats in the dark, they whispered guesses about what they had missed. “We walked into this magical battle, with lights everywhere,” Ms. Blum said. “We were like, ‘What is happening?’”

It didn’t matter. Neither remembers the movie, only that they kept stealing glances at each other and felt comfort sitting side by side. Afterward, they went to dinner at 1-800-Lucky, a food hall in the Wynwood neighborhood.

“We found we could talk about anything,” said Ms. Blum. For hours, they talked about their careers, their families, music and food. Dr. Flowers, who was used to staying awake for 24-hour shifts at the hospital, said he would not have minded another sleepless night to keep it going. “Dani was a real person,” he said. “I was at home with her. She wasn’t putting on an act.”

He went home that night feeling lovestruck. “The whole night, it was like we were 13,” he said.

The next day, they met for a beach picnic and Ms. Blum introduced him to slacklining, similar to tightrope walking. Before leading him toward a pair of palm trees connected by a tightrope, she issued a warning: “When you’re learning, you fall all the time.” He was game anyway.

“I thought, This is not for me,” he said, but “I was ready to fall on my face again and again, because I felt like, this is someone special.”

Fall he did.

By March, they were off Hinge and spending their precious few non-working hours together. In the summer of 2020, while Covid-19 was raging, he moved into her apartment in Key Biscayne. He took on extra shifts in the intensive care unit while she worked from home. “I was scared for him all the time,” she said. “He would send me photos of himself wearing all the things they had to wear to protect themselves, like a spacesuit.” At home, he dropped his clothes in the hallway and showered before accepting his evening kiss from her and their cat, Dante Alberto.

Dr. Flowers’s residency ended in June 2022. By then, he had accepted a fellowship in the pulmonary, critical care and sleep medicine division at NYU Langone Health. But he wouldn’t leave South Florida without Ms. Blum. A month before they moved to New York City, on May 11, he proposed.

“Robbie had the day off, so he planned a picnic at the beach for my birthday,” said Ms. Blum, now a project manager at Partisan Records, an independent label. In addition to a cooler, he packed a ring. When he dropped to one knee in the sand, “I was speechless,” she recalled. “I gave him a terrified face.” Her terror, translated, meant yes.

Ms. Blum and Dr. Flowers started looking into weddings at the City Clerk’s office last September, shortly after they had moved from their Brooklyn apartment to Murray Hill. But celebrating with their families took precedence over carving out time for a legal wedding. On Oct. 7, they exchanged vows in a Colombian forest near Bogotá surrounded by 120 guests. Dr. Flowers’s sister-in-law, Megan Flowers, led the ceremony.

A month later, the Rev. Jennifer Deaton, a longtime friend of the Flowers family, held an Episcopalian marriage blessing for them at the house of Dr. Flowers’s parents in Ridgeland. Religion had not been a factor in their Bogotá ceremony, “but we felt it was important to both of our moms to have some blessing from God,” Ms. Blum said. Her mother traveled from Denver to be there.

By then, they had already started rescheduling appointments with the Manhattan Marriage Bureau — six in total. Work was to blame. “It’s almost impossible for us both to get time off during business hours,” he said. “I spend half my time in the I.C.U.”

When they showed up at 8 a.m. on Jan. 2, Ms. Blum worried they would be turned away since they’d canceled so many times. “I kept telling Robbie, they’re going to say we can’t do it,” she said. But within an hour, Madeline Plasencia, an officiant with the City Clerk’s office, had made it official: The bride, wearing jeans and Converse sneakers, was on her way to work, a married woman.

Delayed But Delighted After work that night, Ms. Blum and Dr. Flowers, who also wore jeans to the ceremony, didn’t do much to celebrate. “We had a quiet night in, we cooked and watched TV,” Dr. Flowers said. The weekend after was a little more festive: The couple escaped for a brief honeymoon in Turks and Caicos.

A Co-worker Comes Through Hope Miodownik, one of Dr. Flowers’s colleagues from the hospital, served as a witness. Because of the holidays, loved ones “either weren’t in town, or they were working,” Dr. Flowers said. “I mentioned to Hope that we were having difficulty finding someone to witness, and she gladly volunteered.” Dr. Flowers hopes to complete his fellowship at NYU Langone Health in June 2025.

Facing Forward Ms. Blum and Dr. Flowers chose to sit on a sofa instead of stand during their ceremony in Colombia. “When I first heard that they’d be sitting on a sofa looking at their guests, I thought that was weird,” Ms. Flowers said while performing the ceremony. “But I realized it made sense because this is how Robert and Dani are — they would never give their backs to their friends and family.”
