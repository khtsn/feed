nafany on TDK project: "We're living at a loss; I feel the burden a small organization feels"

After AMKAL ceased operations at the end of 2025, 24-year-old in-game leader Vladislav "⁠nafany⁠" Gorshkov turned to familiar faces Sergey "⁠Ax1Le⁠" Rykhtorov and coach Konstantin "⁠groove⁠" Pikiner to help start a new project heading into 2026, TDK.

The team, originally rounded out by Amin "⁠sugaR⁠" Azimov, Kirill "⁠Xant3r⁠" Kononov, and Aleksandr "⁠zorte⁠" Zagodyrenko, has spent much of 2026 grinding a variety of online and a handful of local Russian LANs, looking to climb the Valve Regional Standings (VRS). "We started playing in February, and we understood that our chances of qualifying for this Major were minimal because we missed out on registering for many Moscow LANs," nafany said of his team's aspirations for the year.

"Our goal for next season is to accumulate as many points as we can, and we're working towards that. And then we'll see. There's no point dwelling on whether we'll make the Major or not — we're not in a position to think that far ahead."

TDK's situation is further complicated by the players' contracts. nafany, sugaR, and Xant3r are still tied to AMKAL, Insilio, and Nemiga, respectively, while Ax1Le and zorte are still contracted with BetBoom, a situation which culminated in the lattermost player being called back to his organization's starting lineup ahead of TDK's LAN outings, forcing the team to utilize Vladislav "⁠h1te⁠" Khait as a stand-in.

Before his team's campaign at MPKBK CIS LAN Season 4, where TDK ultimately finished in 5-8th place, nafany had a lengthy chat with HLTV discussing his post-BetBoom career, including his tenure with AMKAL and the formation of the TDK lineup. nafany further expanded on the team's financial and contract situation, the team's goals for the remainder of this season and heading into the next, and his thoughts on the "crazy unrealized potential" of his teammate Xant3r.

Editor's note:

This interview was conducted in Russian and translated by HLTV staff.

You're well known for your successful career with Gambit and Cloud9. But after you left that team, and after BetBoom, very little was heard about you. What can you tell us about that period?

There's actually a lot to tell. Firstly, after we parted ways with BetBoom, for a long time, I was trying to put something together with groove, but for various reasons, something always fell through at the last moment. There was one time where we were genuinely incredibly close to having a really competitive roster with a good org and proper conditions, but at the last moment, it didn't work out.

Meanwhile, I was playing in various mix lineups I assembled myself — sometimes successfully, sometimes not. But either way, it was genuinely good experience, even in terms of in-game practice. Because you can look at my stats from that period, even though they don't mean much: in BetBoom I was 0.90-something, but after joining AMKAL, I think my rating is around 1.10. Honestly, I don't follow it that closely; I just know it's good.

And during that period, I was talking to various players, and I believe I earned a decent reputation among them. In any case, I heard positive things, a lot of people wanted to play. Then AMKAL reached out, we agreed on certain conditions. Unfortunately, about a month after I signed my contract, the bookmakers in Russia got hit really hard, and what we'd agreed on became essentially unworkable. AMKAL is really not to blame — it was just a set of circumstances. So me and PASHANOJ had to work with what we had.

Russia's bookmakers face massive changes with a new tax structure, making the country's market much more expensive to operate in.

Overall, looking at the results in AMKAL, it was obviously terrible. We probably had a below 50% win rate. But either way, it was genuinely great experience for me. And I think PASHANOJ is a really strong coach, he gave me a lot. I'm confident it was a very good experience.

If you look at AMKAL's ranking, it was steadily declining for a long time. Was the uncertainty and the lack of support the main reasons for such a period of underperformance, or were there other reasons?

It's hard to say. The system I wanted to play in required certain players who fit that system. When the bookmaker situation happened, to me it seemed likely that it would kill the org before the end of the year. I didn't know for sure, I just sensed that's probably how it would go. Then, certain decisions, like the bootcamp, only confirmed my suspicions, and I think the team felt that internally too, so it 100% played its role.

Obviously, even looking at our expenses — we have a spreadsheet — we're all genuinely living at a loss. Essentially, I feel the burden that any small organization feels

Tell me about your collaboration with groove — why specifically do you want to build a team with him? And what's your vision for your career?

Look, having worked closely with five different coaches over the course of my career, I can say with confidence that groove is the best coach I've worked with. Obviously, if he's the best, I'd want to continue with him. I'd probably put PASHANOJ in second place. After AMKAL, PASHANOJ and I talked, but there wasn't really an idea about how to continue that thing further.

After AMKAL ended, groove came to me and said that he and Ax1Le had been talking, and Ax1Le seemed open to putting something new together. So, groove and I sat down and thought about who we could bring in. But again, you have to understand the market situation: everyone knows that now that the bookmakers are gone, the chances of an org coming to AMKAL, BetBoom, Nemiga, or anyone, and offering gazillion dollars to buy a player are essentially zero.

Basically, the chance of anyone paying big money for established players right now is unrealistic. Sure, I have experience, but why take someone like me when you can take some young player who hasn't proven himself yet, risk a smaller amount, and maybe get someone who breaks through? Most organizations are going that route right now. And I think that was the key reason why we decided to build something like this.

nafany has reunited with longtime teammate Ax1Le as part of the TDK project

Obviously, even looking at our expenses — we have a spreadsheet — we're all genuinely living at a loss. Essentially, I feel the burden that any small organization feels. Because we're paying really significant money just to show up here for this tournament. I live in Serbia, zorte lives in Serbia, we had to book a hotel in Moscow, ideally close to the venue — that's a lot of money. Flights — also a lot of money. On top of that, tournament entry fees are also significant.

In Europe, from what I've heard, average entry fee is around €800. Here they're not even close €800 — even at the previous LAN it wasn't €800. And that really hits hard financially if you're a team without an organization. But one way or another, it's high risk, high reward. If you're willing to put in your own money and everyone gives 100%, sooner or later it will produce results.

So is it just exclusively your own money with no external support?

It's all our own money. Obviously, we're all still on contract and getting salaries from our respective orgs, but we're at risk of what happened with zorte — he went to play for BetBoom, but it wasn't his decision, he had no choice. He's under contract, the org comes to him and says: we need you. I mean, he kinda can say no, but contractually, he can't really say no.

So effectively, we're all still tied to our respective orgs, and we're not insured against that. Which is why we have to play this tournament with a stand-in who's a slightly weaker player overall. Although h1te actually made a good impression on me, I think he's actually quite strong. Either way, playing with a stand-in and playing with your actual player are two different things.

So me specifically, right now, I'm living at a loss. I need to pay for food, rent, everything else. There's some salary from the org, obviously, but I'm spending my own savings regardless.

The time I spent sorting out schedules and trying to juggle everything, I could have spent on my actual game. Obviously, when you have an org, a manager, people who are invested in all of this — it's so much easier

What do you see as the future you're investing in this team for? What do you want to achieve?

We can take a look at the current market — setting aside Spirit, BetBoom, PARIVISION. If we don't take actual results into account, every other CIS roster is, in my opinion, on paper, either on the same level as us or weaker. And I think every player in our team understands that. So if tomorrow some team came to me and said, "Come join us," I wouldn't even have a thought that maybe it'd be worth it.

I'm not desperately broke right now, and fortunately, I managed to earn some money while playing in tier one. I'm ready to invest in my own future, and so is each of us. Even with Ax1Le, he had certain plans for February. But I sat down with him, talked it through, showed him the schedule, and said it'd be great if he could rearrange things. In the end, Ax1Le actually cancelled his own plans entirely for the team. And that means a lot to me.

Is there a goal of building your own thing, or is the plan to become strong enough that you get bought together as a competitive lineup?

I think building our own org is unrealistic, we simply couldn't afford the buyouts. And even if we were released from our contracts for one reason or another, I think it would just be complicated and unrealistic. And the energy we're already spending just to participate in tournaments takes focus away.

We were playing in two different events simultaneously, NODWIN and ESL Challenger League, and I was handling all the organizational side. Coordinating with admins and operators so schedules don't conflict is genuinely hectic. We got lucky in some moments, and thank god the admins and operators met us halfway in others. But if you look at other teams in the same situation — some fucked up due to scheduling conflicts, or a TO just couldn't accommodate them.

And that takes focus away from you. The time I spent sorting out schedules and trying to juggle everything, I could have spent on my actual game. Obviously, when you have an org, a manager, people who are invested in all of this — it's so much easier.

You mentioned the roster looks very strong on paper. If you manage to continue playing together, what would you expect from the team by next season?

I don't set long-term goals, but here's what it looks like today. We started playing in February, and we understood that our chances of qualifying for this Major were minimal because we missed out on registering for many Moscow LANs. You also have to understand that right now, the game is such that you get punished for any fuck up instantly, and you can lose to absolutely any team.

This season is more of a warmup. The reason we're even playing this tournament isn't this Major, we just want to accumulate as much VRS as possible so that next season we start playing events like NODWIN and CCT, not from closed quals but from the group stage. And ideally, we'd start from playoffs, but that's not realistic today. So step by step.

Our goal for next season is to accumulate as many points as we can, and we're working towards that. And then we'll see. There's no point dwelling on whether we'll make the Major or not — we're not in a position to think that far ahead. If we had the resources even for a bootcamp, we could start setting those longer-term goals. Because when you're in the same room as your teammates it's easier to communicate, you can make sure they're not slackers, and no one can bullshit you that they're not on their phones during reviews. All of that matters. Right now, unfortunately, that's not something we can do.

I mean, really — huge thanks to our respective organizations for letting us play at all, because any org could have said no. And what could we have done? Nothing

Your roster leans toward experience — don't you feel that's a vulnerability, that you don't have some uncharted, explosive young talent who could propel you forward?

Look, let's take Ax1Le and me: obviously, we have a lot of experience. Take zorte, he has quite a bit of experience too. But if we consider Xant3r, even setting aside the fact that he played at a Major, what he played there with Nemiga is very different from what we play now. And Xant3r had zero experience with this system at all.

Actually, Xant3r is a player with a fucking crazy unrealized potential. His micro is incredibly sharp, genuinely insane. But the understanding of the global picture of what we're doing is completely absent. Not because he's a bad player, but simply because Nemiga played something completely different, and he just has no understanding of how these things work in the way we play.

I think Xant3r still needs a fair amount of time to settle into it. But I think he could actually become the kind of player who steps up and makes things a bit easier for us. And speaking specifically about our situation — I have a player with a 1.20 rating. So what? Do I need another player who plays with 1.40, another donk? How could we even find someone better? Ax1Le is at his prime — screw it, let him fuck.

I genuinely see no reason to think we need to upgrade somehow if your player has a 1.20 and your AWPer has a 1.18. You will find a few AWPers who put up those same stats, it's just a handful of those. After an HLTV rating update, they typically run around 1.04. That's roughly the average, I think.

So overall, I don't think the problem is that we lack some young explosive players. We have a pretty good balance. There might be a slight imbalance in that we have relatively quiet players and I'm the only one who is vocal like that. But I don't think that's a major problem. As long as you talk things through, the others will develop their voices naturally.

If I understand correctly, if BetBoom qualifies for the Major they'd be forced to play with FL4MUS or zorte. How big of a problem would that be for you if they qualify and decide to use zorte?

I can't even say. That's not something that depends on me. If BetBoom tells zorte "Sanya, you're playing the Major with us" — there's nothing we can do about it. We're under contract, we're basically almost slaves. What are we going to do? They pay our salaries.

The only thing I'd say is — I'm not even sure how it's all arranged, since I'm not part of those decisions, but if that happens, at least pay him his full salary. That's at least something, I'd already be happy with that (laughs). What can we do?

We're not an org, we're just people on contract, we essentially have no rights. Well, thank god at least they allowed us to play here. I mean, really — huge thanks to our respective organizations for letting us play at all, because any org could have said no. And what could we have done? Nothing.

What are your expectations from this tournament? Do you feel your team can show a result that gives you a good VRS boost?

At the last tournament, I had some expectations — like if we play well here, we still have one more tournament; if we play that one well too, maybe we can even fight for the Major. But given that we're playing with a stand-in here, and considering the experience of last time... zero fucks given. If we play well — great; we fuck up — it sucks, we'll have to keep working. But what else can you do? At this point, no pressure, just grind CS as best we can. If it goes well — fucking great. If it goes badly — that's sad.
