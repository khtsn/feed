Ferguson 10 Years Later: How Protests Gave Way to Politics and Policy

The shooting death of Michael Brown created a political incubator of emerging local leaders, some of whom are finding themselves in the corridors of power.

Audra D. S. Burch

Audra D.S. Burch writes about race and identity and spent time in Ferguson, Mo., to report on the 10th anniversary of Michael Brown’s death.

  • Aug. 9, 2024

When Michael Brown Jr., an unarmed Black teenager, was fatally shot in 2014 by a white police officer and his body left in the street under the August sun, the small St. Louis suburb of Ferguson roiled and plunged into crisis.

Community grief, long-simmering fury and distrust of government gave way to waves of protests that continued for months. There were violent confrontations between protesters and heavily armed law enforcement officers. A neighborhood convenience store was looted and burned to the ground. The protests drew activists from across the nation, amplified the national Black Lives Matter movement and fueled larger questions about race in America.

And at a more grass-roots level, it created a political incubator of emerging local leaders: A new generation of young protesters who came of age during the Ferguson uprising have found ways to chip away at the racial disparities in Ferguson and nearby St. Louis, shifting from protests to politics and policies.

Since Mr. Brown’s death, Ferguson elected its first Black mayor. The police chief and more than half of the police officers are Black. And St. Louis County elected its first Black prosecutor.

“Ferguson gave birth to all kinds of young people who were committed to civil rights and social justice and representation,” said Kimberly Norwood, a Washington University law professor and editor of “Ferguson’s Fault Lines: The Race Quake That Rocked a Nation.”

Mr. Brown’s death was part of a series of high-profile police killings over a decade, from Eric Garner to Sonya Massey. Two years before Mr. Brown died, Trayvon Martin had been killed by a neighborhood watch volunteer, drawing protests across the nation. Taken collectively, the violence against African Americans catalyzed the national Black Lives Matter movement.

Residents say there is more work to be done to rebuild trust in the community, but there have been some changes over the decade, including the use of body cameras in police departments across the country. In Ferguson, the police department is under a federal consent decree to improve its practices.

Ten summers after Mr. Brown’s death, some of the early protest front-liners in Ferguson are now in the corridors of power, elected to seats in government or leading campaigns to help elect progressive Black candidates.

“Over time, we started to realize that this issue with police brutality and what happened to Mike Brown was a lot bigger,” said Rasheen Aldridge, 30, a protester who now serves on the St. Louis Board of Aldermen in the city’s 14th Ward. “It’s about how people that are in local government have input on the way that people live.”

The most high-profile example of the political reach of the Ferguson uprising was this week’s Democratic primary in Missouri’s First Congressional District, where the front-runners were two Black candidates whose political identities were formed, in part, during the protests.

The two-term U.S. Representative Cori Bush, who drew support from the movement and was endorsed by Mr. Brown’s father, lost her seat to Wesley Bell, 49, the chief prosecutor for St. Louis County. His campaign was bolstered by a powerful pro-Israel lobby group that poured in more than $8 million through a political action committee.

Ms. Bush, 48, was among the most prominent protest voices, spending hundreds of days demonstrating alongside community members. Two years after Mr. Brown’s death, she entered politics with an unsuccessful bid for the U.S. Senate, followed by a House of Representatives loss two years later. But in 2020, she unseated Representative William Lacy Clay, a 10-term Democrat, becoming the first organizer from the larger Black Lives Matter movement elected to Congress. Ms. Bush won again in 2022.

During the unrest, Mr. Bell, a lawyer, acted as a mediator and later hosted community listening sessions. He was elected to the Ferguson City Council in the first election after the protests and later unseated Robert P. McCulloch, the St. Louis County prosecutor who was widely criticized after declining to indict Mr. Brown’s killer. (Mr. Bell would later reach the same conclusion in his own investigation, much to the disappointment of many in the activist community.)

The congressional race “demonstrates that movements can take one of their own and advance into places where they never thought they would be,” said Starsky Wilson, who served as a co-chairman of the Ferguson Commission, a group assembled to examine racial and economic disparities in the St. Louis region. He is now president of the Children’s Defense Fund.

Fran Griffin, 43, attended her first City Council meeting after Mr. Brown’s death. In the weeks after the shooting, she had been protesting, marching, demanding justice. What she wanted, she said, was accountability, but also a way to make sure community voices were heard. In 2016, she ran unsuccessfully for a council seat as a write-in candidate. Three years later, she won and served a single term.

“I had never considered this kind of position before,” she said. “Our whole community was mourning, and the police had treated us inhumanely. It was the principle for me. I knew during the protests that I was going to get involved.”

In the hours after Mr. Brown was killed along Canfield Drive — now home to a memorial plaque embedded in the sidewalk — Kayla Reed joined the growing crowd, drawn in by images racing across social media.

That night changed her career path. She went from being a spectator to a protester to organizing the protests to starting campaigns to elect progressive candidates.

Those initial protests were mostly about justice and accountability. Both state and federal prosecutors would later decline to charge the police officer, Darren Wilson, who resigned after the shooting.

But Mr. Brown’s death and the circumstances would come to carry even more import — a symbol of indifference, and failed relations between law enforcement personnel and Black communities.

At the time, about 70 percent of Ferguson’s 21,000 residents were Black, yet the mayor, five of the six City Council members and the police chief were white. Voter turnout was also depressed, with elections drawing about 12 percent of registered voters. There was something else at work in Ferguson that deepened the divides. A 2015 Justice Department investigation reported that the city routinely violated the civil rights of African Americans and used its police force and courts to generate revenue by writing tickets and assessing fines and fees, disproportionately to Black people, to feed the city budget.

Ms. Reed eventually went to work as an organizer for the Organization For Black Struggle in St. Louis. Her first two campaigns were the Ferguson municipal elections and the creation the St. Louis Civilian Oversight Board.

“We could shout the policies at the people we didn’t vote for and who have no concern about us, or we could change the demographic one seat at a time,” she said.

In 2016, Ms. Reed co-founded Action St. Louis, a racial justice organization dedicated to building Black power in the St. Louis region, and Action STL Power Project, its political and advocacy arm. Among its first campaign wins was its push for the closure of a medium-security jail in St. Louis known for its inhumane treatment of prisoners. The group also took on the 2017 St. Louis mayoral race, backing a progressive candidate, Tishaura Jones. Ms. Jones was narrowly defeated. Four years later, Ms. Reed’s team expanded its campaign, knocking on about 60,000 doors, helping usher in a Jones victory.

In the decade since Mr. Brown’s death, Ms. Reed has been focused on organizing the St. Louis region to create better outcomes for Black people.

“Ferguson creates this moment where everybody comes outside of their house and has to think about a different scale of us,” Ms. Reed said. “‘Us’ is not just me and my kids — ‘us’ is Black people in St. Louis. And when we start thinking about a different scale of us, what becomes possible is power. ”

Kirsten Noyes contributed research.
