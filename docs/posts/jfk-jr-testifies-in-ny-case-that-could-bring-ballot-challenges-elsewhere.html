JFK Jr. Testifies in NY Case That Could Bring Ballot Challenges Elsewhere

In the wake of the revelation that he once dumped a bear in Central Park, Kennedy appeared in court over a challenge to the address on his presidential nominating petitions.

Jesse McKinley

Jesse McKinley, reporting from Albany, has covered trials involving two candidates for president this year, including Donald J. Trump’s prosecution.

Aug. 6, 2024Updated 4:49 p.m. ET

Robert F. Kennedy Jr. took the witness stand in Albany on Tuesday in his fight to keep his place on the New York ballot, a case that opponents believe could lay the groundwork for challenges of his candidacy in other states.

Mr. Kennedy, whose independent presidential campaign has found a small but potentially critical slice of supporters, has been challenged in a lawsuit in New York brought by voters who say that he used a false address on nominating petitions.

They say that address — in Katonah, N.Y. — is not his residence, but that of a friend, and thus the signatures on Mr. Kennedy’s petitions are invalid. A decision to knock the candidate off the ballot in New York would be likely to lead to lawsuits in other states where his campaign used that address information to gather signatures.

With a confession of a bear-dumping prank a decade ago still drawing criticism from public officials and late-night mockery, Mr. Kennedy has taken two days off the campaign trail to contest the suit in State Supreme Court. He swore under oath on Tuesday that he lived in Katonah, a Westchester County town about an hour north of midtown Manhattan.

He said that he didn’t feel that those that had signed his petitions had been misled.

“I think they were signing up to support me,” he said, adding, “I think most of these people did not care” about the address.

On Sunday, Mr. Kennedy made a jarring admission that he had gathered up a dead cub he found alongside an upstate highway in 2014 and then dumped the carcass in Central Park.

Mr. Kennedy, an environmental lawyer, made the confession in a video he posted on social media Sunday, a day before The New Yorker published a critical profile with a same anecdote.

In the video, Mr. Kennedy recounted the story to comedian Roseanne Barr, who occasionally went wide-eyed at the details: That Mr. Kennedy saw the bear hit and collected it in his van. That he wanted to skin it and keep the meat. That he went to a dinner at Peter Luger’s steakhouse in Brooklyn with the carcass apparently still cooling in his vehicle.

Later, he deposited the dead animal in Central Park and placed a bike next to the carcass at a time when a spate of bicycle accidents was plaguing the city.

“Everybody thought, ‘That’s a great idea!’” Mr. Kennedy said in the video — a judgment that now appears to have been flawed.

In a fiery early morning post on X.com on Tuesday, Mr. Kennedy addressed “the mainstream media,” and indirectly, the firestorm over the dead bear.

“Every time you write “roadkill,” I will mention dirty C.I.A. regime-change wars and our $35 trillion debt,” Mr. Kennedy wrote. He then reeled off other controversies and curious headlines he’s generated, including his vaccine skepticism, a dead worm once found in his brain and some strange photos involving a barbecued animal.

“The more you smear me,” Mr. Kennedy concluded, “the more I’ll keep speaking.”

But the story of the dead bear has done little to counter Kennedy opponents’ argument that his campaign is nothing but a fringe effort by a former Democrat from a famed family. The challenge to his candidacy in New York is being backed by Clear Choice, a Democrat-aligned political action committee.

The hearing in Albany was focused on whether Mr. Kennedy’s claim of residency was valid, right down to testimony about whether he kept books, clothing and keepsakes in the Katonah house. That home is owned by a friend of his, Barbara Moss, who testified on Tuesday that Mr. Kennedy had recently started paying $500 a month to rent a bedroom, though they had no written agreement. She said he also received mail at the house.

But Ms. Moss said that she had started to receive those rent payments only in late May, receiving the first check — $6,000, or one year’s rent — a day after The New York Post wrote a story questioning his residence.

On the stand, Mr. Kennedy was grilled by Keith M. Corbett, a lawyer for the voters, about his home in California, which he shares with his wife, Cheryl Hines, the actress, and a menagerie of still-living animals, including — at one point — an emu.

Mr. Corbett asked about his move to California from New York, after he married Ms. Hines in 2014.

“Did the emu move from New York to Malibu?” the lawyer asked. Yes, Mr. Kennedy said. The emu, named Toby, was later killed by a mountain lion.

Mr. Kennedy’s running mate, Nicole Shanahan, also lives in California, and under the 12th Amendment, presidential and vice-presidential candidates who come from the same state are ineligible to receive its electoral votes. And California is the nation’s richest electoral prize, with 54 votes.

On Tuesday, about a dozen of Mr. Kennedy’s supporters were in the courtroom, some in campaign T-shirts, as the candidate struck a relaxed pose on the witness stand.

Even before he testified, Mr. Kennedy caused a stir. At one point on Tuesday morning, he abruptly left the courtroom, leaving both the judge, Christina L. Ryba, and his lawyer, William F. Savino, perplexed.

The judge stopped Ms. Moss’s testimony and asked where Mr. Kennedy had gone.

Mr. Savino shrugged.

“It’s hard to control a presidential candidate,” Mr. Savino said.
