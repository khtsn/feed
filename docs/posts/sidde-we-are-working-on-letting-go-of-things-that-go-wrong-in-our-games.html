sidde: "We are working on letting go of things that go wrong in our games"

FURIA are one win away from making the playoffs at IEM Cologne Major after beating MOUZ in the 1-0 pool, and are benefiting from the lack of pressure that came with being one of the favorites, as they were in Budapest.

But that is a double-edged sword. The Brazilian team's status as a dark horse is because they haven't challenged for trophies the way they did at the end of 2025, and though Sid "⁠sidde⁠" Macedo admitted to HLTV that he expected his team's level to dip, trying to rediscover that form and overcome the frustrations from not being contenders has been difficult.

"Frustration comes a lot, at least for us, with the pressure," he said after FURIA's win over MOUZ. "When we are pressured, we start getting frustrated because we feel like we should be winning games.

"But in the end, we have teams having a better year than us, so if you take, for example, Vitality, NAVI, Spirit, and some other teams, like PARIVISION had a great moment this year, so we need to realize and understand where we are at right now."

The 29-year-old coach also touched on Yuri "⁠yuurih⁠" Santos's drop in form, lessons they learned from losing to Falcons in Astana, and their expectations against some of the favorites they will likely face in the 2-0 qualifying round.

Listen to the interview here.

A win over MOUZ. Took a little while to get there in the end, especially after Inferno, so talk me through that series and how it went for you.

We started very hot in the series, we had a very good game plan against their Nuke. We expected the map pick from them, and we prepared good against it. The clutches on T side helped us because, you know, we got 8-4 on CT side, and it's not... I would say it's a fair score, it's not really guaranteed that you're going to finish the game. But our players played very good in clutches, and that kinda defined the map.

Inferno, they played amazing, and we had some things on T side that didn't go according to plan. We didn't do the things the way that we had planned, but then on Overpass, starting CT side is such a huge advantage, and molodoy had a crazy game. When your AWP is playing like that on Overpass, it gets a lot easier.

That's how we lost to the Falcons, by the way, we didn't play serious enough when it mattered

Talk to me about your preparation coming in to the Major. Astana obviously didn't end the way you wanted, but that was a crazy game against Falcons as well, so what was your conversation like coming out of that and after that match?

Our impressions after Astana, they were like, 'Okay, we still know how to play, we just need to learn how to close games again.' Actually, before Astana, we had a rough moment in terms of how we were playing. We were not playing to our qualities and the level that we can play, but during Astana I felt like we were playing pretty okay.

So the conversation after that was just like, 'Hey guys, it's okay, we just have some small tweaks to do. Our game is back where we know it can be, but we need to play more serious in moments that we need to close the game.' That's how we lost to Falcons, by the way, we didn't play serious enough when it mattered. So I knew when watching Astana, but also the scrims afterward with the way we were practicing, that we still know how to play. It was just a matter of us learning how to close games, and I think we are displaying a good level here.

That search for your form has been happening for this whole half of the year, right? It's not close to where you were at the end of last year. What do you think has been missing? You touched on it a bit, but why has it been so up and down, and what is missing to get over the line?

Honestly, after winning four tournaments last year, I realized there is only one team that can keep the same level for so long, which is Vitality. It's so hard to keep playing to the same level because when you win, a lot of things need to go your way, and this is what happened to us last year. We were winning because we were lucky sometimes, players had crazy individual form, so basically everyone peaked at the right time, at the same time, together.

After that, people started studying our game a lot more, you kinda set a lot of trends, so when people are studying your trends, they know how to play against it. So I feel like, naturally, every team is going to have a drop down in their level — other than Vitality, it's crazy what they do.

I imagined something like this would happen, in terms of our level dropping a bit, but the problem was that not only did that happen, we also started having some problems that we were not having last year. I wouldn't say we started having disagreements on how to play, but you kinda get into your own head in terms of, do we keep doing what we were doing, how much of the new things do we need to bring?

So we started kind of trying to figure out a lot at the same time, and this is when you start doubting yourself a little bit. But I would say our individuals, it's hard to replicate what they did last year, and we are trying to get to that same individual level right now.

Frustration got the better of FURIA in Astana

YEKINDAR kind of hinted towards that when he talked to me in Astana, that molodoy was trying some different things in his game, and there has been a lot of trying to rediscover your form. I also noticed in Astana that it did seem like there was a lot of frustration at times, so how have you been managing that?

Frustration comes a lot, at least for us, with the pressure. When we are pressured, we start getting frustrated because we feel like we should be winning games. But in the end, we have teams having a better year than us, so if you take, for example, Vitality, NAVI, Spirit, and some other teams, like PARIVISION had a great moment this year, so we need to realize and understand where we are at right now.

We need to grind our way back into the top, and once we understand that and we can focus on ourselves and how to play the game, I think the results will naturally come. We're trying to get rid of those frustrations, like you said that happened in Astana a little bit, but that's it. We just need to play our game and try to find that form from last year.

When we are pressured, we start getting frustrated because we feel like we should be winning games

So would you say the frustrations come from the expectations being kind of where you were last year, and that's not actually the reality now?

Yeah. Not only comparing to last year, but actually during games. You feel like you have to be winning some situations, you have to be winning some halves without giving them too many rounds, but sometimes it happens, you know? And the reality is, when you lose a frustrating round, you need to play the next round the same way as if you lost a normal round, so to say.

We are working on that, on letting go of things that go wrong in our games, and that is exactly what happened here, for example. After Inferno, we could have been a lot more frustrated than we were, but we understood that Inferno is in the past, we can't change Inferno. That's what I told the team by the way, that we need to play a BO1 on Overpass right now, and we're starting CT. That's where I want your minds to be because I don't want us to think about Inferno anymore. The more we learn to let go of those things, not only between maps, but also in between rounds during the game, I think we're going to improve a lot.

I also wanted to ask about yuurih's form. He changed a lot of roles last year and he was doing decently, but this year has seen a pretty big drop in his level and a lot of attention has come on that. What do you think is going wrong for him, and what does he need to focus on to bring himself back?

His drop in performance is related a lot to our drop in T side performance in general. We had very bad T sides at the start of the year, and this is where yuurih was playing the best last year. His CT side roles, we know that he plays very difficult positions, and he can put up some good performances even when playing difficult positions. But the T side is where he shines more, and playing some rough T sides since the beginning of the year, it was kinda obvious that his form was going to drop.

But I'm happy because he's keeping his confidence up, he's not dropping the ball in terms of believing [in himself]. He's still playing the same game, and it's just a matter of fitting more into our structure in terms of the T side because our T side was a bit frustrating. For him, I think the faster we get into our level of connection that we can play to sometimes, like sometimes we hit a very good level of connection, for him it's easier to play. So it's those two things: T sides and team connection.

For me as a coach, preparing the game mentally, it's easier to be in this position because we also expect a bit less from ourselves

Lastly, you're 2-0 now. Coming into this Major, you're not in the same position you were at the last one in Budapest. A lot of people had you as one of the favorites for that, and you're a little bit lower down the rung here. What do you think about your position now that you're 2-0 and heading into these qualifying matches with potentially the likes of Spirit, Falcons, and Vitality up ahead?

Yeah, like you said, people have more reasons to put others as favorites, and I think it's honestly more comfortable to be in this position where people see us as a dark horse and they're not counting on us to just come here and beat everyone. For me as a coach, preparing the game mentally, it's easier to be in this position because we also expect a bit less from ourselves.

This is benefiting us, and that's why we are playing so light-headed; we don't have a lot of concerns. We just need to come here and play, and whatever result we get, we get. Especially now, after the 2-0, we're going to play against the favorite teams, so we just need to understand — like I said in the other answer — where we are as a team, and what we need to do to keep playing good. And the results, they might come, or they might not come, we never know.
