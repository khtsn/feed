How the Trump Prosecutions Could End Up Weakening Prosecutors

News Analysis

The effort to hold Donald Trump to account has already yielded a Supreme Court decision giving former presidents broad immunity. Now another case could make prosecuting political figures more complicated.

The practical effect of Judge Aileen M. Cannon’s bombshell decision this week tossing out former President Donald J. Trump’s classified documents case was immediately clear.

Barring a quick reversal by an appeals court, it eliminated what had long been thought of as the most straightforward of the four criminal cases Mr. Trump has faced. And it did so just as Mr. Trump was about to accept the Republican Party’s nomination for president.

But the ruling could also have a broader impact down the road, especially if the Supreme Court eventually gets to consider the issue at its center: whether the special counsel Jack Smith, who has filed two indictments against Mr. Trump, was appointed to his job unconstitutionally.

Should the court take up that question and uphold Judge Cannon’s findings, it would be the second time the justices have made a decision that could make prosecuting powerful political figures like Mr. Trump more complicated.

Just two weeks ago, the court’s conservative majority issued a landmark ruling in Mr. Trump’s other federal case — the one in which he stands accused of plotting to overturn the 2020 election. In that ruling, the justices granted Mr. Trump (and all other future former presidents) broad immunity from prosecution for many, if not most, of their official acts.

If Judge Cannon’s reasoning about special counsels prevails, Mr. Smith’s attempts to hold Mr. Trump accountable in both criminal cases might paradoxically end up having a very different effect by leaving most official acts of a president in office beyond the law and making the process of appointing independent prosecutors in politically sensitive cases at a minimum more cumbersome.

Mr. Trump’s courtroom victories so far on those issues could have consequences if he regains the White House. He and his allies have repeatedly promised to use the Justice Department to go after various enemies, and he would bring to office an expansive view of presidential powers and an aggressive plan to use them.

While it may take a while for the Supreme Court to be in the position to entertain the question of special counsel appointments — and any potential appeal would most likely be wiped away if Mr. Trump is elected in November — the possibility of the justices taking up the issue is hardly hypothetical.

Justice Clarence Thomas has already made it clear that he believes Mr. Smith was unlawfully appointed to the job of special counsel. In an unusual concurrence to the court’s immunity decision, Justice Thomas all but invited Judge Cannon to strike down Mr. Smith’s appointment, which she ultimately did.

Leaders of the conservative legal movement have been talking for years about the purported dangers of special counsels — whether it was Mr. Smith or Robert S. Mueller III, who oversaw the investigation into connections between Russia and Mr. Trump’s 2016 campaign.

The argument has always been that special counsels should be appointed by the president and confirmed by the Senate, or in an alternate route should be governed by specific federal laws, not by internal Justice Department regulations.

That is precisely the reasoning Judge Cannon used in her ruling on Monday.

She did not call for the elimination of special counsels. Rather, she sought an assurance either that their appointment be authorized by congressional legislation — an outcome that could be difficult to achieve in a deeply divided government — or that they be treated like U.S. attorneys and be nominated by the president and confirmed by the Senate, a time-consuming process subject to partisan influence.

In a kind of leitmotif that ran through her decision, Judge Cannon also suggested that under the department’s current rules, special counsels like Mr. Smith had too much freedom to pursue criminal cases — an independent streak that the courts or Congress needed to rein in.

“The special counsel regulations,” she wrote, “impose almost no supervision or direction over the special counsel and give him broad power to render final decisions on behalf of the United States.”

That skeptical view of prosecutorial authority was an echo of the Supreme Court’s ruling on immunity, which was based on the majority’s assertion that the threat of partisan prosecutors firing off political indictments was far more dangerous than the specter of former presidents being placed beyond the reach of the law.

Writing for the majority, Chief Justice John G. Roberts Jr. said that without immunity, there would be an endless tit-for-tat of politically driven cases, “with each successive president free to prosecute his predecessors.”

For now, Mr. Smith’s team has promised to appeal Judge Cannon’s ruling. Its first stop will be the court that sits above her, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the 11th Circuit in Atlanta.

At the same time, defense lawyers in other cases involving special counsels have already sought to use Judge Cannon’s ruling to their advantage. Just hours after the decision was handed down, lawyers for Alexander Smirnov, a former F.B.I. informant charged with fabricating claims that President Biden and his son sought bribes from a Ukrainian company, filed a motion seeking to disqualify the special counsel who is handling the case, David C. Weiss.

For decades, lawmakers and officials in the Justice Department have gone back and forth over the question of how to appoint and govern independent prosecutors working on political cases.

In the wake of Watergate, for instance, Congress passed the Independent Counsel Act, which gave special prosecutors broad autonomy to conduct criminal inquiries. But in 1999, after another political scandal consumed the Clinton White House, the law was allowed to expire and was replaced by a series of internal Justice Department regulations.

Moving control of special counsels back into the department was an effort to strike a balance between independence and accountability. There was a consensus in Washington at that point that special prosecutors needed to be independent enough to handle political inquiries free from outside pressure, but not so independent that they would run amok and abuse their power.

In her ruling this week, Judge Cannon cast her lot with those who feared that special counsels like Mr. Smith had far too free a hand in conducting investigations and needed to be more accountable to the attorney general.

She interpreted the Justice Department’s regulations governing special counsels with a skeptical eye, saying that the attorney general had no “day-to-day supervision” over them and could not remove them from their offices “at will.”

She also worried in particular about what she described as Mr. Smith’s broad mandate, pointing out that he had “established a very significant operation in terms of staffing and resources” and had spent nearly $13 million as of September.

Moreover, she seemed concerned that there was nothing keeping him from pursuing his cases against Mr. Trump — as, indeed, he has said he will until someone steps in and stops him. As Judge Cannon put it, she had seen no evidence so far that “provides a concrete sunset provision for the cessation of his work.”

All of this, she suggested in her ruling, was why she had taken the initiative in striking his appointment.

“In the end,” she wrote, “it seems the executive’s growing comfort in appointing ‘regulatory’ special counsels in the more recent era has followed an ad hoc pattern with little judicial scrutiny.”
