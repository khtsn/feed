    From Sunrise to Sunset, a Perfect Playlist for the Beach

Why our food critic tossed the fake glasses and quit his dream job.

Published July 19, 2024Updated July 20, 2024, 11:35 a.m. ET

Pete Wells announced on Tuesday that he was stepping down as The Times’s restaurant critic. Wells became the restaurant critic in 2012, and his insights into the dining experience, alongside his biting wit, have made him a favorite of our readers. But recent health issues have caused him to reconsider his relationship with dining out. Wells joins Melissa Kirsch, the deputy editor of Culture and Lifestyle for The Times, to discuss why he’s leaving his post, the highs and lows of his life as a critic, and what he hopes for the future.

Below are a few excerpts from their conversation, lightly edited for clarity.

Why he felt moved to disclose so much.

Melissa Kirsch: When you wrote about the very real health implications of being a restaurant critic, what I was struck by was how vulnerable you were. When I think about a critic, I think about how you have to make these pronouncements on food and on restaurants. But this piece in The Times was so interesting because you were talking very explicitly about things that people are shy about, like going to the doctor and hearing that your numbers are off, or talking about your weight. I don’t think of a critic as necessarily offering that much intimate information.

Pete Wells: Well, certainly. I mean, it’s normally not relevant. But I do think any kind of culture writing starts in your body and happens in your body, right? So if I was a music critic and I lost my hearing, I think it would be appropriate to write about that — if certain frequencies were going out and changed how I heard things or made certain things intensely pleasurable. I think you’d want to share that with readers. That would be interesting. We can pretend that we’re existing in some very rarefied realm of aesthetics and sensation. But the art is happening in your body. It’s happening to your body.

Melissa Kirsch: That’s right. And so with your body as an important element to being a food critic, did you feel like you were losing touch with those sensations of, like, satiety or what tasted good or what felt good?

Pete Wells: Once I had resolved to change the way I eat, I did worry about not really being able to give myself to the meal, you know? You want to be able to let it happen to you. Which is hard to do if you’re, you know, scared of bread. And I also didn’t want to be in a position of eating something and then being beset by the feeling that I shouldn’t have eaten it. Which introduces an element that has nothing to do with how the food is. There are subjective responses that you want in your criticism. And then there are the ones that you don’t want. I’m not going to write restaurant reviews that say, “I’m not really a dessert person, but this one struck me.” That’s just not the way to do it.

The peculiarities of a critic’s social life.

Melissa Kirsch: I can imagine that it would be very hard to let go of a position like this one. I would imagine that over the years you’ve heard so many expressions of envy from other people that you get to do this thing.

Pete Wells: It is enviable in many, many ways. I have a much better social life than I would if I didn’t have this job. But then the job overlaps with the social life in strange ways. Are they really my friends, or do they just like free food? Are they laughing at my jokes because I’m funny? It’s like being the only kid in high school who has a car, right?

Melissa Kirsch: When I think about being a restaurant critic, I think it would be just heaven to be able to take a group of people out to dinner every night and order everything on the menu. Then I think how stressful that would be for me as just a social being, to be the organizer of a dinner party every night.

Pete Wells: Yeah, right. There’s a kind of a sheepdog role too, where you have to look at the menu, decide what you’re going to have, or let me tell you what you’re going to have. When the waiter comes, remember your role. And remember what we agreed you’re going to eat. You’re not here to suddenly decide at the last minute you just want the salad, right? That’s not why I brought you.

Melissa Kirsch: Yes. You can’t authentically order everything like what you would order if you weren’t here with a restaurant critic.

Pete Wells: But it’s funny, because I do want people to look at the menu and kind of choose what they want, and then I reserve the right to edit them. So, if three people want the lamb, I’m going to say, “No, only one of you is going to get the lamb,” or, “I’ve had the lamb every single time I’ve been to this restaurant, and it would be great to try something else.”

Melissa Kirsch: Are you an extrovert?

Pete Wells: No, I don’t think so. But I’ve had to override a lot of my natural inclinations just to be in journalism. Just to be able to ask people questions and try to make them comfortable. Sometimes I do get really quiet because I’m just thinking about the food and everyone’s talking around me.

On anonymity — and when anonymity fails.

Melissa Kirsch: So now that you’re coming to the end of your tenure as restaurant critic, I’m wondering if there are things that you can share about the experience of going out incognito that you haven’t been able to talk about before?

Pete Wells: Oh, when I first started, I bought a novel to the restaurant with me: “A Perfect Spy” by John le Carré. And I just thought I’d get some pointers on what he calls tradecraft. The art of blending in. The art of not being noticed. So I was carrying this around, and I carried it one night into a restaurant, and then I left it behind. A week or two later I got an envelope in the mail, and I opened it up and it was the book:

Melissa Kirsch: A real spy move to send it to you.

Pete Wells: I was like, oh, this is so weird. And then I opened it up, and I was using my pay stub from The Times as my bookmark. So it had not just my name and my address, but how much I make, what my deductions are …

Melissa Kirsch: So your pay stub was inside the le Carré book?

Pete Wells: Right.

Melissa Kirsch: This was a real flub if you were trying to stay incognito. To leave your spy book.

Pete Wells: It never really got any better. I mean, I didn’t make that mistake, but I made every other mistake. I forget the name that I reserved under or give the wrong name or tell different guests different names. I’ve used my own credit card when I didn’t mean to. It’s just a whole litany of mistakes and trying to just not be noticed. Drawing more attention to myself.

Regrets? He’s had a few.

Melissa Kirsch: Are there instances where you feel like you got it wrong? Or a review that you wish you hadn’t written?

Pete Wells: Well, one thing I did in the beginning that I learned from, and I would undo if I could, was I would really emphasize the negative no more than necessary. I think the readers are smart and they can get it. If you just flick at what was wrong and without hammering it. That took a while to figure out how heavy you have to draw in the shadows and how light the bright spots need to be.

Melissa Kirsch: So as you got further into your career as a critic, you found more subtlety.

Pete Wells: Yeah, it was effective.

Melissa Kirsch: Just as effective.

Pete Wells: It’s much easier to be funny about something that’s bad than something that’s good. It’s very hard actually to be funny and praise something at the same time. It’s just weirdly hard. It’s very easy to be funny while you’re criticizing something. And I think as time went on, I thought, well, maybe there’s other ways to get a laugh that aren’t just dwelling on how terrible a dish was and not to make a dish, like, the laughingstock.

A word of caution to his successors.

Melissa Kirsch: You make a pretty good case for the role of food critic being an inherently unhealthy job. What advice would you have for somebody who read your piece and read about the toll of the job over a dozen years?

Pete Wells: Think about it before it’s too late. That’s something I didn’t do — you know, think about it. Also, just be aware that if you’re doing it for the long haul, conditions will change. For years, I used to walk to my kid’s school in the afternoon and pick him up, and then he changed and went to another school and started taking the subway home on his own. I wasn’t picking him up, and I suddenly wasn’t getting all this great exercise that I’d been getting pretty regularly every weekday. I didn’t take that into account right away. You do need to be aware that you’re doing things to your body that most of the people around you are not doing. Just a little thing, like a change in routine, can make a big difference in your fitness. I would also say to be really conscious of the alcohol because you need to at least explore the wine service. But you can do that by letting somebody else at your table order and see how the sommelier talks to them. You don’t necessarily need to be drinking it all.

Melissa Kirsch: What are you looking forward to now that you’re no longer going to have to give your body to your job the same way?

Pete Wells: It’ll be nice to be in a restaurant and not have to go through this little charade of them pretending they don’t know who I am, and me pretending that I believe them. It would just be nice to ask, “Hey, how are you tonight?” There are some chefs who I think are interesting, and I’d like to have a normal conversation with them one day. There is a sort of cloistered element to the job where you have to remove yourself from society a little bit. At least I did. It’ll be nice to step down among humanity again.

Pete Wells will continue to write for the Food section of The New York Times.
