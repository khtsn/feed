Leviatán bid farewell to Shyy

Hope Boykin radiated generosity and joy as a performer. As a choreographer and director, she is showing the struggle the audience couldn’t see.

Near the beginning of the new dance-theater work “States of Hope,” the dancer Jessica Amber Pinkett, smiling, begins a series of gracious, majestic bows — the kind the work’s creator, Hope Boykin, took many times over the course of her 20-year career with Alvin Ailey American Dance Theater.

Then, Pinkett pauses and looks up. Still smiling, she deadpans: “When will it end?”

As a dancer, Boykin, who retired from Ailey in 2020, seemed to draw from bottomless wells of generosity and joy. But as a choreographer and writer, she has explored the doubts and worries that hummed underneath that onstage persona.

“When you watched Hope dance,” said Matthew Rushing, Ailey’s associate artistic director, “you felt like you were seeing her whole heart. What you didn’t know was how much struggle was behind the beauty she was making onstage. And now she’s bringing us into that struggle.”

“States of Hope,” which Boykin’s company, HopeBoykinDance, premieres Oct. 17 at the Joyce Theater, is perhaps her most vulnerable, and ambitious, work yet. It features a poetic script that runs more than 70 pages, with seven dancer-actors personifying different facets of her personality: the Determined, the Conformist, the Cynical, the Convinced, the Angry, the Daughter of Job and the Worried. (Boykin acts as a kind of narrator offstage.) The characters’ arguments about love and self-love — philosophical, wryly funny, often heated — evoke both Plato’s “Symposium” and the Pixar movie “Inside Out.”

Since the end of her performing career, Boykin, 51, has seemed increasingly confident in her voice as a choreographer, writer and director. But while she was developing “States of Hope,” her actual voice almost disappeared. Last year, she was diagnosed with an autoimmune disease that affects her lungs, making it difficult for her to speak.

Though her symptoms have improved, she found in the experience a metaphor for “States of Hope.” “With an autoimmune problem, your body is fighting against itself,” Boykin said. “And I realized that’s exactly what these seven characters are doing. They are me, and they are fighting against each other.”

On a recent Zoom call, Boykin talked about how she communicates through both steps and words. Here are edited excerpts from the conversation.

You started choreographing while you were still dancing. Did those two forms of creativity feed each other?

I think I became a better choreographer by working, as a dancer, with lots of choreographers. It definitely taught me how to speak to people, that it’s important to be really clear with my words. A choreographer would say something like, “Well, if you could sort of do this” — there was so much vagueness, which led to insecurity. When I’m leading the room now, I try to say, “I want you to” — to be very specific and direct.

From the outside, you appear certain of your vision as a choreographer and director — your “I want you to” seems very clear. Is it?

Absolutely not. But I believe that it’s OK to fake it a little bit. And by “fake it” I mean, maybe I just need to say a thing, trusting that it’s right, and then later come around to figuring out why it’s right. Or, if I really don’t know, one of my lines is, “I know no thing.” That’s Book of Hope, Verse 25: “I know no thing.” [laughing] I’m just going to let my heart say some things, and we’ll see if they turn into facts.

When did you start writing?

Please laugh at this: The first time I realized I had words in my head that I needed to get out, I was in an airport bathroom. This was maybe in my first year at Ailey. I had to rummage in my purse for a pen, right there in the stall, and write everything down. I was afraid it’d go away. Then when I read it, it had a rhythm to it. And so I kept writing.

How do you think about the relationship between dance and language?

I think movement naturally intertwines with words. What I’ve realized is that the cadence of my choreography matches the way that I speak.

When a preacher points at you from the pulpit, when they clap their hands to emphasize a point, it’s because they are living the words they’re saying. Why can’t an arabesque serve the same purpose? Whatever the sound is, music or speech, I like to see it represented in the body of the dancer. I want to bring it right into your lap.

How did you develop the movement for “States of Hope”?

It took intuition and investigation. A lot of times, if I can’t get to the point, I will make “filler” movement — similar to the idea of saying the thing and then figuring out why it’s the right thing: “I haven’t found the reason you’re doing this step yet, but I know this is you.” And then the dancers’ collaboration and research and commitment to the work help it unfold.

The piece addresses your struggles with the dance world’s punishing body standards. How did they affect you?

I always felt that there was something wrong with this body. I just looked different. I was shorter, my bum was curvier. At Ailey, there were some ballets that I was told I would not do. So in my head, I was never right. I started to feel that artistically, too — I thought I was just keeping up. And then during the pandemic, I started watching old videos of myself, and I was like, wait, I was good. I didn’t know I was good!

Most of your recent works have highlighted your speaking voice. Has that become central to your storytelling, or will there be a day when you’re not narrating?

Oh, the day when I will not be narrating is close. The day is nigh! And it starts with “States of Hope” because all of the dancer-actor-artists in the cast are helping articulate the story for me, even though I come in here and there. I think soon, I will enjoy just sitting and watching someone else figure out how to manage my words.

What role does dance play in your life now?

A couple of weeks ago, I found myself thinking, “What are you going to write next?” Not, “What are you going to choreograph?” but, “What are you going to write?” I mean, wow.

I would love to dance more. Physically, though, I am hurting. I still move in the studio with my dancers, and I’m happy that I can be in that space with them and show them what I’m asking for. But one of my favorite dances is moving my hands back and forth, to clap for all the other dancers who are doing what they love.
