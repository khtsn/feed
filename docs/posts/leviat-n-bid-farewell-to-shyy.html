Oct. 12, 2023, 5:47 p.m. ET

Israel moved troops into position for a multipronged invasion of the Gaza Strip in an effort to dismantle Hamas, the militant group who massacred more than 1,200 Israelis in an attack over the weekend.

No decision has been made yet about when or even whether to launch a ground war, officials insisted, but Israel has called up 360,000 reservists, sending many near the Gaza border.

That a major operation is coming is hardly in doubt, but there are tactical arguments over how one should start. And hanging over everything is the question of how long Israeli forces would stay. There is little appetite to reoccupy the Gaza Strip, Itamar Yaar, a former deputy head of Israel’s National Security Council, told The New York Times.

Six days of Israeli airstrikes targeting Hamas have devastated parts of Gaza. The U.N. issued an official warning of “disaster” as hospitals ran short on fuel for generators and corridors were crammed with wounded people. More than 300,000 Palestinians in Gaza have lost their homes, and the Health Ministry said that 1,417 had been killed. The only power plant in the territory shut down yesterday.

Egypt said it would facilitate a humanitarian corridor to move urgently needed aid into Gaza, but officials there were adamantly opposed to allowing Gazans to enter the country, their only viable exit.

U.S. Secretary of State Antony Blinken met with leaders in Israel today, condemning Hamas’s “reign of terror” and promising American support while calling for “every possible precaution to prevent harming civilians.”

War scenes: The images streaming onto our phones from Israel and Gaza, many coming from those fighting or caught up in the war, don’t just document the story — they shape it.

House Republicans struggle to choose a leader

Representative Steve Scalise of Louisiana, the No. 2 Republican, may have narrowly won his party’s nomination for House speaker. But for the second consecutive day, Scalise found himself unable to scrounge together enough support to be elected on the House floor.

In fact, resistance to Scalise’s speakership appears to have grown. His detractors include lawmakers still loyal to former Speaker Kevin McCarthy and ultraconservatives who favor Representative Jim Jordan of Ohio, a founder of the far-right Freedom Caucus.

In the meantime, legislative business remains halted. Here’s what can’t get done, including an aid package for Israel for its war against Hamas.

Inflation holds steady

Consumer prices rose 3.7 percent in the year through September. That matched the August reading, and was slightly higher than economists had predicted, suggesting that the Federal Reserve’s stated goal of bringing inflation back to 2 percent remains a long and bumpy road.

One notable aspect of today’s report: Housing continues to put pressure on consumer prices. Shelter prices accounted for more than 70 percent of the rise in core inflation last month.

For retirees, Social Security benefits will rise by 3.2 percent next year.

Senator Robert Menendez faces new charges

Robert Menendez, the Democratic senator from New Jersey who was charged last month with taking bribes in exchange for political favors, was indicted again today. Federal prosecutors accused him of conspiring to act as a foreign agent of Egypt even as he served as the chairman of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee. His wife, Nadine Menendez, was also charged, and prosecutors asked a judge to seize their residence in Englewood Cliffs, N.J.

One of TV’s longest-lived characters is back

Almost 40 years after he was first introduced to viewers at the famous bar on “Cheers,” Frasier Crane is back on television. Kelsey Grammer reprises the role in a Paramount+ series, familiarly titled “Frasier,” premiering today.

Several original cast members make guest appearances, and the show even returns with the formidable sitcom director James Burrows, but our critic James Poniewozik calls it a mediocre, anticlimactic reboot. “While this ‘Frasier’ has the occasional sentimental moment, it’s too busy re-creating the past to engage with the past,” he writes.

The art world is finally celebrating Judy Chicago

The pioneering feminist artist Judy Chicago was pushed to the industry’s sidelines for decades. But her message — that art by women can shake everything, all of us, to the core — has been gaining attention, and at 84, she is busier than ever.

The New Museum is exhibiting her first major New York survey. Spanning four floors, it includes a grounding show-within-a-show of more than 80 artists and thinkers, including Hilma af Klint, Georgia O’Keeffe and Frida Kahlo, giving their pieces and hers an imagined sisterhood.

From bland to exclusive

For many years, whiskey connoisseurs have looked down on one of the distilling world’s most famous names: Jack Daniel’s. Its flagship bottle has been criticized as bland and unctuously sweet. But recently, the brand has quietly been remaking its image.

The distillery has begun rolling out a series of new, limited releases with the kind of characteristics that make whiskey hounds salivate — single-barrel bottling, high alcohol levels, heavily charred barrels. And the brand has converted a number of longtime skeptics. One collector we spoke to didn’t just add his first bottle of Jack Daniel’s, he added dozens.
