K27 coach: "For the past three weeks, the talks about Major have been banned within the team"

K27 have been in a tight race for one of the final European invites to the IEM Cologne Major after a strong run of results, winning MPKBK CIS LAN Season 3 and CCT Season 3 Europe Series 18 back-to-back and finishing third in ESL Challenger League Season 51.

But all that work still may not be enough as the team sits below the invite cut-off on our predicted list, with several teams around them also making gains and a loss to magic in the PGL Astana qualifier dealing a significant blow.

A bounce back in the online event's lower bracket and a title run at MPKBK LAN Season 4, where K27 have reached the playoffs, are musts to keep a hold of those hopes, but the team's coach, Gregory "⁠balblna⁠" Oleinick, says the team has been barred from talking about the topic for weeks.

"For the past three weeks, the talks about Major have been banned within the team," he told HLTV after K27 made the playoffs in Moscow. "About two weeks ago I told the guys: don't even think about it. And for about a week now we've genuinely convinced ourselves that we won't make the Major, and that seems to help. It helps us stop thinking about it, because the weight of that pressure is real."

Editor's note:

This interview was conducted in Russian and translated by HLTV staff.

Your team has been showing solid results for quite a while, and now you're in the race for the Major. What do you think is the key factor behind your success so far?

Probably, like with any team, the key factor is persistence and the work every member puts in, like in every success story. All of us together, six or seven people counting our CEO, who lives with us at the bootcamp, work around the clock. We've been living at the bootcamp for seven months now and are fully dedicated to the game.

In seven months, we climbed from the very bottom of the world rankings to a high position

With VRS being the main performance metric now, how important was the strategy around which tournaments to attend or skip to your success? Who gets to define it for your team?

It is definitely crucial. There's a defined strategy around which tournaments we should play and which to skip, but it's very hard to navigate right now. We tracked everything very carefully, planning two to three months ahead: which tournaments we'd enter, which we'd skip, where we needed to earn bounty points, and what LANs we needed.

We tracked all of this closely. Our entire schedule was built around it, I personally follow every tournament and calendar, and together with the CEO, we decide which ones we go to or pass on.

I think we had a pretty good run. In seven months, we climbed from the very bottom of the world rankings to a high position, partly thanks to this. That said, I'd say we made about two mistakes in deciding which tournaments to play. One of the most recent was Challenger Cup 3.

How difficult was it to figure all this out — did you turn to anyone for help? Did you know someone who had already worked it out?

In general, I've spent the last two years coaching and learning the system. So when I came to K27, I already knew how it all works. There were still some nuances around point splitting and the online bounty plan, but we figured those out along the way, through practice and participation.

Your team, like any team from this region, faces higher difficulty and cost when it comes to European tournaments. Series like DraculaN and RIC offer a lot of points. How much does that limit you, and how do you evaluate the regional tournament scene overall?

In terms of limitations, yes, it's hard to get into European tournaments, but we compensated by trying to compete in CIS events instead. We went to Georgia, we went to Kazakhstan, won a few tournaments there, and also competed in almost every tournament in Moscow. So we have an alternative. Of course, the field at European events is stronger, but the competition here isn't bad either. I think we're managing it so far.

The organization doesn't have a main sponsor yet, we're still looking for one. It's simply easier for us to participate in local tournaments

Was your absence from European tournaments due to visa or financial reasons? Or is it possible to accumulate the necessary points while staying within the region?

First and foremost, it's a matter of resources. The organization doesn't have a main sponsor yet, we're still looking for one. It's simply easier for us to participate in local tournaments. We did attend one European event, DreamHack in Sweden, where we had a poor run. But fortunately, nobody really noticed (laughs).

Do you see a future for this roster similar to teams like PARIVISION and FUT? They reached very high results, and what they had in common was a core of young, relatively unknown players who managed to rise to the top. Do you see similar potential in this roster?

Definitely, this roster has enormous potential. We haven't reached our ceiling yet, there's still room to grow. We may still be lacking experience in some areas, as some official matches have shown, because we made a massive leap, and some players took a big step forward, but others a smaller one. So we still need some time after this run to process what happened, draw conclusions, and move forward.

Who is the most exciting talent on your team in terms of market demand? Looking at your roster objectively, based on the offers and inquiries you receive or stat numbers, who do you think is most at risk of being poached?

Without meaning to slight anyone else, I'd highlight qw1nk1. He's a high-rated anchor player who understands the macro game very well despite his age. I think he could potentially move to a tier-one team. But the rest are all great too, they're young and talented. There's a shortage of good captains right now; we have a very strong one. The AWP role is also at a generational transition; the next generation is coming up. But I'd highlight qw1nk1.

What can you tell us about your captain? How comfortable are you working with him, and how much potential do you see?

I'm very comfortable working with Danya [X5G7V]. We've built a clear system for how we interact. Sometimes, I take more initiative; sometimes he does. Overall he has quite a lot of freedom, which he puts into practice and clearly feels comfortable with. Over the past few years he's transformed from just a support player into a good tier-two IGL. We still need to grow to step up to get to the tier-one level and prove it, but he's a solid tier-two captain.

We absolutely need to win the two remaining tournaments we have: CIS LAN and the PGL Astana qualifier. But we're trying to put the Major out of our minds

You've already made playoffs here at MPKBK CIS LAN #4. What are your expectations from the tournament?

Overall, talking about the Major run, we absolutely need to win the two remaining tournaments we have: CIS LAN and the PGL Astana qualifier running simultaneously. But we're trying to put the Major out of our minds, because it puts enormous pressure on us and we make more mistakes. So the goal here and now is to simply qualify for PGL Astana. What happens with the Major after that, fate will decide.

Am I right to presume that you're actively trying to push players away from those thoughts by actually cutting off conversations on the topic?

Yeah. For the past three weeks, the talks about Major have been banned within the team. About two weeks ago I told the guys: don't even think about it. And for about a week now we've genuinely convinced ourselves that we won't make the Major, and that seems to help. It helps us stop thinking about it, because the weight of that pressure is real. We barely might make it, and it presses down hard and clouds your thinking.
