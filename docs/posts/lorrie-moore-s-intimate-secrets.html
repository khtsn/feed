Lorrie Moore’s Intimate Secrets

“It’s like taking Milwaukee polka music and Italianizing it,’’ said Lorrie Moore. She was trying to explain to me what to expect from “La Traviata,” because I’d admitted I didn’t know that much about opera. “Do you find opera music beautiful when you hear it?”

All around us, elegantly dressed Berliners converged on the state opera house in the early evening light. “I think what I struggle with is finding more in it than the beauty,” I said. “Or learning to be satisfied with that.”

“The music has to get in you and do its thing,” she said.

We were at the opera house steps now, and she asked if I wanted to check my wool sport coat. I gestured at the Staatsoper’s grand columns. “I have to make an effort,” I said.

“Will you be too hot?” she asked, then grinned slyly. “Don’t I sound like a nagging wife? I haven’t been a wife for 23 years.”

“Still got it,” I said.

“Still got it.”

Lorrie Moore is good with men. “Very, very good with them,” Lauren Groff, a novelist and former student of Moore’s who is a three-time National Book Award finalist, told me. “One time, I looked across and she had the most handsome man in the room in the palm of her hand, and she raised her eyebrow to me, like, ‘Hubba hubba.’” In my time with her in Berlin, where she was researching family history, Moore filled every silence in our conversations with polite questions about my family, my writing, my experience with the opera. This habit seems like an example of her social graces — she is unfailingly well mannered — but it is also a clever trick that defuses the interviewer’s tactic of letting silences linger, to encourage the subject to fill them with revelations. It’s a tactic that works especially well on men, of course, already so eager to talk about themselves to nice women.

Occasionally Moore would make me laugh with some perfect observation, or we would banter like the characters in her stories, and I would feel, for a moment, the welcome sense that our minds were running on parallel tracks. She would reveal something about herself, and then moments later shift the focus away, becoming, again, opaque. I could feel myself being led through my paces like one of the hapless guys in Moore’s stories, the ones her “tragically, heroically self-aware women,” as the novelist Dana Spiotta put it, are always forced to deal with.

Moore was creating intimacy while keeping me at arm’s length — a skill she has mastered across four generation-defining short-story collections and four novels. In books like her debut, 1985’s “Self-Help,” and the 1994 novel “Who Will Run the Frog Hospital?” she presented a voice that felt unusually intimate, even a little transgressive. Her young women are as genteel as Moore herself, who was raised in a churchgoing family in small-town upstate New York. But the barbed wit with which her characters treat their relationship emergencies, an archness that fails to conceal a desperate, sincere sadness, offered readers — especially young people with literary aspirations of their own — a new way to think about the painful predicaments of their own lives. By the 1990s, Moore’s stories had won her much acclaim: She was being called the best American writer of her generation by The Times of London.

Yet despite the connection readers feel to Moore’s heroines, her writing maintains a careful distance, epitomizing a formal rigor that never confuses the use of the personal for artistic effect with the confessional. Moore will not confess. She mines the events and characters of her life, not in search of closure or catharsis but to bend and shape them to her artistic purpose.

Her new novel, “I Am Homeless if This Is Not My Home,” her first in 14 years, is a stylistic departure that finds her stretching her voice to explore the surreal borderlands of grief. An experiment jumping between the 19th and 21st centuries, it strays from the contemporary world she’s a master of portraying. Chronicling a road trip taken by a mourning man and the walking, talking corpse of his dead lover, it leaps into the fantastical in a way Moore never has before. But like all her writing, “I Am Homeless” uses elevated, often dazzling language to evoke a complex emotional state. Like all things that dazzle, Moore’s language can illuminate, and it can obscure — sometimes it can even hurt your eyes. Moore seems to find all these effects equally useful.

Four decades into her career, Moore’s fiction is becoming ever stranger, ever knottier, ever more devoted to a project increasingly at odds with literary trends. She doesn’t draw from life because she’s trying to come to terms with her problems, or work her way through her trauma, or instruct the reader on a moral. Instead, in starting from life, Moore hopes to capture a particular emotion — the way grief transforms its sufferers into fools and fantasists — and then surround the reader in all its terrible beauty, as the aria surrounds the listener. The story has to get inside us and do its thing.

In 1980, a 23-year-old Moore arrived at Cornell’s M.F.A. program with, to her mind, one solid story. In her first workshop, the other students hated it. “They all had a list of things I was doing wrong,” she remembered. Her professor, the novelist Alison Lurie, took her aside and told her that the story was good and that she should ignore her classmates. “But I already was ignoring them,” Moore added matter-of-factly. “Because I had read their fiction, and it wasn’t good.”

She was trying to figure out what to do about the boyfriend she’d left in New York City, a teacher, “a very nice boy.” She read a lot of poetry, devouring the language, and loved Richard Hugo’s “Degrees of Gray in Philipsburg,” with its second-person imperatives: “Say your life broke down”; “Say no to yourself.” You could tell a whole story in that mode, she thought. The story she wrote was called “How” — though, she said, she could just as easily have titled it “How to Break Up With Your Nice Boyfriend.” With its poetic second-person perspective (“Pace around the kitchen and say you are unhappy. But I love you, he will say in his soft bewildered way, stirring the spaghetti sauce but not you”), it gave Moore’s fiction an entirely new voice, terse yet emotional.

Read that story now, alongside the other second-person pieces in “Self-Help” — most of them written while she was a student at Cornell — and you’ll feel the same intense but false connection you get from listening to the same podcast every day. You know this person, you think, but what’s actually happening is that your idea of her is taking residence in your brain. “For me to read a story where a white woman is telling you, almost ordering you, to put yourself in that character’s shoes — it’s so direct,” said Sidik Fofana, a Whiting Award-winning writer who studied with Moore. “But you can never lose track of the ‘you’ who’s reading, so there’s a little bit of distance.” Moore exploits that narrow space between her “yous” and you, Dana Spiotta said, as you laugh with her characters but feel yourself implicated in their bitter self-critiques. “She’s puncturing these vanities,” Spiotta said — her characters’, but yours as well.

In Moore’s stories, funny people banter with their loved ones as playfully as we all do, creating little bubbles around our relationships. But for Moore, those inside jokes are warning lights. Banter in Moore’s stories between lovers or friends “creates a kind of intimacy,” Spiotta said, “that is underwritten with sadness, because it isn’t sustainable.” In a story from her second collection, a young woman dating two men at once finds her shared jokes with one boyfriend edging into aggression. She has started stepping on his punchlines. “They had begun to do imitations of each other, that most violent and satisfying end to love.”

In 1984, Moore was hired by the University of Wisconsin, the youngest professor in an English department staffed mostly with older men. After the publication of “Self-Help,” she became one of the most intriguing young writers in America, but her real life felt different. “I was too young to be a professor, really,” she said. “The students were sometimes my age.” Missing New York — she joked that she spent her entire salary on long-distance phone calls — she returned to Manhattan her first few summers of teaching, subletting the type of apartment where, for example, she did her dishes in a bathtub in the kitchen. She could afford dinner and a cheap ticket to the opera, but she couldn’t afford to throw out leftovers, so she would smuggle takeout containers into the Met and shove them, reeking, under her seat.

Loving Moore’s work, in those days, felt like belonging to a kind of secret society. You would never recommend her work to just anyone — only to someone who would truly get it, which is to say, who would get you. “I call her the It Girl,” said Victoria Wilson, Moore’s editor of nearly 40 years at Knopf. “Because of her physicality as well as the writing, Lorrie is sort of the quintessential crush.” Ann Patchett remembers piling into a car with three classmates in the late ’80s, planning to drive from the Iowa Writers’ Workshop to Madison, Wis., to meet their idol. “That’s crush behavior for sure,” Patchett said. The car broke down on the way, and they had to get towed back.

What is it about her work that generated such fervor? Moore’s characters only sometimes resemble her, but the crises they’re undergoing always mirrored her own life, and thus the ongoing lives of the readers who loved her the most. “You see these various stages of life,” Wilson said. “Dating, dealing with being single, being a single mother, being divorced.” On the stage of American fiction, she took the materials of life — women’s lives, particularly, recognizable and harrowing — and made out of them elaborate works of art. “We were coming out of a Carver-esque moment,” Patchett said. Moore, in contrast, was “plush, rich.” She added: “We just worshiped her.”

“What is the story?”

That is what the Mother asks upon finding a blood clot in the Baby’s diaper. Moore’s “People Like That Are the Only People Here” is about a mother Moore’s age, a writer, desperate to answer that question, to understand what kind of narrative she’s in — even as she determines that she must be the one to tell it. It’s difficult to overstate the impact this story had when it was published in The New Yorker in 1997. Even decades later, every writer I asked to choose a standout Moore story to discuss with me picked that one. It transformed Moore’s career, and, in some ways, her life.

The excitement surrounding the story had to do not only with the way it violated certain rules of propriety and form; it had to do with the way the extreme temperature of its situation cracked Moore’s gleaming shell. “It’s so complicated and brutal,” Groff said. “You feel her great reserve is gone, and she’s bearing down with all her might.”

Moore is not a celebrity, but she exhibits a celebrity’s caution about discussing the personal.

“Take notes,” the Husband in the story tells the Mother. “We are going to need the money.” The Mother protests: “I’m not that good. I can’t do this. I can do — what can I do? I can do quasi-amusing phone dialogue … But this? Our baby with cancer? I’m sorry.” Nevertheless, she takes the notes. She meets the other mothers of the pediatric oncology ward, all of them clad in sweatpants. Even though she is a woman who mail orders oysters from SoHo, she also buys the sweatpants. She notices that her husband’s friends, when they visit, never tell jokes. She longs for jokes.

During surgery, the Mother and the Husband sit in the waiting room, too wrung out even to trade Mooreian witticisms. “Do you want some coffee?” the Husband asks; the Mother replies with dialogue so unsparkling, so unsparing, it takes my breath away: “I don’t know. No. I guess not. Do you?” The Baby survives surgery, and the family flees the hospital. The story’s final two lines are funny, angry, exhausted: “There are the notes. Now, where is the money?”

Moore explained the story’s inspiration elliptically. “Everything came to a standstill that year,” she said. Long afterward, she would open cupboards and find items with expiration dates from 1996. “This was it,” she said. “If I was going to call myself a writer, I would have to write a story about this.”

The New Yorker published “People Like That” with its text surrounding a large photo of Moore — a candid shot, capturing her not smiling wryly but mid-sentence, mid-worry, her hands blurred in motion. Moore was very unhappy with the magazine’s fiction editor at the time, Bill Buford, who, she said, told her that the photographer he’d sent was meant to get a portrait for a contributors’ page. “But then he used it as an illustration.”

Buford later apologized, Moore said. “He didn’t know what he wanted to do with the story,” she added. “He didn’t understand it.” Buford (who told me he didn’t recall making the decision) now agrees with her: “I’m glad she remembers I apologized, because I certainly should have apologized.” But it seemed to me that Buford understood the story well, and published it perfectly. The story’s power comes, in part, from its tightrope walk between fiction and memoir — not for voyeuristic reasons, but because it’s the story of a woman, an artist, accomplishing a thing she never believed she could. She was that good. She did “our baby with cancer.” She applied all her intelligence, all her humanity, all her morality, to an impossible situation. And the bitter last lines answer the question: In what way did it help?

I argued that the photo had benefited the story enormously. “But maybe it benefited it in ways you find too offensive to brook.” She disagreed. Such filigree was unnecessary, she said, for a story whose subject already was the friction between life and art. “ ‘Do you write about this?’ ‘How do you write about this?’ All of that’s in there.”

“The story does enough on its own, you mean,” I said.

“I thought so,” she said quietly.

Moore is not a celebrity, but she exhibits a celebrity’s caution about discussing the personal. She’s divorced from that husband, and her son now lives in Madison, but aside from that, she told me — not unkindly — “I don’t want to talk about family stuff.” When I asked why, she said: “I don’t want any innocent victims. I’ll be the victim.” “People Like That,” more than anything she wrote before, pierced the veil between her life and her work. Yes, because of the photograph, but really because the story itself wields the knife.

In an essay about one of her literary heroes, Alice Munro, Moore describes the literary life’s “emotional distances … and its willing trade of the human for art.” That trade doesn’t always come off clean. “My mother would go through my work and say, ‘Why are all the mothers dead?’” Moore said. “And then there was one where the mother didn’t die — and I’m sure she wished the mother had.” You hurt the people you use in your stories, she knows. “But it will always happen,” she added, shrugging. Her parents are dead, and she misses them, but now she can write about them without worrying about their response. More important: “Now you can make stuff up. You can embroider. You can get it all wrong.” It’s that embroidering that makes the characters not your mother, the art something bigger than your own story.

Her arm’s-length relationship to the confessional stands in opposition to a dominant strand in contemporary literature, in which trauma is named and claimed for the purposes of processing it and coming out the other side a more admirable person. Moore recently wrote a magnificent, comic, tactically offensive complaint about young people in the form of an essay on Sally Rooney’s “Normal People” and its TV adaptation: “Millennials are boundary-conscious and cannot be touched by anyone, even on the sleeve, without consent, but with consent will have hookups with total strangers.” One thing Moore doesn’t understand about young people, her students included, are their notions of writing as catharsis. “I do feel, with my stories, that I really try to be emotionally brave,” she said. But she’s not convinced grappling with their personal pain through fiction is effective. “Writing doesn’t really work as catharsis for most people, I don’t think. So you might as well choose art.”

Moore, a great American short-story artist, sometimes speaks of writing novels with a slight discomfort. “A novel is a daily labor over a period of years,” she told The Paris Review, unlike the story, “a mad, lovely visitor, with whom you spend a rather exciting weekend.” Her previous novels — 1986’s “Anagrams,” 1994’s “Frog Hospital” and 2009’s “A Gate at the Stairs” — hew, like most of her stories, at least somewhat to the circumstances of her own life. Unlike in the stories, the power of her heightened language is sometimes defeated by the novel’s formal requirements, the longueurs introduced in a form that deals, at its heart, with the passage of time. Sometimes in a novel, you just have to explain who’s going where, and why.

“I Am Homeless if This Is Not My Home” feels different in how far afield its unusual story line takes readers from its origin — Moore’s desire to write about grief. “Everyone seemed to be dying around me,” she said when I asked where the novel began. Though she started writing before Covid, the pandemic haunts its pages. (In 2020, Moore published a devastating story called “Face Time” about an adult daughter watching her father die from hundreds of miles away.) Grief fools you, she knew; or rather, grief encourages you to fool yourself and invent tricks to pretend a loved one is still there. Those tricks become literal, corporeal, in the novel, and the sheer disorientation she creates in the reader evokes the fractured, impossible confusion of death’s aftermath.

In the 21st-century passages of the novel, Finn, another one of Moore’s hapless men, finds himself lost in a kind of dark folk tale. Though his ex Lily has died of suicide, when he arrives at her grave, she stands there waiting, wearing her death shroud, her mouth full of dirt. Together they drive through the South, trading lovers’ road-trip chatter.

No explanation is offered. Finn soon stops even asking. “You think you can be the artist of your own death but surprise!” Lily says. “It always turns out crappier than you planned.” The novel stays locked into this baffling, beautiful mode, preferring to perplex rather than explain. As Spiotta said to me, it’s proof that difficulty isn’t necessarily boring: If you open yourself up to it, it might lead you to some surprising emotional spaces. In its refusal to even hint at catharsis, “I Am Homeless” resembles no book anyone else would write about grief right now. Moore has made high art and low comedy — zombie comedy, almost — from the catastrophe we’ve all been through, but she doesn’t mind if you, like Finn driving the dark back roads of Tennessee with a chatty corpse in the passenger seat, get lost along the way.

Though the novel, like Lily herself, doesn’t always hold together, it might come closest of any of Moore’s longer works to maintaining the operatic heights of her short stories. “Language is hobbled,” Moore said fervently, “with its physical self and the physical world. But it is trying to get to a musical, abstracted place where a feeling goes directly into a reader.” She shook her head. “To a listener. Whatever.”

I asked Moore if she thought this unruly novel would confuse people. “Yes,” she said immediately. “But I’ve read lots of books that are confusing, and I’m willing to back up and start over.” She laughed grimly. “I’m afraid I’ve written something that needs to be read twice.”

“That’s a challenge in 2023,” I said.

“That’s a challenge any year,” she said.

“In the 1800s, people only owned like seven books,” I pointed out. “You could count on them reading your book over and over.”

She nodded. “They had more time, but shorter lives.”

When the curtain came down at the end of “La Traviata,” Moore was wiping away tears. “I always start to cry when the love theme begins again,” she said.

The cool air of the evening felt good; I’d been sweating in my sport coat. Cabs lined up, their headlights a string of pearls running down Unter den Linden. “What did you think?” she asked. “I’m sorry it had all that sort of weird, busy stage business.” The production ended with a group of bodysuited dancers exiting the stage before, at the moment of Violetta’s death, she slipped backward through an enormous mirror.

“It’s still very affecting, despite all that,” I said.

Typically, she explained, the opera ends simply: “The stage darkens, and she’s all alone.” She hummed a snatch of Verdi as we crossed the street. “That’s what we like,” she added. “A darkened stage, a woman alone.”

We walked up Friedrichstrasse, navigating around teenagers riding scooters on the sidewalk, girls clutching boys, laughing at their private jokes. When we boarded the S-bahn, Moore slipped on her sunglasses. “So you can’t see my opera eyes,” she murmured.
