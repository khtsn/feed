May 11, 2023, 11:41 a.m. ET

A bus carrying about two dozen migrants that left from New York City arrived in Orange County, N.Y., on Thursday morning, even as officials there sought a last-minute court injunction that might block the city’s plan to house people at the Crossroads Hotel in the town of Newburgh, about 60 miles north of Manhattan.

As of Thursday at 11 a.m., the migrants, numbering “a couple dozen,” had already moved into the hotel, said Fabien Levy, a spokesman for Mayor Eric Adams.

Steven Neuhaus, the Orange County executive, said he expected the injunction to be granted “within the next 24 hours.” It was not immediately clear what would happen to the migrants should the injunction go through.

The back-and-forth is the latest dust-up between New York City and several counties to the north, where Mr. Adams’s administration had hoped to rent space to house the migrants who are arriving daily from the United States’s border with Mexico.

Mr. Adams last Friday announced that he would send migrants, on a voluntary basis, to hotels in Orange and Rockland counties, because New York City’s own shelters have become overcrowded.

But the announcement prompted complaints — and legal action — from officials in both counties.

“We had protesters on both sides yesterday there,” Mr. Neuhaus said, referring to the hotel. “It’s just poorly executed and indicative of how they’ve been handling this thing from Day 1. They really need to start communicating more.”

Gil Piaquadio, the supervisor of Newburgh, was not immediately available for comment.

More than 60,000 migrants have arrived in New York City in the past year, according to City Hall officials, including 4,200 in the last week alone.

Citing federal sources, state officials have warned that the weekly arrivals were likely to increase to as many as 5,000 with the looming expiration of Title 42, a pandemic-era policy the United States has used to expel hundreds of thousands of migrants, including some who might formerly have been granted asylum.

More than 37,500 migrants are now in city care at more than 120 emergency shelters and eight larger-scale centers.

New York City has been frantically searching for additional space in which to house them, including at an abandoned psychiatric hospital and at airplane hangars at Kennedy International Airport. City officials even asked an owner of the Flatiron Building if there was any room there (he said no).

Orange and Rockland counties have met the prospect of migrants with lawsuits, and in the case of the Rockland County executive, Ed Day, with a posture that Mr. Levy said shows “he is incapable of demonstrating a shred of the humane and compassionate care New York City has shown over the past year.”

The executives of both counties have faulted Mr. Adams for failing to communicate effectively with them, and for behaving in a high-handed manner.

The mayor on Thursday said he had no choice but to send migrants further north. He also pointedly noted that unlike Gov. Greg Abbott of Texas, New York City would be paying for the migrants’ hotel rooms and support services.

“Everything is on the table,” the mayor said.

“I mean, that is an ‘oh shucks’ moment,” he said, referring to the influx of 4,200 migrants in the last week alone. “And I’m only saying ‘shucks’ because I’m being televised. Listen, that’s scary. And Title 42 has not been lifted yet.”
