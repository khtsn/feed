Inside GamerLegion: Workplace misconduct, Paris Major sticker drama, and history of late payments

It took Ørjan "TheViper" Larsen one livestream to finally receive all the money he was owed.

On January 29, the Norwegian Age of Empires player fired up his stream and revealed that he was in a dispute with GamerLegion, the organization he had represented for over five years until the end of 2025, over outstanding salary and prize money. He added that he had hired a lawyer to help him in his case.

"They owe me a lot of money, and it's very frustrating," he said. "I think they have the intention of paying me. I hope so, because otherwise, it can have very big implications on my life."

Just 24 hours later, he posted a video on his YouTube channel revealing that all debts had been settled.

GamerLegion had been the leading team in the Age of Empires' esports scene since entering the game in September 2020. It was a project championed by Nicolas Reber, who founded GamerLegion in 2017 after retiring from World of Tanks, in which he had competed professionally. GamerLegion was initially established as a coaching platform before it became a competitive organization.

In addition to having the best team in the scene and the best player in Hamzah "Hera" El-Baher, GamerLegion ran several community initiatives: it had its own podcast, and in December 2024, it hosted the Noble Apartment Cup, an international tournament with ten players and $50,000 in prize money, at its Berlin gaming house.

GamerLegion were "basically the only professional team in the scene," a source close to the squad told HLTV. Their continued support for the Age of Empires scene earned them enough goodwill that their players were willing to overlook occasional late payments of salary and prize money. But the issue became more pronounced in 2024 as those delays grew more common, finally reaching a breaking point in 2025. That summer, TheViper suddenly quit the podcast over late payments, the source said.

Amid the team's efforts to receive the money they were owed, GamerLegion expanded into Dota 2, signing the Apex Genesis roster in November 2025. "That just rubbed a little bit of salt into the wound," TheViper admitted on his stream.

At the end of the year, he and the rest of the team left GamerLegion.

"It's important to point out the amount of patience and trust the AoE players had," the source said. "Waiting that long without pushing more for it or taking legal action.

"Some people at GamerLegion definitely took advantage of that. If you don't know how long you can hold people, you will not risk it. There clearly was an assumption of how long they could make people wait until something happened."

HLTV first received reports of delayed payments across GamerLegion in the summer of 2025. When TheViper spoke up, more and more people came forward, their accounts taking this article in new directions.

Overall, nearly two dozen people spoke with HLTV about their time at GamerLegion, providing an inside look at the company's culture and internal operations. The picture they painted was of an organization that tried to scale too quickly off the back of its Paris Major run, yet remained dysfunctional in many areas, plagued by a toxic workplace culture, poor management, and chronic payment delays that affected players and employees at many levels.

All sources asked to speak anonymously, fearing retaliation. HLTV accepted these terms, given GamerLegion's profile and the public interest in reporting on allegations of workplace misconduct and late payments.

Some of the sources quoted have since left esports, but they're still wary of potential repercussions from Reber, the CEO and founder.

"He has a regular thing of threatening to take people to court," one source explained. "That is kind of his way of dealing with things if he feels something isn't going his way."

HLTV contacted Reber by email with a variety of questions for this article. He said the following about the former Age of Empires team: "All outstanding payment obligations to the former AoE players have been settled in full."

"Nothing is too much for GamerLegion right now," Jacob "⁠Pimp⁠" Winneche said on the analyst desk at the Accor Arena.

As GamerLegion's players stepped onto the stage before the Paris Major grand final against Vitality after one of the wildest underdog runs in Counter-Strike Major history, the future looked bright for both the team and the organization.

Behind the scenes, however, not everything was as it seemed.

Tension had begun earlier in the tournament over the team's cut of the proceeds from the sale of the Major's digital items. This came after the players learned they were entitled to only a percentage of the team's sticker revenue, while players on rival teams also had a cut from the sale of other items.

"I think it was around the quarter-final or semi-finals that we got told we were not going to be getting the 10% [of the items]," one player on the roster at the time told HLTV.

The situation became so hostile that forfeiting the final was even considered at one point, two people with knowledge of the situation told HLTV. In the end, the strike didn't take place as the players didn't agree on what they should do, and the final went ahead, with Vitality running out 2-0 winners to lift their first Major crown.

In the two weeks following the event, the players and management discussed this matter.

According to a player contract reviewed by HLTV, players were entitled to 50 percent (10 percent each) "of the received revenue from the sale of team items." The contract clause suggested only GamerLegion-branded items were covered under the 10 percent split, not souvenirs or viewer passes. But players strongly believed the wording was ambiguous and that they were entitled to 10 percent of the entire Major proceeds.

This becomes especially significant in a Major that generated a record $110 million in earnings from the sale of digital items. With team stickers accounting for approximately 56% of the $2.6 million each team in the Challengers capsule received, it means players would each miss out on over $100,000 they felt was rightfully theirs.

By the end of the Paris Major run, it had become clear that Kamil "⁠siuhy⁠" Szkaradek and Mihai "⁠iM⁠" Ivan had one foot out the door amid interest from MOUZ and Natus Vincere, respectively.

That didn't keep GamerLegion's management from trying to keep the roster intact. In an effort to convince siuhy and iM to stay, they offered every player a percentage of the items not covered by their contracts.

"Guaranteed 5% share per player of total amounts received from all items if we continue with this team without changes past July," Reber, the CEO, wrote to the team.

This didn't go down well with the players.

"He [Reber] tried to make siuhy and iM stay and not take the offers from MOUZ and NAVI by pressuring the other players, I think, and make them feel bad about it," one player recalled.

GamerLegion's efforts were in vain, though, as both players still left the team, with iM announced by NAVI on June 30 and siuhy by MOUZ days later.

In a letter to the five players who competed in Paris, Reber and Julian "morxzas" Miculcy, then GamerLegion's Head of Operations, acknowledged that using stickers as leverage during negotiations may have come across as inappropriate. They also pledged to give the team a 5% share of the sale of non-team stickers from the Rio and Paris Majors, and the same cut for all future Majors. This in addition to the 10% share of the sale of team stickers.

"On Monday 29th of May, Kamil [siuhy] requested 5% per player as a fair revenue share," the letter read. "We said, we will have to think about it, but did not decline the ask. We also said that we need to think about it in perspective on how the team will change or stay the same.

"Trying for Kamil to stay and connecting it to the Stickers seemingly felt very wrong for you, but that was not our intention at all. We tried to show you a path for the future on how things can make it easier for us, looking towards possible buyouts with Mouz, EPL-Spots and Blast Partnership and so on.

"If you perceived it differently, we owe you an apology for that because the intention was never bad, nor did we under any circumstance think about using this situation against you and would most likely have gone for a solution similar to the one where we are now."

When asked by HLTV to reflect on the use of sticker money as a bargaining tool in negotiations, Reber provided the following statement:

"​​The facts are presented in a manner that significantly distorts the actual course of events. In addition, your question is leading. Any initial confusion was clarified by GamerLegion. Ultimately, the players received increased shares as a gesture of goodwill and recognition of their performance.

"Irrespective of this, it is in any organization’s interest to foster an environment in which players feel valued, thereby encouraging a successful team to remain with the organization. Sticker payouts were not, as the question wrongly suggests, abused as bargaining/negotiation tools."

More precise contract wording might have prevented the dispute, but it's worth remembering that GamerLegion were still new to tier-1 Counter-Strike at the time, with only one prior Major (Rio) under their belt. "They were very raw as an org," one source recalled.

GamerLegion's Paris run surpassed everyone's wildest expectations, even inside the organization. Multiple sources told HLTV that an employee had to make an emergency drive from Berlin to Paris ahead of the final, hauling boxes of merchandise to distribute at the Accor Arena — a desperate effort to prevent the crowd from being entirely dominated by Vitality supporters.

"They were not prepared whatsoever to make it to the final," one former employee recalled.

"Never anything properly planned but always 100% commitment expected," another ex-staffer said.

The players eventually accepted the terms set out by GamerLegion, though the episode left a lasting sense of distrust in at least one of the players who remained with the organization.

"I was just frustrated with GamerLegion," the player said. "I just had this thing in the back of my mind that they scammed me. It was not nice."

For years, late salary and prize payments were also a persistent issue at GamerLegion, affecting players and backroom staff alike. Prize money "regularly took a year or longer to get paid", one source said, while late salaries were constantly a topic, even at big events like RMRs, where Major spots were on the line.

"It was a big distraction quite a few times," a source said. "This feels unnecessary. You want support when you're playing these events. And it's kind of the opposite."

But not everyone was affected by this issue the same way. One source described GamerLegion's payment policy as "selective": those with aggressive agents or a higher profile were normally paid first. The other ones, like a former staff member who said he had to borrow money for food at a bootcamp after not receiving his salary, were regularly left to wait.

"Salary was usually late, sometimes even two months late," one former player recalled.

"I feel like, for me, salaries were paid on time, as I remember," another ex-player said. "But I think I didn't have the highest salary on the team."

Another former player recalled waiting a year to get his share of a Major's sticker money. It took "many months of asking" for him to know exactly how much money he had to invoice GamerLegion, and then almost half a year for the payment to come through.

Quizzed on how long it took GamerLegion to pay the sticker money from the Rio and the Paris Majors, Reber said: "The payment of sticker proceeds was made individually to each player in accordance with industry standards and the legal obligations. All claims were settled in full."

When pressed again for an exact timeline, Reber provided the same response.

When a player gets benched, communication with GamerLegion often becomes even more challenging. "They cannot get in contact with them at all," a source described. "They refuse to reply."

HLTV is aware of two players who have been in dispute with GamerLegion over alleged failures to meet contractual obligations, such as streaming quotas and minimum social media activity, after their benching. One player viewed these claims as just another stalling tactic, saying all such requirements had been fulfilled. It took GamerLegion nearly six months after his departure to pay all outstanding salaries. The other player is exploring legal options.

"If something is written in the contract, he [Reber] will do everything he can to enforce it," one source said. "But if it's not and it's your issue, it doesn't work the same way."

Mismanagement also plagued GamerLegion's academy roster, which was established in November 2023 and notably featured Andreas "⁠aNdu⁠" Maasing, who would go on to be promoted to the main team.

The first three months ran smoothly: the team bootcamped before the announcement, content around the players was created, and salaries arrived on time. But by early 2024, cracks had begun to show: payments started coming late, promised bootcamps never materialized, and management ignored the coach's meeting requests.

"It was a different reason each time," one team member recalled.

The issue hit a breaking point in the summer, by which point players were owed three months' wages.

"The finance team is working every day to finish the year 2023 — that will give us the possibility to reach out to our funds and use them for payments," morxzas wrote in an email to the team in the summer of 2024, during a period of time the academy roster referred to as the ‘Great Salary Delay’.

"For now, we are trying to pay for small things one by one. Therefore, in the next week, I think a few of you, hopefully all, will get paid. I'm trying to get a definite date from our finance department, but I can't promise."

In one case, an academy player waited three weeks for his contract to be finalized, costing him a month's salary despite actively playing for the roster during that time. His signing wasn't announced until nearly three months after he had joined the team.

"At one point during my time on the team, we had a delay of four months that was only ended because ash and, I believe, a player from the main CS team agreed to wait for some of their salaries to make sure that we received ours," the player told HLTV.

"This was a very stressful time as I kept having to explain to my parents that I wasn't being scammed and that I would get this money eventually to pay them rent."

In January 2025, GamerLegion announced that the academy was being discontinued. The project remains shelved to this day.

"GamerLegion could have helped grow and nurture players and help them become professional," one of the team's earliest members said. "They made it the worst environment possible."

Asked about the regular late payments in the Counter-Strike division, including the academy roster, Reber said:

"The facts are presented in a way that significantly distorts the actual course of events. GamerLegion always makes every effort to settle any outstanding payments on time. In the rare instances where delays occurred, payments were always made before the next payment cycle (typically immediately after GamerLegion became aware of it).

"Prize money was paid out in accumulated installments, which is industry standard. At present, there are no outstanding or overdue payment obligations. Therefore, the term 'commonplace' does not reflect reality."

For anyone considering a position at GamerLegion, a brief visit to Kununu might give them serious pause.

The website is one of the leading online platforms for workplace reviews in the German-speaking world, with companies rated on categories such as working conditions, atmosphere, work/life balance, salary, and equality.

GamerLegion holds a Kununu score of 3.1 (on a scale from 1 to 5), based on ten reviews — the two most detailed of which paint a particularly damning picture of the company.

One of these reviews, updated in March 2026, is titled 'Lektion fürs Leben- nicht nochmal' (A Life Lesson — Never Again).

  • Suggestions for improvement: Basics like corporate culture, appropriate leadership behavior, and respectful communication would be a start.

  • Work atmosphere: Daily interactions are mainly characterized by unfounded and sexist remarks made by "managers." The focus is solely on competing with one another and making others look as bad as possible.

  • Work-life balance: Non-existent. "Managers" have no regard for what day or time it is — you are expected to work regardless. You get called in the evenings out of boredom, just to fill their commute home, with no consideration for the fact that your working day has already ended. Vacation days are handed out stingily, and even days off are not respected.

  • Team cohesion: Barely to non-existent. There is manipulation and a lot of talking behind people's backs. "Managers" badmouth employees to other employees.

  • Management behavior: Catastrophic. All "managers" are essentially unqualified to be in a leadership role. Disrespect and reprimands are woven into their "leadership style." Supervisors have no interest in employees developing — rather, they are kept small so that no one questions anything. Appreciation, praise, and recognition are completely foreign concepts in this company.

Such remarks are in line with what many former employees described in interviews with HLTV.

One former employee recalled weekly meetings among department heads routinely devolving into shouting matches filled with hostility, finger-pointing, and name-calling — behavior the CEO not only tolerated but actively encouraged.

"It's like the Colosseum in Rome, and the gladiators are fighting," the source said. "They were screaming at each other, cursing and raising their voice.

"And it got worse every time. And instead of saying, 'Okay, guys, we need to settle down to speak about certain topics,' he [Reber] was just lying back, like, 'I will enjoy this.'

"It was just horrible."

Other former employees provided similar accounts of the company atmosphere and confirmed minimal management intervention when unprofessional behavior occurred.

"We are talking about yelling, degrading and being highly condescending," one source said. "Nicolas would be sitting around, watching it and not intervening at all."

Multiple sources described a working environment that felt highly controlled and reactive, with little regard for work-life balance, contributing to low morale and a high turnover rate.

Several sources said they avoided coming into the Berlin office as much as possible due to the tense atmosphere — something that frequently put them at odds with Reber. "Nicolas was no friend of the home office," one source said.

Two former staff members also described a culture of bullying at the company, where the atmosphere could suddenly turn hostile towards an employee for no apparent reason.

"They always pick a victim," one former employee said. She recounted constant but sometimes subtle harassment, from belittling comments to gossip between co-workers. She claimed she discovered by accident that a private Slack channel had been created where colleagues were sharing memes about her.

"It was the most traumatizing time of my career, and I was in esports for ten years," she said. On her last day at the company, she was required to come into the office, where, she recalled, "not a single soul talked to me" the entire time.

GamerLegion's job postings describe the company as respectful and progressive. "We celebrate diversity and are committed to creating an inclusive environment for all," GamerLegion writes.

Several former employees painted a different picture, describing an environment where sexist remarks were not uncommon, including from those in leadership positions.

One former employee recounted one such instance at a dinner in Paris during the 2023 CS Major.

"There were some people from Monte Esports, the Ukrainian team, and the first comment [then-​​Director of Sales and Marketing] Michael [Bier] made was, 'There's the Monte team. Look at the girl. How old is she?'

"After like half an hour, they walked past us again, and he said: 'What do you think? How much does she charge per hour?'"

This story was backed up by two other sources who were there at the time.

HLTV reached out to Reber and Bier for comment. Reber initially denied that any staff team dinner involving Bier had taken place — but when presented with claims that the incident had occurred at a French restaurant called L'Auberge Aveyronnaise near the Accor Arena, and that Bier's voice could be heard in footage allegedly recorded that same night and published on GamerLegion's TikTok shortly before the final, he walked back that position.

Bier then provided his only statement to HLTV, saying:

"You're right about the evening at L'Auberge Aveyronnaise," he wrote. "It took place, I was there, and the TikTok you mentioned is from that night. When we put our first response together, we were looking at whether there had been any proper team dinner during the Major, there wasn't, and in that frame, we missed the evening you were actually asking about. That's on me, and I should have caught it. I want to address it properly now.

"On the remark itself, I honestly don't remember saying what you quote, and neither do the people I have personally reached out to who were there that evening. That said, I can't sit here and pretend I can reconstruct every single exchange from a group of people sitting around street tables late in the evening. If something that was said landed as disrespectful toward anyone there, I take that seriously and stand behind that with an apology."

One former employee told HLTV she had been informed that part of the reason she was hired was that "a woman looks better for the company than some random dude."

"They always made sexual jokes and all that stuff," she added. "This manager that I shared an office with heard that I had broken up with my boyfriend and started celebrating me going single."

According to two former employees, there was an instance where a woman was hired over more qualified applicants because of her looks.

"We had a couple of interviews, and one of the managers told me, 'She's not that good, but she's looking damn hot. We will get so many views, so we will hire her,'" one source said.

The other employee confirmed this story and named Bier, GamerLegion's current Commercial Director, as the one who had made those comments.

"His strategy was that if we put somebody that is really good-looking in front of the camera, we'll get loads of followers," they said.

"These are the sort of remarks that Michael would make. It might be something that anybody would say, but when you hear the rest, where he makes bad remarks or sexist remarks about women, that sort of compounds, and you understand what he's meaning with it or where he's coming from."

HLTV contacted Reber and Bier for comment. Reber was the only one who replied, providing the following statement: "To the extent that your inquiry concerns any alleged sexist remarks, it is of a general and hypothetical nature and not tied to any specific case and therefore cannot be reasonably answered. GamerLegion places the highest importance on ensuring that all employees and partners are treated with respect and dignity."

Beyond the routine sexist remarks that permeated the company, one female employee faced more direct harassment. According to her, a department head made unwanted advances toward her on two separate occasions.

She also described another instance where a different director made remarks about her looks, including the size of her breasts, in the middle of the company office for everyone to hear.

Because of the culture at GamerLegion and Reber's role in fostering it, she didn’t report those incidents, fearing she would be perceived as "too weak." She eventually left GamerLegion.

When asked for a comment about the harassment this employee faced and her reluctance to report such incidents to him, Reber provided the following comment:

"To date, the CEO is not aware of any allegations of this nature. Had any such report been raised, it would have been treated with the utmost seriousness and addressed promptly, thoroughly, and in accordance with all applicable standards and procedures.

"From the respondent’s perspective, there is no basis for the suggestion that the working environment at GamerLegion could reasonably be perceived otherwise. The organization is firmly committed to maintaining a professional, respectful, and safe workplace at all times. Any form of inappropriate conduct is categorically rejected and will not be tolerated under any circumstances."

Four sources recalled Bier openly addressing an employee as "fetti" (fatty), rather than by his name, because of his weight.

"I think it started as a joke because they kind of knew each other and they got along well," one source said.

"I don't know if he [Bier] has the sense or the empathy to get that it's not appropriate to call someone that on a regular basis."

The source added: "This kind of stuff happened all the time, where he [Bier] was calling people out for, I don't know, what they were wearing, what they looked like, what they were doing."

HLTV contacted Reber and Bier for comment. Only Reber responded, saying: "The situation has been presented out of context and is therefore misleading. The example in question involves two long-standing friends and colleagues who communicate with each other in an informal manner specific to their relationship. There is no indication that either party perceived this exchange as offensive or demeaning."

During the course of our reporting, an April 2026 Kununu review, titled 'Viel gelernt, gute Kollegen, Rest mehr als suboptimal' (Learned a lot, great colleagues, everything else less than ideal) was posted and then suddenly removed. It gave GamerLegion a rating of 1.9 and mentioned a "catastrophic" leadership behavior, delayed salary payments and expense reimbursements, and "joking insults" in the workplace.

Online, several people have reported that Kununu often removes negative reviews that are challenged by employers. Kununu, like any platform of its kind, is not immune to manipulation — fake reviews, whether glowing or damning, are always possible, and this should be kept in mind when weighing what is written there. And there's a chance that dissatisfied employees are more likely to leave reviews than those with a positive experience.

In an email to HLTV, Reber confirmed that the review in question was "immediately challenged" by GamerLegion after it was posted. It was reinstated on April 22.

HLTV is aware of two other instances in which GamerLegion legally challenged unfavorable reviews, resulting in their temporary removal from Kununu. In both cases, the platform requested that the person who submitted the review substantiate their employment. One of the reviews has been restored after proof was supplied.

When asked if GamerLegion had challenged reviews on Kununu, Reber said:

"We fully recognize the importance of reviews as a source of information. However, reviews can only serve this purpose if they are factually accurate and expressed in an objective manner. Therefore, we are unwilling to accept reviews that are detrimental to our company by false factual claims, defamatory or abusive commentary.

"Accordingly, acting within the scope of our legal rights, we reserve our right to take appropriate action against such content, consistent with standard business practice. This also applies to reviews on the Kununu platform."

When discussing the company's culture, two distinct people used the same German expression:

Der Fisch stinkt vom Kopf her.

It translates to "the fish rots from the head down" — a saying that attributes organizational failure to poor leadership.

Several former employees described reporting workplace problems to Reber, who would either dismiss their concerns or promise to address them but never follow through.

One source said they raised concerns about Bier with Reber, asking that the matter be kept confidential — only for the CEO to bring it up openly in a strategy meeting.

"I was told by someone who was in those meetings," the source said. "This is how GamerLegion worked. You found out everything, one way or another, because everybody was unhappy."

In addition to unaddressed workplace issues, employees and freelancers faced late payments.

"Financially, it was draining because I wouldn't really get my money reliably, which is very annoying," a former employee said.

As with the Counter-Strike team, payments were often made selectively across the company. Managers found themselves constantly chasing higher-ups to ensure everyone (including themselves) received their salaries — an exhausting distraction from their day-to-day work.

"When it comes to the late payments, my perception is that it was on purpose," one source said. "The lack of transparency was, like, 'The longer I don't reply, the more time I buy for myself.

"I think not getting paid on time is always a negative thing. But not being communicated about it is the worst.

"There were a few people who got paid really quickly, especially the ones who didn't earn much. The CEO tried to sell it as goodwill, in the sense of, 'I know they don't have much money, so I give them money.'

"Yes, good that poor people get their money, but it was also very ironic by you not getting paid."

Another source added: "I tried to keep it amicable. I tried to give people the benefit of the doubt. But when it happens ten months in a row and I'm having this conversation every single month, it just becomes tiresome. It's awful."

Asked about the delayed payments to staff and freelancers reported by several sources, Reber provided the following comment:

"This statement is inaccurate. Salary payments were made regularly on the contractual payment date. The same applies to freelancers if they have performed their services without defect. Isolated discussions with a single employee regarding the contractual payment date do not constitute evidence of systematic delays."

The Paris Major run was supposed to be a watershed moment for GamerLegion, prompting a hiring spree as the organization sought to capitalize on its newfound momentum. It hoped to diversify its revenue streams and build other esports teams capable of matching the success of the Counter-Strike division.

GamerLegion's team has not been able to reach the same heights it did in 2023

GamerLegion's hierarchy expected that reaching the Major final would bring in a horde of new fans, so they put in a massive merchandise order. The bulk of it remains unsold on the company's website.

"That wasn't the greatest idea," one former employee said. "I think the idea was, 'We made it to the Major final. We need to be big. Everyone is going to run in our door,' which is obviously a ridiculous thought. A lot of delusions."

"It was not the first time it had happened," another former staffer said. "Lots and lots of old merchandise, which was very ugly, got thrown in the bin, not recycled, because nobody wanted that and it was not desirable.

"Some people took some home as safekeeping, but there were so many boxes' worth of stuff. Then obviously, we had the new merchandise that was brought in, hundreds of boxes. The margins on it were good, but there was no interest."

"In the end, we burned a lot of money there," a third source said.

Nearly three years on from Paris, not much has changed about GamerLegion. The company has closed its office in central Berlin and reduced its workforce across multiple departments, according to sources familiar with the situation. Meanwhile, it currently fields only two esports teams following the departure of the Age of Empires roster.

Even the Counter-Strike team, the crown jewel of the organization, hasn't come near to replicating the Paris performance. In 2025, it missed the Austin Major — putting more financial pressure on the organization —, while it exited the Budapest Major in Stage 1 with a 1-3 record.

According to two sources close to the Counter-Strike team, late payments have become less frequent in the last 12 months, and a source close to the Dota 2 team told HLTV that salaries have been paid on time.

Among staff, the origin of GamerLegion's funding has long been a topic of speculation.

"No one really knows where the money comes from," one source said.

Two sources said they had on multiple occasions feared for GamerLegion's future, believing that the company's fate was tied to the success of the Counter-Strike team. One referred to the last-minute invite to PGL Major Copenhagen — after 9 Pandas were forced to withdraw due to visa issues — as "the miracle" because of the unexpected cash injection from the event's digital items after the team had stumbled in the Europe RMR.

That uncertainty has also fueled speculation about the nature of GamerLegion's relationship with their longest-standing partner, Re-Bo, an industrial saw blade manufacturer owned by Nicolas' father. The ties between the two companies have frequently been a topic of internal conversation.

Several sources described constant frustration over the absence of medium and long-term planning, with one recalling being reprimanded simply for asking about the company's strategy for the coming year. They also recounted that Reber often seemed detached from the company's day-to-day operations; he was seldom present at the weekly all-hands online meetings and barely used Slack, the company's main communication channel. Reaching him required going through WhatsApp, email, or Discord.

"Most of the time, it felt like Michael was running GamerLegion," one source argued. "He [Reber] doesn't know what's going on in the business; he really doesn't care about his staff," said another.

"There was like a short-term plan," a third source noted. "But it changed all the time, from year to year, sometimes every few months. There was no real direction."

A recurring theme among those HLTV spoke with for this article is that the company is destined to struggle unless significant changes are made at the leadership level.

One source said: "Even if they get new money, it's going to get burned at some point because it's just the wrong people. You can do whatever you want. You can hire and fire and change. There is a core of two, maybe three people, who are just in the wrong place.

"And, unfortunately, it's the ones that run the show."
