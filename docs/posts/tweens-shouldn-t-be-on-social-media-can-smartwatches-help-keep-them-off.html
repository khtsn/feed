Tweens Shouldn’t Be on Social Media. Can Smartwatches Help Keep Them Off?

On Tuesday, Surgeon General Vivek Murthy released a public advisory about the hazardous potential of social media on youth mental health. I was pleased to see that it’s a balanced summary of what we know and a clear explanation of the gaps in the evidence and not just a typical anti-social-media screed. The report explains that social media has some benefits, like social connection, particularly for marginalized groups, but the risks may outweigh the rewards, depending on the individual kid and propensities toward maladies like depression, anxiety, neurosis and eating disorders.

I’ve long been convinced by the data, which the surgeon general included, that there are certain ages when kids are especially vulnerable to negative outcomes from social media use. As his public advisory notes, “Adolescent social media use is predictive of a subsequent decrease in life satisfaction for certain developmental stages including for girls 11 to 13 years old and boys 14 to 15 years old.” Despite the fact that the most prominent social media platforms have rules prohibiting kids under 13 from creating accounts, “38 percent of tweens have used social media (up from 31 percent in 2019), and nearly one in five (18 percent) now say they use social media ‘every day,’” according to a 2021 report from Common Sense Media.

Because I’ve been aware of this information for a while, I want to keep my daughters, who are 10 and 6, off social media until high school, if I can help it. (Yes, I can hear the collective “Good luck” and feel the eye roll from all of you middle school parents out there. And while Dr. Murthy calls on tech companies to create safer environments for kids and for politicians to strengthen protections, I’m not optimistic this is happening any time soon.) Part of my strategy is that despite my fifth grader’s frequent entreaties, my husband and I have refused to buy her a smartphone.

Instead, for her most recent birthday, we got her a smartwatch, which allows her to text us if she plans to go to a friend’s house after school and lets us reach her in an emergency. It also allows her to be on group chats with her friends, so she’s not a total pariah. I see little harm in a daily flurry of kitten GIFs. Crucially, though, she can’t download apps, including ones for social media, without our permission.

I’m not the only parent using this tactic. According to reporting from September by my newsroom colleagues Kalley Huang and Brian X. Chen, more parents are buying smartwatches as “a stopgap cellphone for the kids. With the watch’s cellular abilities, parents can use it to reach and track their children, while the miniature screens mitigate issues like internet addiction.” (While it’s possible to get social media on a smartwatch, it can be more of a hassle.)

Another option that parents are using to delay their children getting a smartphone is a dumbphone, the colloquial and potentially insulting name for a more retro mobile phone that has texting and calling capabilities but not much else. Some of them look kind of like smartphones, so it might be slightly less embarrassing for your kid to take them out after school when seemingly everyone else has an iPhone (though it will probably still be embarrassing).

I asked Jacqueline Nesi, an assistant professor at Brown who studies the role of social media in adolescents’ mental health and development, whether there’s been any research on kids and smartwatch or retro phone usage. Does it help keep them off social media longer? Does it improve longer-term mental health if we’re simply delaying the inevitable social media brain worms that her dad and I have?

Nesi, who writes a newsletter called Techno Sapiens, said this in an email:

I’m not aware of any research specifically on dumbphones/smartwatches as an alternative to smartphones. I think we do have some evidence that holding off on social media makes sense (e.g., this study, summarized here, showing that social media has a more negative effect on kids’ well-being at younger ages, and some cross-sectional studies showing younger social media users encounter more online risks, like harassment, and get less sleep). Dumbphones/smartwatches are one very logical way to accomplish that. When it comes to holding off on smartphones, specifically, we have some evidence pointing us in that direction (like the study I wrote about this week), but nothing definitive.

The surgeon general’s public advisory also notes that one of the gaps in the research is the mode of social media access; it doesn’t tell us if getting it through a smartphone is worse than getting it through a browser on a desktop computer, for example. Still, knowing there’s at least a possibility it will help my kids grow into the confident teens I hope they’ll be, I’m really trying to wait until eighth grade to get my daughter a smartphone, even though when I related this to my child-free editor, he said, “Really? You think you can hold out that long?” There’s a pledge some parents have signed as part of a movement called Wait Until 8th, and I’m hoping that there will be strength in numbers.

In the meantime, I told my fifth grader that if she wants an iPhone, which many of her friends are apparently getting for their impending elementary school graduation, she needs to convince me that she needs one, in the form of a persuasive essay — one that shows some age-appropriate understanding of how fortunate she is to have a smartwatch in the first place. So far, she’s got nothing beyond “So-and-so got one,” which isn’t very persuasive. If she doesn’t come up with anything better than that, I don’t think the three-year embargo will be that hard.
