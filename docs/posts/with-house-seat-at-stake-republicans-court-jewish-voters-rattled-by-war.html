To fill George Santos’s seat, Republicans nominated Mazi Pilip, an Israeli military veteran, in the Feb. 13 special election in New York.

Nicholas Fandos

Reporting from the campaign trail in Plainview, N.Y., and across Long Island.

Feb. 3, 2024, 3:00 a.m. ET

On the campaign trail, Mazi Pilip wears a Star of David around her neck. She speaks with a thick Ethiopian-Israeli accent. And when it comes to the Israel-Hamas war, Ms. Pilip, the Republican nominee in a special House election in New York, adds no political polish.

“Oct. 7 changed me forever,” she said the other day, her voice cracking, at a vigil for a local man who was taken hostage by Hamas. Ms. Pilip, an Israeli military veteran, called him “my brother.”

“The vicious attack of Hamas on my people, on my brothers and sisters, and our Jewish land — a vicious attack — we are never going to forget,” she said, as the packed gymnasium of the suburban Jewish community center nodded along.

Many politicians have rushed to stand by American Jews since the war began. But few have made the conflict so viscerally central to their political identity as Ms. Pilip, 44, a little-known county legislator whose remarkable personal story and unusual overtures to Jews across the political spectrum have propelled her into a dead heat with Tom Suozzi, a former Democratic congressman.

Their Feb. 13 contest will turn on myriad national issues, particularly the crisis at the border. But Ms. Pilip’s approach has also transformed the race into a test of just how thoroughly the conflict and fears over rising anti-Israel sentiment have shaken political alliances for suburban American Jews, long a bedrock of the Democratic coalition.

“Oct. 7 was a seismic event in more than just Israel,” said Lawrence Levy, dean of the Center for Suburban Studies at Long Island’s Hofstra University. “Fairly or not, there’s a perception I hear from Jews, who I know have been loyal Democrats with their time and money, and now wonder if the party is safe for them.”

It is far from clear whether it will be enough to tip the race, called after the expulsion of George Santos from Congress. Mr. Suozzi has undertaken a frenetic campaign to showcase his own support for Israel. He made a surprise stop in Israel and has argued that he — not Ms. Pilip — is the most useful foil to progressives whose criticism of Israel has alarmed more mainstream Democratic Jews.

But across the district in recent weeks, there were signs the debate will not be easily settled. Outside a Pilip event on antisemitism in suburban Great Neck, two Jewish voters had to be separated amid a shouting match over the candidates.

At the Jewish community center about 15 miles east in more liberal Plainview, a man attending the hostage vigil confided he was hiding his support of Mr. Suozzi from his wife, newly a “one-issue voter” for Israel. And rabbis described congregations split down the middle between a familiar ally and the opportunity to elect one of their own.

“We’ve had plenty of Jewish government officials here, but it’s probably fair to say she’s one of the more Jewishly committed candidates that we’ve ever seen,” said Rabbi Steven Conn of the Plainview Jewish Center. But he added that Mr. Suozzi was also well known and liked.

“I don’t think anybody knows right now how it will come out,” he said.

It is little wonder. Stretching from Queens to Nassau County’s affluent suburbs, New York’s Third District is one of the most Jewish in the country; depending on voter turnout, Jews could make up as much as 20 percent of the electorate. Jewish voters here have long tended to be more moderate than their urban counterparts, as uncomfortable with the far left as the far right.

In reality, there is little difference between the candidates’ policy positions on Israel. Both oppose placing conditions on American military aid to Israel, which lost 1,200 lives to Hamas’s attack on Oct. 7, according to Israeli authorities. Both oppose a cease-fire in Gaza, where 25,000 people have died in Israeli bombing campaigns since, Gazan health officials say.

But there are signs, first picked up in local elections last November, that voters are looking beyond policy papers.

“My race last year was kind of a shot across the bow,” said Jon Kaiman, a Democrat who lost a bid for town supervisor in North Hempstead, normally friendly territory.

Mr. Kaiman said he saw several shifts at once. Voter turnout surged in Great Neck, Ms. Pilip’s hometown, where Iranian and Orthodox Jews have voted reliably Republican in recent years. At the same time, he began hearing from Jewish voters who typically side with Democrats but now seemed less interested in his views, or President Biden’s support for Israel.

Their concern was with the growing voices on the party’s left flank — especially from the group of progressive House members known as the squad — demanding a cease-fire.

“There was almost an anger with the Democratic Party,” Mr. Kaiman said. “We constantly hear, what are you doing about the squad?”

Enter Ms. Pilip.

Even though the Nassau County legislator had little political or policy experience, Republican leaders chose her as their nominee in large part because of her unique potential to accelerate that trend among some Jewish voters to back Republicans.

She speaks in compelling personal terms about antisemitism and the fragility of the Jewish state. Ms. Pilip was 12 when Ethiopia slipped into unrest and the United States and Israel raced to airlift thousands of Jews to the Middle East, including her family.

At 18, she signed up for the Israeli Defense Forces. She was stationed at Beit Lid, just years after a deadly bombing by the Palestinian Islamic Jihad. (Ms. Pilip became a dual American-Israeli citizen in 2009, after marrying a Ukrainian-born Jewish American.)

For some Jewish supporters, the appeal is unusually deep.

“She embodies my tradition, she embodies everything that I believe in,” said an Iranian Jewish businessman in Great Neck, who would only agree to be identified by his first name, Dennis.

But Ms. Pilip is also clearly facing her own obstacles reaching across party lines. Several Jewish voters said they were impressed by her passionate stances, but simply could not get past her personal opposition to abortion and refusal to disavow former President Donald J. Trump.

Andrew Laufer, a civil rights lawyer and registered Democrat, said he would vote for Mr. Suozzi, even as he acknowledged that Oct. 7 had “shifted the Overton window to the right for a lot of Democrats.”

“A lot of what I’m hearing and what I’ve felt myself is that the far left of the Democratic Party has just stabbed us in the back,” he said. “That shouldn’t bleed into Tom,” he added, referring to Mr. Suozzi, “but unfortunately I think he may pay a price.”

Ms. Pilip had “great appeal,” he said, but he feared she would be a “Trump sycophant.”

Ms. Pilip has also faced questions about how she has described her military service.

On official biographies on Instagram and X, she calls herself a “former IDF paratrooper,” invoking one of the Israeli military’s most vaunted combat roles. But, as Ms. Pilip herself has acknowledged, she was an armorer maintaining weapons in the paratrooper’s brigade, not a paratrooper or trained parachutist. She did not face combat.

Ms. Pilip’s campaign declined to make her available for an interview. Michael Deery, a Nassau County Republican official, dismissed the distinction as “form over substance,” and argued that the term “paratrooper” was widely used in Israel.

But former Israeli military leaders and scholars who study women in the armed forces said that Ms. Pilip’s use was neither common nor accurate. The Hebrew word for paratrooper, they noted, does not have a feminine form.

“As a gunsmith, she was not a paratrooper,” said Nachman Shai, a former Israeli military spokesman and Labor Party minister.

In an interview, Mr. Suozzi rejected Ms. Pilip’s attempts to lump him with left-leaning Democrats on Israel or any other subject, saying that he was ready to fight that faction of his party.

“The key to long-term success of the U.S.-Israel relationship has been bipartisanship,” he said in an interview. “You need strong, outspoken Democrats, now more than ever.”

While his stance has helped reassure some Jewish voters, it could have a political counter effect, particularly at a time when polls show Democratic voters are increasingly wary of Israel’s tactics.

The district is home to roughly 17,000 Muslim voters, according to the Asian American Institute for Research and Engagement, many of them traditionally Democrats.

“I have spent the last couple of weeks practically begging people to vote,” said Sarah Eltabib, a legal historian in the district. She said she was supporting Mr. Suozzi with “a really, really heavy heart” and had resorted to hoping fellow Muslims were not paying attention to his comments about the war.

Mr. Suozzi has acknowledged the tension coursing through the race. But when he took the mic at the vigil for the hostages in Plainview, he asked Ms. Pilip to come stand next to him to show unity.

“It’s a very, very tough time in our country with everybody so divided,” he said. “We have to find a way to let some of that go and come together.”
