Carol Rosenberg

Reporting from Guantánamo Bay, Cuba

Aug. 2, 2024

For years, defense lawyers in the Guantánamo cases have spoken of reviewing disturbing government photos of the prisoners being held by the C.I.A. in the Bush administration’s secret overseas prison, the black sites. But they were marked classified, and the world was not allowed to see them. Until now.

Lawyers in the Sept. 11 case have released a single photo taken by the C.I.A. of one prisoner, Ammar al-Baluchi, showing his gaunt, malnourished naked body, circa 2004 at an overseas prison.

The lawyers said the photo, which was first published by the Guardian newspaper, emerged through a classification review process at the military commissions, the war court at Guantánamo Bay, Cuba.

While photos have leaked of U.S. troops abusing prisoners in the aftermath of the attacks of Sept. 11, 2001, including from the Army-run Abu Ghraib prison in Iraq in 2004, none have ever emerged from the C.I.A. black sites. In fact, in 2005 the agency’s leadership destroyed videotapes of interrogations at a black site in Thailand to make sure they were never seen.

This is the kind of material defense lawyers have long sought to present to a judge or jury as evidence of outrageous government conduct, to avert a death penalty or have a war crimes case dismissed.

Hundreds of such photos are believed to exist. Prisoners have said they were routinely stripped of their clothing and photographed as they were moved through the black site program, from one secret prison to another. Defense lawyers for at least eight Guantánamo prisoners have said they were given classified photos of their clients in C.I.A. custody to prepare their cases for trial.

Daniel J. Jones, the lead investigator in the damning 2014 Senate Intelligence Committee study of the C.I.A. program, said he and his team reviewed hundreds of such photos, some of which were included in the full 6,000-page, still-classified report.

Mr. Jones described the photos as “highly disturbing and clear evidence of the brutality” at the black sites. He called them among “the most highly protected records of the C.I.A.’s torture program.”

Mr. Baluchi has so far declined to join a plea agreement by three of his co-defendants — and his case goes forward, so far, to a death-penalty trial. He is accused of helping some of the men who hijacked aircraft on Sept. 11 with travel arrangements and money transfers into the United States at the request of his uncle and the man accused of masterminding the plan, Khalid Shaikh Mohammed.

Mr. Baluchi, who was born in Kuwait, was working in the United Arab Emirates as a software engineer at that time. He was captured in Pakistan in 2003 with another accused conspirator, Walid bin Attash.

Mr. Baluchi’s lawyer, James. G. Connell III, said this week that plea negotiations were continuing in the case of his client, who is seeking treatment at Guantánamo for the torture he suffered in C.I.A. custody, including a traumatic brain injury.

In 2019, defense lawyers for Mr. Baluchi put the image on Page 7 of a filing that asked a military judge to exclude all of the prisoner’s self-incriminating statements because of his “torture and cruel and inhuman and degrading treatment” in C.I.A. custody from 2003 to 2006. They then submitted the 202-page document for a “classification review,” a process at the court of delineating what information is still secret and what can be made public.

To their surprise, when the document was released from the review recently, much of the legal argument was blacked out, but not the photo. The photo emerged exactly as the lawyers had submitted it — with Mr. Baluchi’s genital area covered by a black box “to preserve his dignity,” Mr. Connell said.

The court censors declassified the photo, just after this paragraph: “The prosecution seeks the blessing of this military commission to use statements wrung from Ammar al-Baluchi though physical and mental torture in secret custody by organizations possessed of unrestrained power. This motion fundamentally presents the question: Does America actually have that kind of government?”

The U.S. government removed the metadata from C.I.A. photos before turning them over to defense lawyers as possible evidence in the Guantánamo cases. But defense lawyers believe, based on a secret chronology of his odyssey through six black sites, the photo of Mr. Baluchi was taken in 2004, during his second year of detention.

By then, according to disclosures during years of pretrial hearings, he had been routinely kept naked and beaten. He was also deprived of sleep for days at a time. This was accomplished by shackling him at the ankles and wrists in a way that forced him to stand, naked with a hood on his head.

One day he was repeatedly slapped and his head slammed into a wall by a series of C.I.A. employees training to be interrogators. Some days he received no food. Some days he was fed cans of a liquid nutritional supplement as part of approved “dietary manipulation.” After a year in U.S. custody, the 5-foot-11-inch man weighed 119 pounds, down from 141 when he was captured.

One military member of Mr. Baluchi’s team was so affected by the photos and other evidence of the prisoner’s mistreatment that he was treated for post-traumatic stress disorder, Mr. Connell said.

Rick Kammen, a lawyer, recalled his horror at seeing photos taken by the C.I.A. of his former client, Abd al-Rahim al-Nashiri, who is accused of plotting the bombing of the U.S.S. Cole off the coast of Yemen in 2000.

“It was genuinely shocking,” Mr. Kammen said. “This was not Nashiri as we knew him at Guantánamo. He looked like someone who had been horribly abused” and had “a demented look about him.”

“Part of it was the weight loss,” he said. “Part of it was, like, he looked terrorized.”

The Defense Department strictly limits photography at Guantánamo Bay. The U.S. military and intelligence agencies only permit the release of photographs of the prisoners taken by representatives of the International Red Cross, which are sent to their families. Families, in turn, provide them to the detainees’ lawyers, who sometimes release them to the news media.

In 2022, The New York Times, through the Freedom of Information Act, obtained a once-secret collection of military photos of the first prisoners arriving at Guantánamo Bay.
