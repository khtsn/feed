At First, He Was in His Head, but Her ‘Gut Instinct’ Prevailed

VoWS

Yuna Yang and Christopher Seck became good friends before she proposed becoming something more. He took three months to consider.

The soccer World Cup final in December 2022 ended with a nail-biting win for Argentina. For Yuna Yang and Christopher Mun-Yin Seck, the day was exciting for other reasons, too: After watching the match at a private club, the two spent hours together in New York, where they both live, and by the end of the day, realized that their friendship had romantic potential.

“I thought that was quite a lengthy conversation, from 9 a.m. to almost 9 p.m.,” Mr. Seck said.

Ms. Yang, for her part, decided then and there that she wanted to try being more than friends. “I make decisions, 90 percent based on gut instinct,” she said. “Life only happens once. Thinking is good, but when you feel something and you want to do it, you have to just jump.”

But Mr. Seck was more hesitant. “I was very cautious,” he said. “I felt if I try to do more, it’s very risky. I could lose her as a friend.”

For nearly three months, she waited while he deliberated. Then, on March 9, 2023, Mr. Seck’s birthday, Ms. Yang invited him to Oiji Mi, a Korean restaurant on West 19th Street. After dinner, they went to Mr. Seck’s apartment and she told him to make a choice.

“I asked for three more days,” Mr. Seck said. She refused. He deliberated for another hour or so, then made the call. He was in.

“From that point on, we were on a very serious track,” he said.

In July 2023, when Mr. Seck’s parents came to visit from Singapore, he introduced them to Ms. Yang. Mr. Seck, 38, was born in Philadelphia and lived in New York and New Jersey as a child. When he was 5 years old, his family moved to Singapore, where his parents are from.

After completing mandatory military service in Singapore at 21, Mr. Seck moved to California to attend Stanford, from where he graduated with a bachelor’s degree in economics. He went on to Harvard Law School, from which he graduated in 2013, magna cum laude. Today, he works as a litigator at the New York office of Quinn Emanuel Urquhart & Sullivan, LLP.

In August 2023, Mr. Seck flew to Korea to meet Ms. Yang’s parents, her brother, her sister and some of her friends. Ms. Yang, 45, was born in Seoul and lived there until she was 21. After graduating from Ewha Womans University in Seoul with a bachelor’s degree in fine arts, she moved to Milan in 2001 to challenge herself by living in a different culture.

She decided to work in fashion and completed a one-year fashion design course at the Istituto Marangoni in Milan in 2002. She went on to receive a second bachelor’s degree, this time in womenswear fashion design from Central Saint Martins College at the University of the Arts London. In 2009, she founded her eponymous clothing company.

She and Mr. Seck initially met in May 2022, through a mutual friend, Kyunghee Pyun, a professor at the Fashion Institute of Technology, who invited them both to brunch at New Wonjo, a Korean BBQ restaurant on West 32nd Street.

Ms. Yang immediately noticed Mr. Seck’s style. “He was wearing a suit and tie because he had come from church,” Ms. Yang said. “Not that many people dress that way.” She thought he had sweet features, and when he told her he worked as a litigation lawyer, Ms. Yang was surprised. “I thought, baby-faced litigation lawyer, that’s interesting,” she said.

Mr. Seck found Ms. Yang easy to talk to and was intrigued by her work. After brunch, he went home, Googled her and found himself “quite intimidated” after discovering that she “is actually a really big deal,” he said. Ms. Yang’s designs have been worn by Maye Musk, a model and the mother of Elon Musk, as well as by the model Kendall Jenner, the singer Carrie Underwood, and Son Ye-jin, the star of the TV show “Crash Landing on You.”

Several months after they started dating, in August 2023, Mr. Seck visited Korea with Ms. Yang and had three meals with Ms. Yang’s parents; during the last one, he planned to request permission to marry Ms. Yang. But her mother beat him to the punch, asking when they would have a wedding.

“In my parents’ generation in Korea, if you introduce someone to your parents, it means you want to marry,” Ms. Yang said.

Mr. Seck told her parents that there would be “no unnecessary delay.”

Soon after, he began trying to pin down a wedding date, and placed an order for an engagement ring. Though he never officially proposed, in early fall, he and Ms. Seck attended a couple of premarital counseling sessions with the Rev. Thomas Evans, a senior minister at the Brick Presbyterian Church, of which they are members.

“He sat down with us to ask the big questions,” Mr. Seck said. “How do you define a successful marriage? What’s the difference between someone you love and someone you marry?”

Mr. Seck decided that a successful marriage should have three dimensions: longevity, depth of knowledge about each other and harmony that is visible to others. Ms. Yang’s answer was more figurative. “Having someone next to you, no matter what happens,” she said. “Almost like a tree, a root digging inside the earth.”

They were legally married by Madeline Plasencia, a city clerk, on Dec. 12 at the New York City Marriage Bureau, with Ms. Yang’s best friend, Cate H. Bae as their witness.

On Jan. 6, the couple celebrated their union by inviting 120 guests from all over the world — Taiwan, China, Singapore, Switzerland, Germany, Japan, and more — to attend three different ceremonies that reflected their particular mix of cultural identities.

“I lived in several different countries, and I have friends from all different cultures and backgrounds,” Ms. Yang said. “But I still want to remember and respect my own culture. I wasn’t afraid to say very loudly at our wedding, ‘This is who we are.’”

The first event was a Western religious ceremony at the Brick Presbyterian Church, officiated by the Rev. Evans, during which the bride and groom exchanged rings, and several hymns were sung by the choir, including “A Mighty Fortress is Our God” and “Joyful, Joyful, We Adore Thee.”

Afterward, the guests moved to a private club in New York, where a buffet lunch with salmon, chicken and sandwiches, along with brownies and cookies for dessert, was served, followed by a Chinese tea ceremony. During the ceremony, Ms. Yang and Mr. Seck served jasmine tea to their parents. Ms. Yang wore a red, high-collared, fitted qipao and Mr. Seck wore a blue Tang suit, with a mandarin collar and frog buttons.

The third ceremony was a Korean pyebaek, during which the couple bowed to the floor, poured wine for their parents, and served each other tea. For this, Ms. Yang changed into the traditional Korean bridal gown with long billowing sleeves, called hwarot, and a heavy headpiece. At one point, the parents tossed chestnuts and dates at Mr. Seck and Ms. Yang, and the couple tried to catch them in one of the strips of fabric that made up her outfit.

“If we catch many, it means we’ll have many children,” Ms. Yang said. Each chestnut represents one boy, and each date represents one girl.

“Chris’s parents were having so much fun, they kept throwing,” Ms. Yang said.

“By that count, we will have 40 kids,” Mr. Seck said.

Afterward, Ms. Yang hopped onto Mr. Seck’s back, and he carried her around in circles, an act that symbolizes how reliable he will be as a partner and how long their marriage will last.

“I lift weights three times a week and I exercise almost every day, so I managed to carry her for 5 to 10 minutes,” Mr. Seck said.

Ms. Yang had to show athleticism as well. “I had all of these big clothes on,” she said of the hop. “I was trying to jump very high because I heard some brides fall down.” But after a few minutes, she had to ask Mr. Seck to put her down. “He was doing an amazing job,” she said. “But it was time.”

Sentimental Tune During the luncheon, Ms. Yang sang “Moon River,” first performed by Audrey Hepburn in the film “Breakfast at Tiffany’s,” as an ode to her relationship with Mr. Seck. The lyrics — “two drifters, off to see the world” — reminded her of herself and Mr. Seck, who both left their home countries to live abroad. Ms. Yang thought her wedding was an opportune time to perform. “Even if I sing very badly, no one will criticize me,” she said.

A Good Date During the Korean ceremony, Ms. Yang and Mr. Seck bit into one date from two sides at the same time. According to custom, the person who ended up with the seed would be the breadwinner. “Chris was so competitive,” Ms. Yang said. Mr. Seck declared innocence, saying he “got the seed by accident.”

Her Own Design For the church ceremony, Ms. Yang designed a silk charmeuse wedding dress, with a waistband, draping shaped like “falling water,” and 80 buttons along the sleeves and the back. She said she wanted something subtly decorative with a clean and simple cut that would not be too heavy. “I’m not impressed by corsets and structurally heavy dresses,” she said. “I think women should wear something beautiful but easy to move in.”
