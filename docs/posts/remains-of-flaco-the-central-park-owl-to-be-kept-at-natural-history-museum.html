Remains of Flaco, the Central Park Owl, to Be Kept at Natural History Museum

New York|Flaco the Owl’s Wings to Be Kept at a Museum in His Neighborhood

https://www.nytimes.com/2024/05/28/nyregion/flaco-owl-natural-history-museum.html

Remains from the Eurasian eagle-owl, whose year on the loose captivated New York City, will become part of collections at the American Museum of Natural History.

May 28, 2024Updated 2:22 p.m. ET

Partial remains of Flaco, the Eurasian eagle-owl whose escape from the Central Park Zoo and year on the loose enthralled New York City before his death in February, will be kept at a museum near where he spent most of his life, zoo officials said on Tuesday.

Flaco’s wings and tissue samples have been transferred to the American Museum of Natural History, where they will become part of the scientific collections, according to a statement from the Wildlife Conservation Society, which operates the Central Park Zoo.

The collections are “used extensively by scientists and also by artists who develop images for educational materials, including birding field guides,” the society said in the statement, which noted that the remains would not be on public view.

A spokeswoman for the museum declined to comment.

The tissue samples will be kept in the museum’s frozen tissue specimen collection, the society said. The rest of Flaco’s remains have been archived at the Bronx Zoo’s Wildlife Health Center.

The society announced in March that a necropsy by Bronx Zoo pathologists had found enough rat poison and pigeon virus in Flaco’s system to kill him even if he had not died after apparently striking an Upper West Side building.

Initial necropsy results released the day after Flaco’s Feb. 23 death suggested that he had sustained an acute traumatic injury to his body, with signs of substantial hemorrhage under his sternum and in his back near his liver.

The findings validated widespread concerns about the hazards that Flaco, who would have turned 14 in March, faced living as a free bird in Manhattan for just over a year.

“Flaco’s severe illness and death are ultimately attributed to a combination of factors — infectious disease, toxin exposures and traumatic injuries — that underscore the hazards faced by wild birds, especially in an urban setting,” the society said in a statement at the time.

Flaco, having lived his entire life in captivity, was not a wild bird. But had to learn to live like one starting on Feb. 2, 2023, when someone shredded the mesh on his enclosure at the Central Park Zoo. No one has been arrested; the police have said that their investigation is continuing.

Despite significant questions about whether he could thrive on his own, Flaco taught himself to feast on the park’s abundant supply of rats and quickly became an underdog whom many New Yorkers were rooting for. Bird-watchers, ornithologists and everyday people closely tracked his activities, in person and online.

After society employees abandoned their retrieval efforts, Flaco established a comfortable routine at the park’s north end. Around Halloween, he began to roam around Manhattan, thrilling those whose air-conditioners, balconies and fire escapes he appeared on.

Experts and others tried to temper the excitement over his improbable sojourn. Eurasian eagle-owls can live more than 40 years in captivity, but only 20 on average in the wild. No one could predict whether, or when, urban hazards like rat poison or a building strike or collision with a vehicle might cut his life short.

In the wake of his death, proposed state legislation that would require building owners to take steps to reduce the potential for birds to hit windows was renamed in his honor. A City Council member introduced legislation, also with Flaco in mind, to limit rat poison’s use in the city.

As of Tuesday, about 5,300 people had signed an online petition calling for a life-size statue of Flaco to be placed in Central Park. About 60,000 had signed one urging the police and the conservation society to “relaunch” the investigation into who vandalized his enclosure.

For the last several months of his life, Flaco was a roving fixture on the Upper West Side, ranging from Central Park to Riverside Drive, from the 70s to the 90s, perching on roof tops and water towers and hooting for hours into the darkness.

A part of him will now remain nearby in perpetuity.
