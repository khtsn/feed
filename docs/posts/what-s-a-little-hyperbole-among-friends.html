Gail Collins and Bret Stephens

Ms. Collins and Mr. Stephens are Opinion columnists. They converse every week.

Gail Collins: So Bret, Donald Trump gets a roughly $355 million fine for fibbing about his fortune. Too much? Too little? Am I wrong for starting to chortle?

Bret Stephens: Gail, I would never want to stop you from chortling, but here goes: I think the case was ridiculous and the verdict outrageous. And dumb. It lends credence to the Trumpian belief that the justice system is rigged, which will help him politically.

Gail: OK, chortles desist. But this is the trial where we learned that the 30,000-square-foot Trump Tower triplex its owner listed as an asset was actually about a third that size. That’s a nice example of his biographical … oversell.

Bret: “Biographical oversell.” I’m stealing that.

Gail: I know you’re worried all this will make him look like a martyr to a whole lot of voters. But I can’t help thinking a string of 91 pending felony counts has to also look pretty darned messy.

Bret: Most of those cases will convince only those who are already convinced that Trump must be stopped at all costs. Many others, including anti-Trumpers like me, will wonder whether at least two of these cases — brought by progressive D.A.’s who had promised to go after Trump before they had found a crime to charge him for — would have been brought against any other defendant.

Gail: Bret, you take the fun out of opinion.

Bret: Look, I realize Trump lies about most everything, probably owing to feelings of inadequacy about his … apartment size. And I get that an arcane New York legal statute allows this kind of case to go forward. That said, you have Trump being accused of fraud without any of his creditors complaining of being defrauded, making the case ripe for being overturned on appeal, or at least the amount he has been fined reduced. The result will not only help Trump, it might inspire right-wing state attorneys general or local D.A.s to return the favor in spades to Democratic candidates they don’t like.

Also, since we’re on the subject, any thoughts on whether Fani Willis, the D.A. in Fulton County, Ga., should be removed, or remove herself, from the Trump electoral-fraud case?

Gail: I knew, knew, knew you’d get us to Fani Willis. You were never a fan, even before she wound up in the center of a conflict-of-interest-improper-relationship problem, not to mention the mysterious-money-trail controversy.

Bret: I’d barely heard of Willis until I learned that the prosecutor she hired and has paid more than $650,000 was also her lover. I don’t think there’s much to gain in trying to figure out who paid for what on their joint vacations in Belize or Napa Valley, but it’s in the interest of everyone that she step down from the case.

Gail: And I admit that the Willis mess is casting a shadow over a critical Trump prosecution, centering on the charge that he tried to overturn the 2020 election results.

I don’t think her affair with a prosecutor she hired to work on the case will undermine the overall case. But I sadly agree it’d probably be better if she stepped away.

Bret: My guess is that the case will be postponed, if not dismissed altogether, and I don’t think that’s an entirely bad thing. Democrats seem to be investing nearly all their hopes in landing Trump in jail before he lands in the White House. But the best and probably only way to stop Trump is at the ballot box, not in the courthouse. And to do that, you need … a good, confidence-inspiring candidate.

Tell me: Why are you a fan of Kamala Harris?

Gail: Beg pardon? I remember telling you that if Biden was persuaded to give up running for re-election, she was one of the Democrats who’d have to be in competition for the next nomination. Not get the next nomination.

Bret: I know. I was just trying to put the fun back into opinion.

Gail: You don’t have to be a Kamala fan to feel that the first female vice president shouldn’t be tossed out of consideration. She’s fulfilled all her current duties. True, she didn’t solve the border crisis, but I sincerely doubt the problem there is about her.

Tell me: What is it she’s done during her stint as No. 2 that makes you feel she has to be wiped off the slate of possible contenders?

Bret: Let me count the ways.

First, popularity: She has a 37 percent approval rating, according to the latest 538 poll, which is even lower than Biden’s calamitous 39 percent, in what is a must-win year for Democrats, if not democracy itself. Second, competence: Obviously, Harris isn’t solely or mainly to blame for the border crisis, but can you think of one thing she’s done to help resolve it — other than her laughable claim a couple of years ago that “the border is secure”? Third, political chops: Her 2020 run for the presidency was notable mainly for her inability to figure out what her positions were and for flaming out before the Iowa caucus. Fourth, her office has been notorious for chaotic management and high staff turnover, a bad sign of her executive capacity. Finally, as our colleague David Leonhardt has pointed out, she has a penchant for ridiculous phrases, such as, “It’s time for us to do what we have been doing, and that time is every day.”

Gail: Hey, what better time to do what we have been doing?

Bret: Hehe. By the way, I think that if Biden replaced Harris on the ticket with someone voters could get truly excited about, maybe the president’s frailties would start to look like a plus. Suggestions? People on my list would include the Harvard political philosopher Danielle Allen, the chief executive of J.P. Morgan, Jamie Dimon, the Pennsylvania governor Josh Shapiro and the retired admiral Jim Stavridis.

Gail: The governor of Michigan, Gretchen Whitmer? It wouldn’t have to be a woman, but it would be good.

Bret: Definitely.

Gail: You and I both desperately wish Biden would change his mind about running again.

Bret: Amen.

Gail: But I must admit that once in a while he really does step up to bat. Did you hear his short speech attacking Trump’s Russia-NATO policy?

Bret: The one in which Biden called Trump’s comments inviting Putin to attack underspending NATO members as “dumb,” “shameful,” “dangerous” and “un-American”? Right on, Mr. President. Now he should follow it up, seize Russia’s billions of dollars in frozen assets and give them as military and economic aid to Ukraine in memory of the Russian opposition leader Alexei Navalny.

Switching topics, Gail: Your thoughts about Joe Manchin opting against a third-party run?

Gail: Well, third parties drive me crazy in general, as you know. And the dreaded No Labels movement had been courting Manchin to be its nominee. But thank God he’s decided not to run — rightfully citing a fear that he could drain votes from Biden and return Trump to the White House.

Bret: Agree. But I’m kinda hoping that Nikki Haley might run as the No Labels candidate, along with some moderate Democrat like, well, Jamie Dimon, and drain votes from reluctant Trump voters. The danger now is that, with Bobby Kennedy Jr. and Cornel West each running as left-wing independents, there’s no counterbalancing third party (or, rather, fifth party) to drain moderate votes on the right.

Gail, we probably shouldn’t finish this conversation without mentioning the shooting at the Kansas City Chiefs’ victory parade last week.

Gail: Thanks. I know you’re not into sports, but this year’s Super Bowl was a really good game. And it’s always nice to see a city’s residents — all ages, races and even political parties — get together for such a happy community event.

Bret: Don’t tell anyone, but I watched it. Incredible game.

Gail: And then — gunfire. One innocent woman killed and more than 20 people wounded, including 11 children. In a state where, with few exceptions, 19-year-olds can carry concealed weapons without a permit.

You can follow all my thoughts from there.

Bret: The mind-set that allows this is illogical, incomprehensible, insane — and apparently incurable. The most obvious thing any state can do is raise the age of gun ownership to at least 21 if not older, and to impose long jail sentences on anyone possessing or selling weapons to anyone younger. If you can’t buy a beer, you shouldn’t be able to buy a gun. Shouldn’t be a hard call.

But as with so much else in America these days, the extremes rule and compromise is ruled out. Those two facts define American decline.

Gail: You’re right. but let’s try to end on a happier note. Still celebrating last week’s special congressional election in New York, in which the Democrat, Tom Suozzi, won the seat previously occupied by the horrific George Santos. Arguing for a sane, moderate plan to fix the border crisis. Exactly the kind of candidate you’re worried has gone out of fashion.

Bret: Suozzi may be the single best reason to move to Long Island. Sadly, he’s also the only one. See you next week!
