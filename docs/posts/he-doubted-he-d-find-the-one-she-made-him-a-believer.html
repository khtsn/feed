He Doubted He’d Find ‘the One.’ She Made Him a Believer.

Vows

Danni Liu and David Choi, who was diagnosed with muscular dystrophy as a child, quickly embraced the joys and challenges in their relationship together.

On his Hinge dating profile, Yuwoon Choi answered a prompt: “All I ask is that you … be open to dating someone in a wheelchair.”

The prompt, in conjunction with his profession as general counsel at Samsung, caught Danni Liu’s eye when she came across his profile in May 2023. She matched with him and started a conversation, but he responded at snail’s speed. She thought he wasn’t interested.

Four days after they matched, Mr. Choi, 37, asked her out on a date for the following Saturday. (Not the upcoming Saturday.)

“He had me waiting for two weeks, basically,” Ms. Liu, 30, said. “From my experience, if someone really likes you, they want to see you sooner.”

But in his defense, when they connected, he was in a “pessimistic streak,” he said. “Having a disability obviously brings challenges to dating. I felt, maybe I’m just not going to find the one.”

Mr. Choi, who goes by David, was very much interested in Ms. Liu, but he was “playing defensive,” he said. He was hesitant to pour himself into a potential connection because he anticipated that it wouldn’t work out anyway. And he didn’t propose to meet that upcoming Saturday because his assistant was off that day and wouldn’t be able to drive him to the date.

On the morning of their first date, Mr. Choi explained to Ms. Liu that he has muscular dystrophy, a disability that weakens muscle strength. When he was 6, he started falling a lot. At age 14, he began using a wheelchair.

Ms. Liu went to Google to learn more about muscular dystrophy, and she found out that it was also likely to shorten his life span. But that didn’t dissuade her.

When Ms. Liu was 16, her father died in a car accident. As an only child, she and her mother had leaned on each other for support ever since. “I know that even though he’s not around, it doesn’t change the fact that we had a good time when he was alive,” she said.

Ms. Liu and Mr. Choi met for lunch at Hi-Collar, a Japanese restaurant in Manhattan’s East Village. He showed up early, and when she arrived, she was “a little shocked,” she said. “I think I only pictured him in a manual wheelchair, and then I saw his power chair.”

They ordered pasta and fried rice for the two of them to share. But when the food came, he said he wasn’t hungry. He couldn’t reach the communal dishes, and he didn’t want to ask her for help.

“I’ve never wanted to take a girl aback by the type of needs I have or the challenges I have on the first date,” Mr. Choi said. “People have different comfort levels with interacting with someone with a disability.”

While Mr. Choi has a calm demeanor, Ms. Liu, who works with toddlers with autism and developmental delays as an applied behavior analysis therapist, is more bubbly. “It’s pretty easy for me to start a conversation with anyone, to ask them questions,” she said.

Indeed, they were having a great conversation. The two talked about the churches they attend and the countries they’ve visited. They talked about where they grew up: He was born in Seoul, grew up in Singapore and lived in Edgewater, N.J.; she was born and raised in Beijing and then moved to Manhattan for graduate school at Columbia.

An hour and a half into their first date, the restaurant was closing, but they still had a lot to discuss. She suggested they go to a cafe. They tried to go to a few cafes nearby, but they all had a step or two leading to the entrance, which made them inaccessible for Mr. Choi. Finally, they ended up at a restaurant in the East Village for drinks.

They asked for a table, but the hostess said that if they wanted drinks, they could only sit at the bar. “Can you make an exception for us?” Mr. Choi asked, after which the hostess led them to a table.

Ms. Liu had noticed that at Hi-Collar, Mr. Choi drank his water with a straw. So she asked the waiter for a straw and put it in his glass.

“Her asking that showed me she was paying attention to me and to my needs, and she wasn’t awkward about it,” Mr. Choi said, adding that he tends to feel self-conscious on dates. That gesture made him feel more comfortable and relaxed around her.

And Ms. Liu was feeling attracted to him, with the physical proximity of sitting next to him as opposed to across from each other. The conversation was flowing even more naturally. They even started being silly with each other.

Then, Ms. Liu had to leave. She was running late to dinner with a friend. They said their goodbyes, and that they wanted to see each other again.

Three days later, Mr. Choi attended an event at the New-York Historical Society, near her Upper West Side apartment. He texted her that he was in her neighborhood, so she told him to stop by her place. (Ms. Liu’s mother was staying at her apartment at the time. She can’t speak English, and he can’t speak Mandarin, so they just smiled at each other.)

When Ms. Liu and Mr. Choi had some alone time together, she asked him if she could hold his hand, and he said yes. She was hoping he would kiss her, but he didn’t. After she rode the elevator with him down to the ground level of her apartment building, she asked him if she could hug him. She was a bit sad when, again, he didn’t kiss her. (He just wanted to respect her boundaries, he said.)

On their third date, one week after they met in person, Ms. Liu made the first move and kissed him. They had spent hours at Fornino, a pizza restaurant in Dumbo, Brooklyn, and then wandered around at a nearby park, sat down and soaked in the view of the Statue of Liberty. It was here that Mr. Choi told her that he really likes her, and that he would love to date her.

She agreed to be his girlfriend on their fifth date, and on the sixth date, she met his parents.

Over the months to come, Ms. Liu got to know his parents well. “They’re also first-generation immigrants like me — we’re on the same wavelength,” Ms. Liu said. She was surprised by how similar their cultures were. She also grew up listening to K-pop and learned some Korean, which she was able to practice with his parents.

In July, Mr. Choi’s parents invited Ms. Liu’s mother to their home in Harrington Park, N.J., for dinner. Mr. Choi and Ms. Liu were both a bit nervous, in part because of the language barrier. But Ms. Liu was the translator, and everyone was eating, laughing and having a great time.

“I remember sitting back at certain moments and just kind of observing, taking it all in,” Mr. Choi said, “and being like, ‘This could really work.’” Mr. Choi’s father started studying Mandarin every day to be able to communicate with Ms. Liu’s mother.

They both grew fond of each other’s parents. For Mr. Choi, her mother’s love for him was “healing,” he said. And Ms. Liu loved being pampered by his parents, who made her food and treated her to dinners.

Through trips to Broadway shows, movies, jazz clubs and museums, the couple got to know each other better and fell deeply in love. Three months after their first date, she sent his father a birthday card that read: “Don’t you worry about David. I’ll take care of him.”

On Dec. 15, Mr. Choi proposed at the High Line. Ms. Liu’s two friends took her and her mother to the elevated park for a walk, where he surprised her with a diamond ring in his hand. They then had dinner with family and friends at Eat Offbeat, a restaurant in Chelsea Market.

Ms. Liu, who works at the social services organization CARES, graduated from Jinan University in Guangzhou, China, with a bachelor’s degree in teaching Chinese as a second language. She has a master of social work degree from Columbia and a master of special education from Arizona State University.

Mr. Choi is general counsel at Samsung SDS America, which provides cloud and digital logistics services. He has a bachelor’s degree in political science from N.Y.U. and a law degree from Columbia Law School.

On Jan. 2, the couple were married at the Manhattan Marriage Bureau, accompanied by Mr. Choi’s parents and assistant and Ms. Liu’s mother. Wanyi Mai, a city clerk, officiated. They then went to Jing Fong, a dim sum restaurant in Chinatown.

After their wedding, the couple moved into an apartment together in Jersey City, N.J.

“I can make sure that he exercises every day and eats healthy,” Ms. Liu said.

Has he been eating healthy? “He ate Cheetos today,” she said. “I have to pick my battles.”

Mr. Choi has also been a vital support system for Ms. Liu, she said: “He encourages me when I feel discouraged and frustrated. He comforts me when I am anxious. He celebrates my joy and recognizes my successes and acts of kindness.”

She’s determined to help him with his health through physical and occupational therapy and acupuncture. And Mr. Choi has been receptive to her encouragement. Before meeting her, he had given up on such efforts: “I didn’t really see the point,” he said.

“Meeting Danni has definitely just changed the meaning and purpose of life for me,” Mr. Choi said. “She’s given me motivation — I do have a family, and we do plan to have kids. She’s given me something to fight for.”
