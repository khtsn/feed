Move Over Hot Chicken, There’s a Hotter Chicken in Town

To be fair, Panda Express never pretended that Sichuan hot chicken was a Sichuan dish. In posts online, the company described it as “an authentic Chinese twist on an American comfort classic” — a homage to Nashville hot chicken, whose origins go back to the city’s segregated Black neighborhoods of the 1930s. (It was purportedly first concocted as a blistering punishment for a philandering lover, as the historian Rachel Louise Martin recounts in “Hot, Hot Chicken: A Nashville Story.”) This combination proved so popular that other food outlets soon took up the idea, with Sichuan hot chicken popping up from Columbia, S.C., to Wenatchee, Wash. “Sichuan” became a shorthand for a vaguely Asian spiciness, achieved via a quick slug of chile oil or sriracha (named after the coastal town in Thailand where it was created). Even McCormick, the global spice company, has a recipe on its website, pairing Sichuan pepper with Frank’s RedHot cayenne sauce.

Food may be hot in Sichuan, but in Chongqing it’s hotter.

In Sichuan, of course, people do eat chicken, often hot. If there is a dish that could properly bear the name Sichuan hot chicken and convey the thrill of málà, the cuisine’s signature alliance of Sichuan pepper and chiles, it would be lazi ji, the food scholar and cookbook author Fuchsia Dunlop tells me. A dish of chicken and chiles, it is a folk specialty of Geleshan in Chongqing, once part of Sichuan Province and now an autonomous municipality. Chongqing is a sweltering city of tormenting hills, scaled by stairs and churning escalators, including the second-longest in Asia, with a change in altitude equivalent to more than 10 stories. To live there is to sweat. Food may be hot in Sichuan, but in Chongqing it’s hotter. (Tempers, too, or so they say.)

The cooks of Geleshan double as butchers, dismantling birds and chopping them into small hunks, leaving the bones in for happy grappling. (Cooking at home, you may prefer boneless chicken thighs.) “The first time you encounter this dish, it appears terrifyingly spicy,” Dunlop writes in her 2003 cookbook, “Land of Plenty.” She’s had it in Geleshan on a tray big enough to speak to satellites, the chiles spilling rim to rim, bright as fresh blood. But you’re not meant to eat them. (Part of the pleasure is hunting for the hidden chicken.) And the chiles are fried only briefly, just long enough to give off their fragrance and lend the cooking oil a little sting. So it’s not overwhelmingly hot, according to Dunlop — although, warning, this is by Chongqing standards.

Dunlop advises staying away from the most powerful chiles, the ones that can induce near hallucinations. Seek out the long, slender, medium-hot variety sold by the bag at Chinese groceries, or anything that measures 15,000 to 30,000 heat units on the Scoville scale, like smoky Mexican chile de arbol. Whatever you do, be careful they don’t blacken in the wok. You want that red, untainted, unextinguished, blazing on the plate. You won’t have to say a word. Everyone will know: This is hot chicken.
