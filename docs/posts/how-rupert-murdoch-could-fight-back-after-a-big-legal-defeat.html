How Rupert Murdoch Could Fight Back After a Big Legal Defeat

Murdoch’s next move

Rupert Murdoch has lost his bid to hand control over his media company to his elder son, Lachlan Murdoch, after he dies. A Nevada commissioner ruled against his attempt to modify the terms of a family trust that would have stripped three of his other children of their voting rights over his empire.

The media mogul, who plans to appeal the ruling, is used to getting his way, The Times’s Edmund Lee writes for DealBook. Now what?

A recap: Under the current arrangement, when Murdoch dies, four of his children — Lachlan, James, Elisabeth and Prudence — will have an equal say in what happens next because they will inherit his voting shares.

But Elisabeth and James have different political leanings from Lachlan and their father, and James has indirectly criticized how Fox News operates. The three siblings contested the attempts to change the trust.

This was perhaps the last maneuver of Murdoch’s seven-decade career. The 93-year-old wanted to give Lachlan control over his news outlets — which include Fox News, The Wall Street Journal, The New York Post and major newspapers and television outlets in Australia and Britain — to maintain their right-leaning slant.

Fox News stars have been instrumental in helping President-elect Donald Trump through his three presidential campaigns.

Murdoch may have feared a corporate coup after his death. But according to the ruling, the three had “disavowed any plan to oust their brother,” and did not have any aim to change the management of Fox News.

The ruling included some notable details:

  • Edmund Gorman Jr., the Nevada commissioner, characterized the plan to change the trust as a “carefully crafted charade” to keep Lachlan in power.

  • An episode of HBO’s “Succession” that dramatized the chaos after the death of a Murdoch-like patriarch spurred a discussion among the children about how to manage their affairs once their father dies. It even prompted Elisabeth’s representative to the trust to write a “‘Succession’ memo,” to avoid what happened on the show. (The show’s creator, incidentally, has said he was directly inspired by Murdoch.)

  • Lachlan instigated the plan to change the trust — named “Project Family Harmony” — in mid-2023. It deemed James the “troublesome beneficiary.”

  • Lachlan, Murdoch and their advisers considered trying to cut out James by severing his “sub-trust” from the larger trust. But Lachlan and Murdoch decided they could maintain tighter control by keeping James’s shares in the family trust.

What could happen if Murdoch loses the appeal? He could sell the rest of his company before he dies. When Murdoch sold the bulk of his empire to Disney in 2019, it surprised many, but helped him largely dodge the expensive streaming wars.

It also underscored an often overlooked aspect of Murdoch: He’s practical and unsentimental, and willing to make big moves when others are skeptical.

Supply chain wars

Nvidia shares fell sharply on Monday after Chinese regulators began an antitrust investigation into the chip maker.

It’s the latest sign that Beijing is preparing to act tougher in the face of continuing pressure from Washington and ahead of a second Trump presidency, which could include more tariffs.

Nvidia is in the crossfire between Washington and Beijing. China said on Monday that it was looking into whether Nvidia violated antimonopoly rules when it acquired Mellanox Technologies, a networking equipment maker, in 2020.

Regulators gave the green light provided that Nvidia maintained supplies to China and didn’t discriminate against Chinese companies.

The chip maker’s dominance in artificial intelligence has put it in the spotlight. Its role in powering A.I. applications has sent the company’s share price soaring.

But the Biden administration has limited its ability to sell its most cutting-edge chips to China, saying that they could be used for military applications. (Such moves had dented Nvidia’s stock price before.)

China is signaling that it’s ready to fight back, especially before President-elect Donald Trump takes office. The Chinese authorities have introduced a series of measures ahead of his inauguration, including:

  • Banning the export of some materials that are crucial to making technologies including semiconductors and fiber optic cables. For the first time, Beijing also extended the ban to other countries that transfer minerals to America after buying them in China.

  • Directing Chinese companies to avoid buying American-made chips.

Could other American companies be squeezed? General Motors, Apple and Starbucks are just a few companies that are finding it harder to do business in China after years of growth and investment.

Tesla also has big manufacturing and sales in China, but Elon Musk’s influence with Trump could help protect his electric vehicle maker.

TikTok: What happens now?

TikTok’s presence in the United States has been under siege for years, but the short-form video app is facing its biggest crisis yet after a court upheld a law that would force a sale or ban of the popular platform by mid-January.

President Biden signed the law in April after it moved through Congress with wide bipartisan support. Lawmakers say that TikTok is a national security risk because it is owned by the Chinese company ByteDance. TikTok argues that a sale is impossible and that a ban would violate the First Amendment rights of Americans.

Here’s what could happen next, The Times’s Sapna Maheshwari writes for DealBook.

The Supreme Court could take up TikTok’s case.

TikTok is hoping for a Hail Mary by way of the Supreme Court or the Trump administration. On Monday, the company asked a federal court to temporarily freeze the law pending review of a planned appeal to the Supreme Court.

There’s no guarantee that the court will take up the case, but experts say it’s likely given the free speech concerns and the app’s 170 million U.S. users.

ByteDance could sell TikTok.

TikTok has named many reasons it can’t be sold, including that China could block a transaction. One of the judges in the ruling was skeptical of that, writing that Congress “need not legislate around another country’s preferences to exercise its own powers constitutionally — much less the preferences of a designated foreign adversary.”

Steven Mnuchin, the former Treasury secretary, and the billionaire Frank McCourt have expressed interest in buying TikTok, though a price tag is expected to be upward of $200 billion.

Trump could get involved.

It’s hard to say how President-elect Donald Trump would fulfill his campaign promise to “save” TikTok. If there is no sale or reprieve, the law would goes into effect on Jan. 19 — the day before he is set to be inaugurated. It’s unlikely that Congress would repeal the law, and Trump is notably hawkish on China, anyway. (He had tried to block the app in the United States in 2020 and force its sale to an American company, though he has since dropped his opposition to the platform.)

Trump could try to help orchestrate a deal so that TikTok and ByteDance meet the letter of the law for a divestment.

A matter of trust for Hershey

Mondelez is said to be trying again to buy Hershey, seeking to create an international giant in the business of snacking. News of the approach, which was first reported by Bloomberg, sent shares in Hershey up as much as 19 percent on Monday.

But there’s one big question hanging over any such deal: a century-old charitable trust that controls Hershey’s voting stock.

Any deal needs the blessing of the Hershey Trust Company, which was set up in 1905 by Milton Hershey, the company’s founder, to look after his chocolate producer’s hometown and, more important, the boarding school for needy children that he had created.

The Hershey Trust controls Hershey’s Class B shares, which have 10 times as much voting power as standard shares. That effectively gives the trust about 80 percent of the company’s voting power.

The trust has a specific mission: to protect the school and other Hershey-related charitable endeavors. Over the years, that has prompted the trust to block potentially transformative deals by Hershey, including:

  • A $12.5 billion takeover offer from the Wm. Wrigley Jr. Company in 2002, representing a 42 percent premium, at the last minute;

  • A potential bid for Cadbury in 2010, which allowed Kraft — the former parent company of Mondelez — to swoop in and buy the British chocolatier;

  • And a $23 billion acquisition bid by Mondelez in 2016.

Pennsylvania law further complicates matters. The state’s attorney general is empowered to ask a court to block any transaction that would be unnecessary “for the future economic viability of the company.”

Have times changed? The trust has been slowly selling down its holdings to diversify its portfolio. But in its annual report, Hershey warned that the trust’s current setup made “it more difficult for a third party to acquire a majority of our outstanding voting stock and thereby may delay or prevent a change in control of the company.”
