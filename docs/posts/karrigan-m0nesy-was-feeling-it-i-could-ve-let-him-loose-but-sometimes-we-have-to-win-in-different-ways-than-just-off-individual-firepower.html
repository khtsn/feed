karrigan: "m0NESY was feeling it, I could've let him loose, but sometimes we have to win in different ways than just off individual firepower"

Finn "⁠karrigan⁠" Andersen has made it to the grand final of his debut event with Falcons, after the team blew away magic in the semi-final.

After downplaying expectations before the event, karrigan was keen to do the same before tomorrow's final against Spirit, saying that they are "not ready" for a BO5 final after just eight or nine days of practice before the event.

"If we win, then we are ahead of schedule in how I want the team to perform," karrigan said.

TeSeS: "Playing with karrigan is such a joy in these close games; he takes you under his wing"

The system is clearly a work in progress. karrigan says that "there's a lot of microing" so far in the playoffs, which is having an impact on his individual form as he focuses on the pieces around him.

But given how hard it has been for Falcons to find titles in the last twelve months, any chance at a title as big as PGL Astana is one the team know they need to take. And karrigan's impact has already been felt; Falcons were known for their brittle nature in playoffs, but defended seven map points against FURIA yesterday.

That resilience will be needed again, with one more match to go for the $512k first prize.

Listen to the interview here.

Congratulations, you're in the grand final. NiKo and a couple of you were trying to tame expectations of winning the event, but right now you're looking pretty good, so just give me your immediate thoughts on making the final after this run.

Yeah, for me it's important we had two completely different games, the quarter-finals and the semi-final today. The quarter-final was very [much a] stress test of the team, of mentality, coming back, some shaky CS, the crowd against us and everything.

Today, there were different things we got tested [with] mentally. The first three rounds, we could feel slightly more frustration, but the last three halves we really came back with kind of the same fire and energy that we needed to finish the game. Because whoever makes it to a semi-final, you can never underestimate them. But let's see; tomorrow is going to be very fun.

I'm talking a lot during rounds and, for sure, my individual level is nowhere near where it has to be

I do have to take you back to yesterday, an insane game. Before the event I asked you if you brought the FaZe magic (karrigan smiles), but it does just seem like it's the karrigan magic now, you know?

I think the community can decide that (laughs). I feel like I am part of these games wherever I go; it was in the first FaZe, in MOUZ, then back to FaZe, and now the first game in the arena [with Falcons] is like that. I just started laughing during the game because I've been there so many times, I know how it is, but I was really proud of the team yesterday because there were so many rounds that could have gone the other way.

I think we had to defend seven match points or something overall, and that's very hard as a new team. Especially with the past performances of Falcons in playoffs, we showcased strong resilience, and that's the takeaway from yesterday. Today, I think we finished the job off, and tomorrow is a new day, a new test, and new experiences.

You talked about leading the team inside and outside the server, and now you've had some experience doing that, especially on stage. What has it actually been like in practice?

It's been very fun. I can feel it when I talk in the huddle before games, everybody is locked in on me, listening, and nobody is just doing something else. It's a good feeling, and I feel like they trust me. In the key moments yesterday, I'm just saying B split, and within two seconds we're doing it, everybody is just following, no hesitation. If that's the way we want to play, we need to have zero hesitation, and we have to change our style on the fly a little more.

For now, we've [had] really good chemistry between all the players, of freedom, NiKo chiming in with some stuff, and me just coming with the finishing of the calls. It's been great to lead these players, and I feel the fire in them when they look at me, and when I look at them, I see some crazy, insane individual dudes.

You've also gotten a lot of praise from all of them so far in all the interviews, on stage and in ours. But you also haven't had a lot of time to iron out some of the kinks and get a lot of that practice in, and NiKo mentioned that in the group stage, you were having a bit of trouble with some of the T sides where people weren't really speaking up as much. What has that process been like for you? How are you adapting even though you haven't had a lot of time yet?

Yeah, I think it's a balance where I need to realize when some players are not speaking up, and I can tell them to speak up, but I don't like to do forced stuff. I'll just change the approach or be more where the area is needed, floating around the map, so I can see with my own eyes. Today, on T side Dust2 to finish this game, I shut a lot of freedom, a lot of individual moves down, just because I wanted to finish as a team. I trust the players, but sometimes when I see how this game could get out of control, the only way [is] if we are losing one-on-one fights, or unlucky fights, so it's a balance that I'll find. In the end, I'm here to lead the team and find a way to win, and sometimes it's me not seeing how we need to approach the rounds.

Today, I chose the approach and followed me gut here, and I made sure everybody was disciplined in some of the rounds to finish the job. So it's a balance; I learned some of my lessons in the group stage, but so far in playoffs, I think we've done a great job on T side, but there's a lot of microing; I'm talking a lot during rounds and for sure my individual level is nowhere near where it has to be. Today I felt like it wasn't a problem, and the other days obviously my form has been a problem, and I acknowledge that. But in the end, the guys know how much I'm talking and microing during the mid-rounds. That will be better in the future for sure.

Comes with implementing the system so early on, yeah.

Yeah, I mean with the system there is freedom, right? And if they don't understand the freedom, I expect them to take a little more initiative in certain areas, and if they don't, I'm talking from the other side of the map, 'Do this pressure.' In the future, that will be better. That is not fully an excuse, but I am doing a lot of work before we came to this tournament on the team, and now I'm rewatching how we're playing the rounds instead of playing DM.

I have responsibilities to make sure the system works, and the coaching staff is really helping me a lot; they're doing a fantastic job, so I know for the future that I'm going to have a lot more support, or give them things they can do for me. But to begin with, I really want to make sure I am in control and responsible for the things we're doing.

With eight or nine days of practice coming into this event, we're not ready for a BO5 final

You mentioned how good m0NESY is, that he is the best AWPer you've played with. He's having a pretty incredible tournament too, so how has it been playing some officials and being on the stage with him? I saw your reaction to one of his flicks this game, you were blown away.

(Laughs) Yeah, I still think I have good reactions, but sometimes I don't understand if he killed [someone], so I have to look up to the right and understand like, 'Okay, Ilya [m0NESY] killed one.' The players are insane, I think Maksim [kyousuke] had an incredible, impactful game yesterday. He had not the best first map today but he came back on the second map, and obviously Ilya has been performing really well. He really strives in this, taking initiative, locking rotations, coming with ideas. Right now, I feel like we have really good chemistry, or I individually have really good chemistry with everyone, when it comes to understanding what they want to do in the game.

Now the balance comes with sometimes shutting [it] down, sometimes letting [them] free. Obviously Ilya was really feeling it today, I could've let him loose, but sometimes we have to win different ways than just on the individual firepower. I think I said sorry to him for saying, 'No, no, no, don't peek Long,' (laughs) and then I was like, that sounded really bad because I said, 'No, I don't want to give away an entry,' but he laughed about it after. But yeah, I think [there's] incredible players in the team.

Last question, tomorrow you're going to play Spirit in the final. Some expectations have been tamed, some of you don't feel like you have to win the trophy yet, and it's a BO5 too (karrigan smiles). So with little time to actually practice and iron out the map pool, it's going to be a hard one, but what are your overall thoughts on that and how the team is going to do?

I'm looking forward to the game, I think it's going to be a fantastic game. Obviously with eight or nine days of practice coming into this event, we're not ready for a BO5 final, right? So there are going to be mistakes, we have to understand that, and there is fatigue as well in a BO5 and we don't have a system. So I maybe need to try and simply some stuff for tomorrow for my own individual level, because that is going to be a key factor for tomorrow as well against a really heavy-firepower team like Spirit.

I'm just looking forward to playing them. Last time they lost to them in the group stage in Rio, and then they wrecked the team in playoffs, so let's just try to improve and win one map at a time, and if we win, then we are ahead of schedule in how I want the team to perform.
