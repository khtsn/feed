The World Mourned Pope Francis

Tributes poured in from around the world today after the Vatican announced that Pope Francis had died at 88. Tearful mourners gathered in St. Peter’s Square and Buenos Aires, his birthplace. Candles were lit in New York, New Delhi, Jerusalem and Nairobi. Catholics offered prayers from churches in London, Seoul and Kyiv.

Francis, according to the Vatican, died in his home after a cerebral stroke. His health had been declining, but he kept busy, against his doctors’ orders, until his final day: Just hours before his death, Francis appeared in his wheelchair to bless the faithful on Easter Sunday. The Vatican said he had asked to be buried in a simple tomb, “without particular decoration” and with only the inscription: “Franciscus.”

Our obituary traces Francis’ extraordinary life: Born Jorge Mario Bergoglio, he rose from modest means in Argentina to become the first Jesuit and Latin American pope. He clashed bitterly with traditionalists, and he used his 12-year pontificate to make the Catholic Church more inclusive. He also opened space for debate, and catalyzed the Catholic right in the U.S.

“Pope Francis was a pope of surprises,” Jason Horowitz, our Rome bureau chief, said. “He defied expectations for the church he led, and the world he cared so much about, but he was also deeply consistent on the issues he cared most about: migrants, the environment and inequality. He made important changes within the church, but on the global stage, I think his voice on those priorities may be most missed.”

Trump said concerns about Hegseth were a ‘waste of time’

President Trump threw his support behind Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth today after The Times reported over the weekend that Hegseth had shared sensitive military information in a second Signal group chat that included his wife and brother. The president characterized concerns about the texts as a “waste of time.”

Hegseth’s office has also been shaken by the sudden firings of three of his top advisers. They were escorted from the Pentagon last week after being accused of leaking sensitive information. Another former official, who left last week, wrote in Politico that the Pentagon is “in disarray” and that Trump should remove Hegseth.

The U.S. asked a judge to break up Google

Lawyers for the Justice Department told a judge in a Washington, D.C., courthouse today that the best way to address Google’s monopoly in internet search was to break up the $1.81 trillion company. The government encouraged the judge to force Google to sell its popular Chrome web browser, while Google’s lawyers are pushing for far narrower remedies.

A peek inside Joan Didion’s mind

After Joan Didion died in 2021, her raw and intimate first-person reflections of her therapy sessions were found lurking in a box near her desk. Tomorrow, those writings will be released in a slim book called “Notes to John” — John Gregory Dunne, Didion’s husband, to whom the notes are nominally addressed.

The pages touch on sensitive subjects like anxiety, trauma and, most of all, Didion’s agony over her daughter’s addiction and mental illness. Some of Didion’s fans have condemned the project as invasive and unethical. Our critic Alexandra Jacobs disagrees. In a review, Alexandra called the new book “slightly sordid and utterly fascinating.”

A hidden masterpiece

Last year, Davide Renzoni, an electrician in Rome, climbed through a small trap door in the Pompeian Hall of the Villa Farnesina, a sumptuous suburban mansion. There, he found a second trap door, behind which were broad frescoes of cherubs floating amid rolling hills and thistledown clouds in a summery blue sky.

Researchers have since determined that the frescoes he happened upon were most likely the works of Carlo Maratta, one of the last masters of Baroque classicism. Apparently executed during the late 17th century, the forgotten works were in pristine condition and had been hidden for nearly two centuries.
