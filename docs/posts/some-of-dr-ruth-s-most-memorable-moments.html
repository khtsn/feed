When Dr. Ruth Westheimer, the sex counselor better known as Dr. Ruth, died on Friday at 96, she was remembered largely for her intelligent, candid and humorous views on taboo subjects.

Her radio show, “Sexually Speaking,” premiered on WYNY in 1980 and provided a springboard for decades of call-in shows, a column in Playgirl magazine, guidebooks on sexuality and even a board game.

The risqué subject matter, which was at its time revolutionary for the talk-show format, could be blunted by her frank responses and maternal tone.

On the “Late Show” with David Letterman in 1982, Dr. Westheimer told the host that she took phone calls on the air. About what? Mr. Letterman asked.

“About 20 phone calls,” she said, before Mr. Letterman steered her toward what he was driving at.

A beat, then a realization.

“About sex!” she said.

Dr. Westheimer’s career was filled with examples of letting the air out of awkward conversations in an attempt to help, guide and understand. Here are some of her most memorable moments over the years.

‘Sexually Speaking’ becomes an unexpected hit.

When Dr. Westheimer’s program, “Sexually Speaking,” a 15-minute segment heard after midnight on Sundays, premiered in 1980, sex wasn’t widely discussed on the airwaves.

But she demonstrated her ease with the subject as she moved from humor to advice, acknowledging a caller’s nerves and adding a joke to give a little “we’ve all been there” assurance.

In one episode, she took a call from a man who was unsure he was prepared for a physical relationship with his partner.

“I’m a virgin,” he said.

“OK, now I understand why you talk so fast,” Dr. Westheimer said, with a grandmother’s smile. First and foremost, she said, use a contraceptive. And then, “take your time.”

Dr. Westheimer gets a bigger stage.

After the success of “Sexually Speaking,” Dr. Westheimer hosted a television program, “The Dr. Ruth Show,” that sometimes showcased celebrities discussing their personal lives.

In one episode in 1986, the comedian Jerry Seinfeld appeared and discussed the recent end of his engagement.

Aren’t there any women now? Dr. Westheimer asked.

“Nobody wants you after you’ve been on this show,” Mr. Seinfeld said. “You know too much. They’re intimidated.”

He added that her programs had changed sex for Americans.

“Don’t you tell me that it’s less good now,” Dr. Westheimer said, a glint in her eye.

“It’s better now,” Mr. Seinfeld said. “Now it’s like a sport. Now people suit up for the game.”

She had her moments in movies.

Dr. Westheimer was so familiar to the public that she landed a role in the French film “One Woman or Two,” starring Gérard Depardieu and Sigourney Weaver, which was released in the United States in 1987.

She played herself in the movie, and though her acting career never fully took off, she lent a sense of charm to her scenes.

“Dr. Ruth will never be mistaken for an actress,” said a review in The New York Times, “but she does have pep.”

She made several more appearances, playing herself in the movie “Forever, Lulu,” starring Alec Baldwin and Debbie Harry, and on episodes of television shows, including “Ally McBeal” and “Melrose Place.” She also appeared in ads for shampoos, cars and condoms.

In 2008, she received a sign on the Bronx Walk of Fame.

In 2019, Dr. Westheimer was the subject of a documentary, “Ask Dr. Ruth,” which tracked her journey from a therapist with a radio show to a celebrity known around the world.

She was the author of dozens of books.

During an appearance on “The Ellen DeGeneres Show” in 2019, Dr. Westheimer discussed her new book, “Sex for Dummies,” which was in its fourth edition and was her 45th book overall.

She published more than two dozen books on sexuality alone, and in 2003 she published a memoir, “Musically Speaking: A Life Through Song,” where she described the music she grew up with as a child in Frankfurt.

She found the joy in life.

At a TEDx talk in 2015, Dr. Westheimer gave a lecture about the joy of life. The talk was imbued with a serious, reflective tone as she discussed ways to cherish pleasure and find happiness.

“Some people are afraid to exhibit joy, because they think that if they exhibit joy that will mean that something bad is going to happen,” she said. “Take every single aspect of life — significant others, falling in love, having sex, anything that comes your way — with both hands and make the best of it.”

Dr. Westheimer could not help but add a little humor to her earnest tone. Stay away from negative people, too, she advised.

“Except if it’s your mother-in-law,” she said. “If it’s the mother-in-law, you have no choice.”

She was tapped to help fight loneliness.

In 2023, Gov. Kathy Hochul of New York named Dr. Westheimer the nation’s first “loneliness ambassador,” an honorary title in which the doctor would “help New Yorkers of all ages address the growing issue of social isolation,” the governor’s office said.

“I am deeply honored and promised the governor that I will work day and night to help New Yorkers feel less lonely!” Dr. Westheimer said at the time.

“I don’t want to be known only as a sex therapist,” Dr. Westheimer said. “I want to be known as a therapist.”
