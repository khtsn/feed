Biden’s trip abroad and challenge at home

President Biden left this afternoon for Japan to attend the annual Group of 7 summit meeting, which begins tomorrow in Hiroshima. Leaders of the major industrial democracies will discuss efforts to keep the global economy stable.

But the major potential threat to that stability this year could come from the U.S. When Biden arrives, the U.S. will be two weeks from a possible debt default, which could push its economy into recession and jolt those of the other countries at the table.

Just before boarding his flight, Biden said that he was confident “America will not default.” His optimistic tone came after the White House sent two aides to negotiate with Republicans to reach a deal to raise the debt limit. Almost simultaneously, House Democrats pushed forward on a complex, long-shot plan to raise the limit by forcing a vote.

G7 leaders will also turn their focus to Ukraine and China. “The real question is whether or not Biden can get a full agreement among the Western allies who will be present at the G7 about what the future of Ukraine and Russia should look like,” our colleague David Sanger told “The Headlines,” The Times’s new podcast. “The other big issue will be how to handle China and the threat of its economic, technological and military rise.”

Biden has canceled the second leg of his trip to Asia to return to Washington for the debt negotiations.

Heat will very likely set records in the next five years

Warming caused by human activity and a climate pattern known as El Niño are expected to cause a sharp rise in temperatures, forecasters at the World Meteorological Organization said.

The record for the hottest year on Earth was set in 2016. There is a 98 percent chance that at least one of the next five years will exceed that, forecasters said, while the average from 2023 to 2027 will almost certainly be the warmest for a five-year period ever recorded. “This will have far-reaching repercussions for health, food security, water management and the environment,” the head of the meteorological organization said.

A new push for F-16s to Ukraine

Britain and the Netherlands have joined a growing number of European allies who are pushing the U.S. to approve of giving Ukraine the American-made F-16 jets.

For months, Ukrainian officials have asked for F-16s, saying the transfer of even those aging planes would help bolster their air defenses. For now at least, the Biden administration still appears to be pumping the brakes, citing concerns about costs, lengthy training time for pilots and the possibility that Russia would view the move as an escalation.

On the front line, Ukrainian forces have in just a few days retaken territory around the city of Bakhmut that it took the Russians many weeks to capture.

Hundreds of drugs are in short supply

Drug shortages are approaching record levels in the U.S. generic drug market, which accounts for about 90 percent of domestic prescriptions. Hospitals are scouring shelves, many cancer patients are facing delays and the federal government is scrambling to figure out how to fix the problem. The American Cancer Society warned that the delays could directly result in worse outcomes for patients.

“This is, in my opinion, a public health emergency,” one medical professor said.

Sometimes grief can be funny

On the stand-up scene, dead baby jokes were once a juvenile niche. Now, comedy about the death of a child has become its own heartbreaking genre.

Just this month, Liz Glazer released a stand-up album about the stillbirth of her daughter (“a comedy show meets shiva”) and Michael Cruz Kayne premiered a show about the death of his son. Early on, he warns us that we might cry. “If you don’t,” he adds, pausing, “that’s rude.”

Our critic Jason Zinoman writes that the trend shows how comedy has become more ambitious, as comics display a broader emotional palette than they did a generation ago.

World’s oldest building plans found in the desert

Massive prehistoric stone structures in desert landscapes from Saudi Arabia to Kazakhstan have baffled archaeologists for decades. Resembling a kite with strings, each can stretch for miles and is only fully visible from the air. They are believed to have been used to trap and kill animals.

A team of archaeologists reported today that two monoliths had been found in Jordan and Saudi Arabia with precise depictions of nearby desert kites, many of them from thousands of years ago. These are by far the oldest known to-scale architectural plans in human history.

Can you find Picasso’s lost dog?

During restoration work on Picasso’s “Le Moulin de la Galette” ahead of the Guggenheim Museum’s “Young Picasso in Paris” show, new subtleties of brushwork and color palette were found beneath the grime and the varnish.

But there was also a much bigger surprise: A dog, in the foreground — a King Charles spaniel, to be exact — that appeared to have been hastily painted over. Art experts say it was not uncommon for Picasso to leave behind such Easter eggs.
