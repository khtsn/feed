Justice Dept. Watchdog Finds That Barr Did Not Order Protesters Cleared in 2020

The inquiry, which called the attorney general’s response “chaotic and disorganized,” covered Donald J. Trump’s polarizing decision to walk through Lafayette Park for a photo opportunity at a nearby church.

July 31, 2024Updated 12:07 p.m. ET

A Justice Department watchdog found that Attorney General William P. Barr did not initiate the removal of protesters in a park near the White House during demonstrations over George Floyd’s murder in 2020, but that his overall response during that period was “chaotic and disorganized.”

The four-year investigation covers one of the most polarizing moments of Donald J. Trump’s presidency: his decision on June 1, 2020, to walk from the Rose Garden through Lafayette Park, which federal law enforcement officers had violently cleared of protesters, for a Bible-brandishing photo opportunity at a nearby church.

Video taken that day showed Mr. Barr, known for his aggressive, hands-on management style, conferring with officials in the park just before Mr. Trump walked through.

But Michael E. Horowitz, the Justice Department’s inspector general, found that Mr. Barr was, at that moment, not in the chain of command at the scene, according to a report released Wednesday. The clearing of the park by baton-wielding officers, some on horseback, was directed by the Park Police and the Secret Service, not the Justice Department, the report added.

“We found that the decision to clear the area of protesters on the evening of June 1 was made by the U.S.P.P. and U.S.S.S. unified command at Lafayette Park and not by Barr, and that Barr did not impact the timing of the clearing operation,” Mr. Horowitz wrote in an exhaustive 168-page report that included more than 100 interviews. Mr. Barr and other top department officials declined to be interviewed.

“We determined that the unified command had already decided to initiate the clearing operation and had begun preparations to do so prior to Barr’s arrival,” he added.

Still, the inspector general, who has released three reports over the past two weeks into Mr. Barr’s conduct, found that the attorney general seemed eager to deploy the department’s uniformed work force to prove he was willing to stand up to left-wing protesters he saw as a threat to civil order — at Mr. Trump’s urging.

During the investigation, Mr. Horowitz and his team reviewed Mr. Barr’s overall response to the unrest in the District of Columbia from the end of May, when Mr. Floyd was killed by a police officer in Minneapolis, to the beginning of June, when protests peaked.

He said he shared “the serious concerns” relayed by law enforcement officials about the department’s tendency to deploy officers from its divisions, including the Bureau of Prisons and the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives, without proper planning or procedural guardrails.

“We found the department’s efforts at times to be chaotic and disorganized,” he wrote. “And that Barr, in an attempt to demonstrate that law enforcement could handle the civil unrest without active-duty military intervention, pressed D.O.J. law enforcement components to deploy personnel without sufficient attention to whether those personnel were properly trained or equipped for their mission.”

Late on June 1, Mr. Barr’s chief of staff deployed prison bureau officers with no guidance on their mission or clear rules of engagement. That deployment, and the subsequent use of F.B.I. personnel to patrol the area around the park, was also made without an assessment of their legal authorities — and posed “safety and security risks for the agents and the public,” the investigation concluded.

Investigators also faulted a top aide to Mr. Barr, his press secretary Kerri Kupec, for giving the impression that he had played a more important role than he actually had.

Ms. Kupec, who now works as an editor at Fox News, told reporters on June 2 that Mr. Barr had ordered law enforcement officials in the park to “get it done” the day before.

“Witnesses told us they did not hear Barr make such a statement, and we therefore concluded that Kupec’s statement gave the inaccurate impression Barr had personally ordered the operation and led to significant public confusion regarding D.O.J.’s role in it,” Mr. Horowitz wrote.

Mr. Barr and Ms. Kupec did not respond to requests for comment.
