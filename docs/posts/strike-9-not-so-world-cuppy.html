Strike #9: Not so World Cuppy

Strike is a (not so) regular column written by HLTV Editor-in-chief Milan "Striker" Švejda, which mainly focuses on new realities of a Valve-regulated circuit.

I remember a time when Nations Cups (sort of) worked.

In the last few years of Counter-Strike 1.6, when I started following the game extensively, they were some of the most fun tournaments to watch for me.

As a proud Czech, watching and commentating Tomáš "⁠oskar⁠" Šťastný matching up against the world's elite was a joy, because it was a rare thing back when the local teams weren't competitive enough and the dawn of international lineups was years away.

But more so than that, they gave you a taste of what a dream world could look like in reality, seeing some of the best players from different lineups finally team up as if there had been a big shuffle. Sweden had so many players to choose from, even beyond the top two teams from fnatic and SK, you watched just about every combination of them possible as they so often stormed through the tournament to win it all.

In Global Offensive, the tradition of ESL's and ClanBase's versions of the same thing disappeared, before the European Championship and The World Championships took up the mantle to produce a successor for a couple of good editions in the early years.

The World Championships 2015 produced one of few French iterations we had never seen before

But that didn't last long. As the tier-one circuit began to fill up with more and more tournaments, 2016's edition of TWC fell short of its goal of attracting the best players, when even the likes of France, Denmark and Sweden, arguably the three best Counter-Strike nations at the time, fielded second and third-tier lineups.

It's telling that the IESF World Championship is the most notable attempt to run a World Cup-type tournament since then. The series had the support of the national esports federations and still rarely had any tier-one players attend, which meant existing second-tier teams that happened to be all-national would often come out on top. Not very World-Cuppy.

And it's a shame that the concept was gone for so long. As more and more organizations turn international, national identity is fading away, especially across Europe, and it would be more exciting than ever to see some of a country's favorite players return to their roots, even if for a moment.

Just think of the possibilities: Who wouldn't like to see Danil "⁠donk⁠" Kryshkovets and Ilya "⁠m0NESY⁠" Osipov team up, Dan "⁠apEX⁠" Madesclaire and Mathieu "⁠ZywOo⁠" Herbaut join 3DMAX to remind everyone what superpower France used to be, or a real Brazilian super-team that has eluded us for so long because of contracts?

The donk and m0NESY team-up may be left in the dream world for some time

Unfortunately, I don't believe we'll see any of that for some time. Enter the Esports Nations Cup, the latest attempt.

To give it some credit, first of all: Whatever you think of the motivation, Saudi Arabia has taken a proper stab at it. A $1.3 million prize pool, 24 teams, LAN finals and an incentive program rewarding clubs that release their players for the competition is not too shabby, and it will certainly make it more successful than its IESF-hosted predecessor.

However, it is fraught with issues that will, in my opinion, prevent it from reaching its lofty goals.

Regardless of where you put such a tournament, you'll find that most players who attend upwards of 15 events per year nowadays are not very willing to play another if they don't have to. We saw that in the early CS:GO attempts — the moment the calendar started getting more and more busy, nations cups went to the side.

You might argue that the summer break is the best place for a World Cup. In a way, that is true because it means it will always have its own place. But it's also called a break for a reason; players are either on vacation after a long season or preparing for the upcoming one with the rest of their real teams. Especially if they've just made changes, as they so often do around then, it's unlikely they'd put practice to the side.

But this year the timing is especially terrible. Taking place on November 10-15, the Esports Nations Cup overlaps with a tier-one event, the second BLAST Rivals, and ends just a week or two before teams have to travel to the PGL Singapore Major, depending on the stage they start in.

What elite team would sacrifice either a tournament or rest and preparation ahead of a Major just so that their players can waste a week away? Spoiler alert: I've spoken to some of them, and the picture is bleak, even if some of the players would be happy to go.

And don't get me started on it being a ranked tournament. This is 'Strike', after all.

On one hand, the VRS is a motivating factor. Teams are allowed to send three-man cores, which means those with majority-national rosters will have an easier tournament to farm VRS points in. If I were a team like Astralis, 3DMAX or paiN — in other words, any national team in danger of losing invites to tier-one events —, I'd be stupid not to go.

On the other, I'll leave it to you to consider Graham "⁠messioso⁠" Pitt's hypothetical. "Spirit are sending donk, magixx, sh1ro. Would Falcons give them m0NESY and kyousuke to boost Spirit's VRS in an event they cannot play themselves?"

The politics involved are certainly going to prevent some from attending, especially in cases between two direct competitors for space on the circuit.

Of course, the organizers had no choice in the matter. I know they tried to make the ENC unranked, but it needed Valve's exception if it was to offer a meaningful prize pool, because of the Tournament Operation Requirements.

Tournament Operation Requirements

The total compensation (including prize pool and any other forms of compensation) for an individual Unranked Tournament may not exceed $100,000.

Valve, obsessive over hard data as they are, wanted to see it in action first.

They missed the fact that we have seen the effects of the IESF World Championship already. At the end of 2024, it wreaked havoc on the ranking for months to come, eventually seeing teams like Nexus, Partizan and Metizport go from the verge of irrelevancy to the top 30 and feeding them a series of invites to some of the first tournaments of the VRS era.

You can extrapolate that to the teams on the verge of tier-one instead of tier-two, and, voila, you know what the ENC will do to the ranking just in time for the 2027 season.

Not all these issues are easily solvable. The calendar will be this busy for the foreseeable future, so until (if ever) we return to a franchised circuit, there will never be the perfect spot for a World Cup.

But you can do a lot to help make it the least disruptive, so let's get constructive. Convincing Valve to leave it unranked for future editions hopefully won't be too difficult once they see how much misleading data it produces for the VRS. That's step one.

Step two, find a better spot for it on the calendar. It is a big festival for loads of esports titles, I know, but as long as Counter-Strike is shackled to them, it won't work. Make it its own thing.

As a tier-two or unranked competition, the ENC doesn't have to follow the same years-long deadlines as the tier-one scene, so it's easy to wait for the calendar to unfold and find the best possible place for it.

One suggestion is right after a Major invite cut-off in early October or April, where teams are no longer under pressure and far enough from the Major itself to be willing to dispense their players.

Your move, ENC.
