North Carolina Republicans Pick Their Man, and He’s a Doozy

Beyoncé’s songs sound “satanic,” and Jay-Z is “demonic.” Michelle Obama is a man. Gay and trans people are “filth.” The 1969 moon landing may or may not have been “faked.” Hitler is someone to be quoted, and of course, Donald Trump is someone to be believed on 2020 election conspiracy theories.

North Carolina Republicans sure know how to pick ’em.

In one of the more chilling choices of this Super Tuesday, G.O.P. voters in that state chose Mark Robinson as their nominee for governor, a man who says and seemingly believes some pretty weird things. North Carolina’s lieutenant governor, he has a long history of remarks that would have been the kiss of death in politics once upon a time but will put Tar Heel voters to the test in November.

Now, North Carolina is no progressive redoubt — this is the state of Jesse Helms, after all — but it’s also not a state that we think of as especially radical anymore. Barack Obama carried the state in 2008, and a Democrat, Roy Cooper, has been governor there since 2017. Robinson will face the state’s low-key attorney general, Josh Stein, who won the Democratic nomination for governor.

My colleague Frank Bruni wrote a great article last weekend about Cooper and other Democratic governors who have won in red states and battleground states by focusing on pragmatic voter concerns and bipartisan issues. That will clearly be Stein’s playbook in the fall. What will Robinson’s playbook be? Well, he’s awfully good at attacking people, and he likes to write in CAPITAL LETTERS on social media, so he’s right up Trump’s alley.

But Robinson — and North Carolina Republicans — will need to decide if being Trump adjacent is enough of a winning strategy. It might just be, with Trump probably on the ballot and turning out voters in November, too, but I think Bruni’s insight is a good indicator of how Democrats may prevail against Robinson. As we saw in 2022 with Kari Lake, Dr. Oz and some other Republicans, candidate quality still matters to voters in battleground states.

First Joe Manchin. Now Kyrsten Sinema. The two Senate Democrats who the party most loved to hate are both headed for the door at the end of this congressional session.

Technically, Sinema was no longer a member of the blue team, having become an independent in December 2022. But she was elected to the Senate as a Democrat in 2018, still aligns with the party on many matters and maintains her committee assignments. And she, like Manchin, knows how to get under Democrats’ skin in a way that only members of one’s own family can.

Sinema’s not-so-progressive position on economic issues has often drawn accusations that she is a tool of her corporate supporters. It didn’t help that rather than play down her differences with her party, she reveled in them. After a 2021 vote against including a minimum-wage increase in a pandemic-relief bill, she flashed a saucy thumbs-down to emphasize her opposition, prompting howls of outrage that she was being callous. And her unyielding support of the legislative filibuster was an enduring sore spot, for which Arizona Democrats censured her in January 2022.

What made Sinema extra annoying to many was that they simply could not understand why she did what she did. Manchin is from West Virginia. Of course he veers right from time to time. But Arizona is more purple than red. Why, Democrats wondered, couldn’t Sinema be more like the state’s junior senator, Mark Kelly? All her paeans to bipartisanship and finding common ground struck many progressives as self-serving, self-righteous drivel.

Some Arizona Democrats began recruiting a challenger for Sinema well before she quit the team, with Representative Ruben Gallego a leading contender. Gallego, a progressive, is in fact the front-runner for the Democratic nomination, while Kari Lake, the election-denying super-MAGA failed nominee for governor, leads the Republican pack. Democrats can now stop freaking out about whether Sinema running as an independent would split the vote to give Republicans the win. Win or lose, the path ahead is clearer and simpler for Democrats.

A recent Emerson poll showed Gallego comfortably ahead of Sinema. So maybe the embattled senator wanted to get out before she got pushed. Or maybe she just wants to settle back into the private sector for some major buckraking. Or maybe she is just tired of all the nastiness. Whatever her motivations, Sinema’s decision not to run again is a gift of sorts to her former party. For that, at least, it should thank her.

It’s a strange thing that millions of people understand and accept that nuclear weapons can destroy life on Earth as we know it, yet we don’t discuss them very much. Sure, there are headlines whenever a world leader issues an implicit or explicit warning about using The Bomb, but it’s difficult to gauge what it means if the threat becomes reality.

Explaining that ominous “what if” is precisely what we set out to do with the first article in the new Opinion series, “The Brink,” about the modern nuclear threat.

The debates around nuclear weapons have fallen to a nuclear priesthood of strategists, generals and politicians, rather than the people most likely in the cross hairs of a nuclear attack. That needs to change.

To best understand the unnatural horrors of nuclear attack, we spoke to the only people who have experienced it: survivors of Hiroshima and Nagasaki. We spent days with them in Japan, listening to their stories, visiting their homes and neighborhoods, trying to fathom what occurred in August 1945.

The hibakusha, as they are called in Japan, described an unrelenting daily routine of agony and death, details of which are reflected in the essay’s interactive scenario. Men and women, now in their 80s and 90s, spoke about those days as if it were happening in front of them.

“There were so many dead bodies that they brought them to the elementary school, put them in seven trenches, poured gasoline on them and burned them,” Shigeaki Mori said in his living room. “The smell was terrible, unbearable for about three months.”

Hearing these anecdotes turned the errant nuclear threats of world leaders into a cold reality. We then took these individuals’ experiences and intertwined them with years-old scientific, environmental and government research that was publicly available.

On the rare occasion that nuclear war has been examined in film or the media, the focus has centered on apocalyptic but abstract visions of full-scale holocaust, with megaton hydrogen warheads raining down on city centers. In our essay, we sought to bring it closer to home by describing the detonation of a single small weapon, a threat looming over the active battlefields in Ukraine and perhaps future ones elsewhere.

The purpose of the project is to bring the peril of the current times back into the forefront of the American consciousness. The essay published on Monday is just the first step.

“I was a little disappointed that Katie Porter chose to run,” Karl Rubin, an emeritus professor of math, told me on the patio of a community center on the campus of the University of California, Irvine, on Monday morning.

He said that Porter, currently a Democratic congresswoman from a suburban swing district south of Los Angeles, would be great as a senator and he would be thrilled to have either her or Adam Schiff represent California in the Senate, but he believed her choice to run left her House seat vulnerable to being taken by a Republican.

Rubin was one of 13 people I spoke to who work at the university, where Porter is a tenured law professor. They are all Democrats, except for one who registered Republican to vote against Donald Trump; they live in the same faculty and staff housing that Porter has lived in; they know her better than most.

And so it was particularly striking to hear so many of them say they are unhappy about her decision to give up her House seat to run for the Senate, even though the consensus was that they respected and admired her. In fact, only four of those 13 neighbors said that they were voting for her.

Caroll Seron, an emeritus professor of criminology, pointed out that some people were “quite disappointed” that Porter announced her run for Senate so soon after being re-election to her congressional seat; another colleague said Porter’s ambition got in the way of her service to the district.

There was a clear sense in this group of resignation rather than enthusiasm about Schiff, the front-runner in Tuesday’s primary, even from those supporting him. As Mark Fisher, a neurology professor who is voting for Porter, put it, Schiff “is not emotionally engaged” and “he’s too intellectual, too cerebral.”

Kev Abazajian, a physics professor, had a more policy-driven opposition to Schiff, calling him “almost a conservative” because “he’s never seen a war he doesn’t like, he wasn’t part of the progressive caucus, he was part of the Blue Dog coalition.” He added: “His record, other than defending democracy, which I appreciate, has not been great in terms of progressive values.”

But in the end, most of these voters seemed to believe that Porter’s blind ambition was going to lose out to Schiff’s bland ambition.

I’m not a naturally wrathful person. I don’t like to (and usually don’t) lose my temper. The exception is when drivers ignore pedestrian right of way. I will, whether I’m a few steps or a few miles into my walk, admonish them — loudly and none too gently. (They, of course, cannot hear me inside their cars.)

It’s hard to shake the impression that New York City’s drivers have become more aggressive. They run reds, viciously honk and yell at pedestrians who clearly have the right of way and generally endanger the lives of those on foot.

From 2009 to 2022, an average of 136 pedestrians were killed each year by traffic in the city. The numbers have gone down slightly — to 99 in 2023 — but cyclist deaths have climbed, though they remain a small fraction of pedestrian deaths. (The city’s decision to give pedestrians a head start at many intersections, where walk signals turn white before streetlights go green, may be helping.)

In the United States generally, walking has become far more dangerous. The number of pedestrian fatalities nationally has climbed more than 80 percent in the past 15 years, largely because of increasingly hazardous automotive design. Some police departments effectively no longer punish drivers for any traffic infractions. San Francisco’s police officers “have almost entirely abandoned enforcement of traffic laws,” The Times’s Heather Knight reported.

All of this calls not just for policy changes such as automobile regulation, improved policing and better urban planning but also for a more assertive pedestrian culture. Aggressive drivers have exacerbated this problem; aggressive pedestrians can help solve it.

That’s why I assert my right of way with my body, walking the moment I have a walk signal, even if a car that just ran a red light is already halfway across the intersection. It’s on the driver, the dangerous lawbreaker, to stop, not on me to change my walking habits. Of course, this doesn’t work for everyone — older and slower walkers or those with mobility issues may not be able to follow this advice — but one hopes we can all yell at drivers.

Let’s assert our rights as pedestrians and enjoy the basic freedom of walking, of not relying on highways and automobiles. Like latter-day Dustin Hoffmans, don’t be afraid to state that we’re walkin’ here.

In an authoritative essay published in Opinion on Friday, the cultural historian Mark Harris considered the state of Hollywood, and the verdict was: not great. The business model may be irreparably broken, A.I. is looming, and the studio heads and the talent they rely on are still fuming over last year’s contentious strikes.

But Harris also noted that in Hollywood, corporate chaos often leads to creative flourishing, as in the late 1960s and early ’70s, when industry upheaval led to what we now consider a golden age for American film.

Is something similar happening now?

Just look to this past weekend’s big opening, “Dune: Part Two.” At first glance, this sequel might be easy to dismiss as another I.P.-driven franchise spectacle. But the director, Denis Villeneuve, has a singular visual style, and he has long declared “Dune” to be a personal passion project. For Hollywood, adapting the Frank Herbert novel the films are based on had proved thorny: The scuttling of Alejandro Jodorowsky’s effort caused such a stir that there’s a documentary about it, and David Lynch’s “Dune” was nearly a career-ending disaster. Villeneuve’s success is not a case of an interesting and idiosyncratic director being brought to heel in the service of some franchise’s uniform aesthetic; it’s his distinctive talents and vision that have made the “Dune” series soar.

The best picture nominees at this year’s Oscars tell a similar story. The Oscars have long been criticized for encouraging cookie-cutter, award-targeted films. But this year, the best picture list, with the arguable exception of “Maestro,” doesn’t feature much Oscar bait. Instead, it’s visionary directors all the way down, from Christopher Nolan and Greta Gerwig to Yorgos Lanthimos (“Poor Things”) and Justine Triet (“Anatomy of a Fall”). The biggest Oscars controversy — that Gerwig was squeezed out of a crowded best director field — speaks to just how many worthy visionaries helmed films this year.

Make no mistake: As a business proposition, Hollywood is faltering. But when no one’s sure what works anymore, that can lead to an environment of reckless creative risk. That’s good news for the filmgoing audience; we’re already reaping the benefits onscreen.

At least it was unanimous.

The Supreme Court’s ruling Monday morning that Donald Trump may remain on the ballot — despite the plain language of Section 3 of the 14th Amendment, which says insurrectionists (even presidents on live TV) are ineligible to serve — was widely anticipated after oral arguments in the case last month.

But unlike the court’s similarly consequential 2000 decision in Bush v. Gore, the ruling in Trump v. Anderson was not 5 to 4, with conservative justices on one side and liberals on the other. Instead, the court agreed 9 to 0 with the basic proposition that states can’t enforce Section 3 against candidates for federal offices. (The three liberal justices wrote separately to argue that the court went much further than it needed to in deciding the case.)

And that’s about the best that can be said for the decision. The rationale of the opinion itself is much harder to swallow. If states can decide which presidential candidates are eligible, this “disruption,” the court said, “could nullify the votes of millions and change the election result.” It added, “Nothing in the Constitution requires that we endure such chaos.”

Nothing — really? Let me introduce you to federalism, the way elections are actually run in this country. Each state decides for itself which candidates will appear on its ballot, based on arcane laws regarding the number of signatures they need to gather. Ask Robert F. Kennedy Jr., the independent presidential candidate who is currently fighting to get his name on the ballot in states across the country, how that’s working out for him. Or No Labels, the bizarre group that has managed to secure a ballot line in 11 states so far, even without a candidate.

The point is that these widely varying state laws determine who is and is not on the ballot. As we saw in Florida in 2000, the placement of names on ballots can easily tip the vote in a given state and, with it, the entire election. That sure seems like a lot of chaos for the nation to endure.

If the court has decided that it would be better to have a uniform national standard for running federal elections, one that doesn’t unduly empower individual states to “change the election result,” I say hallelujah. Let’s abolish the Electoral College. But that’s not the system we live under.

Every Monday morning on The Point, we kick off the week with a tip sheet on the latest in the presidential campaign. Here’s what we’re looking at this week:

  • Four years ago, Super Tuesday turbocharged Joe Biden’s drive for the presidency. He beat rivals in nine states, changing perceptions about whether this centrist establishment figure could build a coalition of voters to go the distance. The simplest fact proved a powerful one: He looked like a winner. He laid to rest questions about his abilities and memories of ugly earlier losses. Soon Biden was leading the most unified Democratic Party that I’ve seen in my lifetime, determined to beat Donald Trump.

  • One year ago, Biden delivered a punchy State of the Union speech in which he challenged Republicans on Social Security, Medicare, the economy and bipartisanship and looked nimble in the face of hecklers. That performance laid to rest questions about his abilities to seek and win re-election — for a time, at least.

  • The big question Biden faces this week is whether, once again, he can lay to rest questions about his abilities. Winning the Super Tuesday contests won’t give him the same juice as 2020, however, since he’s all but uncontested.

  • That leaves Thursday’s State of the Union speech, and here Biden has an opportunity that I think he’ll take advantage of — to flip the script against his opponents and leave them facing questions.

  • Biden is skilled at rallying people and allies to his side when he has foils — Donald Trump, Vladimir Putin, Covid — and he will have the chance to do so on Thursday in the face of House and Senate Republicans who have killed the bipartisan border bill and delayed aid to Israel and Ukraine. He can paint Putin and Trump as Enemies No. 1 and 2 to democracy and more broadly frame Putin as a nuclear-armed evil in the world. And rather than brag about the economy, which risks sounding tone-deaf, he could take a page from Bill Clinton’s 2012 Democratic convention speech and lay out why there’s still so much economic pain and what he wants to do about it.

  • Biden can’t count on the courts to stop Trump (the Supreme Court ruled Monday that Trump can remain on the ballot in Colorado and elsewhere this year), and he can’t count on more people to give him credit on the economy. Just as he flipped the script on Super Tuesday in 2020 and got people to start seeing him as a winner, he will need to go on offense in the face of polls and doubters about his abilities. This week will be a big test of whether he can still go the distance.
