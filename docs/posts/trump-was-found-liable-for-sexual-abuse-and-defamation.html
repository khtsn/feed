Donald Trump is found liable for sexual abuse

A Manhattan jury found former President Donald Trump liable for the sexual abuse and defamation of the magazine writer E. Jean Carroll, awarding her $5 million in damages in a civil trial.

After three hours of deliberations, the jury unanimously found that the “preponderance of the evidence” — a lower threshold than that in criminal cases — supported Carroll’s accusation that Trump attacked her in a Bergdorf Goodman dressing room in the mid 1990s.

It also held the former president liable for defaming Carroll, when he called her case “a Hoax and a lie.” But the jury did not find that Carroll had proved that Trump had raped her, as she has long claimed.

On his Truth Social website, Trump said: “I have absolutely no idea who this woman is. This verdict is a disgrace — a continuation of the greatest witch hunt of all time!” He vowed to appeal.

Carroll is one of more than a dozen women who have accused Trump — the leading 2024 Republican presidential contender — of sexual misconduct over the years, but hers is the first case to be successfully argued before a jury. The verdict is in civil court, meaning Trump has not been convicted of a crime and faces no prison time.

Biden and McCarthy’s debt talks end with no deal

President Biden sat down with Speaker Kevin McCarthy at the White House in a critical face-to-face meeting to discuss ways to avert a devastating default on U.S. debt. They emerged after roughly an hour with no agreement or clear path forward. The Treasury expects to run out of money in as little as three weeks.

“I didn’t see any new movement,” McCarthy told reporters after the meeting, adding that he would again meet with the president on Friday.

My colleague Catie Edmondson told me that the lack of breakthrough at today’s meeting was widely expected, as McCarthy — whose caucus already passed a debt limit bill — continues to push the president to negotiate. The Biden administration, which McCarthy said declined to engage with him today, has adopted a different approach.

“The White House seems to have settled on this strategy where they’re just trying to pummel the hell out of the so-called Biden Republicans in an effort to get them to work with them, to buck their party and potentially vote with Democrats,” Catie said.

Protests in Pakistan

Supporters of Pakistan’s former prime minister, Imran Khan, took to the streets today soon after Khan was arrested in connection with one of the dozens of corruption cases against him. The arrest represented a major escalation in a political crisis that has engulfed the country since Khan was removed from power by a no-confidence vote in Parliament last year, seemingly buoying his popularity.

“For many people in Pakistan, this feels like a turning point, political tensions that have been simmering for months finally boiling over,” Christina Goldbaum, our Afghanistan and Pakistan bureau chief, said.

The dog days of Westminster

It’s the big moment for the dogs at the Westminster Kennel Club Dog Show. The dogs competed in agility and obedience championships over the weekend before being judged in their respective breed categories. Tonight, the best-in-show prize will be awarded.

Ahead of the competition, my colleague Sarah Lyall visited Striker, a white Samoyed, in Toronto. After finding fame at last year's show, Striker retired to the joyful life of a former champion.

What he doesn’t know, is that he actually fell just short of winning best in show for two consecutive years. Does he care? “No,” his breeder said, “as long as he gets attention.”

The Met says it’s done with looted works

In recent years, the Metropolitan Museum of Art has returned a steady stream of objects that it deemed stolen or of questionable provenance. But now, facing increasing scrutiny from critics and law enforcement officials, the Met is promising to broaden its efforts.

The museum’s stature and the scope of its new efforts are likely to affect how other institutions grapple with the increasing pressure to return ancient items that bear evidence of having been looted.

Can rats save the mall?

Malls were struggling even before the pandemic effectively brought them to a standstill. Online shopping has become so ubiquitous that stores alone are no longer a big enough draw.

To reverse the trend, some malls are trying to lure customers with dogs, cats and even rats, offering abandoned storefronts to animal rescue groups for free or at a significant discount. Such arrangements help attract animal-curious customers to the shopping centers and find homes for rescue pets.
