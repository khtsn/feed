molodoy: "I can only try to help FalleN win more tournaments in his last year"

HLTV sat down with Danil "⁠molodoy⁠" Golubenko during the BLAST RIvals 2026 Season 1 media day in Fort Worth, where FURIA's AWPer talked about the news of Gabriel "⁠FalleN⁠" Toledo's retirement and how he's reinventing his game in his sophomore year.

"Yeah, it's good when you're playing the individual game, but if you're not winning trophies, then it's not so good," molodoy said of how he's tweaking his game in 2026 as FURIA.

FURIA are slated to make their BLAST Rivals start on Wednesday against GamerLegion, a match that molodoy does not see as an easy game. "We're just trying to improve, do our best, and for me, that's kind of all," he says.

Listen to the interview, here.

First, I want to talk about FalleN's big news from Rio, about retiring at the end of the year. How has that affected the team now?

Before we knew he wanted to retire because for him it's hard to play tournaments all of the time. He's kind of old already, and he wanted to play, but he wanted to change his life. He wants to be with his family and enjoy family life. Before Rio, when we talked to him, he didn't say anything about wanting to retire. He just said, 'Maybe I want to retire.' But when we went to IEM Rio, the day before he said he wanted to retire, he told me, 'OK, Danil, I have big news, I want to retire.'

I was so shocked when he told me because he told me kind of jokes every time, he didn't say it seriously, but now he was saying it full serious. For me, it's hard because he invited me to the team, and he's like a father in Counter-Strike. He helped me a lot, so for me, that's not such good news.

I'm trying to change my game, change my mind about playing only for the team, and just try to help the team and not just my individual game

I remember you talking about how when you first joined the team, he went to Serbia to teach you early on. As a mentor, how do you take this in emotionally?

I understand this, but what can I do other than help him, maybe? Just take some responsibility to play with this team and my job on the team, and do better. I understand that this is life, and sometimes life changes. I can only try to help him win more tournaments in his last year.

Speaking of winning tournaments. You got to win some at the end of last year, but this year not so much. In part because we have the invincible Vitality, but you haven't quite managed to reach the same level. What do you think has changed, and what can you do to get back in your best shape?

First, we're trying to fix the system, and trying to play as a team and not individually. I can say about me that I'm not trying to play for myself. Not just the individual game. I'm trying to change my game, change my mind about playing only for the team, and just try to help the team and not just my individual game.

Yeah, it's good when you're playing the individual game, but if you're not winning trophies, then it's not so good. You have to think about how you can help the team, and for me, that's the only job.

You were Rookie of the Year last year. You had an amazing breakout season, but sometimes in sports, the second year is kind of harder than the first after the initial hype dies down. Have you found that a bit yourself, after you got Rookie of the Year, and were even overconfident, that in the second year, you're having to rethink how you're playing and what you're doing?

What you say about overconfidence, I'm not losing confidence in the game, and doing stuff in the game, I'm just now thinking about... I'm changing all of my game, because when I played before, in my first year, I was just trying to play my individual game and not always playing for the team.

But now, I'm trying to play for the team and, you know, when you try to do new stuff, you never have easy situations. Sometimes you can play badly, sometimes you can play good, but now I'm trying to play good every time, and every time I'm trying to improve myself and only trying to help the team.

For me, it works like this, if you want to play individually, you're not a team player because if you think that 'I can only do individual player, I can win us the game,' for me it doesn't work like this. You can win only with the team.

When I played my first tournament, I couldn't understand like 75 or 85 percent in the game, and was just playing how I was feeling

Do you think it's always the case, that you always have to be a team player, or does the team sometimes have to play for the star?

It's about how the team plays in a system. Some teams play for the superstar, but maybe some play for the team. If the team wants to play for the superstar, it's okay, but if you have a conclusion that you have a superstar and you need to play for him, and you don't want to, that's a problem.

But if you have a team who want to play for the superstar, it's good. Every time you can have a conclusion about this, but Counter-Strike is a team game. If you don't think about the team, for me, you're not a team player.

It's an interesting period because there's quite some time between Major qualification and the Major itself. I know Astana will be a sweet one for you. But this little period between now and the Major. How are you going into these events? Is it any different than events a month ago, or is it the same?

It's always the same because before tournaments, every time we're talking, kind of, like, 'Guys, it's okay, we have a tournament in Rio,' but you don't need to think about this special tournament because it's Rio. No, we're just trying to enjoy every tournament.

Of course, you want to win every tournament. If you don't want to win the tournament, this is magic. Now, after Rio, we have time to practice. Not so much, but we practiced. You always want to improve yourself and the team.

Rio was big because it's Brazil, of course, and Astana will be big because it's your home tournament. How is that, dealing with having these special tournaments now?

You don't have to overthink. If you overthink, you already lose. You need to enjoy every tournament, and just when you have time after the tournament, improve yourself. See what the problem is if you lose the tournament, and that's all. You need to improve, always.

Astana last year was when you broke out. We're almost at a year out. Look back on this year and tell me the first things that pop into your mind.

Better English, that's first [laughs]. When I played my first tournament, I couldn't understand like 75 or 85 percent in the game, and was just playing how I was feeling. Now I can understand the system, and I can understand if people want me to do something.

The second thing, I think, when I joined FURIA, it was a new life for me. You need to just enjoy this life, and need to be happy because if you're not happy when you're playing this well, it's hard. You just need to be happy, enjoy this life, and that's all.

Are you enjoying it?

Yeah, of course!

You're opening against GamerLegion here in Dallas, a chill game. Any expectations you're coming into this tournament with? Or any goals or anything like that?

It's not an easy game. It's not a chill game because when you play against a good team, now in CS2, you can always lose. In CS:GO, you could not lose, but in CS2, for me, you can always lose.

You just need to stay hard every time, you need to think about the game always, and about the tournament. We don't have expectations that we have to win the tournament or anything. We're just trying to improve, do our best, and for me, that's kind of all.

You won some tournaments last year, you know what it takes. Where do you think you are now in your ability to go deep into a tournament, make finals, and fight for trophies?

We always have a chance to win a tournament, but sometimes during a game or before a game, we have a problem, and we're trying to fix it. If we fix it in the tournament, we can win, but the problem is we always have good opponents, like here we have Vitality and NAVI, but the problem in CS2 is that you always have good opponents, and you can always lose.
