Stock markets tumbled on Wednesday on renewed signs that the global trade war could dent the financial results of some of the world’s largest technology companies.

The S&P 500 opened 1 percent lower, while the tech-heavy Nasdaq Composite index fell even more, sinking about 2 percent in early trading.

Nvidia, the American chip giant, revealed that the U.S. government would restrict sales of some of its chips to China, signifying the first major limits that the Trump administration has put on semiconductor sales abroad. Nvidia dominates the market for chips used in building artificial intelligence systems and will now require a license to sell A.I. chips to China.

In a regulatory filing on Tuesday, Nvidia said it would take a $5.5 billion hit because of piles of chips it would not be able to sell and orders it would not be able to fill. Shares of Nvidia, which exert great influence over market indexes because of the company’s size, fell about 6 percent in early trading.

AMD, another chip maker, said in a filing on Wednesday that the export controls would result in charges of $800 million to its “inventory, purchase commitments and related reserves.” The company’s stock also sank 6 percent.

And in Europe, shares of ASML, the Dutch company whose machines are essential for manufacturing the most advanced semiconductors, fell more than 4 percent on Wednesday after it said that orders for its equipment had fallen short of expectations. Christophe Fouquet, the company’s chief executive, said the Trump administration’s tariffs had “increased uncertainty.”

The Stoxx Europe 600 index fell about half a percent, with most markets in the region trading lower.

Stock market benchmarks in Japan fell 1 percent on Wednesday. Share prices were down 2 percent in Hong Kong and 2 percent in Taiwan, a hub of global chip manufacturing.

The maker of most of the world’s advanced chips, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company, dropped 2.5 percent. Its South Korean chip-making rivals, Samsung and SK Hynix, each fell over 3 percent.

Mr. Trump’s whipsawing tariff policies are still driving sentiment in global markets, especially in sectors facing the threat of more levies or potential reprieves.

A recent survey by Bank of America showed that global investors have cut their U.S. stock holdings by a record amount in the past two months, and that the potential for a recession spurred by Mr. Trump’s trade war poses a major risk to markets.

Gold, a traditional safe haven during times of turmoil, rose to another record high on Wednesday, pushing above $3,300 per ounce for the first time. The price of the precious metal has jumped more than 25 percent this year.

Census Bureau data released on Wednesday showed that shoppers ramped up spending on big-ticket items in March, ahead of Mr. Trump’s April 2 tariff announcement. Retail sales, which are not adjusted for inflation, rose 1.4 percent from the previous month — the jump in more than two years.

Sales of cars and car parts, in particular, rose 5.3 percent in March. Electronics and appliance purchases rose 0.8 percent. Consumer spending is the engine of economic growth, and the fresh data, while robust, came against a backdrop of falling consumer confidence and concerns about the potential inflationary effects of tariffs.

“While normally that would be a good thing for markets, it is very possible that consumers are front-loading their purchases, and we may be seeing an artificial bump in sales,” said Chris Zaccarelli, chief investment officer at Northlight Asset Management.

Tripp Mickle and Adam Satariano contributed reporting.

Danielle Kaye is a business reporter and a 2024 David Carr Fellow, a program for journalists early in their careers.
