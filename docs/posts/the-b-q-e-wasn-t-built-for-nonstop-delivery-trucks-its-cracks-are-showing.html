The B.Q.E. Wasn’t Built for Nonstop Delivery Trucks. Its Cracks Are Showing.

Big CITY

Wildfires and truck crashes bring a new sense of urgency to fixing New York’s worst highways — and a search for radical alternatives.

By Ginia Bellafante

Ginia Bellafante writes the Big City column, a weekly commentary on the politics, culture and life of New York City.

Published June 23, 2023Updated June 24, 2023, 1:11 p.m. ET

In 1982, the city of Boston embarked on a plan for the most ambitious and expensive highway project in the country’s history, one that replaced a crumbling, six-lane elevated artery of Interstate 93 with a tunnel and two bridges crossing the Charles River.

Known as the Big Dig, the project resulted, at various phases, in charges of bad design, shoddy workmanship, fraud and death; it ran more than five times over budget and took a quarter century to complete, missing its deadline by almost a decade.

What can this history teach us about the future of the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway? Studied and debated for years with virtually no progress to speak of, the B.Q.E. is considered an even more complicated undertaking. Construction and engineering challenges distinct to the most problematic segment of the highway — a deteriorating mile-and-a-half stretch containing a triple-deck cantilever running below the Brooklyn Heights Promenade — are part of what makes any grand effort at rehabilitation so hard to implement.

Increasingly though, the broader, more intractable issue would seem to demand a philosophical response as much as a logistical one, a resolution to questions around urban identity and the extent to which we want to articulate it through submission to an idle, habitual and environmentally exhausting style of consumerism.

These questions acquired a new urgency in recent weeks after the air in New York darkened from the smoke of Canadian wildfires and our anxieties around ecological disaster were felt more acutely. A few days later, as if by divine warning, a truck carrying 8,500 gallons of gasoline lost control as it was getting off the highway in Philadelphia and caused a stretch of I-95 to collapse.

That the road was relatively new and sound — which the B.Q.E. obviously is not — only increased the worry that something catastrophic could happen here, especially given that the city has pushed back crucial repairs on two sections of the cantilever, which are checked quarterly by engineers.

Scheduled to have begun this summer, the repairs won’t start until later this year. (“The work was postponed as we onboarded our contractor,” a spokesman for the Transportation Department offered by way of an emailed explanation.)

The problem, at its essence, is that New Yorkers now receive an average of 2.3 million packages per day, an increase of 500,000 over prepandemic levels, most of them entering the city by truck, roughly 15,000 of which travel along the B.Q.E. every day.

Recently, Andrew Gounardes, a Democratic state senator representing the long western edge of Brooklyn, tried to determine the vehicular impact of new warehouse development in Red Hook meant to serve e-commerce. His informal study found that every time the warehouses (existing and proposed) are filled they would require the equivalent of 37,000 40-foot trucks.

Theoretically a shift to electric trucks would eliminate many of the existential concerns around the way in which we have grown accustomed to getting our Cheerios and AirPods and gallons of Tide. But some of that optimism might be misplaced. E-trucks are expected to be heavier than conventional ones and pulverize the road, sending up polluting asphalt particulate.

In the same vein, there is not an obvious eco-hack around reconstruction of the highway. As Cindy McLaughlin, who lives along the B.Q.E. and also works as the head of product for a manufacturer of low-carbon concrete, laid it out, there is simply not enough low-carbon concrete and steel available yet to rebuild in a way that accommodates climate goals.

In Mr. Gounardes’s view — which is also the view of so many of his constituents living close to the B.Q.E. — more meaningful solutions come with the creation of a world in which there are far fewer trucks of any kind, diverting shipments off road and reducing our reliance on the high that the words “Add to Cart” so often provide.

Last month, at a symposium on the future of the B.Q.E. held by the Institute for Public Architecture on Governors Island, Hank Gutman, a former transportation commissioner for the city, made the point that New York is substantially more dependent on trucks for freight delivery than its peer cities at home and abroad.

During his tenure, the Transportation Department issued a report outlining ways in which we could shrink our dependence on trucking for the final phases of delivery through the revival of defunct rail tracks in Brooklyn and Queens, cargo bikes, and most intriguingly the waterways, which historically had been such an important means of moving freight and are now used largely for moving garbage and sewage.

The city’s ferry service, which essentially shuts down at 10 p.m., could be deployed overnight, for example, to move packages, which would then be deposited at waterfront hubs and picked up by delivery people via mobile bins or cargo bikes.

While alternatives like this seem to excite officials at the conceptual level, they remain terminally slow to materialize. Some of this has to do with maddening issues around jurisdiction: cargo bikes, for example, are subject to size restrictions governed by the state, meaning that new legislation is required to put them toward any significant commercial application. 

The use of sensors to automatically ticket trucks in violation of weight limits — thousands of them passing through the B.Q.E. surpass the legal size — now seems closer to reality. Nevertheless, it has taken years after considerable structural damage to the highway, both the result of historically inadequate enforcement and the dangerous corrosion that comes from the mix of salt, water, concrete and rebar. Until Mr. Gutman ended the practice in 2021, salt had been applied to the B.Q.E. for 70 years, nearly every time it snowed. Waterproofing the cantilever would help, but that hasn’t happened yet either.

Since last fall, the Adams administration has devoted considerable energy to engaging Brooklyn communities adjacent to the B.Q.E. about its future, including those to the north and south of the cantilever, previously left out of the conversation and where in many cases asthma rates are quite high. In one sense, the B.Q.E. is its own leveler, blighting the lives of wealthy people in townhouses and less wealthy people in public housing, giving the city an opportunity, should it exercise it, to unite the communities Robert Moses sought to divide.

The Department of Transportation has already held more than 20 meetings with community groups and offered a dizzying number of proposals and conceits for a reimagined B.Q.E. that are often accompanied by pretty renderings of green space — “what architects call eyewash,” Michael Canter, himself an architect, told me. Proposals can be complex, difficult for laymen to understand and leave participants in these meetings feeling frustrated and confused.

“The city has really funded public outreach,” said Mr. Canter, who lives near the highway and has been active in fighting for a sustainable approach to reconceiving the B.Q.E. “But it’s simply about saying, ‘We are doing continuous outreach’. Nobody knows what the current thinking for the highway actually is.”

Two years ago, a portion of the B.Q.E. running along the Brooklyn waterfront switched to two lanes in each direction from the three. One result, according to city data, has been fewer crashes. Still, for the city to qualify for money from the federal infrastructure bill, a rebuilt highway would have to revert to three lanes or the road would need to be widened. The federal government has its own size requirements for highways, part of safety standards devised long ago, however counterintuitive it seems in addressing the current problems.

The paradoxes of modernizing urban roadways were given no better showcase than in two public events held last week: one a Department of Transportation Zoom meeting with a Cobble Hill neighborhood group about the B.Q.E; the other a town hall organized by local businesses in Greenpoint, Brooklyn, protesting the city’s plan to add bike lanes to McGuinness Boulevard.

In Cobble Hill, the D.O.T. was floating the truck-friendly idea of adding a new entrance ramp to the B.Q.E. on a residential street, with the notion, the department said, of reducing congestion on Atlantic Avenue. But in reality the plan seemed geared toward easing traffic on the B.Q.E. The on-ramp would seemingly bring more traffic to a zone that has already been troubled by pedestrian fatalities, although the city maintains that the change would improve “the pedestrian experience.” People in the meeting were apoplectic. As one participant said, “The solution is worse than the problem.”

In Greenpoint, the opposition to the bike lanes emerged after local businesses became concerned that the new street plan would make it harder for them to receive deliveries. Missing from the calculation was that life made easier for trucks also made it easier for people to shop online and avoid those businesses altogether.
