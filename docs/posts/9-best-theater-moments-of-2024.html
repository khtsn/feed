The New York Times

Published Dec. 9, 2024Updated Dec. 10, 2024, 7:50 a.m. ET

Climate protesters disrupting a performance of “An Enemy of the People,” the outdoor walking scene in “Sunset Boulevard” and the giggles prompted by a character’s reaction to a hunky celebrity’s glutes in “Hold On to Me Darling”: The rewards of live theater were aplenty this year. Here, nine other stage moments that especially stood out, listed chronologically. NICOLE HERRINGTON

Expert Flopping

Sutton Foster’s performance as the unorthodox Princess Winnifred in “Once Upon a Mattress” was full of playful mugging. But it was in the show’s indelible scene that her best physical comedy shone through: sprawling atop a tower of mattresses stacked on a pea, flailing, flopping, hopping and then propped, rear-end up, like a fitful child protesting bedtime. It’s the kind of clowning that few can pull off with Foster’s ease and charm. MAYA PHILLIPS

Coroner’s Cabaret Act

The beguilingly strange new Off Broadway musical “Dead Outlaw” retold the true tale of an Old West bank robber whose mummified corpse landed, in 1976, on the Los Angeles autopsy table of Thomas Noguchi, coroner to the stars. Noguchi is this dark comedy’s conscience — and in Thom Sesma’s performance, a fabulous showman, too. Grabbing the dangling microphone intended for postmortem notes, he delivered a slab-side nightclub number, boasting of celebrities he had cut up. Suddenly, surreally, death was a cabaret. LAURA COLLINS-HUGHES

Virtuosic Violence

The first blow lands just eight minutes into “The Outsiders,” when a group of entitled kids jumps Ponyboy. A kick to the head. Oozing blood. The piercing shriek of tinnitus, the rumble of a train, a flash of white light, and blackout, for him and for us. This musical’s violence, a music-less and often slow-motion symphony of bodies, sound, light and projection that peaks with a balletic rumble, is stagecraft at its best. MICHAEL PAULSON

A Perfect Contrast

Arriving late in Amy Herzog’s “Mary Jane” as the Hasidic mother of a chronically ill child, Susan Pourfar was brusque, resigned and heart-rending as she traded hospital stories with Rachel McAdams in the title part. A prickly woman of faith, Pourfar helped turn a few stage minutes with McAdams’s character, a sunbeam of desperate optimism, into one of the Broadway season’s most moving scenes. SCOTT HELLER

A Sister’s Rockin’ Return

When Joan (Laura Donnelly) makes her appearance in Jez Butterworth’s “The Hills of California,” the anticipation has been building — for almost two hours, her sisters have been wondering if their older sibling, AWOL for 20 years, will turn up at their reunion. The immediately identifiable intro of the Rolling Stones’ “Gimme Shelter” resonates in the theater and Joan walks in, slowly, methodically taking possession of the space. I have chills just typing this. ELISABETH VINCENTELLI

Romeo Gets a Workout

There have been more tragic versions of “Romeo and Juliet,” more audacious and erotic ones. But the director Sam Gold’s revival of the Shakespeare tragedy ranks among the most athletic, and never more so than at the finish of the balcony scene. Kit Connor’s Romeo, desperate to kiss Rachel Zegler’s Juliet, lunges for her suspended bed and does a pull-up to bring his lips to hers. Would a rose by any other name have biceps like those? ALEXIS SOLOSKI

Gory Curtain Call

Meet Norma Desmond by way of Medea: When she takes her bows at the end of “Sunset Boulevard,” Nicole Scherzinger is still covered in blood from the finale. Her black slip of a dress is drenched, her grim face caked in gore as “the greatest star of all” stares down the audience. It is the final exclamation mark for Jamie Lloyd’s operatically demented revival. ELISABETH VINCENTELLI

A Resonant ‘Ragtime’

I could chalk up my tear-stricken reaction to “Ragtime” to the fact that I saw it three days after the presidential election, but that would underplay the breathtaking performances of Joshua Henry and Nichelle Lewis, whose dramatic acting as reunited lovers was matched by their stellar harmonies in the Act I number “Wheels of a Dream.” Henry’s rich, sonorant tones wove with Lewis’s sky-bound vocals to embody two Black characters aspiring for more from their country than the injustices and vile epithets thrown their way. So when tears started falling down my face, it was because I felt their disappointment — but also the solace that only sensational theater can bring. MAYA PHILLIPS

An Epic Disaster

In 1884, the Mignonette, a 52-foot cruiser, was struck by a giant wave. In real-life this was a catastrophe. On Broadway it’s a sensation. In “Swept Away,” the Avett Brothers musical drawn from the tragedy, Rachel Hauck’s set, Kevin Adams’s lighting and John Shiver’s sound combine to create calamity. Winds blow, timbers shiver and the ship tilts at a thrilling, impossible angle. Disaster never looked so good. ALEXIS SOLOSKI
