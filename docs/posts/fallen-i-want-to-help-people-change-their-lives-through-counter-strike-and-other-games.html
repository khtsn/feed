FalleN: "I want to help people change their lives through Counter-Strike and other games"

IEM Rio was a significant tournament for Brazilian legend Gabriel "⁠FalleN⁠" Toledo, and not just because it was a chance for him to fight for a trophy in his home country. The 34-year-old also took the opportunity to announce the end of his over 20-year career, with 2026 set to be his final year competing professionally.

Unfortunately for FalleN, he and his team were not able to take down Vitality in their semi-final bout, robbing the home crowd of a chance to see The Professor grab a title in his home country. "It's part of the game," FalleN told HLTV when reflecting upon the defeat. "We came to play and you never know what's going to happen, and this time they were better."

Not only did he talk about the match with Vitality, he also provided some further insight on his retirement and the timing of the announcement. "It's 23 years playing now, so I think it's quite reasonable that to stop something like that, you kinda need some time," he said. "It would be weird to just (snaps fingers), 'Next week, I'm gonna be someone else.'"

Read on to find out what FalleN had to say about his team's bout with Vitality and their risky Ancient veto, as well as his reflections on a storied career and upcoming retirement.

FalleN, a hard loss in the semi-finals. Not the way you would've wanted to go out in Rio, but just give me your thoughts after that series.

It was a great match. Both teams could have won, we definitely had our chance on Overpass. I feel a little bit bad about the way we played the T side; I felt like we didn't really get things done as fast as we should. We got flanked, even knowing that this would happen in this game, so I didn't think I called a great game on the T side. I didn't really make the engine work, you know? And playing against some of the best, if you're not really playing at your best and getting the right reads, or doing the right moves at good timings, it's going to be difficult.

So we lost the first map in my opinion because of that, even though the start was great. We were up 6-0, we were always one step ahead, the crowd was hyping up every kill, every single moment, so this was a highlight from the game for us because it was amazing to see that happen. You could feel the vibe, you could feel the team rolling, and then they paused and came back from 6-0. I knew what they were doing, we knew, we had good things to do against it, but they were just performing good, getting trades.

So Overpass went like that, and honestly, Ancient, again it was just like Chengdu. It was just a, 'Let's see what happens,' right? Like they don't really play the map much, and if there's a chance they're kinda bad on a map it's this one. And okay, it might not be our best map, but playing against some of the greatest, sometimes playing a non-standard might be good for you.

So we had our chances on Ancient too, definitely some moments. yuurih's clutch was amazing to see and I felt like we could steamroll from there, but then ZywOo showed up, got three kills right after, and got a good situation for them once again. It's just how it is. Last time we beat them on Ancient, the match kinda went the same way, but we were the ones actually finishing up on the line. So it's part of the game. We came to play and you never know what's going to happen, and this time they were better.

Do you think the Ancient pick was a bit of desperation from you, in that sense?

Honestly, more of a ballsy move, I would say. But looking at the options as well, you could pick Nuke, start T side, you could pick Anubis, start CT side, so the maps that were up there for us to play, they were difficult maps as well. And then we're a team that doesn't want to have any regrets, so a lot people wanted to play Ancient, wanted to try it again, and then we just went for it.

You mentioned that when you were going up 6-0, the crowd is yelling, they're hyped, but once Vitality started winning, the crowd was silent. Did that play into your minds at all?

No, not really. Of course you already feel it's difficult because the other team is doing better things than you, is ahead of you, but the crowd going silent, I don't think it's something like, 'Oh my god, now they're not cheering for us.' It's just something that can add up and make you feel better, but I don't think it has the power to really let you down. Unless you, someway somehow, are so connected to the crowd that your goal is to make them happy, and not to really play the game. During the game we are focused on the next step, on the next job, and I don't think that's really a factor when it comes down [to it].

A big thing during this event was you announcing your retirement. You spoke about it in Portuguese at length, but are there any thoughts you wanted to share in English in terms of the way you came to this decision and having this 'Last last last dance' for the end of the year?

(smiles) Yeah, for me, the last six years have been crazy because it's 23 years playing now, so I think it's quite reasonable that to stop something like that, you kinda need some time. It would be weird to just (snaps fingers), 'Next week, I'm gonna be someone else.' So it's been a tough process for me mentally, personality-wise as well.

When I decided to go for the Last Dance with my friends on Imperial, I really just wanted to play with people that I really like, and we were just doing... quite good, for someone who was only looking to have fun. And that was kinda where we lost ourselves because we were like, 'Okay, this is serious as hell, where are we going?' (laughs) and then I decided to really go for FURIA to give it a last try, in a sense where I wanted to try competitively to see how I can stand with the best players.

I decided to really go for FURIA to give it a last try, in a sense where I wanted to try competitively to see how I can stand with the best players

In the end, when I was about to leave as well, you know what? 'Why not try international, why don't you change all the roles?' Boom, the team was great, so how can you leave like that, right? And that's why it felt like I took long to really decide, it's because the things that were happening in my career went that way, and now I wanted to extend to play with these guys a little bit more because I really like them. They're great players, we love the team that we created, and I feel like I also needed some time to make the right replacement.

We have a team that's playing for the top now since last year, it's been what, eight or nine months now, and replacing my job is not an easy task. Now that we have six or seven months to really finish the job and start looking slowly into options, I think we can make another great move like we did when we bring them. So all those things played a role, and that's pretty much career-wise how the decision-making went.

How involved in the process are you going to be in finding that replacement and helping the team evolve after you leave?

Yeah, so basically, one of the things I wanted to do is to really stay at FURIA in many capacities. One of them is going to be to really look at the CS division and what we can do to stay at the top, and the first real job is, 'Okay, who the hell is replacing you and who is going to be that person?'

So I'm going to connect the dots with the players we have, with the other minds, we're going to see what the options are. Talk with guerri, talk with sidde, the coach, the board, and see who we can get for next year. But I think this talk is really important as the end of the year approaches because right now, we just have more than seven months to play, so it's too early to be talking about these things.

And obviously, you will leave behind an incredible legacy in the game when you do retire at the end of the year. Are there any parting words you wanted to give for this interview as it will be your last time competing for a championship in Brazil?

Yeah, I really wanted to win a tournament here. It would be amazing to lift a trophy in front of my own people, in front of my crowd, but you don't get to choose those things. They just either happen or they don't, and it's not something that I really regret or I feel like is going to be missing.

I felt like I achieved a lot of things in Counter-Strike, the game really changed my life, changed the life of my family. I helped with building and shaping the game in my country, so there are so many things that went my way that, if I really decided, 'Oh, that tournament, if I had won, or if I had won in that place,' I think that is more for the ego than for the soul, you know? So I'm fine with whatever happens, and I just want to enjoy these last couple of months that I have to be playing with these guys.

I'm looking forward to the new cycle next year as well. I know for a fact that I want to stay in Counter-Strike, I want to help people to change their lives through Counter-Strike and other games, and with the voice that I built over the years, I have so many opportunities to do cool stuff and enable people to live their dreams in gaming as well.
