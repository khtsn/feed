The Comedy, and the Horror, of the Infertility Plot

Critic’s Notebook

Onscreen, assisted reproductive technology is a double-edged device, representing women’s empowerment, or their exploitation.

“Scrambled” is a romantic comedy about a woman who falls in love with her decision to freeze her eggs. Nellie, a 34-year-old perma-bridesmaid, is wasted and alone at yet another wedding when she is struck by the fear that her fertility may peak before her romantic situation is resolved.

The conventional romantic comedy may culminate in marriage, but “Scrambled” leads Nellie toward a procedure that extends the timeline of her own marriage plot. Nellie (Leah McKendrick, who also writes and directs the film) gets her happy ending from an embryology lab. “You were no accident,” she tells one of her cryogenically preserved eggs. “You were one of the most intentional things that I have ever done.”

Reproductive technologies are increasingly assisting in human conception (even as the Alabama Supreme Court has complicated their use), and they have become familiar narrative devices, too. Their meaning is double-edged. “Scrambled,” with its oddball cheer, gives fertility treatments an empowering gloss. But an emerging horror genre sharpens the same technologies into instruments of exploitation, turning clinics into torture chambers and doctors into demons. The deus ex machina of assisted reproduction can be a blessing or a curse, depending on the god who sent the machine.

After swigging from the sentimental techno-optimism of “Scrambled,” I chased it with a wave of recent downers: I watched “False Positive,” the 2021 horror movie in which Lucy (Ilana Glazer) is subdued by a creepy fertility clinic; “Dead Ringers,” the 2023 limited series in which Rachel Weisz plays a pair of twin gynecologists; and “American Horror Story: Delicate,” the latest installment of the FX horror anthology series about an actress (Emma Roberts) who attempts to secure a baby and an Oscar with the help of her ambiguously sinister publicist (Kim Kardashian).

As I watched these horror stories, I found myself counting their clichés on both hands. In the standard fertility-horror plot, a wealthy white couple will report to an experimental clinic. Its staff will forgo scrubs for bespoke costumes resembling clerics or Stepford wives. An inscrutable and potentially supernatural ultrasound reading will occur. A woman will struggle to conceive, and this difficulty will be blamed on her careerism. She will be instructed to ingest strange tinctures and coached to mistrust her own mind. Her terror will be dismissed as “pregnancy brain” or “hormones.” Her pain will be denied. Her male partner will collude with a male doctor behind her back. Her female friend will be in on it, too. In the end, her pregnancy will be simulated, sabotaged or terminated without her knowledge or consent.

Nurse’s costumes aside, there’s nothing inherently false about these notes, which exaggerate the bewilderment and stress that many report experiencing during these procedures. “Scrambled” hits many of the same points from an off-kilter angle. Egg freezing is a challenging subject for comedy, and that leads Nellie through some tender and unpredictable terrain, though the ambiguities of her experience are ultimately smoothed over with an uplifting pop soundtrack.

The fertility horror plot suffers from the opposite problem: Stories of women’s pain and violation in the medical system are so well established, they can come to feel bland and routine.

As “False Positive” plods through its paces, it flattens the emotional range of fertility treatment into a lifeless exercise. Glazer, who emerged as such a bright avatar of young womanhood in “Broad City,” is thoroughly subjugated here, all of her light dimmed to accommodate the movie’s disempowering message. And the central twist to her story (Glazer has one of the three writing credits) is so predictable, “Law & Order” ripped it from the headlines decades ago. It’s horrible, but it’s not surprising. At least “American Horror Story,” an empire built on the efficient extraction of horror clichés, leans into that predictability. The central feeling produced by “Delicate” is a cozy familiarity with sadistic doctors and pliant victims.

These stories twist fertility clinics into dens of evil, and that choice lands differently now that conservative lawmakers are hammering the same themes. The Alabama Supreme Court recently decided that the state’s frozen embryos have the status of “extrauterine children,” frozen in what it calls a “cryogenic nursery.” When Chief Justice Tom Parker wrote in a concurring opinion, “human life cannot be wrongfully destroyed without incurring the wrath of a holy God,” he sounded as if he was smiting I.V.F. patients. The decision has put fertility care in the state at risk, mooring panicked hopeful parents in what The Cut called “Embryo Purgatory.”

The power dynamics of the fertility industry are ever-shifting, and it is “Dead Ringers,” the gender-swapped remake of the 1988 David Cronenberg film, that is most committed to exploring their layers. In the original, Jeremy Irons plays the twin gynecologists Eliot and Beverly Mantle, who perform fertility treatments on women whom they also pursue sexually and romantically. In the update, the Mantles are played by Rachel Weisz, her hair parted two different ways. This choice is both modern (most OB-GYNs practicing in America are now women) and deft. It blurs the line between victims and perpetrators, cracking open a space for a more complete investigation into the relationship between women’s bodies and the medical technologies that work on them.

In the show, Eliot is a sexy and demented techno-futurist who wants to grow babies in her lab, and Beverly is a careful and empathetic clinician who wants to grow babies inside herself. (She is, in fact, a patient of Eliot’s.) The twins have a symbiotic relationship (building the same clinic, pursuing the same women), and their dependency challenges the distinctions between an empowering procedure and an exploitative one, an altruistic motive and a cash grab, a good twin and a bad twin.

Most figures of consequence in the show — the doctors, their patients, their craven pharmaceutical company investor — are women, suggesting that this whole problematic system can be regenerated by the complicity and visibility of female stakeholders. As she plays both Eliot and Beverly off one another, Weisz appears to be debating herself, drawing out the complexities of the field of assisted reproduction. In the show, women are at once patient and consumer, entitled and exploited, natural and artificial, seen and erased, hormonal and sane. Its topsy-turvy moral landscape makes it the rare entry into the genre that’s actually fun to watch.

I thought of “Dead Ringers” as I watched a very different program: “Paris in Love,” a reality show that follows Paris Hilton and her husband, Carter Reum. In its second season, Hilton and Reum decide to have a child by way of a gestational surrogate. Here, all of the horror tropes are reconstituted as a comedy of manners. In the days before her baby is born from a woman who is neither shown nor identified, we watch Hilton float through her airless mansion, drifting from sunglass closet to medispa. She seems to appear as a pure, glittering surface, even as her body is portrayed as a site of profound suffering and alienation: abused in a youth correctional facility, exposed by paparazzi, skewered by the media, preserved by aesthetic devices, insulated by wealth.

Over the course of the show, “Paris in Love” attempts to pull Hilton’s posh alien bearing into alignment with the cultural ideal of motherhood. This leads to some dystopian high jinks. In order to simulate a maternal connection to the baby in utero, Hilton asks her surrogate to watch her performance on her previous reality show, “The Simple Life.” She and her husband choose to keep the surrogate and her pregnancy secret, so they banish their fleet of domestic staff before the birth, leaving Hilton befuddled in front of their washing machine, which she must learn to use herself.

Hilton chose to use a surrogate, she says, to shield her unborn child from the stress and exposure that would attend a pregnancy in her own body. It is through assisted reproductive technology that she performs the intimate physical work of new motherhood. She uses her own eggs to create an embryo that is transferred to the surrogate, and the physical process of egg retrieval becomes a stand-in for the discipline and sacrifice that pregnancy represents. She describes her punishing schedule of hormone injections, and invites the camera to watch her slowly plunge the burning liquid into her abdomen. Just like for Nellie in “Scrambled,” Hilton’s thumb on the needle represents her choice to have children on her own terms.

In Hilton’s material world, the relationship between reproductive choices and consumer ones is hard to ignore. She is undergoing many rounds of egg retrievals, she says, because though she has already created “a whole football team” of male embryos she could use to create future children, she really wants to make a girl in the next round. At the same time, the paid work of her gestational surrogate is totally obscured. When Hilton meets her newborn baby in the hospital — a nurse delivers him to her room, already diapered and swaddled — all the messy traces of his birth have been washed away. I watched the whole show with a numb horror, but when I saw the baby, I couldn’t help it, I cried.
