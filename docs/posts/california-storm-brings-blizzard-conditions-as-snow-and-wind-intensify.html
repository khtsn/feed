California Storm Brings Blizzard Conditions as Snow and Wind Intensify

Snow, high winds and intense rain hit parts of the state Friday. Blizzard warnings were in effect until Saturday afternoon in the mountains of four California counties.

Eduardo Medina, Judson Jones and Vik Jolly

Judson Jones is a meteorologist and reporter for The Times. Vik Jolly is a reporter based in Southern California.

Published Feb. 24, 2023Updated Feb. 25, 2023, 2:27 a.m. ET

Whiteouts in the mountains of Southern California. Heavy rain, violent winds and major flooding in Los Angeles and other cities. Snow in places that had not seen it for decades.

The weather lashing California on Friday presented a vivid contrast to the sun-drenched images typically associated with the state. Instead of clear blue skies, millions of residents looked outside to see sheets of snow and rain.

“This is definitely rare,” said Kristen Stewart, a meteorologist with the Weather Service in Los Angeles.

It was dangerous, too. At elevations above 2,000 feet, the storm was a true blizzard, making travel hazardous along mountain roads. And in Los Angeles County, the nation’s most populous, intense rain and high winds rattled residents late into the evening. A flash flood warning was in effect for the cities of Los Angeles, Glendale and Santa Clarita until 2 a.m. Saturday.

On Friday night, a county official said that a vehicle had driven off the road and into a flood control area managed by the county, resulting in one death. It was not immediately clear if the death was storm-related, said the official, Kerjon Lee of Los Angeles County Public Works.

Meteorologists expected additional snow in the mountains on Saturday, along with thunderstorms in lower elevations that could flood more roads and produce hail and tornadoes. Millions of people in the West, mainly in California and Nevada, were under winter weather alerts on Friday night. A National Weather Service forecast warned of potential power outages and tree damage.

Warnings of possible blizzard conditions — meaning winds above 35 miles an hour, considerable snowfall and drastically reduced visibility — were scheduled to be in place until late Saturday afternoon for the mountains of Los Angeles, Ventura, Santa Barbara and San Bernardino Counties.

There was also a high risk for avalanches across the Sierra Nevada on Friday into Saturday morning, according to the Sierra Avalanche Center. And in the San Francisco Bay Area, forecasters said that subfreezing temperatures could create black ice on roads overnight.

Officials across California warned of the potential impact on the state’s residents.

Ms. Stewart of the Weather Service said a concern was that people not used to driving in the rain would venture out on potentially flooded roads. Mayor Karen Bass of Los Angeles said on Twitter that the storm was “a matter of life or death for many of our unhoused neighbors.”

The worst of the storm was not expected to arrive until Saturday, but impacts were already being widely felt as midnight neared. There was heavy flooding in the San Fernando Valley and 26 inches of snow in Mount Wilson, north of Los Angeles. About 80,000 electricity customers in California, mostly in the northern part of the state, were without power.

Los Angeles International Airport also saw a record 1.23 inches of rain on Friday, and the downpour was expected to intensify overnight.

In another sign of how unusual this winter storm was, snow or graupel was seen falling Thursday morning near the Hollywood sign, the Weather Service Office in Los Angeles said, and a brief flurry was again spotted there on Friday.

A person standing in downtown Los Angeles can see a 10,600-foot peak that will typically have snow on it, said Andrew Rorke, a forecaster at that office. But by Saturday, that snow will extend much farther down the mountain, underscoring the strength of the storm.

“Everything is adding up for a major snow event,” he said.

On Thursday, the storm paralyzed parts of Oregon, trapping some drivers for hours while others abandoned their vehicles on the side of the road. At least three highways in the state were closed.

Rick Graves, a spokesman for the Portland Fire Department, confirmed that a baby in Portland died at a hospital after firefighters responded to a medical call just after 6 a.m. on Thursday of a child not breathing. The ambulance had been delayed, in part, because of poor conditions on the roads that were covered in two inches of “bulletproof ice,” Mr. Graves said.

On Friday in Southern California, areas between 2,500 and 4,500 feet were expected to receive up to a foot of snow, and up to five inches of rain were forecast to fall along the coasts and valleys. Blizzard conditions at the higher end of that elevation included winds of up to 80 miles per hour, reduced visibility and “treacherous” whiteout conditions, said Eric Boldt, a Weather Service meteorologist in Oxnard.

In the Bay Area, Mount Hamilton had already received more than a foot of snow as of Friday evening. Calistoga, a city in Napa Valley, had gotten about eight inches.

As the storm intensified, it snarled major transportation arteries, prompting road closures, high wind warnings and advisories for drivers to use chains on their tires.

Parts of Interstate 5, a major north-south route that runs from the border with Mexico to Canada, were shut down in areas in Siskiyou and Shasta Counties in the northern part of the state. The Grapevine, a 40-mile stretch of I-5 from northern Los Angeles County to Kern County, was shut down on Friday morning, and the authorities said there was no estimated time for when it might reopen.

The weather was unusually cold, too: The overnight forecast late Friday was for temperatures in the 20s and 30s in the Bay Area and along the Central Coast.

“This is the coldest I’ve seen in a while,” Joe Merriam, who has lived in Corona, about 50 miles southeast of Los Angeles, for 30 years, said earlier in the day. “This is very unusual — very crazy.”

The West Coast can be one of the hardest places in the country to predict weather because of the lack of observations over the Pacific Ocean, and the intensity of the latest storm caught many people — including forecasters — by surprise.

But forecasters across California already know this much: The storm won’t be the last of the winter, because another one is expected early next week.
