Stocks Gain After Trump Says He Has No Plans to Oust Powell

Remarks on the Fed and tariffs by President Trump and his Treasury secretary fueled a rebound in stocks.

Published April 22, 2025Updated April 23, 2025, 10:09 a.m. ET

President Trump’s remark that he had “no intention” of firing the Federal Reserve chair, Jerome H. Powell, lifted markets on Wednesday, offering some relief from trade war tensions that have gripped investors.

The S&P 500 gained nearly 3 percent in early trading on Wednesday, extending the gain from the day before, when the market jumped 2.5 percent.

Mr. Trump’s statement late Tuesday, along with earlier reports that Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent told investors that he expected the tariff standoff with China to ease “over the very near future,” combined to send stocks sharply higher.

The rally came after a steep slide in global markets on Monday, echoing the whiplash earlier this month when Mr. Trump paused many of his “reciprocal” tariffs hours after they were imposed, turning a steep decline into a forceful gain.

“We have been seeing deep down days in stocks reversed the next day by big up days, leading to a sideways market,” said Gaurav Mallik, chief investment officer at Pallas Capital Advisors. “Additional progress on trade deals would help to end the sideways market.”

The S&P 500, the main benchmark for U.S. stocks, sits about 9 percent below its level on Jan. 20, when Mr. Trump took office. The index has fallen about 11 percent from its peak in mid-February, and 4 percent since Mr. Trump’s April 2 tariff announcement.

Wall Street remains captive to the latest statements from the White House. “The erratic threaten-retreat-threaten-retreat cycle has economic consequences,” Paul Donovan, the chief economist of UBS Global Wealth Management, wrote in a note. “The uncertainty this causes may impact consumer and business decision-making.”

Elsewhere in the markets:

  • The technology-heavy Nasdaq Composite index rose almost 4 percent. Amazon’s stock jumped more than 7 percent and Meta gained 6 percent, moves worth hundreds of billions of dollars in market value, given the size of the tech giants.

  • Shares of Tesla rose about 4 percent, even though the automaker reported a steep drop in profit the day before. Elon Musk, the company’s chief executive, said he would spend less time in Washington; his involvement in the Trump administration, competition from Chinese carmakers and a lack of new models has contributed to a slump in sales.

  • Markets gained across much Asia and Europe. Japan’s Nikkei 225 rose 1.9 percent; Hong Kong’s Hang Seng was up 2.5 percent; and in Germany, the DAX jumped 2.8 percent.

  • The dollar regained some of its recent losses. An index of the dollar against a basket of major currencies rose 0.5 percent.

  • The price of 10-year Treasury bonds rose strongly, a sign that investors were more willing to buy U.S. debt. The yield, which moves inversely to price, dropped to 4.3 percent.

  • Gold, which hit a record $3,500 an ounce on Tuesday, eased back. It fell to around $3,300 on Wednesday, down more than 3 percent for the day.

Danielle Kaye is a business reporter and a 2024 David Carr Fellow, a program for journalists early in their careers.
