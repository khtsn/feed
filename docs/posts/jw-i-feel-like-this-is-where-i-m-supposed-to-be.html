JW: "I feel like this is where I'm supposed to be"

At PGL Bucharest EYEBALLERS are playing the most significant LAN event since the organization's revival in 2022, serving as part of a new wave of Swedish talent that is starting to bubble up through Europe's online scene.

At the helm, however, is grizzled veteran Jesper "⁠JW⁠" Wecksell, a man who won the first-ever Major nearly 13 years ago. Despite the fact his team opened their campaign with a loss against The MongolZ, JW was in good spirits when he spoke to HLTV.

"I think it was not the best game but also not the worst from our side," JW told us in the aftermath of the defeat. "A lot of lessons to be learned I think, but all in all it was our first game here, and the first game of the tournament is usually a bit rocky, especially for our young group. All in all I'm not disappointed."

For his own part, JW is "very happy to be back" at a LAN event of this stature, and told HLTV he is still motivated to earn an opportunity to play in front of fans despite his long career. "I still feel like I want to play in a big arena one more time at least before I call it quits," he said. "So we'll see if I make that happen."

JW also spoke to HLTV about the state of the Swedish scene, the difficulties of grinding through the tier-two scene, and how his young squad are adapting to the unfamiliar environment at PGL Bucharest.

Obviously a tough game, what are your thoughts on that match with The MongolZ?

I think it was not the best game but also not the worst from our side. I think we could have done stuff way better, but also obviously stuff way worse. We were a bit too sloppy on the anti-ecos today, and on Overpass I think we had a decent grip of the match, but we lost both pistols, won a force-buy then straight lost their force again, so it's tough. You can't really afford that when you're here to try and upset a team like The MongolZ.

So a lot of lessons to be learned I think, but all in all it was our first game here, since we got a walkover in the first one, and the first game of the tournament is usually a bit rocky, especially for our young group. All in all I'm not disappointed, but I'm also not very very happy.

I am kind of impressed with how calm and good our communication was in certain situations, for a tournament of this size

Like you say, you have a young group. How are they handling this environment, because I imagine it's a bit different to what they are used to?

I think they are handling it good. I am kind of impressed with how calm and good our communication was in certain situations, for a tournament of this size. Maybe it helps that they know I have done this a million times before, they know they have someone to rely on or fall back on. I'm impressed by them, but I think we can all step up our game going forward.

You say you've done this a lot of times; how does it feel to be back at a tournament like this?

It feels amazing. Obviously it was quite a few years ago, but I feel like this is where I'm supposed to be. Hopefully we can do some good results here, climb some VRS ranking and get more opportunities in the future. I feel very happy to be back and hopefully I can do the best out of it.

I mean it's been hell, kinda. It's very rough out there

You've been grinding away now in the tier-two scene for quite a while with the EYEBALLERS organization, what's that been like for you? You had a long career before this in the tier-one scene, what's it been like grinding tier-two?

I mean it's been hell, kinda. It's very rough out there. When you are in the tier-one scene you can expect a certain kind of playstyle from every matchup, people tend to play Counter-Strike in a certain way, everyone sees the game pretty much the same. Where we're grinding in tier-two, one day it's full rush through smokes, one day it's full rush through Molotovs, one day it's full camping in T spawn. Playstyles are all over the place and you have to be ready for so much, so it's very hard to be very very consistent I feel like in tier-two.

Other than that, it's so individually like… you need to be good on the day, and you can't be good every day. A lot of games cost you because simply they have a better day, and that's how Counter-Strike is, and that's really rough in tier-two because there are so many games that are so important. You can almost afford not to lose any, but obviously you are going to have to lose some. It's been very rough, but getting this opportunity at this kind of event gives some hope, feeling like it's worth it, everything you put into it. I said it many times before but I still feel like I want to play in a big arena one more time at least before I call it quits. So we'll see if I make that happen.

Yeah I definitely think Sweden is back. We're finally having some good teams be able to compete with very good teams

A lot is being said about the Swedish scene in general at the moment. Obviously back in the day in CS:GO, Sweden was a bit of a leading light, but then it had a rough few years. Now it feels like the grassroots in Sweden are starting to make a bit of a comeback; what are your thoughts?

Yeah I definitely think Sweden is back. We're finally having some good teams be able to compete with very good teams. I think what we are lacking now is the kind of entry level teams, I think we need more players to get introduced to the game, because in a year or two I think we're going to run out of talents, which is obviously not good.

We have established a good ground now for this level we are at now, but not Sweden also has to start working on the lowest level within the scene, the really young guns who are people going to school, stuff like that. It's a good sign for Swedish CS, but if we don't step up on that part very soon, I think we're going to fall into the same trap very soon again.

I feel like I want to always bring my knowledge on, to help the scene survive within Sweden

Like I said, you've had a long career and you mentioned earlier that you wanted to play in one more big arena. I wanted to ask; over such a long career, what motivates you now to keep going? You're one of the few people from your era still grinding away and still going for it.

The biggest motivation I have is still getting the opportunity to play in front of fans again, that's what it's always been for me, to entertain people. To me this is an entertainment sport, that's the biggest key in this game I feel like. That's my biggest motivator.

Also I feel like I want to always bring my knowledge on, to help the scene survive within Sweden. I think we have done a very poor job at doing that, so I feel some kind of obligation to do that. All in all I think the biggest thing for me is to get a chance to play in front of fans again, hear the screams, get that good vibe and put on a show for people that pay a ticket to see you play. That's the biggest motivation to me.
