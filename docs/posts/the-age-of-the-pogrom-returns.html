The Age of the Pogrom Returns

Nov. 8, 2024, 3:41 p.m. ET

Contributing Opinion Writer

“These are the times that try men’s souls,” Thomas Paine wrote in 1776. Today, that would include the souls of women, among many other despondent Harris-Walz supporters.

George Washington made sure Paine’s words in his pamphlet The American Crisis (a kind colonial Substack newsletter) were read aloud to his battered troops at Valley Forge before they crossed the Delaware and eventually won the Revolutionary War.

As battle-weary Kamala Harris volunteers consider whether to return to the fray after a major defeat or become mere “sunshine patriots,” they should take note, as Washington did, of smaller but highly significant victories.

A longtime rule of thumb in politics is that a superior get-out-the-vote operation does not guarantee a win but it’s usually worth a point or two in the final results. That continued to be true in 2024, where Harris’s ground game was much stronger than Donald Trump’s.

So why didn’t it produce better outcomes? Trump compensated with devastating advertising (especially on trans high school students playing girls’ sports) and effective messaging on podcasts and social media.

This worked against Harris but was less successful further down the ballot, thanks to Democratic volunteers. While overall turnout appears to be slightly lower than in 2020 (the totals aren’t in yet), it has been higher than average in battleground states, which led to smaller-than-average margins and shorter coattails there for Trump.

While Harris has lost or is losing in all the battleground states, her troops helped turn out ticket-splitters who saved Senator Tammy Baldwin in Wisconsin, as well as other Democratic Senate candidates including Elissa Slotkin in Michigan, and, quite likely, Ruben Gallego in Arizona and Senator Jacky Rosen in Nevada — all by only a point or two.

Right now, it looks like the Senate will have 53 Republicans, a huge difference from a potential 57, which would have put Democrats in a deep, almost filibuster-proof hole.

So all the tapped-out donors, doorbell ringers, postcard senders and phone bank-sters need to know that without their efforts, the next decade would be much worse for the country, with Trumpist judges degrading the judiciary and reams of destructive bills passing the Senate.

With only four votes to spare (JD Vance will break any ties), Republicans now have less hope of a national abortion ban. They won’t have the support of Lisa Murkowski, Susan Collins and at least two or three other G.O.P. senators from red states that have approved abortion rights ballot initiatives.

The same will most likely go for gutting the civil service, abandoning NATO, confirming Robert F. Kennedy Jr. to a cabinet post or elevating Judge Aileen Cannon to the Supreme Court. Probably not enough Senate votes.

In a dispiriting quirk, the Senate map in 2026 offers little chance for a Democratic pickup of more than a couple of seats at best.

The 2028 election will bring hope of returned Democratic control. But it wouldn’t if Senate Republicans could now boast an eight-vote majority.

Yes, all that money and toil from Harris supporters didn’t help elect a Democratic president. But we owe those volunteers what Paine called our “love and thanks.” They helped democracy stay alive to fight another day.

Nov. 8, 2024, 11:51 a.m. ET

In April 1903 an antisemitic mob rampaged through the city of Kishinev, which was then part of the Russian Empire and is now known as Chisinau, the capital of Moldova. Nearly 50 Jews were murdered, women and girls were raped, and some 1,500 homes were destroyed. Among the survivors were my paternal great-grandparents Barnet and Bessie Ehrlich, who decided then and there to emigrate to America with their children.

This week there was another pogrom in Europe, this time in Amsterdam. “Barbarians on scooters are riding through our capital city hunting Israelis and Jews,” David van Weel, the Dutch minister of justice and security, wrote on X. He was referring to an orgy of violent attacks against Israeli soccer fans, who had come to the city to watch a match on Thursday between Maccabi Tel Aviv and Ajax Amsterdam.

“They started hitting us. They broke my face, knocked out a tooth, cut my lip,” an Israeli fan, Yaakov Masri, told Israeli media after locking himself in his hotel room, with a table to block the door. He said that he and his son were set upon “by around 15 young Arab men, some of whom were armed with knives and clubs,” according to the Israeli newspaper Haaretz.

Some news accounts have pointed to provocative behavior by rowdy Israeli fans, including taking down a Palestinian flag and chanting anti-Arab slogans (in Hebrew), for contributing to the mayhem. They also noted heightened tensions connected to the war in Gaza.

Maybe. But that explanation ignores the many years of rising and virulent antisemitism in Europe that preceded the war, much of it within Muslim communities, along with evidence that the attacks were carefully and cunningly coordinated.

“Amsterdam authorities have contacted taxi platforms such as Uber and Bolt to discuss how drivers may have used the apps to screen Israeli phone numbers during the violence,” The Times of London reported.

To their credit, Dutch and other European leaders have been outspoken in condemning the pogrom, which came on the eve of the 86th anniversary of Kristallnacht. Willem-Alexander, king of the Netherlands, compared Thursday night to the country’s failure to protect Jews in World War II, and Olaf Scholz, the German chancellor, wrote that “Jews must be able to feel safe in Europe.”

That’s a fine thought, though whether it will make any difference to the deteriorating situation for Jews in Europe remains to be seen. “Most of those arrested were later released,” The New York Times reported, and El Al sent planes to bring Israelis home. If a reminder were ever needed of why Israel, for all of its travails, came into existence in the first place, this latest pogrom was it.

My advice to Europe’s besieged Jewish communities: Remember what Kishinev foreshadowed — and please get out while you still can.

Foreign leaders have quickly lined up in the last two days to congratulate Donald Trump on his election victory, but behind the diplomatic rituals are fears, hopes and many questions over what the return of “America first” will mean for a troubled world. The refrain, everywhere, has been: Do not try to predict Trump’s actions.

The leader with the most to worry about was among the most effusive in his congratulations: President Volodymyr Zelensky of Ukraine wrote on X that his first call with Trump on Wednesday was “excellent”; earlier he had hailed Trump’s “peace through strength” approach, which, he said, would “undoubtedly benefit” both countries. In fact, during the presidential campaign Trump had little nice to say about Zelensky or Ukraine, and there was rampant speculation that his new administration would push for a cease-fire that could leave a fifth of Ukraine under Russian control.

Not surprisingly, the Kremlin was said to be relatively happy. A source in Vladimir Putin’s administration told the independent Meduza news site that the first Trump administration may have laid new sanctions on Russia, but Trump was still somehow “our guy.” Still, Putin himself did not line up to congratulate Trump. “Let’s not forget that we are talking about an unfriendly country, which is both directly and indirectly involved in the war against our state,” said his spokesman.

Jerusalem rejoiced, or at least at the offices occupied by Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu and his right-wing ministers. Netanyahu had just fired his defense minister, Yoav Gallant, who shared the Biden administration’s calls for restraint in Gaza, and now saw the way open to run Israel’s wars and politics the way he and his nationalists wanted to. Netanyahu congratulated Trump on “history’s greatest comeback.”

The U.S.’s North American neighbors were less sanguine. Mexico’s new president, Claudia Sheinbaum, tried to reassure her citizens that “there’s nothing to worry about,” but everybody knew otherwise: Trump’s campaign promises included punishing tariffs, mass migrant deportations and more if Mexico fails to stop “the onslaught of criminals and drugs.”

Up north, feelings were also muddled. Canada’s prime minister, Justin Trudeau, congratulated “Donald” on his victory and said he looked forward to working with him. But Trudeau himself is in political trouble, and in the past he has been called a “far-left lunatic” and “two-faced” by Trump. “A lot of Canadians are feeling unsettled today,” said Deputy Prime Minister Chrystia Freeland.

In Europe, Germany was even more unsettled, in part because the news from Washington coincided with the (unrelated) collapse of Chancellor Olaf Scholz’s coalition. The Berlin government had cultivated special relations with the Biden administration in the interests of trans-Atlantic relations and the NATO alliance, neither of them pet projects of Trump.

The biggest question mark was China. Trump has called the Chinese leader, Xi Jinping, both “brilliant” and “dangerous,” and things could go either way. Trump pledged in his campaign to impose a devastating 60 percent tariff on Chinese imports, which would lead to a major trade war. But the Chinese also see potential for expanding their global clout in Trump’s unilateralism.

Nov. 6, 2024, 7:00 p.m. ET

The big rise in the value of the dollar on Wednesday looks like a vote of confidence in Donald Trump, who won the presidential election. But it’s not that simple.

There’s a long chain of causality from Trump’s victory to the dollar’s rise, which might seem convoluted to the average voter but is instantly recognizable to professionals in the financial markets, and it has nothing to do with betting that Trump will be a good steward of the U.S. economy (although certainly some on Wall Street do believe that he will be).

Here’s the chain: The dollar rose mainly because Trump has promised to raise tariffs sharply. Higher tariffs would raise the prices Americans pay for imported products. That would lift inflation. The Federal Reserve would then have to fight the inflation by raising interest rates, or at least cutting rates less than it would have to otherwise. And higher interest rates on U.S. debt would attract global investors, raising the demand for dollars. Because traders expect that to happen, they aren’t waiting. They’re buying dollars now.

As I said, it’s convoluted but standard economics. As Olivier Jeanne and Jeongwon Son of Johns Hopkins University wrote in an economic working paper last month: “In theory, tariffs should be partially offset by a currency appreciation in the tariff-imposing country or by a depreciation in the country on which the tariff is imposed.” The dollar’s rise makes foreign goods less expensive, so it offsets some of the effect of the tariffs, which won’t make Trump and his economic advisers happy.

Wall Street economists put their finger on the connection between tariffs and the dollar in commentary on Wednesday. “A looming global tariff war is taking the U.S. dollar up to a one-year high,” David Rosenberg of Rosenberg Research in Toronto wrote. The U.S. economics team of Nomura, the Japanese securities firm, said that higher inflation from tariffs is likely to keep the Fed from cutting rates as quickly as it would otherwise. “We now expect just one Fed cut in 2025, with policy on hold until the realized inflation shock from tariffs has passed,” they wrote.

Bottom line: The stronger dollar is not going to make America great again.

Nov. 6, 2024, 4:52 p.m. ET

Less than 12 hours after the presidential election was called early Wednesday morning, Vice President Kamala Harris did what Donald Trump still can’t bring himself to do four years after his own loss: She conceded.

“Earlier today I spoke to President-elect Trump and congratulated him on his victory,” she said Wednesday afternoon, speaking before dejected supporters at Howard University in Washington. “I also told him that we will help him and his team with his transition, and that we will engage in a peaceful transfer of power.” That principle, she said, is what distinguishes democracy from monarchy or tyranny.

She performed this task with clarity and grace, a fairly low bar that used to be expected of all losing politicians in this country, and for a simple reason: When you live in a democracy, accepting your losses is what you have to do. Not because it’s easy, but because it’s hard.

Especially when it’s hard. Al Gore and Hillary Clinton conceded in the wake of their own agonizing losses in 2000 and 2016, even though they both won more votes than their opponents.

And yet somehow Trump gets a pass from this most basic requirement — not simply for refusing to acknowledge his own defeat in 2020, but also for lying about a “stolen” election, violating the laws and the Constitution, and then inciting a violent insurrection at the Capitol in an effort to overturn it. Despite all this, a few minutes after noon on Jan. 20, 2025, the federal criminal prosecution of him for this abuse of trust (not to mention for stealing and hoarding quantities of highly classified documents) will vanish into thin air.

So forgive me if I scoff at the “Where is Kamala?” memes that circulated on Wednesday before she went on camera. No one in America is worse at accepting defeat than Trump and his followers. And that’s because under his tutelage, most of the Republican Party has come to believe that the only legitimate elections are the ones that it wins.

Contrast this with all the conspiracy theories you are not hearing right now about Trump’s vote totals. For example, we learned Wednesday that Nye County, Nev., a Trump-friendly rural county, did not report many thousands of votes in its initial tally. It’s a significant oversight, as those votes could end up costing Democrats a hotly contested Senate seat.

Now imagine the parties were reversed: Republicans would be raging about fraud, with neither evidence nor good faith — as Trump did Tuesday afternoon, claiming there was “massive CHEATING” in Philadelphia even before the polls closed. There was not, of course, nor was there any fraud in Nye County; minor mistakes happen all the time in elections involving hundreds of millions of people. Yet because it’s the Republican candidate who will likely benefit from this mistake, everyone is suddenly quiet.

This is the world we now live in: One party follows basic rules and norms of democratic politics and loses, and the other lies, cheats and tries to steal — and wins. It’s reverse accountability. And it can’t be sustained.

That is, it can’t be sustained in a democracy that prizes fairness and faithfulness to the law. In America, by contrast, it may be the way things are for the foreseeable future.

Nov. 6, 2024, 2:12 p.m. ET

I’m glad my grandchildren are too little to understand what happened on Tuesday. Like a lot of kids in liberal families, they’re probably wondering why all the grown-ups they know are walking around muttering darkly to themselves, wiping away furtive tears and forcing a smile while trying to comprehend their future in a country that seems so alien.

Eventually our family will have to discuss it. We will have to help the kids make their way through a world made more coarse and less tolerant by the re-election of Donald Trump. We can hope that the values expressed by a majority of voters in this election will not be the kids’ values, that when they come of age they will cast their vote thinking about the common good, instead of as an instrument of divisiveness, bigotry and narrow personal interests.

It will be up to their generation to somehow reconstruct a consensus of fundamental decency. And the only way they will be able to do that is if we grown-ups find a way to move past despair, defeatism and outright bewilderment to demonstrate that we haven’t given up on this country and its promise. Don’t let your kids hear you joke about moving to Canada; let them hear you talk about sticking around and finding a way to keep this disaster from happening again to the country they will inherit.

They are the ones who will have to live under the rulings of a Supreme Court and a judiciary that will probably curtail many of the freedoms that so many of us have come to take for granted. They’re the ones who will watch wide-eyed as immigrants and people of color are demonized by an ascendant white nationalist movement that now has an ally in the White House. They will see a government intended to promote the public welfare turned into a weapon against the administration’s enemies in the bureaucracy, the news media and the culture.

And it’s up to us to show them that it doesn’t have to be that way. We will have to acknowledge, in some humility, that this country has failed tens of millions of our children’s neighbors whose bitterness is so palpable that they will cling to false promises. To counter that, in small acts and large, we and our political leaders will have to demonstrate that possibilities for progress still remain.

When a judge rejects a draconian order to deport millions of families, we should celebrate that act and explain why we still need jurists with a conscience. When Ukraine and possibly other countries hold out desperately against an emboldened Vladimir Putin, we should cite them as examples of courage.

And for the next generation’s sake, we must rebuild an education system that has failed its students, leaving too many Americans ignorant of their civic culture and too susceptible to lies and manipulation.

Canada is full, anyway; let’s reassemble a United States that no one wants to escape.

There are many things about Donald Trump’s enduring support that I understand in my gut, like opposition to the stupidest woke ideas, such as defunding the police, or the hunger for a strongman who could stop the winds of change. Many Americans feel like strangers in their own homes, thanks to illegal immigration, rapidly evolving social norms and a job market increasingly unsympathetic to the less educated.

But the thing I don’t understand, and will never understand, and will forever be appalled by, is the laughing.

Every time I watched a clip where Trump was riffing to the crowd about Liz Cheney or “fake news” reporters being shot, or telling people that Kamala Harris is “a shit vice president,” or when he approved of audience members who referred to Harris as a “ho,” you saw crowds of people surrounding him who were laughing uproariously at his crude and violent language.

The implicit message was that the joke is on us, on the rest of us — liberals or conservatives — who still think you can succeed by adhering to norms and playing by the rules, who still think you can succeed as a politician by inspiring your followers and not by insulting your opponents.

My wife, Ann, who is from a small town in Iowa whose schoolteachers she credits to this day for pounding good fundamentals of English grammar into her, shared with me this Washington Post article from Nov. 20, 2023, because it reflected an Iowa so unlike where she was raised that she couldn’t recognize the place:

The next day, Trump swooped into Iowa for his own event — where he lobbed insults, made crude references and casually tossed out baseless and false claims designed to belittle his opponents and critics in vicious terms. Children wandered around in shirts and hats with the letters “FJB,” an abbreviation for an obscene jab at President Biden that other merchandise spelled out: “F— Biden.”

The fact that Trump only seems to know how to win by denigrating others, and the fact that so many Americans knew this from his first time in office — and raucously welcomed the sequel — well, that bothers me. Because that kind of mind-set and behavior is what leads to a full-blown breakdown in norms, and when the norms go, no laws will protect you. That’s when I get scared. That’s when I feel we’ve passed through some normative membrane into a new and very dark room.

Trump could be so much more successful if he actually extended a hand to his opponents, showed them respect and shared credit. But the fact that his default option is always set on crudity or cruelty first — well, watch out.

Because he is going to discover that the problems we face as a country, from debt to competing with China to adapting to A.I. to absorbing extreme weather, are now so big and hard they can only be fixed together, not by him alone. And mocking half the country is not going to be a strategy for success.

In the end, without a change in his behavior, the joke won’t just be on the rest of us, but on his supporters as well.

Nov. 6, 2024, 11:22 a.m. ET

Some of the angriest people I have ever interviewed were parents who lived in blue areas of the country in 2021 and 2022. I wonder if their lingering rage fueled Donald Trump’s decisive win on Tuesday.

Their schools were still closed, even after teachers had been vaccinated against Covid. Their preschoolers were still wearing masks, even though, as I wrote at the time, there was compelling anecdotal evidence that masking was creating social and emotional delays. There was never any real explanation from public health authorities as to why children under 5 were masking in America, a practice that went against recommendations from the World Health Organization.

I remember these parents feeling both furious and politically adrift. They felt especially betrayed by the Democrats they had always voted for. Were they a pivotal part of the coalition that helped Trump win?

While early exit polls are almost always incomplete, I will be eager to see who pushed Trump over the edge in purple places like Bucks County, Pa., where parents sued the Pennsbury School District for continuing mask mandates after the state had lifted the requirement.

Kamala Harris and Democrats in general were the institutional candidates this cycle. If you were angry at any institution for any reason, Trump offered a more compelling argument. Some moderates would say that during the pandemic, teachers’ unions and school boards were mucking around far outside their scope while a true educational crisis was unfolding.

I spent a long evening earlier this year hearing from Michigan parents, who were livid that the school board in Ann Arbor was embroiled in an argument about passing a resolution calling for a cease-fire in Gaza and Israel rather than addressing chronic absenteeism or falling test scores.

Trump’s K-12 education plan is totally nonsensical and it involves the direct election of principals by parents, though there is no mechanism to make that happen. His overall message, though, was: “we will put PARENTS back in charge and give them the final say.”

And for some number of parents, the ones who felt completely out of control from 2020-22, that message may have had lasting appeal.

Mara Gay

Editorial Board Member, reporting from Detroit.

In recent weeks, volunteers for Kamala Harris have assembled in Detroit from states as far away as Washington and New York to knock on doors. Democrats have executed an exhaustive field operation in Michigan and other battleground states. Voters in Michigan have sometimes received up to a dozen text messages and phone calls a day reminding them to vote, some including a photograph of their polling sites.

We’ll know soon whether it was enough. But if Harris wins, these field operations may be the reason.

By Election Day though, many likely voters had already cast ballots, and some volunteers were met by residents who said they didn’t plan to vote at all.

“I don’t like this male-versus-female thing,” Herbert Miles, a 36 year-old Black man, told canvassers at his door in Detroit, explaining his decision not to vote. Behind him, his three small children pressed their faces against the window and smiled and waved at the canvassers. “I have two daughters and prefer Kamala,” he said. But then he added, “We don’t need a woman president to inspire them.” Miles said he hadn’t voted in 2020 either.

The threat to democracy isn’t only Trumpism, but also disaffection among wary voters in complicated times. Listening to these voters drives home in real time the vital importance of Black women to the Democratic coalition, and, increasingly, college-educated voters of all backgrounds.

When I asked Emma Mitchell, a 73-year-old Black woman from Detroit, why she was volunteering for Harris, Mitchell told a story about growing up in the Jim Crow South. Mitchell, who was raised in Louisiana, recalled an incident during a trip to Mississippi when she and her siblings were subjected to racial slurs and attacked when they tried to visit a burger shop. “We couldn’t even get a doughnut unless we knew a white person,” she said. Mitchell said she believed Trump wants to ban abortion in part because he is concerned about the decrease in the white birthrate.

In Ann Arbor, students at the University of Michigan said they understood the added weight of their vote in an election that was too close to call. Droves of students walked through campus wearing “I voted” stickers. But the campus was unusually quiet. Many students I spoke to said they were filled with anxiety about the possibility of Donald Trump returning to office.

“We were in fifth grade when he was elected,” Allison Jackson, a 19-year-old freshman from Dallas, said of Trump. Jackson told me that she remembered that time as “very dark.” She voted for Harris, as did most students I spoke with here.

Several students here said they had considered voting for Jill Stein because they were angry about American support for Israel’s war in Gaza. But all said that after doing their research, they had ultimately chosen to vote for Harris instead, in many cases because of women’s rights.

“I decided, we just need to get Trump out of the way, then we can deal with the rest of it,” Zoe Vorwald, 18, told me.

If Harris wins, it will be because of voters like these.

The protests in Israel began almost as soon as the word came down on Tuesday that Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu had fired Defense Minister Yoav Gallant. Crowds began to gather near the prime minister’s residence in Jerusalem and in Tel Aviv, Haifa, Beersheba and other towns to protest the firing.

The opposition leader, Yair Lapid, said it was an “act of madness” to sack the defense minister in the midst of a regional conflict against Hamas, Hezbollah and their Iranian patron and called on “all Zionist patriots” to take to the streets.

Netanyahu no doubt anticipated the reaction. The last time he fired Gallant, in March 2023, after the defense minister had publicly criticized Netanyahu’s legislative efforts to subvert the independence of the judiciary, huge street protests compelled the prime minister to rescind his order.

The Hamas attack on Oct. 7, 2023, and the massive Israeli assault in Gaza that followed temporarily pushed the personal animosity of Netanyahu and Gallant into the background. But the defense minister’s disagreements with Netanyahu over the conduct of the war, as well as on other issues — including the drafting of ultra-Orthodox Jews into the army (which Gallant supported) — became increasingly evident and rancorous.

Especially galling for Netanyahu was that the Biden administration, fed up with the prime minister’s refusal to heed Washington’s calls for restraint in Gaza, turned instead to dealing with Gallant. The Israeli public, too, having grown tired of Netanyahu’s machinations to stay in office at any cost to avoid prosecution on corruption charges, began viewing Gallant as the antithesis of Netanyahu and his ultranationalist allies.

Gallant was the no-nonsense politician who spoke out against the judicial changes; the defense minister who insisted on the priority of bringing home the hostages over an all-out, murderous campaign; the former commando who contradicted the ultranationalist hawks with the argument that “not every goal can be achieved by military action alone.”

The breaking point was apparently a letter Gallant sent last week to the government arguing that “the war is being conducted without a compass” and proposing new war goals for Gaza, Lebanon, Iran and the West Bank. This direct challenge to Netanyahu and his far-right allies drew an icy response from the prime minister, who said he found the letter “quite puzzling.” In a video posted on his X account, Netanyahu said he had fired Gallant because of a breakdown in trust and “significant differences in managing Israel’s war.”

Less puzzling was the timing of the firing: Election Day in the United States. Netanyahu no doubt counted on political fever in the United States to drown out any reaction from the Biden administration or critics of Israel’s brutality in Gaza. Israelis are also closely watching the election, the outcome of which will strongly affect their relations with America, and thus might be slow to once again take to the streets against Netanyahu.

Gallant, for his part, had only a brief, one-sentence, response, posted on X: “The security of the state of Israel is and always will be my life’s mission.”

Nov. 5, 2024, 3:33 p.m. ET

Opinion Columnist, reporting from South Fulton, Ga.

The nature of terror is that a threat doesn’t have to be fulfilled in order for it to work.

On Election Day here in Fulton County, Ga., the county with the largest Black population of any county in the state, authorities had to contend with several bomb threats aimed at polling places.

I met Khalid Kamau, the mayor South Fulton, at Feldwood Elementary School, one of the polling places in his city that was targeted with a threat. South Fulton bills itself as the “Blackest city in America.”

He said at that point, around noon, there had been seven bomb threats around the county, they had all been deemed “noncredible,” and none of them had resulted in a polling location being closed for more than 30 minutes. Brad Raffensperger, the Georgia secretary of state, said the source of the phony threats was in Russia.

About an hour later, the mayor texted:

“Just got word of another threat at SW Arts Ctr.” The Southwest Arts Center is where the City Council meets. He texted that the police and fire departments were on the scene.

When I asked Kamau, who is changing to the one-word name Kobi, what he made of the bomb threats, he said, “I think that there’s folks that are scared about people voting.” He continued, speaking specifically about the citizens of South Fulton:

“We’re the sons and daughters of people who have faced down lynch mobs, water cannons and canines to vote. So this isn’t going to turn us around.”

Even if there are no bombs — and we must all hope that these threats continue to ring hollow — the objective of the threats can still be achieved: the interruption of voting and an ambient danger associated with the casting of ballots.

It is impossible to know if these threats will have any measurable effect on voter turnout, but the tactic is one that many Black people remember in their bones. It represents the opponent of their enfranchisement using every available tactic to deter them from voting.

And in cases like this, you must ask yourself two questions:

If you believe your vote doesn’t matter, why would people go to such extreme lengths to keep you from casting it?

And who would benefit if people in this largely Black county had a lower turnout?

Nov. 5, 2024, 2:30 p.m. ET

Like everyone else on the planet, I’ve been wondering who will win this neck-and-neck nail-biter of an election. So I called up Shannon Mulcahy to ask how she’s voting.

Mulcahy, who lives in Whitestown, Ind., and lost her job as a steelworker when her plant moved to Mexico in 2017, became my personal bellwether on the American mood when I wrote a book about her factory closing and what became of all the workers in the years that followed.

I noticed during my research for that book that whenever she shrugged off some outrage like the Stormy Daniels affair, Donald Trump weathered the storm. Whenever she got angry about something Trump had done, like the child separation policy at the border, he was forced to change course. That’s because Mulcahy’s people are Trump people, the very essence of his base.

Mulcahy loved Trump in 2016 because he promised to renegotiate NAFTA, which she blamed for the loss of her job. By 2020, she had soured on him because of his behavior during the pandemic. She ended up protesting Trump at rallies and cast her ballot for Joe Biden. (It wasn’t her first time voting for a Democrat: She supported Barack Obama in 2008.)

So how is she feeling now?

Two weeks ago, she told me that she was torn about how to vote, much like the country itself. On the one hand, she likes Kamala Harris. “She’s tough,” she told me, after watching the debate. Mulcahy, a blue-collar feminist who had to fight for every opportunity she got in her male-dominated factory, believes in a woman’s right to choose and likes the idea of a female president. She doesn’t like what took place at the Capitol on Jan. 6.

On the other hand, she lost four close relatives, including a young niece, to fentanyl overdoses over these past four years. Fox News, and Trump, blamed the situation at the Mexican border for such deaths. And now groceries have become unaffordable. A family-size bag of Doritos now costs $8, she told me.

“I just feel like things were better when Republicans were in,” she said. As for Harris, Mulcahy said, “I feel like she’s had her chance,” adding: “She’s been in there four years. What’s going to be different this time?”

On Election Day, she answered my call just as she left her polling place.

“I wouldn’t be unhappy if Kamala won,” she told me. “But I just voted Republican, all the way.”

Working variously as a writer, producer, conductor and arranger for more than half a century, Quincy Jones — who died on Sunday in Los Angeles at the age of 91 — brought a jazzy originality and sophistication to recordings across genres.

One way to grasp his extraordinary range is to listen consecutively to three disparate recordings that bear his stamp. As Election Day approaches, I’ve been having trouble sleeping. So I thought it would be useful to try to separate out those things that keep me up at night from those that don’t.

What Doesn’t Concern Me: Vote Certification

There is no shortage of doomsday scenarios about electoral chaos floating around out there, and it’s not unreasonable to be concerned. We have already seen cases of officials refusing to certify vote totals and taking matters into their own hands because they don’t like the outcome.

The good news is that there are many buffers in place, more than in 2020. First, the decentralized nature of American elections means that no one person’s actions can spread beyond a certain area.

Second, all states, including swing states, have legally binding deadlines for certification of their vote tallies, most in late November. Meeting these deadlines is not a choice; it is a requirement.

Third, Congress in 2022 passed the Electoral Count Reform Act, which, among other things, sets expedited procedures for federal courts to hear election disputes, including cases in which a local official somewhere refuses to certify a vote count.

I’m not including all the poll workers and election officials who have left or been pushed out of their jobs since 2020, in part because of harassment or threats of violence. That brings me to:

What Does Concern Me: Violence

The history of American elections is replete with threats, intimidation and violence by those who apparently lacked the confidence that they could win through voting and majority rule. In recent decades, the most glaring examples of such behavior have come from Republicans.

There was the Brooks Brothers riot in 2000 that helped shut down the recount in the Miami-Dade area, the precursor to George W. Bush’s victory; the caravan of Trump supporters who aggressively surrounded a Biden campaign bus on a Texas highway; armed militia members standing near ballot drop boxes; and, of course, the attack on the U.S. Capitol that Donald Trump incited on Jan. 6, 2021, in an attempt to overturn the election and install himself as president against the will of the people.

This year the threats are pouring in, putting decent Americans in fear for their lives and undermining the basic functioning of civil society. And thanks to mass disinformation peddlers like Elon Musk, the threats and lies are being amplified and disseminated like never before. It’s not just Musk; YouTube and Facebook have essentially given up on trying to police the worst offenders on their platforms.

It is a tense and dangerous moment, and we can only hope it will end without more people getting hurt. The whole point of electoral politics is to resolve our differences peacefully and to accept our losses as well as our wins, knowing that there will soon be a chance to try again.

But when one party removes itself from that agreement and refuses to acknowledge defeat, bad things will inevitably happen.
