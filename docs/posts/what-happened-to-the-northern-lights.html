What Happened to the Northern Lights?

U.S.|What Happened to the Northern Lights?

https://www.nytimes.com/2025/06/02/us/northern-lights-storm-sunday.html

A geomagnetic storm that was forecast to produce streaks of colorful light across much of the country was weaker than expected.

June 2, 2025

The forecast looked promising for the northern lights to be visible across much of the United States on Sunday night.

From Michigan to Washington State and as far south as Alabama, people expected to be able to look up and see a light show, according to data from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration.

But the geomagnetic storm that was expected to make the lights visible across such a wide swath of the country weakened, and instead of a light show, many got a lesson in how storms can change course.

Sometimes the storms come as predicted, and sometimes the forecast changes.

What happened on Sunday night?

The northern lights display, also known as the aurora borealis, is set off by geomagnetic storms, which are caused by coronal mass ejections, or eruptions of materials on the sun’s surface. On Sunday morning, the storm forecast for Sunday night was rated as severe enough for people to start chasing the aurora borealis.

Some people in northern Europe, Wyoming and Canada did report seeing the northern lights on Sunday night. But the storm weakened, disappointing people elsewhere who had hoped to see a show.

The magnetic orientation of a geomagnetic storm can change “in a minute’s notice,” and the energy transfer can become less efficient, said Lt. Cmdr. Bryan R. Brasher, a project manager at the Space Weather Prediction Center at NOAA.

“To get the aurora, you need a very strong C.M.E. or several C.M.E.s,” he said, referring to coronal mass ejections, and for the “magnetic field to be southward and stay southward,” he said.

While there are multiple characteristics that influence the northern lights, a strong southern magnetic field is most favorable for creating a high-ranking geomagnetic storm. The colors of the northern and southern lights are then generated by the particles flying from the sun and interacting with that magnetic field.

Why is it so hard to forecast these storms?

We’re talking about a space storm here. It’s a far cry from trying to measure the chance of rain, Commander Brasher said.

Space weather is monitored using telescopes and other instruments, and the Space Weather Prediction Center has a five-level scale to rate geomagnetic storms. The storms are rated on a “G” scale of 1 to 5. The higher the rating, the more severe the storm. In May 2024, for example, a global northern lights show was ranked as G5.

Forecasters are looking for coronal mass ejections on the sun some 92 million miles away and rely on cameras to determine their trajectory and speed.

Despite those challenges, the forecasters are pretty good at it, he said.

Will there be another chance to see the northern lights on Monday?

The storms predicted for Monday are on the weaker end of the scale, ranked G1 and G2, Commander Brasher said, but that means there’s still a chance to see the northern lights overnight across parts of the northern United States, including Alaska, northern Montana and northern Minnesota. (The key word here? “Chance.”)

Make sure to keep an eye on the weather forecast, too. Clouds and rain are expected in northern Minnesota, which would hide the aurora borealis.

What’s the best way to chase the northern lights?

To start, go somewhere really dark away from city lights. Aim for a midnight viewing far from any light pollution, Commander Brasher said. (If the sky is cloudy, you are unfortunately out of luck; the clouds will block out the light show.)

“These things wax and wane very quickly,” even in a big geomagnetic storm, Commander Brasher said. Every once in a while there will be a burst of aurora and then it will go quiet for one to two hours.

So if you really want to see the northern lights, you’ll need to be patient. Keep an eye on the Space Weather Prediction Center’s aurora forecast, and hunker down.

“True aurora watchers are there for several hours,” he said.
