Israeli Forces Reached Gaza’s Main Hospital

Israeli forces advanced today to the gates of Gaza’s largest hospital, where doctors describe a dire situation: Fuel, medicine and food are running out for hundreds of patients and thousands of others sheltering.

Dozens of corpses are decomposing at the hospital, known as Al-Shifa, because there is no way to preserve or remove them, a chief nurse said. Doctors said they were desperately trying to keep premature babies warm after removing them from incubators that were now useless.

Israeli and American officials say Hamas uses hospitals in Gaza, including Al-Shifa, as shields for its fighters and says that militants have built a vast command complex under the hospital. Hamas and doctors at the hospital deny the existence of such a command center.

Israeli troops have reached at least two other hospitals in northern Gaza, stepping up their push to empty the facilities, according to Israeli military officials, as fighting around them intensifies.

The Supreme Court announced an ethics code

For the first time in its more-than-200-year history, the Supreme Court issued an official ethics code for the justices. The court has been under intense pressure to adopt such a code over the last year after a series of revelations in which justices received gifts that were never disclosed.

The code, laid out over nine pages, does not place specific restrictions on gifts or explain how the rules would be enforced. But it does say the justices should not take part in outside activities that “detract from the dignity of the justice’s office,” “interfere with the performance of the justice’s official duties,” or “reflect adversely on the justice’s impartiality.”

Britain’s prime minister reshuffled his top team

Prime Minister Rishi Sunak of Britain today fired Suella Braverman, one of his most senior and divisive ministers. Her departure came after she wrote an opinion article in The Times of London criticizing the police and referring to pro-Palestinian protesters as “hate marchers.” In her spot, Sunak elevated a more conciliatory minister.

Sunak also unexpectedly brought back one of his predecessors, David Cameron, as the foreign secretary. Cameron’s return is a stunning turn in the career of a man who comes with plenty of political baggage.

Trees can fight climate change, just not alone

Restoring forests could potentially capture 226 gigatons of planet-warming carbon, equivalent to about a third of the amount that humans have released since the beginning of the Industrial Era, according to a new study. But trees are not a silver bullet for climate change, scientists said, and their benefit would only pay off if the effects of climate change do not worsen.

The fight to rid the internet of fake reviews

If you want to know whether a vacuum, a doctor or a hotel is any good, an online review might not help. That’s because hundreds of millions of reviews that are submitted each year are fake, and it’s incredibly difficult to discern the authentic from the fraudulent.

Fake reviews are illegal, and regulators and major tech companies have agreed in recent months to get them off the internet, but experts warn that the problem may be insurmountable.

Will the tie ever make a comeback?

These days, few people are everyday necktie wearers. The accessory has been quietly losing ground for years, between the advent of casual Fridays and the pandemic’s blurring of the lines between our professional and personal lives.

However, ties are probably here to stay, at least for certain occasions, writes our chief fashion critic Vanessa Friedman. “The demise of the tie as a standard part of dress could mean the rise of the tie as an optional accessory to signify individuality,” she said.

A spread only Australians could love

For decades, every jar of Vegemite has begun its journey in a factory in Melbourne, Australia, that smells like one of its crucial ingredients: leftover yeast from local breweries and bakeries. The spread, which is celebrating its 100th birthday this year, evokes visceral reactions. It is adored by Australians — and loathed by almost everyone else.

“You have to have done it as a child,” said Ben Shewry, a celebrated chef in Melbourne, about eating the paste. “It’s too painful to learn it as an adult.”
