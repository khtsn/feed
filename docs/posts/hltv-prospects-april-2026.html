HLTV Prospects: April 2026

Džiugas "⁠dziugss⁠" Steponavičius has jumped to number one on the HLTV Prospects list after starring at PGL Bucharest, knocking Kacper "⁠xKacpersky⁠" Gabara off the top spot the Pole had held since January.

Bucharest MVP Nikita "⁠cmtry⁠" Samolotov is also on the rise, breaking into the top ten. Swedish prodigy Liam "⁠MaiL09⁠" Tügel is another player making moves, after finding his feet on loan at Alliance and posting a 1.21 rating in the last month.

Before we dive into the minutiae of the risers and fallers, however, it's worth noting that with the April edition, the formula behind ranking the best up-and-comers in Counter-Strike 2 has been tweaked.

The previous formula did a decent job at the top of the ranking, but it was too easy for players with low (or non-existent) samples offline or against tough opposition to climb the ranking. After three months of that iteration, we are moving ahead with an improved method based on nine factors:

Most of these were already present in the old formula, but the metrics that go into the final rating have been finetuned. We have also moved to percentiles, rather than z-scores, which fits better onto the distribution of our prospects.

Some factors are new, like FACEIT ELO, which aims to account for prospects like MaiL09 going number one on the third-party platform before he played that many officials.

But the logic behind the formula remains the same: Its goal is to find the youngsters who perform best relative to the opposition and scenarios they find themselves in.

Because it is a monthly ranking, there's plenty of emphasis on recent performance (45% of the ranking), with ample room for offline pedigree (45%) and a splash of outside factors (10%).

HLTV Prospects factors

weightedRating: Weighted rating against top 50 teams in the last 6 months

weightedLAN: Weighted rating on LAN in all of CS2

Age: Days old. Means a 16yo with the same stats outscores a 20yo

Each factor accounts for performance against their peers, and also a player's sample size. We have also improved the formula behind weighted ratings, in order to adjust for opponent difficulty more fairly.

In practice, that means players like Vyacheslav "⁠slaxejezzz⁠" Vinokurov, who burst into the top 50 last month, fall far down the ranking, since he has not played on LAN yet and only has seven maps on record against top teams.

It also applies to players like Gleb "⁠gr1ks⁠" Gazin, who the old system rewarded because of stats like a 1.32 rating vs top 20 and 1.18 vs top 30. In the new system, however, those good performances shrink towards the average because of a lack of sample size (he only has 10 maps against top 30 in the last six months). That means his 1.05 rating against top 50 teams (21 maps) becomes more important to his final score.

gr1ks' impressive record against teams outside of the top 50, where he has a 1.22 rating, is still rewarded via the Form and Round Win factors, but his lack of pedigree on LAN (0.99 rating vs top 50, 32 maps) so far in his career means he has been taken out of the top 10.

As always, you can explore the data behind the ranking in our interactive Tableau, where you can see the scores out of 100 in each factor for every single player in the ranking.

Further down the list, the method behind regional diversity has also been tweaked. We now automatically include the top 40 players, and then 'top up' the last 10 to ensure each region is represented. That means a minimum of four players from Asia and South America, two from North America, and one from Oceania. When players from these regions are already in the top 40, we fill out the list with the next highest-ranked global players.

Alex "⁠poiii⁠" Nyholm Sundgren follows the young brother archetype of professional Counter-Strike player. Despite still only being a teenager, he can say that he started with 1.6 and Source, as he got his hands on the game for the first time at three or four, while sitting on the lap of his older brother, who was babysitting him at the time.

"I started playing 1.6 or Source when my brother was at school," poiii says, "We had two PCs for playing. My oldest brother had his own; I couldn't really touch that one, and then everyone else shared one. But my other older brother also played some games, and my dad used that one quite a bit. If I had time, I would sit there, play some surf and custom maps, fun ones in Source and 1.6."

poiii was quick to realize he had a talent and, after saving up his allowance and buying CS:GO at eight or nine years old, he started grinding matchmaking until he hit Global Elite that same year. "In the friend groups I had as a kid, I was always the best one, so I kind of limited myself to keep playing with the friends I had," he recalls of the early days, "and I never tried to play with better people. I was in the same clique the whole time, but I always knew I was good."

poiii's childhood was also when Sweden was on top of the Counter-Strike world. "'That looks fun, I want to be there one day," he remembers thinking. "But then I never really tried to do anything with it and just played normal competitive for many years and switched from CS to other games. I never tried to play it seriously."

Those were the days of Christopher "⁠GeT_RiGhT⁠" Alesund, Patrik "⁠f0rest⁠" Lindberg, and Jesper "⁠JW⁠" Wecksell. After the downfall of Swedish Counter-Strike from the top, the latter created EYEBALLERS in order to give Swedish youngsters a stepping stone into professional CS, and that's exactly what poiii did.

The Swedish youngster was growing disillusioned with school, and during a bleak Swedish winter in 2024, he decided that it was time to really give Counter-Strike a go. "I played FACEIT, got pretty good at that, and started mixing with some Swedish guys. Eventually, I got into a higher-tier mix-team in Sweden, Lilmix, if you can call it that, and that's where it all began because we started to play some real tournaments and not just random qualifiers where you earn 200 bucks."

poiii played with Lilmix briefly before getting scooped up by EYEBALLERS

Then he was called up by JW's EYEBALLERS. "It was a fun experience," poiii recalls. "It was my first pro team where we got a salary and things. It was a bit rough because we didn't perform too well, but it was a good experience for me to learn the environment."

Beyond the team's results on the server, it was a rough time in poiii's personal life." I dropped out of school a bit before joining EYEBALLERS," he says. "It was a desperate choice, like, 'What do I do in my life? Well, okay, let's try to go pro.'"

Luckily, he had parents who supported his decision as long as there was a future. "Dropping out of school maybe isn't the most fun experience for anyone," he says. "My parents were supporting me as long as I did something, like get work or whatever, because in Sweden you can always do your schoolwork at any time. You can always take the exam later. But it's not really a fun process, and I don't think a lot of people actually do it, but if you really want to, you can.

"So it's not the end of the world, even if it's not really a great thing to drop out. But they were pretty supportive, and coming from a gamer family, or whatever you want to call it, they knew I was pretty good, and I could pursue it."

poiii wasn't really watching anyone in particular or molding his game after anyone, but one undeniable influence, as with many of the young aggressive players coming up now, was Danil "⁠donk⁠" Kryshkovets. At the same time as he was coming up, the donk hype really exploded.

"I already knew it a bit before, because he was really good on FACEIT and all of that, and I played him a few times, but that was the Katowice run and all of that, so of course, I had to take some inspiration," poiii says.

"I also watched NiKo," he adds, "even though he's not really that kind of player. I just like the good performing riflers, but in the beginning, it wasn't really anyone, except maybe donk, because it was the same period when I became a professional."

The doppelgangers we've found include the likes of Drin "⁠makazze⁠" Shaqiri, Laurențiu "⁠lauNX⁠" Țârlea, Guy "⁠NertZ⁠" Iluz, Mihai "⁠iM⁠" Ivan, Ismailcan "⁠XANTARES⁠" Dörtkardeş, or Fredrik "⁠REZ⁠" Sterner, but one stands out for poiii. "I watch pretty much all of them. Their demos, their games. I can see similarities," he says, "but out of all of them, I'd say maybe XANTARES, I think, because he's just... XANTARES, you know?"

There's a pattern among those players, and it's that they're the guys who thrive in the action and are expected to produce above-average output, something which poiii is allowed to do in 100 Thieves. Along with William "⁠sirah⁠" Kjærsgaard, the other youngster on the team and #14 prospect, the two were marked to be the big producers in the veteran squad.

"We do get along, and maybe that helps with not feeling alone in being the dumb kid who doesn't know anything," poiii says, laughing, of his relationship with the young Dane. "Not that we are, but like, maybe it looks like that from the outside or whatever. Or it could be like four veterans and me, who doesn't know too much, so it feels good, and we do get along. I knew he was a good player before we started playing together."

Luckily, poiii has been able to keep his roles, as the two played similar roles in their previous teams. "I'm playing the roles I've had the whole time, so thank God I got to keep them," the Swede says. "It feels good to have the trust in us, or now me, especially, to get these star positions where I need to contribute a lot. It feels great to keep them."

100 Thieves have been on the rise, going from being unranked in the VRS in February to kissing the top 30 in March, and as the stakes grow for the team, so does poiii's will to become a more household name. "A few months back, I would not have thought this way at all," he says, "I would just go in and do my thing without thinking about people's expectations and wanting people to know me. I was just doing my thing.

"But now, yeah, of course, I want to show myself a bit more, as well. We're trying really hard both the team and myself to get to the level where we want to be, and that requires me to show myself, as well. It's different now from before, like now I really want this, whereas before it was more like, 'I'm doing this because I can.'"

The goal for 100 Thieves next season will be to qualify for bigger events and qualify for the Major, hoping to leave behind the loop of second-tier VRS events. "For me, personally, I have no specific goals other than to help the team get there and, in the process of doing that, make myself a better player," poiii concludes.
