The Beginning of Biden’s Long Goodbye

News Analysis

In a speech from the Oval Office, President Biden said it was time to “pass the torch to a new generation.” But he said nothing about his own age or capacity that led so many Democrats to desert him.

He always knew that he would be delivering a speech like this. He just thought, or hoped, that it would be more than four years from now. Yet while it was not technically a farewell address, with six months still to go in office and more presidenting to do, it was the beginning of Joe Biden’s long goodbye.

Mr. Biden’s address to the nation from the Oval Office on Wednesday night was all Joe, love him or hate him — the paeans to American exceptionalism, the evocations of family, the selective boasting about his record, the favorite lofty phrases about an “inflection point” and “saving our democracy,” and yes, the soft, raspy old man’s voice that no longer commands the room the way it once did.

What there was not much of was introspection about how he had gotten to this moment of indignity. He may be focused on the soul of America, but he revealed little of his own. Indeed, if there has been much soul searching over these past days and weeks of personal and political trauma that led to this reluctant end of his storied half-century political career, the search has been called off. Or at least the results were not reported.

He said it was time to “pass the torch to a new generation,” but said nothing about his own age, health or capacity that led so many Democrats to desert him since the calamitous debate on June 27. He did not describe the journey from supreme confidence that he and he alone could beat former President Donald J. Trump to the conclusion that in fact he could not. He offered no elaboration on how he had finally decided to give up his bid for a second term, but at the same time, he held back any bitterness he may have felt.

Instead, it was an opportunity for a reset, to tell his story again on his own terms and recast the narrative as he starts to exit the stage. In his first extended public comments since dropping out, he tried to remind voters who had grown weary or wary of him why most of them had liked him in the first place and maybe, just maybe, to begin to shape his place in history.

“My fellow Americans, it’s been the privilege of my life to serve this nation for over 50 years,” he said with pictures of his family visible behind him. “Nowhere else on Earth could a kid with a stutter from modest beginnings in Scranton, Pa., and in Claymont, Del., one day sit behind the Resolute Desk in the Oval Office as the president of the United States. But here I am.”

The desk, of course, is still his until Jan. 20, and he made clear that he has an agenda to press in these months to come, especially consolidating the gains he has made at home and trying to bring a degree of peace back to the Middle East. He is not done, he said. He has a to-do list that includes growing the economy, stopping Russia from swallowing up its neighbor and overhauling the Supreme Court.

But as attention now turns to Vice President Kamala Harris and his influence invariably begins fading by the day, Mr. Biden also wanted to take credit for what he sees as his accomplishments. He recalled taking over a country gripped by “the worse pandemic in the century, the worst economic crisis since the Great Depression, the worst attack on our democracy since the Civil War,” and leading it toward better times.

Gliding over the setbacks, he boasted of creating “the strongest economy in the world,” noting that wages are going up and inflation is coming down, that the manufacturing sector is expanding and the racial wealth gap is shrinking. “We are literally rebuilding our entire nation,” he said.

He talked of appointing the first Black woman to the Supreme Court, fighting climate change, lowering the cost of prescription drugs for seniors, providing health care to more Americans and trying to reduce gun violence.

And contrary to the dystopian portrait offered at last week’s Republican National Convention, he noted that violent crime was near a 50-year low and that border crossings, after the surge of earlier in his presidency, had fallen to the lowest level since he took office.

It is a record he wanted to take to the electorate and thought he could win on. But as much as he likes to say that he should be judged on what he has already done, what he came to discover in the last month was that he was being judged on what he might or might not be able to do in the future.

Even as Republicans call on him to go the next step by resigning the presidency outright, the White House press secretary, Karine Jean-Pierre, said hours before the speech that the first octogenarian president still believed he could do the job not just for the next six months but even for four more years. Yet in his speech, he implicitly conceded that most Americans did not agree.

“In recent weeks, it has become clear to me that I need to unite my party in this critical endeavor,” he said. “I believe my record as president, my leadership in the world, my vision for America’s future, all merited a second term. But nothing, nothing can come in the way of saving our democracy. That includes personal ambition.”

So a story of weakness, of being forced out of the race by his own friends, becomes in this telling a story of strength, of sacrifice for a greater good. It will become the latest chapter in the Biden saga of reversal and resilience that has played out in the public eye for more than 50 years.

And in that sense, without saying it quite so explicitly, he contrasted the integrity of his concession with what he sees as the lack of integrity of Mr. Trump, the man he beat once and would dearly have loved to beat again.

“We have to decide,” Mr. Biden said without mentioning his now-former opponent by name. “Do we still believe in honesty, decency, respect, freedom, justice and democracy? In this moment, we can see those we disagree with not as enemies but as, I mean, fellow Americans — can we do that? Does character in public life still matter? I believe you know the answer to these questions because I know you, the American people.”

But he now faces a different challenge. No longer will he be measuring himself by the daily polls or the quarterly fund-raising totals. Now he is moving to the stage that all presidents eventually get to, where he will be judging himself against the 44 other men who once held the job.

History was clearly on his mind on Wednesday night as he invoked George Washington, Thomas Jefferson, Abraham Lincoln and Franklin D. Roosevelt. He quoted Benjamin Franklin and pointed to the busts he keeps in the Oval Office of the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., Rosa Parks and Cesar Chavez.

He has lived a lot of that history himself. He was born when Roosevelt was president. His life spans one-third the life of the country and his time in public life covers about a quarter of American history.

For so long, he has given the impression of one who never wanted it to end. There was always another speech, always another election. But now the number of speeches ahead is finite and his last election is in the past.

As that long goodbye gets underway, Mr. Biden has at long last reached his own inflection point.
