National Guard in the Subway? New Yorkers Have Their Doubts.

Some commuters said they felt safer after learning that heavily armed soldiers would patrol the subway system. Others were critical of the governor’s plan.

March 7, 2024Updated 4:23 p.m. ET

The day after Gov. Kathy Hochul announced a plan to deploy National Guard soldiers and State Police troopers in the New York City subway system, commuters expressed mixed feelings about whether the extra vigilance would make them feel any safer.

Some applauded the move on Thursday, while others said they were skeptical about whether it was necessary and concerned about having armed soldiers check their bags.

At many busy stations, the added law enforcement officers were nowhere to be seen. But late Thursday morning, a pair of National Guard troops with rifles walked down the sloping hallway between the Fulton Street and World Trade Center subway stations in Lower Manhattan, past the gelato stand where Aileen Morales works.

Ms. Morales, 31, who travels there by subway from her home in the Bronx, said that she had developed a “poker face” for her commutes in the last two years for self-protection. She welcomed the additional security in a system that feels unsafe to her.

“You don’t know who’s going to come on the train now,” Ms. Morales said. “That’s the scary part.”

Her co-worker Jim Lozada, 29, agreed, and said that an agitated man punched him in the ribs on the subway last year after he asked the man to stop shoving other commuters.

Ms. Morales was less keen on the idea of having her bags randomly searched by troops, calling it “kind of an invasion of privacy.” But she sounded resigned to the possibility.

“I mean if that’s what it takes, so be it,” she said.

But the safety plan did not impress Jeremy Sorese, a 35-year-old painter, even though he said he had broken several bones when he was pushed onto the tracks in 2019. Mr. Sorese said he thought the plan to put more law enforcement in the system was “a waste of resources.”

“It feels very fearmongering without actually telling us why it’s happening, this sort of specter of danger,” Mr. Sorese said as he waited for a train at the Atlantic Avenue-Barclays Center Station in Brooklyn.

He said that when he was pushed, the police were not helpful — it was his fellow riders who pulled him to safety.

The likelihood of being a victim of a violent crime on the subway remains low, but public officials are battling a perception that the system is dangerous. Overall crime fell in the subway last year, and while the number of major crimes in the transit system jumped this January, it declined again in February.

In making her announcement on Wednesday, Ms. Hochul framed the law enforcement surge as aimed at helping New Yorkers and visitors feel safer. Under her plan, 750 members of the New York National Guard and an additional 250 personnel from the State Police and the Metropolitan Transportation Authority will be scattered across the transit system.

They will join what is already a large force of officers from the New York Police Department stationed in the subways, where Mayor Eric Adams ordered an additional 1,000 officers last month.

Ms. Hochul announced her plan about a week after New York City Transit workers stopped performing their duties at two Manhattan stations during the morning commute, following an overnight slashing attack on an A train that injured a conductor. Shortly after the governor’s announcement, another conductor was struck on the head with a glass bottle in the Bronx, the police said.

On Thursday, Alvin Bragg, the Manhattan district attorney, announced that two men had been indicted in two separate punching attacks on M.T.A. employees in February.

In addition to the deployment in the subway, Ms. Hochul proposed efforts to coordinate with prosecutors to track those who repeatedly commit crimes on the subway. Her plan also includes a bill that would allow judges to ban those convicted of violent subway-related crimes from using the system, though such provisions would require legislative approval and be difficult to enforce if enacted.

The National Guard already patrols major transportation hubs like Grand Central Terminal, Pennsylvania Station and the Port Authority Bus Terminal. Ms. Hochul said Wednesday that the newly deployed soldiers would be focused in part on keeping weapons out of the subway system.

But the system is vast, with 472 stations and thousands of train cars in service at every hour of the day. State officials said that the guardsmen would be deployed throughout the system over the course of about a week.

Nate Santos, 36, of Brooklyn, said that he would be likely to change his route to avoid the soldiers to prevent a bag check.

“It’s not that I’m hiding anything,” said Mr. Santos, who works as a brand director for a finance company. “I just don’t want people going through my stuff.”

There were no National Guard troops waiting at the entrance underneath Grand Central Station to the 42nd Street Shuttle early on Thursday afternoon, but a pair of New York State troopers — one carrying a rifle — stood next to a lighted sign that said “Security Inspection Point.”

A few steps away, three police officers were stationed next to the turnstiles. One officer greeted random subway riders with an upbeat “Hi, how are you doing?” and then steered them to a rolling cart table for a check of their bags.

None of the riders refused — and one of the officers said that anyone tapped for a bag check who did not consent to a search would be denied entrance at that location.

But Nancy Slotnick had a moment’s pause.

“I thought, ‘No, I don’t have time; I’ve got to catch the train,’” Ms. Slotnick, 57, said after she had passed through the turnstile following a quick, flashlight-assisted look by police into her shoulder bag.

Riders can refuse bag searches under the Fourth Amendment, but Ms. Hochul warned that those who did would have to leave the station.

Ms. Slotnick, an Upper West Side resident, said that she thought that bag checks were acceptable if they were not discriminatory.

“Hopefully they’re just picking people on a very random basis, not any kind of profiling basis,” she said.

“I wouldn’t necessarily say it’s reassuring in the sense that it makes me more likely to use the subways,” Ms. Slotnick added. “But it’s definitely welcome.”

Camille Baker, Liset Cruz, Eliza Fawcett, Erin Nolan and Sean Piccoli contributed reporting.
