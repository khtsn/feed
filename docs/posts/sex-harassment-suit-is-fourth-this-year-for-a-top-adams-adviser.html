Sex Harassment Suit Is Fourth This Year for a Top Adams Adviser

The latest allegations against Timothy Pearson, a confidant of Mayor Eric Adams, were made by a high-ranking official in the New York Police Department.

July 30, 2024Updated 10:07 a.m. ET

A close friend and senior adviser to Mayor Eric Adams was vulgar and abusive toward female subordinates, referred to a gay police official using a homophobic slur and denied a woman a promotion because she resisted his suggestion that they engage in a “quid pro quo sexual relationship,” a high-ranking New York Police Department official charges in a lawsuit filed on Tuesday.

The adviser, Timothy Pearson, also once sexually abused a female pastor, manipulated her into having sex with him and was abusive during the encounter about 10 years ago — prompting her to file a criminal complaint against him, the suit says.

The lawsuit is the fourth action this year to accuse Mr. Pearson of sexual harassment. Like two of the others, it was brought by a male police official who said he was disgusted by Mr. Pearson’s treatment of women at a small unit Mr. Adams created to improve government efficiency.

Mr. Pearson declined to comment, referring a reporter to a City Hall spokeswoman and a lawyer the city has hired to represent him. That lawyer denied any wrongdoing on Mr. Pearson’s behalf and said he would vigorously defend him and the city against the claims.

The latest complaint, filed in State Supreme Court in Manhattan, was brought by a veteran deputy police chief, Miltiadis Marmara, who has been on the force for more than three decades and who knew Mr. Pearson before Mr. Pearson retired and became head of security at a casino in Queens.

But Mr. Pearson has not just been accused of wrongdoing in lawsuits. He has also come under scrutiny for apparent conflicts of interest and allegations that he abused the authority of his position in city government. He is the subject of two ongoing investigations by the city’s independent anti-corruption agency, people with knowledge of the matter have said. One is examining the sexual harassment allegations and the other his role in a violent melee in October at a city migrant shelter.

Still, despite the drumbeat of sexual harassment and other accusations, Mr. Pearson has continued to occupy a position of extraordinary influence in Mr. Adams’s administration, exercising near absolute control over most discretionary promotions at the Police Department while holding no formal role there, Chief Marmara’s lawsuit said.

The suit also names the department’s highest-ranking uniformed official, Jeffrey B. Maddrey, and its chief of patrol, John M. Chell, as defendants. It accuses Mr. Pearson of using his power to deny a promotion to the female officer who resisted his overtures for the “quid pro quo sexual relationship.” The lawsuit calls Mr. Pearson a “poster boy” for retaliatory behavior against women who complain of sexual harassment at the Police Department.

“It just boggles my mind,” Chief Marmara, the police official who brought the most recent suit, said in an interview last week. “He looks at females,” he said of Mr. Pearson, asking “what I can get from them, and the first thing he wants to get from them is the sexual, obviously, the sexual quid pro quo.”

The mayor himself is a retired police captain, and he and Mr. Pearson have been close for years. In July, when a reporter asked about a security issue at shelters for migrants, Mr. Adams responded by saying that Mr. Pearson was looking into the matter. In May, Mr. Pearson sat on the dais during the mayor’s promotion ceremony for public safety employees.

A spokeswoman for the mayor said in a statement on Monday that the administration “holds employees to the highest ethical standards” but did not address questions about whether a fourth lawsuit against Mr. Pearson would prompt the city to take action against him.

“We take these allegations seriously,” the spokeswoman said. “We will review the lawsuit.”

In a separate statement, John M. Flannery, the lawyer at the firm of Wilson Elser Moskowitz Edelman & Dicker whom the city hired to represent both Mr. Pearson and the city’s interests, said that Mr. Pearson and the city had denied the accusations in the earlier lawsuits and planned to “vigorously defend themselves” against the one brought by Chief Marmara.

Mr. Flannery contended that Chief Marmara was embittered because he lost his position working for Mr. Pearson and added that Chief Marmara and the other plaintiffs had themselves been the subjects of internal investigations.

“Facts matter, and the facts will show” that Chief Marmara was removed from his position “as a result of his own misconduct,” Mr. Flannery said. He said that included threatening Mr. Pearson over the speed at which police officers at a new city office Chief Marmara oversaw were being promoted.

He also said that neither the chief nor any of the other three people who sued Mr. Pearson and the city “had ever reported any alleged misconduct by Mr. Pearson to the city or internally to the N.Y.P.D.” They did so, he said, only after the department’s Internal Affairs Bureau had begun investigating claims of misconduct against them.

But Chief Marmara’s lawsuit says that he complained repeatedly about Mr. Pearson’s conduct.

John Scola, the lawyer for Chief Marmara and the other three plaintiffs in the suits against Mr. Pearson, has previously described the Internal Affairs investigation as retaliatory. And two of the other suits named the police inspector who led at least part of the inquiry as a defendant.

Indeed, the chief’s lawsuit says that Mr. Pearson boasted in April 2023 that he had chosen the chief of Internal Affairs and “owns” him.

Aside from the new set of allegations, Chief Marmara’s suit stands out for who has brought the claim: a senior police official with 30 years’ experience who is highly regarded within the department as both a leader and a manager. In 2022, Mr. Adams named him executive director of Municipal Services Assessment, a new office the Adams administration created to act as a kind of quality assurance unit for the city’s agencies.

Chief Marmara built and ultimately expanded the office to about 20 employees, including several he said he had carefully selected and recruited from the Police Department because of their skills, intelligence and work ethic.

They included Roxanne Ludemann, then a police sergeant, who would go on to sue Mr. Pearson in March, accusing him of sexual harassment and retaliation and saying his actions had doomed her career and prompted her to retire. And another sergeant, Michael Ferrari, sued Mr. Pearson in April alleging harassment and retaliation and said that he had also been driven to retire by Mr. Pearson’s actions. And George Huang, a lieutenant, sued Mr. Pearson in June, saying he had fostered a hostile work environment.

In the early days after the creation of the assessment unit, Chief Marmara said he had spoken with the mayor monthly to apprise him of the office’s progress.

But in 2022, The New York Times reported that Mr. Pearson was holding down two jobs, one as a vice president overseeing security at a casino, Resorts World New York City, and the other as a senior adviser to the mayor collecting more than $240,000 in pay. The disclosure of the unusual arrangement, under which he was also able to simultaneously collect his police pension, exposed an apparent conflict of interest, as the casino was seeking the mayor’s support to expand its operations.

Days after the report, Mr. Pearson left Resorts World, and Mr. Adams eventually put the Municipal Services Assessment office under his purview.

Chief Marmara said he and his colleagues had found Mr. Pearson’s behavior peculiar and troubling on several fronts. After complaining in front of Sergeant Ferrari, who has since retired, and Lieutenant Huang about how he was not getting any “crumbs” from a particular municipal contract, they speculated that Mr. Pearson was referring to kickbacks and began referring to Mr. Pearson as Crumbs, Chief Marmara’s lawsuit says.

Then there was his behavior toward women.

During a July 2022 drive to a Hispanic heritage event at Gracie Mansion, the mayor’s official residence, Chief Marmara witnessed Mr. Pearson howling “like a dog” at the women they passed in the car, according to the suit.

Chief Marmara also witnessed Mr. Pearson making “crude noises” while staring at the rear end of his assistant.

Mr. Pearson used homophobic slurs, Chief Marmara’s lawsuit says, toward people including an assistant police chief whom he believed to be a lesbian. And he refused to attend a meeting with Preston Niblack, the commissioner of finance, because he is gay, the suit says. Mr. Niblack declined to comment.

But it was Mr. Pearson’s treatment of Ms. Ludemann that brought tensions at the Municipal Services Assessment office to a head.

After witnessing Ms. Ludemann grimace as Mr. Pearson touched her bare shoulder, and learning from Ms. Ludemann that such behavior by Mr. Pearson occurred regularly, Chief Marmara urged her to file a complaint with the office of equal employment opportunity, his lawsuit says.

She “vehemently” declined, fearing retaliation. Chief Marmara backed down but directed his staff never to let a woman be alone in the office with Mr. Pearson, whom he describes in the suit as a “sexual predator.”

He also raised the issue “on nearly a weekly basis” with Bernard Adams, Mr. Adams’s brother, a retired police sergeant who also worked at City Hall as an adviser on mayoral security.

Bernard Adams is said to have brushed off Chief Marmara’s concerns, describing Mr. Pearson as “harmless” and saying that the behavior was just “Tim being Tim.” Ultimately, Bernard Adams relayed Chief Marmara’s concerns to Mr. Pearson.

Bernard Adams did not respond to a request for comment.

After learning of the concerns, Mr. Pearson denied Ms. Ludemann a promotion that she had been promised, Chief Marmara’s lawsuit says.

When the chief confronted Mr. Pearson about the unfulfilled promise of promotion, according to the suit, Mr. Pearson responded in a lewd voice, saying, “What is she going to do for me?” Chief Marmara interpreted that as a proposal for a sexual quid pro quo, the suit says.

Ultimately, Mr. Pearson fired Chief Marmara from his position running the new unit.

Chief Marmara’s lawsuit also contains an account of a sexual encounter Mr. Pearson reportedly had with a female pastor in 2014 or 2015, after he had left the Police Department.

During that period, the pastor told Chief Marmara, who was working in Queens, that Mr. Pearson had manipulated her into having sex, which she later described as unnecessarily rough and abusive, the lawsuit says.

She filed a complaint with the police, according to the suit.

A couple of days later, Mr. Pearson had her arrested, Chief Marmara’s suit says, claiming that she had slashed the tires of his BMW. The lawsuit does not detail the outcome of either complaint.

Reached by phone last week, the pastor declined to comment.
