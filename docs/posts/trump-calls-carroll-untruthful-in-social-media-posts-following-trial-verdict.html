Trump Calls Carroll Untruthful in Social Media Posts Following Trial Verdict

During the trial, the judge cautioned the former president about his social media posts. He was not deterred, claiming that E. Jean Carroll had invented her story.

May 10, 2023Updated 7:24 p.m. ET

Hours after a Manhattan jury on Tuesday found that former President Donald J. Trump had sexually abused and defamed the writer E. Jean Carroll, Mr. Trump went on social media to call her a liar.

It was the kind of attack that had led Ms. Carroll to sue him twice for defamation, including this week’s case, in which she won $5 million in damages.

His unrelenting commentary on Ms. Carroll, her lawsuit and even the judge in the civil trial, Lewis A. Kaplan, raises questions about what repercussions Mr. Trump could face in any motions he makes before Judge Kaplan or in a promised appeal, or whether he could face a new defamation lawsuit.

In Mr. Trump’s most recent comments, he again sharply criticized Ms. Carroll, who had accused him of attacking her nearly 30 years ago in a dressing room at Bergdorf Goodman, a Manhattan department store.

“I have no idea who this woman, who made a false and totally fabricated accusation, is,” Mr. Trump said on his Truth Social website on Wednesday morning. “Hopefully justice will be served on appeal!”

As for Judge Kaplan, Mr. Trump called him a “terrible person” who was “completely biased, and should have recused himself.”

Mr. Trump’s attacks were only his latest since the trial began on April 25. Although Mr. Trump did not testify or even appear in court, he posted harsh comments about Ms. Carroll and her case from afar, in interviews with reporters on a golf course in Ireland, where he was traveling, and in posts on Truth Social.

Ms. Carroll had said Mr. Trump raped her, but the jury found him liable for sexual abuse rather than rape. He has not been convicted of any crime — it was a civil case — and he faces no prison time.

After the verdict, Mr. Trump’s barrage of criticism continued. On Tuesday evening, he called Ms. Carroll’s case a “scam” and a “political witch hunt” in a video he posted on Truth Social.

Mr. Trump’s lawyer, Joseph Tacopina, said after the verdict that his client would file an appeal, but it was unclear how Mr. Trump’s after-action diatribes might affect his chances.

“Donald Trump needs to stop talking now,” said Ben Chew, a lawyer who successfully represented Johnny Depp in his defamation lawsuit against his ex-wife, Amber Heard, last year. “The time for him to speak up was at the trial.”

Mr. Chew noted that Judge Kaplan had given Mr. Trump several opportunities to testify on his own behalf, and that he ultimately had chosen not to.

Because the trial has ended and the jury has been dismissed, Mr. Chew said that, as a practical matter, it was unlikely Judge Kaplan would take any action against Mr. Trump for his tirades.

“Courts are loath to restrict speech of any kind,” Mr. Chew said.

When Mr. Trump made his first critical posts in the early days of the trial, Judge Kaplan told Mr. Tacopina that his client’s comments were “entirely inappropriate,” and hinted at the possibility of a contempt sanction. Those posts have since been deleted.

Ms. Carroll could bring a new defamation lawsuit against Mr. Trump based on his recent comments, experts said.

“He doesn’t get carte blanche to defame her because she sued him once and collected and won,” said Stephen Gillers, a legal ethics professor at New York University School of Law.

Ms. Carroll’s lawyer, Roberta A. Kaplan, and Mr. Trump’s lawyer, Mr. Tacopina, had no comment.

Ms. Carroll’s first defamation lawsuit against Mr. Trump is still pending. It was triggered by statements he made in 2019, calling her a liar, after she first accused him publicly, in a book excerpt published in New York magazine, of raping her in the Bergdorf’s dressing room.

Mr. Trump has argued that he made those statements in his official capacity as president and cannot be sued for them.

The second suit Ms. Carroll filed, the one that succeeded on Tuesday, was tied to a statement Mr. Trump made on Truth Social in October, in which he called her case a “complete con job” and “a Hoax and a lie.”

Mr. Tacopina, in post-verdict comments outside the courthouse, said his client “seems to be a survivor” and that Mr. Trump would “march forward.”

“He was found not liable for the rape and that’s it,” Mr. Tacopina said.

Legal experts said it was impossible to know precisely how the jury, which deliberated for under three hours, came to find that Mr. Trump had sexually abused Ms. Carroll but had not raped her.

“To me, this looks like a compromise verdict,” said Jennifer L. Keller, a lawyer who represented Kevin Spacey in October when a jury found him not liable in a lawsuit accusing him of making a sexual advance on a 14-year-old in 1986.

“The jurors clearly believed she was sexually assaulted and what Ms. Carroll described was a rape,” Ms. Keller said. “A unanimous verdict was required, and there could have been a juror or two who simply didn’t want a former president of the United States to be branded a rapist.”

Mr. Chew, the lawyer who represented Mr. Depp, said some jurors in Ms. Carroll’s case could have been influenced by the testimony of two other women who testified on her behalf about sexual assaults by Mr. Trump that fell short of rape.

To find that Mr. Trump raped Ms. Carroll, the jurors would have needed to believe that it was more likely than not that Mr. Trump engaged in sexual intercourse by physical force. Judge Kaplan explained to them that “any penetration of the penis into the vaginal opening” constituted intercourse.

Mr. Chew said that Ms. Carroll’s testimony was “somewhat ambiguous on the issue of penetration,” adding that a reasonable juror might find that Ms. Carroll failed to meet the legal standard of proving that it was more likely than not to have happened.

While Ms. Carroll testified that Mr. Trump inserted his fingers and then his penis into her vagina, she also said, “I couldn’t see anything that was happening, but I could certainly feel it.”

Mr. Trump has 30 days to file an appeal, and if does, he is likely to post a bond to prevent Ms. Carroll from collecting her damages until the appeal is decided, the experts said.

Some of the issues his lawyers might cite include those that Mr. Tacopina raised earlier in a mistrial motion. In it, he complained of “pervasive, unfair and prejudicial rulings” by Judge Kaplan. The judge denied the motion.

Before the trial, Mr. Tacopina also sought unsuccessfully to exclude the testimony of the women who said Mr. Trump had sexually abused them. And on Tuesday, Mr. Tacopina said Ms. Carroll’s lawyers should never have been allowed to play the “Access Hollywood” recording for the jury, in which Mr. Trump was captured boasting in vulgar terms about grabbing women by the genitals.

Rebecca Roiphe, a professor at New York Law School who studies the legal profession, said that whatever Mr. Trump’s lawyers include in any appeal, he would be deeply involved in the process and would try to use it “to make an argument about how unfair the system has been to him.”

“Trump is a hands-on client who uses the legal system both to obtain whatever practical result he wants,” Ms. Roiphe said, “but more so, to make a political point and to achieve whatever narrative benefit he can get.”
