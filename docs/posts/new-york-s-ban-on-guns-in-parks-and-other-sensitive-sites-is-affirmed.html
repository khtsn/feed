New York’s Ban on Guns in Parks and Other ‘Sensitive’ Sites Is Affirmed

In a major victory for the state, a federal appeals court also upheld its right to deny firearms to people whom local officials deem dangerous.

Dec. 8, 2023, 3:33 p.m. ET

A federal appeals court on Friday upheld significant portions of New York’s expansive gun law, ruling that the state can ban the carrying of guns in schools, parks and other places and deny firearms to people whom local officials deem dangerous.

The ruling, by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit, came in response to four separate challenges to the law, which was passed in summer 2022 after the Supreme Court struck down an earlier law that had governed the public carrying of firearms in New York for more than a century.

The new law bans the carrying of guns in so-called sensitive locations, a category that includes schools, government buildings, places of worship and Times Square. The law also requires that people who apply to carry guns in public be of “good moral character,” as determined by local licensing officials.

The ruling on Friday, by a three-judge panel, was largely a victory for New York, keeping in place both the ban on guns in many of the locations outlined by the state and the “good moral character test.”

But the appeals court rejected some parts of the law, barring the state from declaring that guns are presumed to be off limits at places of worship as well as privately owned properties that are typically open to the public like gas stations and grocery stores. Under the court’s ruling, the owners of such properties must proactively signal that guns are banned.

“It’s putting the burden on every property owner to affirmatively come out and tell folks they don’t want guns on the premises,” said David Pucino, the legal director of Giffords Law Center, a gun safety group.

The new law had also required applicants for gun licenses to submit a list of their social media accounts to help officials assess their character and conduct. The appeals court rejected that requirement, finding that it was most likely unconstitutional to interfere with the aliases people use when expressing their views online.

The ban on carrying guns in Times Square has been a particular point of contention. A federal judge last year ruled it unconstitutional, although the provision stayed in place during the appeals process.

Although the Second Circuit did not explicitly address the constitutionality of the Times Square ban in its ruling on Friday, portions of the decision suggested it would stand up to future challenges.

Overall, legal experts said, the appeals court’s ruling was among the most significant on gun regulation since the Supreme Court struck down New York’s previous law last year.

Gov. Kathy Hochul welcomed the ruling in a statement. She noted that the regulations, which she signed into law in July 2022, had been challenged repeatedly.

“Even after a year of legal assault from right-wing extremists, core tenets of our laws remain in effect following today’s ruling,” Ms. Hochul said. “Public safety is my top priority, and I’ll continue my efforts to keep New Yorkers safe.”

Like other states affected by the Supreme Court’s 2022 ruling, New York substituted a straightforward but broadly restrictive law with a long list of specific regulations related to who could carry guns and where. The Second Circuit’s ruling signaled its approval of that approach.

Gun Owners of America, an organization that had backed one of the challenges to the new law, called the ruling frustrating.

“We will continue the fight until this entire law is sent to the bowels of history where it belongs,” Erich Pratt, the organization’s senior vice president, said.

Another challenger, His Tabernacle Family Church of upstate New York, celebrated the provision on places of worship.

“The court made it clear that houses of worship have a constitutionally protected freedom to decide for themselves whether to allow otherwise legally possessed firearms into their facilities,” said Erin Murphy of the law firm Clement and Murphy, which represented the church.

It was unclear what the next step would be for those who challenged the law. They could appeal to the Supreme Court or ask the full Second Circuit to reconsider the ruling, among other options.

In his opinion in the 2022 case, New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen, Justice Clarence Thomas declared that law-abiding citizens had a constitutional right to carry guns for their own defense. He wrote that any new restrictions would have to be justified through analogies to similar regulations from early American history.

In effect, he wrote, new gun laws would have to be defended by citing old ones. The requirement has created significant confusion for judges across the country.

On Friday, the appeals court allowed states broader freedom to justify new gun laws than had been understood by many judges in the wake of the Bruen decision.

The court ruled that the Second Amendment, like the First Amendment, should be read to incorporate “traditional limitations” on the right to carry guns.

For example, the ruling noted, “a reasoned denial of a carry license to a person who, if armed, would pose a danger to themselves, others, or to the public is consistent with the well-recognized historical tradition of preventing dangerous individuals from possessing weapons."

Jacob Charles, a Pepperdine University law professor whose work was cited in the ruling, said in an interview that the interpretation would “allow states to have a lot more leeway in enacting gun regulations.”

“It isn’t imposing these strict rules on the historical analysis, like requiring a set number of laws,” he said. “Instead it says courts should look more holistically at the historical tradition.”
