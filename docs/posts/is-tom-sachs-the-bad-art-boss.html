Is Tom Sachs the Bad Art Boss?

Big CITY

Tom Sachs made an art career on casual shock value and relationships with luxury brands. Now he’s under attack for what employees say was an abusive studio environment.

Ginia Bellafante

Ginia Bellafante writes the Big City column, a weekly commentary on the politics, culture and life of New York City.

Tom Sachs, the downtown artist who has recently come to broader public attention over accusations of epically mistreating his studio assistants, first emerged as a creature of interest to high-rolling, mid-cult New York in 1994, when he built a crèche for Barneys Christmas windows and cast Hello Kitty in the role of Jesus. Outraged, Catholic groups placed hundreds of calls to the store, and the display was quickly removed. Still, it was a time when a calculatedly offensive expression, a tasteless gesture, did not signal the midnight of a career so much as the bright early morning.

By the 1990s, New York was no longer the center of the art world, and the influence of critics was long past a period of decline. Gallerists and curators, whose affinities had once been so entwined with the preoccupations of totemic intellectuals, now found themselves increasingly under the spell of the fashion world’s mercurial vision. The era of Clement Greenberg, who had famously championed abstract expressionism for its resistance to the invasions of commerce, was long over, succeeded by a new age that brought a rapturous enthusiasm specifically to these intrusions of the market.

In this environment Tom Sachs found a trend-chasing audience for his cheeky banalities — sculptures of fast-food meals wrapped in Hermès packaging, a toilet made out of Prada shoe boxes, a homemade gun with a Tiffany logo. For a 2002 group show about art and Nazism at the Jewish Museum, Mr. Sachs built what he described as a “pop up” concentration camp forged out of a Prada hat box.

When the writer Deborah Solomon asked him in an interview in The New York Times Magazine what any of this could possibly have to do with Hitler, Mr. Sachs responded that fashion was like “fascism” because it was about the “loss of identity.” Fashion, he went on, “is good when it helps you to look sexy, but it’s bad when it makes you feel stupid or fat,” suggesting that the coercions of Harper’s Bazaar existed on some continuum with the Gestapo’s.

The notion that Mr. Sachs was critiquing the vulgarities of luxury consumerism, however mindlessly, was just pretense — his own and one passed along by an obsequious fashion press. But the joke was always on us. He was finding his way into the firmament, moving pop art away from its origins as a means of cultural comment toward a newer language of collusion, a descendant of Warhol’s, if we imagined that Warhol held the ambition to actually work for the Campbell Soup Company. In 2006, Fondazione Prada, a gallery space in Milan, held a Tom Sachs exhibition. Six years later, in 2012, he began his ongoing collaboration with Nike to make sneakers, a relationship that now may be jeopardized.

A man’s hypocrisy never drives in a single lane. The trouble for Mr. Sachs began earlier this month, when a Curbed investigation outlined allegations of misconduct by more than a dozen former employees (most who chose to remain anonymous either because they had signed nondisclosure agreements or feared retaliation). They all described a workplace characterized by fear and humiliation. For a while now, Sachs has made big art that requires many hands — spaceship replicas and so on — and his management style has made as much a fetish of process as the work itself does.

Thirteen years ago, he released a short film called “Ten Bullets” meant to serve as an employee guidebook instructing staff on his many exacting rules — keeping fit, answering orders with the words “I understand” or “I don’t understand” — which has a satirical charm until you realize that the sentiment behind it all is largely serious. In the Curbed article, former workers alleged that Mr. Sachs made sexist comments about women’s bodies, lost his temper, kept vintage porn on the walls, threw things, called people “autistic” or “retarded” and made assistants tend to demeaning personal errands.

A week or so later, more allegations surfaced in a piece in Artnet News, among them, from a former assistant who said she had to pick up clothes from Chanel for Mr. Sachs’s wife, Sarah Hoover — whose Instagram page is a showcase of her life in Chanel — and prepare meals for her French bulldog consisting of wild rabbit, spinach, aloe water and coconut oil. In response to that assertion, a studio spokesman told Artnet that assistants were in fact dispatched to make Napoleon’s “veterinarian-prescribed meals.” Neither did the studio deny that Mr. Sachs had once appeared in a Zoom call with Nike in his underwear; it did clarify that changing clothes was a feature of “weekly virtual fittings.”

In a public statement made after the accusations surfaced, Nike said that it was “deeply concerned by the very serious allegations” around Mr. Sachs and that the company was “in contact with Tom and his studio, seeking to better understand the situation and how these issues are being addressed.”

On Tuesday, Mr. Sachs sought to confront what had happened, sending a letter to his staff. Although he said that he “never tried to make anyone uncomfortable,” he acknowledged that he could be “impulsive” and “even aggressive,’’ that he was “a demanding boss” who always expects “the absolute best,” but that the “recent representations” of him in the media did “not portray the totality” of his long career.

Some allegations were false or misleading, he wrote, and while he did not follow that up with any detail about what exactly was false or misleading, he said that he sincerely regretted having referred to a particular room in the studio housing an air compressor as the “rape room.” He was working to “improve” himself and he was in the midst of formalizing “Human Resource policies” — ignoring, perhaps, that the ostentatiously regimented corporatization of the studio was what had caused so much of the conflict in the first place.

For all the recent show that the art and fashion worlds have made of their progressive politics — their efforts to market their alignment with inclusive causes — they remain tied to caricatures of intemperate genius — even when the genius itself is hard to locate. A brilliant artist reads beneath the culture, identifying the undercurrents the rest of us cannot easily recognize. What should we make of one who has managed to miss what is happening right on top, who has been blind to a tidal wave of shifting social norms?

Stories like this typically prompt circular conversations about how we ought to think about the great art of bad men. Here, the question seems a lot simpler. Mr. Sachs ended the letter to his team by saying that he remained devoted to “innovation” and “radical ideas.” Was he ever?
