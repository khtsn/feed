JW: "As long as I feel that I'm contributing to the team, I think I should keep going"

EYEBALLERS are back in Vila Nova de Gaia for Roman Imperium Cup VI, where they will be hoping to get some LAN wins under their belt after being eliminated from the Portuguese event's previous edition in the group stage.

Last month, Jesper "⁠JW⁠" Wecksell became the first player to break the 3,000-map mark on HLTV. More than 14 years after his first game was recorded, the Swedish captain isn't thinking about stopping anytime soon.

"I think as long as I feel that I'm contributing to the team, I think I should keep going," he told HLTV. "When I feel like I am too much of a liability or that I simply don't have the motivation to get up and do all these practice games every day, then maybe it's time to call it quits.

"Hopefully before I call it quits, I will get at least one or two more opportunities to play in front of a decent crowd again, because that's kind of what I have been missing for the past five, six years."

The Swedish scene finds itself in the strongest position it has been in for some time, with emerging talents like Liam "⁠MaiL09⁠" Tügel and Alex "⁠poiii⁠" Nyholm Sundgren leading the charge and several teams vying for places at the top events.

Still, JW said that it is not yet at the point where people can say that it is fully back.

"I would say that Swedish CS needs to have consistent playoff teams in tier-1 tournaments," he noted.

"For now, to say that it's back, we would need to see a full Swedish lineup in a tier-1 tournament from time to time. Then I would say it's back, for sure. But we're on a good path right now."

You're now four years into EYEBALLERS' journey. What have you learned during this time?

I would say I've learned a lot of different things during my period with EYEBALLERS. One thing is obviously getting more knowledge about working with younger players, but I also think I've learned a lot about the business aspect of esports and Counter-Strike in general. It's not that easy, and it's a hard combination if you want to be a competitive team and also want to do good business. It's usually very hard to combine those two.

I would say I've grown a lot as a person, but also as a player. Getting more responsibilities inside the game, outside the game, stuff like that. I would say I've learned a lot during these four years.

EYEBALLERS was created to get Sweden back on track when the next generation after yours failed to take the baton. How do you think the Swedish grassroots movement is doing now?

Perhaps it's a bit far-fetched to say that Swedish CS is back, but I definitely think it's way closer than when I created EYEBALLERS. I think when I created EYEBALLERS, we were perhaps the only full Swedish paid team, but we also had academy teams in NIP, for example, that were fully Swedish. And now I think we have about perhaps four full paid Swedish teams, and most of them, or all of them, came after we created EYEBALLERS.

So I think we started somewhat of a movement within the Swedish scene. And I'd say it's in a pretty good place right now. It's not entirely back because, to me, to be fully back, I would say that Swedish CS needs to have consistent playoff teams in tier-1 tournaments. For now, to say that it's back, we would need to see a full Swedish lineup in a tier-1 tournament from time to time. Then I would say it's back, for sure. But we're on a good path right now.

You're the first player to record 3,000 maps on HLTV. It's a big milestone. How much gas do you have in the tank?

I don't think that much about the records that I am breaking. I was pretty surprised. I think it was on my birthday last year that I went to visit ESL Pro League in Stockholm, and they gave me this little shoutout for being the first to get 50k kills. I think I didn't really think about that. And now I have 3,000 maps. I mean, it's really nice, but I try not to think too much about these things. I just try to put myself and my team in the best position.

And speaking of how much gas I have left in the tank, it's also a hard thing to say because I think as long as I feel that I'm contributing to the team, I think I should keep going. When I feel like I am too much of a liability or that I simply don't have the motivation to get up and do all these practice games every day, then maybe it's time to call it quits.

Right now, I feel very motivated to get back on a stage again and get back in front of a crowd. So right now, I would say I have a lot of gas in the tank, but you never know what the future holds. But yeah, I'm looking forward, and hopefully before I call it quits, I will get at least one or two more opportunities to play in front of a decent crowd again, because that's kind of what I have been missing for the past five, six years.

It's been seven years since you last played at a Major. With EYEBALLERS in their best shape ever, do you think you'll be in a position to reach another Major again?

A Major is obviously the biggest goal there is for a player, myself included. And I think we are in a decent position. Perhaps we made some poor choices when it came to the VRS and playing different qualifiers and stuff online going into January, but it's also a process. You live and learn, and the VRS is new for most of us, being in a position where we had to calculate things was totally new for us.

So maybe we made a few poor decisions that impacted our chances to get to the Cologne Major, but perhaps we will go to the Singapore one. But the big goal for every player is always going to be the Major, and that remains the case for me.

As a veteran of the scene, what is it like to take part in the VRS circuit?

I think it's nice. Since I'm a veteran, I've been around for a long time now, and sometimes it kind of feels like I'm back where I started, as a 16- or 17-year-old, going to these kinds of events, perhaps mainly in Sweden, while now we're trying to do it more all over Europe. So I think it's really nice, and I think it's also nice that you get to see a lot of players in real life that you practice against every day or that you have faced online for many years.

Some unexpected friendships have popped up recently. We hung out a lot with the Gentle Mates guys over in Romania, so I really enjoyed spending some time with Martinez, dav1g and those guys. I think it's fun, and looking forward to more success in these journeys.
