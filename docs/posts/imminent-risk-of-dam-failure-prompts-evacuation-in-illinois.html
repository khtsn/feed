‘Imminent’ Risk of Dam Failure Prompts Evacuation in Illinois

Residents of about 200 homes in Washington County, Ill., were ordered to evacuate on Tuesday morning because of the “imminent” risk of a dam failure as strong storms moved through the area.

The Washington County Emergency Management Agency told residents in Nashville, Ill., about 55 miles southeast of St. Louis, to evacuate, warning that “the failure of the Nashville dam is imminent.”

Alex Haglund, a spokesman for the agency, said in an interview that water had reached the top of the dam, and that some water was spilling over causing flooding downstream and toward homes nearby.

Some residents said water inside their homes was waist deep, Mr. Haglund said in a statement.

It was unclear how many peopled lived in the evacuation area. The emergency management agency had initially said that about 300 homes needed to be evacuated. About 3,200 people live in Nashville, where rain was expected to continue into the afternoon.

One woman needed to be rescued because of flooding in her home, but no injuries had been reported, Mr. Haglund said.

State Senator Jason Plummer said on Facebook that water was rising at the Nashville dam.

“The concern right now is the potential for erosion and, eventually, failure,” he said. “This is a dangerous situation.”

The Nashville Community Center was open for residents to take shelter outside of the evacuation area, according to the emergency management agency.

Portions of south-central Illinois, including Washington County, were under a flash flood warning on Tuesday morning, as strong storms brought heavy rainfall. More than six inches of rain had been recorded as of 1 p.m. local time in Washington County, Mr. Haglund said.

The National Weather Service in St. Louis said that 2 to 5 inches of rain had fallen in some areas, with an additional 1 to 3 inches expected.

“Complicating the dam situation are multiple road closures,” Mr. Haglund said in a video update. “This is an ongoing issue.”

Portions of multiple highways and roadways in the area were closed because of flooding, according to the Illinois Department of Transportation. Images that were circulating on social media showed flooded roadways across the area as vehicles appeared to struggle to move through floodwaters.

The storms on Tuesday came after severe weather on Monday night in the Chicago area, where there were reports of tornadoes and damaging winds.

The heavy rainfall in south-central Illinois, the Weather Service said, could bring “life-threatening flash flooding” to creeks, highways and streets across the area.

“Move immediately to higher ground,” the Weather Service said.
