Amid Uncertainty, Biden Campaign Tries to Keep Control of Delegates

The campaign is quietly reaching out to its pledged convention delegates to ensure they are still on board. But some want a change.

  • July 18, 2024, 11:20 a.m. ET

President Biden’s campaign continued reaching out to Democratic convention delegates on Thursday to ensure their support amid mounting concerns that his candidacy could cost the party the White House and lead to losses down the ballot.

An overwhelming majority of delegates are pledged to Mr. Biden, but a growing if still limited number of them are expressing pessimism about the party’s chances with him at the top of the ticket as his polling has lagged and his public appearances have been uneven.

Since Mr. Biden’s disastrous performance in a debate against former President Donald J. Trump last month, the Biden campaign has been calling hundreds of convention delegates to ask each if they understood their role as a “pledged Biden delegate,” according to interviews with about a dozen of them and others familiar with the calls.

Some said the campaign went further, asking if Mr. Biden could still count on their support.

Party officials and Biden campaign allies have also urged delegates to keep quiet about their concerns, according to interviews and messages obtained by The New York Times. In some cases, delegates said they felt as if expressing dissent might cost them the chance to participate in the convention.

The Biden campaign said that the calls to delegates began well before the debate and that they were part of a normal outreach process to organize thousands of people ahead of a nominating process and convention that is logistically challenging even under normal circumstances.

“Delegate calls are ongoing,” Lauren Hitt, a Biden campaign spokeswoman, said on Thursday. “The president has been clear he has no plans to drop out.”

Behind closed doors, though, the president is said to have become more receptive in the past several days to hearing arguments about why he should end his re-election bid.

Even under less closely watched circumstances, it is not unusual for political parties and campaigns to struggle to corral national convention delegates. The delegates are usually local activists elected by their peers. They sometimes have little experience dealing with the news media and a tendency to sound off in a manner that can cause problems for party leaders and candidates.

The Biden campaign’s outreach comes as the party is facing a crisis about the viability of his candidacy and the prospect of an embarrassing revolt among delegates. A growing number of Democratic politicians have called on Mr. Biden to step aside to make room for another nominee.

A poll released on Wednesday found that nearly two-thirds of Democrats want Mr. Biden to voluntarily withdraw from the race to make way for a replacement.

But delegates are a particular subset of the base, having gone through a vetting process by the party and the Biden campaign before their selection.

A survey of the Massachusetts delegation from last weekend found that 62 percent wanted Mr. Biden to stay in the race, while 15 percent wanted him to drop out. Another 15 percent were unsure, while nine percent preferred not to give an opinion.

Most delegates contacted for this article spoke on the condition of anonymity to discuss their opinions of Mr. Biden and interactions with his campaign and party leaders because they feared repercussions for voicing skepticism, including the possibility of being stripped of their positions.

Delegates complained that the campaign had not adequately addressed concerns about Mr. Biden’s viability, either in public or in private communications with them. Several said that they had not received individualized outreach from the campaign at all before the debate performance, and that the group delegate calls that the campaign staff hosted did not allow for two-way engagement.

Instead, the delegates said, they felt as if they were being fed a rosy picture of how well the campaign was doing, even after the presidential debate.

In a delegate call last week, there was little or no discussion about declines in polling, according to people familiar with it, who said there was no opportunity for delegates to ask questions.

Instead, according to a summary sent afterward to delegates, campaign officials suggested that everything was going according to plan. Julie Chávez Rodríguez, the campaign manager, “expressed her appreciation for all your hard work while outlining the path forward to victory,” according to the email summary.

In some states, state party officials and Biden allies have advised delegates not to post anything on social media critical of Mr. Biden.

In other cases, delegates who remain firmly behind Mr. Biden have tried to shut down debate among their delegations about his viability.

Riju Das, a Biden delegate and lawyer in Connecticut, circulated a draft letter calling on the president to step aside because he “has not convinced us that his condition will get better, and too many polls show the electorate wants another nominee.”

When Mr. Das asked other delegates to sign on to the letter, some chastised him.

“Our opponents would love to get ahold of letters showing that we are at odds,” wrote Brian Anderson, an official in the Connecticut chapter of the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees. “The threat level demands that we work cohesively and carefully.”

Three other Connecticut delegates responded with a draft letter of their own expressing support for Mr. Biden, arguing that he had bounced back after the debate and pushing back on calls for him to step aside.

“Some think if he was a real leader, he would step down, which we believe he would do if he thought that the debate was anything more than a bad night and if the polls showed him he was not the best candidate to defeat his opponent,” the letter said.

Tina Duryea, who was involved in circulating the pro-Biden letter, said she had received her first call from the campaign after the debate but did not interpret it as an effort to assess whether she was still on board with Mr. Biden.

The campaign called “to go over procedural matters of being a delegate and answer any questions I might have,” she said in an email, adding that the caller “barely talked about the president.”

In California, a delegate who moderates a private Facebook group for her delegation warned participants that posts questioning Mr. Biden’s fitness would be deleted.

“We understand there may be some disagreement about whether Biden should stay in the race,” wrote Sascha Bittner, the delegate.

“However, he has made it clear that he is, and the vast majority of us are pledged delegates to him,” she wrote, adding that “we have decided to prohibit any organizing for a new candidate for president in this group.”

Another California delegate, Tom McInerney, responded that he “thought it was the other party that demanded allegiance to their dear leader.”

Mr. McInerney, a lawyer in San Francisco and a Biden donor, added in his post, “I was a delegate elected just like of all you and will fulfill my obligations consistent with the D.N.C. rules, and I’m disappointed you would censor opinions that don’t match yours regarding the future of our party and country.”

In recent years, the nomination vote has been largely symbolic, with little doubt about the results. It has gained new significance as Mr. Biden has faltered. Amid protests from elected officials and the party’s rank and file, Democratic leaders decided on Wednesday to hold off on beginning a swift virtual roll call they had considered starting as soon as next week to cement Mr. Biden as the nominee.

While the plans for the virtual roll-call vote have been in the works since May to deal with a legal quirk unrelated to ballot requirements, it outraged many delegates. Some said in interviews that it undermined the point of shelling out thousands of dollars to travel to the convention in Chicago next month.

Chris Dempsey, a Democratic operative, started a group called Delegates Are Democracy to educate delegates about the various nomination processes, including for a hypothetical open convention if Mr. Biden were to step aside. He said his team had spoken to scores of delegates, and many believed they were being taken for granted.

“Almost without exception, the delegates that we talk to feel that they do not have adequate information about what’s happening,” he said. “They want to be a part of something that is seen as legitimate, orderly, fair, transparent, and they don’t feel that way right now.”

Sylvia Gonzalez Andersh, a first-time delegate from Arizona, said it had been “quite unsettling going from something that was quite a complicated ministerial process” to an intrigue-laden event.

“People keep asking me, like I’m going to make some monumental change in my thinking, when I have been a Biden delegate from the very beginning.” she said, explaining that she still supported him.

Almost all of the more than 3,900 rank-and-file delegates are pledged to Mr. Biden. The party’s rules state that “all delegates to the national convention pledged to a presidential candidate shall in all good conscience reflect the sentiments of those who elected them.”

Dissenters argue that the phrase “in good conscience” allows them some wiggle room.

And a number of delegates pointed to Mr. Biden’s comments at a news conference last week that his delegates were “free to do whatever they want” at the convention, including nominate a different candidate, though he downplayed the odds of that. “It’s not going to happen,” he said in a stage whisper.

Mr. Dempsey said that based on his conversations with delegates, “they are pretty firmly behind Joe Biden,” and “there is not going to be a grass-roots uprising” to try to replace him.
