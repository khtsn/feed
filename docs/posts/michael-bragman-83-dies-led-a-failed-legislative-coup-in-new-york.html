Michael Bragman, 83, Dies; Led a Failed Legislative Coup in New York

As the State Assembly’s respected majority leader, he attempted to oust his boss, Sheldon Silver, the body’s imperious speaker. That move ended his career.

Sam Roberts

Oct. 27, 2023

Michael J. Bragman, a Democratic powerhouse who was deposed as the majority leader of the New York State Assembly and retired from politics after mounting an unsuccessful and politically suicidal coup against the speaker, Sheldon Silver, in 2000, died on Oct. 13 in Syracuse, N.Y. He was 83.

His death was confirmed by his daughter Leslie Bragman. No cause was given.

Mr. Bragman exerted a profound influence in the Assembly. He was credited with successfully sponsoring more than 300 bills and pumping some $200 million in pork-barrel funding for local projects in his Central New York State district, which was centered in Syracuse.

In 1998, after the murder of one of his constituents, Jenna Grieshaber Honis, a 22-year-old nursing student, he helped persuade Mr. Silver to endorse a bill mandating longer prison terms for violent felons.

The legislation, which became known as Jenna’s Law, had already been approved in the Republican-controlled State Senate but was stalled in the Assembly. Mr. Silver was swayed after meeting with the victim’s parents and Mr. Bragman.

His prodigious legislative record notwithstanding, Mr. Bragman may be best remembered for the quixotic mutiny he led in 2000 to depose Mr. Silver, the uncommunicative and opaque power broker from Manhattan who was elected speaker by the Democratic majority in 1994, a year after his predecessor, Saul Weprin, had installed Mr. Bragman as majority leader.

Some rank-and-file Democrats grumbled privately that Mr. Silver had negotiated secretly with the governor, weakened committees, empowered lobbyists, hoarded prerogatives and hogged the spotlight as the sole spokesman for the Assembly majority. But no sitting speaker had been ousted since 1975, when the Democrats seized control and dumped Perry B. Duryea, a Republican.

Mr. Bragman was regarded as a shrewd politician, a prodigious fund-raiser and a firm disciplinarian as floor leader. But Mr. Silver prevailed, victorious though wounded, in May 2000 when legislators, by a vote of 85 to 63, rejected a motion to suspend the normal rules of the Assembly in order to elect a new speaker.

After instituting several reforms to make the legislative process more transparent and cowing any remaining dissidents, Mr. Silver retained the speakership that December by 95 to 1 in the Democratic caucus. The only dissenting vote was Mr. Bragman’s.

Doomed as an outcast, Mr. Bragman was stripped of his leadership and its $34,500 stipend. He was also stripped of the trappings of power, including a spacious office across the street from the Capitol and a $1.36 million budget. He pared his staff from 24 people to two and resigned his seat at the end of 2001.

Sensitive to the whiff of dissent, Mr. Silver instituted several reforms to make the legislative process more transparent. He forfeited the speakership in 2015, after two decades, when he was indicted and later convicted on federal corruption charges. He died last year while serving a prison term.

Michael Jon Bragman was born on Aug. 11, 1940, in Syracuse to Mannie Bragman, who worked at a gear factory, and Helen (Richardson) Bragman, a civic leader who was active in local Democratic Party affairs. He was raised in nearby Cicero and played baseball in high school.

After graduating from Syracuse University with a bachelor’s degree in history and anthropology in 1963, he taught high school.

He married Suzanne Collier, and in 1986 they founded the Bragman Companies, a major developer of residential subdivisions. She survives him, along with their son, Michael Jr.; their twin daughters, Heather and Leslie Bragman; and a granddaughter. His brother, Robert, died before him.

Mr. Bragman began his public career two years after graduating from college when he was elected to the Cicero town council. He won a seat in the Onondaga County Legislature in 1969 and became the body’s first Democratic chairman nine years later.

He was elected to the State Assembly in 1980. Within a year he was indicted on a charge of asking for $1.6 million in bribes from a company that tried to sell a garbage-burning steam plant to Onondaga County when he was chairman of the County Legislature. He was acquitted.

He developed a reputation as independent and, after Republican gains in 1994, helped Mr. Silver nudge state Democrats toward more centrist positions on issues like criminal justice and tax cuts.

His loyalty was rewarded with appropriations for schools, libraries, fire companies, Little League teams, a bus and train terminal, a farmers’ market and other pet projects in his Syracuse district. The stadium at Cicero-North Syracuse High School was named after him.

When he was deposed as majority leader, Mr. Bragman joked in an interview with The New York Times that he was so scorned by fellow lawmakers that he felt “like I am going to have to go into the witness protection program.”

But then, as if the gravity of his downfall had suddenly sunk in, he said: “I’m human. Do you think I want to leave the trappings of this office?”

“There comes a time,” he added, “when you have to do the right thing.”
