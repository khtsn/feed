The Tip Sheet: How Biden Can Flip the Script Against Republicans

March 4, 2024, 5:03 a.m. ET

March 4, 2024, 5:03 a.m. ET

Every Monday morning on The Point, we kick off the week with a tip sheet on the latest in the presidential campaign. Here’s what we’re looking at this week:

  • Four years ago, Super Tuesday turbocharged Joe Biden’s drive for the presidency. He beat rivals in nine states, changing perceptions about whether this centrist establishment figure could build a coalition of voters to go the distance. The simplest fact proved a powerful one: He looked like a winner. He laid to rest questions about his abilities and memories of ugly earlier losses. Soon Biden was leading the most unified Democratic Party that I’ve seen in my lifetime, determined to beat Donald Trump.

  • One year ago, Biden delivered a punchy State of the Union speech in which he challenged Republicans on Social Security, Medicare, the economy and bipartisanship, and looked nimble in the face of hecklers. That performance laid to rest questions about his abilities to seek and win re-election — for a time, at least.

  • The big question Biden faces this week is whether, once again, he can lay to rest questions about his abilities. Winning the Super Tuesday contests won’t give him the same juice as 2020, however, since he’s all but uncontested.

  • That leaves Thursday’s State of the Union speech, and here Biden has an opportunity that I think he’ll take advantage of — to flip the script against his opponents and leave them facing questions.

  • Biden is skilled at rallying people and allies to his side when he has foils — Trump, Putin, Covid — and he will have the chance to do so on Thursday in the face of House and Senate Republicans who have killed the bipartisan border bill and delayed aid to Israel and Ukraine. He can paint Putin and Trump as Enemy No. 1 and 2 to democracy, and more broadly frame Putin as a nuclear-armed evil in the world. And rather than brag about the economy, which risks sounding tone-deaf, he could take a page from Bill Clinton’s 2012 Democratic convention speech and lay out why there’s still so much economic pain and what he wants to do about it.

  • Biden can’t count on the courts to stop Trump (side note: the Supreme Court will probably rule at 10 a.m. today that Trump can remain on the ballot in Colorado and elsewhere this year), and he can’t count on more people to give him credit on the economy. Just as he flipped the script on Super Tuesday in 2020 and got people to start seeing him as a winner, he will need to go on offense in the face of polls and doubters about his abilities. This week will be a big test of whether he can still go the distance.

President Biden’s announcement on Friday that the United States will use airplanes to drop food to people in Gaza is an encouraging sign that the White House is showing more concern for the plight of Palestinians there, after more than 30,000 have been reported killed and many more are facing starvation.

But airdropping relief aid is normally a very last resort in emergencies, and if Biden is serious about saving Palestinian civilians’ lives, there are better ways to deliver aid.

The problem with airdrops — and I’ve seen them in remote areas of South Sudan where there was no other way to deliver aid — is that they are inefficient and require careful coordination with aid workers on the ground who gather the supplies and distribute them. But there isn’t much of a ground operation by aid workers in Gaza, in part because Israel has impeded it.

Israel has tightly limited what aid can enter Gaza, leaving trucks of supplies lined up outside. And it has targeted civilian police officers who would guard the convoys, because they are affiliated with Hamas, leading to a breakdown of security that has impeded deliveries.

Likewise, Israeli authorities have not wanted to work with the U.N. agency that has expertise in delivering aid to Gaza — the U.N. Relief and Works Agency, or UNRWA — after accusations that a dozen of its 12,000 staff members in Gaza participated in the Hamas terrorist attacks on Oct. 7. That’s the backdrop for the catastrophe on Thursday in which more than 100 Palestinians in Gaza were reportedly killed while trying to get food from a convoy; Israel seems to have tried to arrange a convoy with private contractors while bypassing the relief agency and conventional aid groups.

The solution to the problem of starving people attacking food convoys is pretty simple: Allow trucks to cross the border and UNRWA and other aid groups to deliver it, with escorts from the civilian police.

Dropping food from planes is better than nothing but increases the likelihood that Hamas fighters with guns will get it, rather than the most vulnerable people, such as children, older residents and the disabled. And some parachutes won’t open, so pallets may kill or injure people — and in any case, there remains the problem of distributing food from the drop zone to other parts of Gaza.

The simplest path forward would be for Biden to insist that Israel open more crossing points, allow many more trucks through, permit civilian police escorts and accept that UNRWA and aid groups will play a central role in food distribution. That would be less visual than an airdrop, but it would save far more lives.

The spectacle of Alexei Navalny’s funeral on Friday must have filled Vladimir Putin with fury.

He had done everything to silence Navalny, to crush any hint of disobedience from his followers, to eradicate any questioning of his war. And yet Putin could do nothing to prevent thousands of Russians from lining up in a dreary, remote Moscow district to pay their last respects to their fallen hero, to chant “No to the war!” and to defy the massed police in full riot gear.

I remember a similar outpouring of silent defiance in the Soviet Union, after the enormously popular balladeer and actor Vladimir Vysotsky died, when thousands of grieving Muscovites spontaneously gathered outside the theater where he worked. Vysotsky’s ballads spoke to the misery of ordinary Soviet lives, and at that moment popular grief overcame fear. The grief for Navalny was even greater: He spoke to an acute, simmering frustration among many Russians with what Putin has done to them and their country.

They may not be the majority in Russia. Putin still seems to command support among the millions of rural, less-educated Russians who believe in his propaganda about a hostile world out there that only he can hold at bay. But the mile-long line of people, most of them young and many with a clutch of roses, inching across the colorless, slush-covered streets of the southeastern Maryino district, were the people Putin continues to fear.

They gathered in other Russian cities, too, though they knew they would be photographed; they knew that Putin in his fury would seek revenge against them. Yet they walked past the lines of police officers and piled their roses at the grave and nodded to Navalny’s parents, sitting stoically alongside, some whispering, “Thank you for your son.” It was the mother, Lyudmila Navalnaya, who had demanded that Putin release her son for burial in Moscow, rather than in a hole at the remote Arctic labor camp where he died on Feb. 16.

When Mr. Navalny’s coffin was lowered into the ground, Frank Sinatra’s defiant version of “My Way” blared from loudspeakers. “I did what I had to do … I did it my way….” That was what people loved in Navalny, his refusal to bend. One Russian interviewed by an independent Russian online broadcaster said the long line of defiant mourners were evidence that “hope remains, that Russia has a chance for a future.”

Publishing is a slow business. Writing a novel takes time; historians and biographers may toil for years on their subject. After a manuscript is handed in, the editing and production process can often take a full year before the finished product hits bookstores.

That’s not the only part of the business that is slow.

According to the latest diversity report by Lee & Low, an independent publisher dedicated to books by underrepresented authors, publishing remains a largely white profession. As reported this week in The Times, the percentage of white employees in publishing decreased slightly from 79 percent in 2015 to 76 percent in 2019 to 72.5 percent in 2023, the latest figures available.

Industry professionals point to entrenched structural and cultural issues as reasons behind the slowness. But another factor is that publishing, like many creative industries, is going through a period of contraction. Penguin Random House, the largest of the big five publishers, ordered a large-scale round of layoffs and buyouts in 2023. Other houses have similarly trimmed the editorial and business sides of their work forces.

Diversity efforts are rarely purely additive in a field that is shrinking rather than expanding. For each role added, someone else has to go. In publishing, staffs are heavily skewed female, and people have usually been in their roles for a long time. If adding an employee means shedding someone else, that comes with a risk of age or sex discrimination, especially given that the more senior ranks in publishing still skew male.

Another factor, which the Times story discussed, is financial. The average starting annual salary in publishing is roughly $47,000, which is difficult to survive on in New York City.

In this respect, publishing hasn’t changed at all. When I graduated from college in the early 1990s, the starting salary at the prestigious literary imprint Farrar, Straus & Giroux was $14,500 a year. Other houses started at $17,000 or $18,000 — all but unaffordable for anyone without inherited wealth, financial support from family members or a parent’s home to live in. Without any of those benefits, I gave up and moved abroad.

With these persistent barriers to entry, especially for people who have fewer resources, publishing efforts to diversify are likely in for a long haul.

The budding global movement to raise taxes on billionaires may yet fizzle but has recently gained significant support. The goal is to get countries to cooperate on these taxes so that billionaires can’t play them off against one another to lower their tax bills.

Two years ago, President Biden asked Congress to pass a minimum tax on U.S. billionaires of 20 percent of their income, including unrealized investment income that’s currently untaxed. The idea hasn’t gotten much traction, but simply putting it out there widened the window for what’s discussable in polite company.

Brazil, which this year holds the rotating presidency of the Group of 20, consisting of leading wealthy and developing nations, has taken up the cause with fervor. On Thursday, Gabriel Zucman, the founder and director of the E.U. Tax Observatory, a nonprofit group, spoke about taxing billionaires at a meeting of the G20 finance ministers in São Paulo. He calculated that imposing a modest but inescapable tax on just the world’s roughly 3,000 billionaires would raise around $250 billion a year. For comparison, he said, that’s about half what developing nations need each year to fight climate change.

One policy, he suggested, would be for countries to agree that the superrich pay at least the equivalent of 2 percent of their wealth in income tax each year.

That wouldn’t be easy. An earlier international agreement to impose a minimum tax on the income of multinational corporations has been slow to get off the ground. Among the countries that haven’t ratified it yet is the United States, thanks to Republican opposition.

But Zucman argues that the preconditions for a global minimum tax on billionaires are mostly in place. Most countries have the tools to measure wealth because they already tax inheritances, he said. Plus, he said, an international agreement on banking transparency “makes it hard for the superrich to hide assets in offshore financial centers.”

Zucman is aware that no country wants to go first, for fear that its wealthiest citizens will emigrate to escape the levy, but suggested that one country or a small group of countries take the lead in the hope that others follow.

That’s optimistic of him. Maybe overly so. But the energy behind a billionaire tax is building.

The new book “White Rural Rage: The Threat to American Democracy,” by Tom Schaller and Paul Waldman, has been getting a lot of buzz, including a mention in a recent column by my colleague Paul Krugman. It describes white rural voters as “a growing threat to the world’s oldest constitutional democracy” because of their willingness to embrace conspiracy theories and authoritarianism.

I’m eager to read it, and was curious about what progressives who organize in rural areas think of the book’s thesis. Anthony Flaccavento, who made an unsuccessful bid for Congress in rural Virginia and went on to found the Rural Urban Bridge Initiative, told me that he was frustrated by what he heard from the book’s authors during an interview on MSNBC’s “Morning Joe.”

“That kind of the language — a book by that title — is absolutely the kiss of death for the efforts of so many of us in rural areas who have been working to rebuild trust and reverse some of the policies that have hollowed out rural America,” said Flaccavento, whose group has put out a report about what Democrats have to do to win in rural America. He also said he feared the book would serve as confirmation to liberals that it’s hopeless to invest politically in those parts of the country.

Shawn Sebastian, director of organizing at Rural Organizing, an Ohio-based group that supports progressive campaigns in rural areas nationwide, agreed that support for authoritarianism is a growing problem. He just published a survey of his group’s 75,000 members — more than 800 Democrats, most of whom live in Trump-supporting rural areas, filled it out — which found that 25 percent had experienced political violence or threats of political violence.

“That was chilling,” he told me. But he also said rural voters are part of the solution and that giving up on them will only make things worse.

City dwellers should be working with rural voters on the nation’s biggest priorities. Rural organizers have helped score key victories, from the defeat of an abortion ban in Ohio last year, to the election of Gov. Andy Beshear in Kentucky, to successfully lobbying Washington for the establishment of the Recompete Pilot Program, which provides federal grants to rural communities.

Rural people working together to save their hospitals, build a nursing home or establish a mobile food pantry are the antidote to the violent polarization that everyone is worried about.

Texas is enduring its first “gigafire,” just the third in modern American history to burn more than a million acres. The Smokehouse Creek fire began Monday, spread more than 80 miles in the space of a few hours and at some points was growing as much as 150 football fields every minute. By Thursday, it had become the second-largest burn in modern American history. More than 1,500 square miles have gone up in flames, according to Texas A&M University, and the fire is still just 3 percent contained.

February is the heart of fire season in Texas, with 90 percent of wildfires in the state occurring between January and May. But little else about the Smokehouse Creek fire or the conditions that gave rise to it are “normal.” Some measures of fire-weather conditions were near their maximum readings this week, as more than a hundred American weather stations broke monthly temperature records, and a cold front pushing into the Texas Panhandle utterly reshaped the course of the largest of the state’s many fires, essentially creating a 60-mile-wide fire head.

The Smokehouse fire has already burned five times as much land as burned in total in Texas all of last year. On Wednesday, another Texas fire had forced the emergency closure of the country’s largest nuclear weapons facility. High winds prevented firefighters from taking to the skies to try dousing the flames; on Thursday, those traveling to fight the Smokehouse fire woke up to snow. This weekend, temperatures might spike again.

Fire is an eternal feature of the American landscape, but it has been growing much larger and more menacing in recent years. When the new age of megafires and gigafires arrived in California five or six years ago, politicians and fire scientists began talking about a year-round fire season and the need to burn perhaps a fifth of the state in order to reach a new fire equilibrium.

In certain ways, though the landscape is growing ever more fire-prone, the country has begun to consider decade-defining burns as something like unfortunate background noise. But in other ways, the fires still keep surprising us.

And too often, killing us. The Lahaina fire last summer in Hawaii was the deadliest American fire in more than a century. Of the 10 deadliest fires anywhere on earth since 1900, five have come just since 2020.

Elie Honig, a former federal prosecutor, has written an excellent piece in New York magazine pushing back against the notion that the Supreme Court should have considered the timing of the election when it issued its briefing schedule on Wednesday for Donald Trump’s immunity argument.

Honig asks a question that each of us should ponder: “Is it rightly the job of any federal judge to worry about whether a case gets tried before or after an election? Isn’t it inherently political to do that?”

In almost every case, he’s exactly right. Judges should schedule cases without regard to the political calendar. After all, the timing of an election has precisely nothing to do with the merits of a case. There is zero reason for judges to consider the political effect of a prosecution, and if they did so, it would be inherently improper.

But there is one salient difference between virtually every other criminal case involving a politician and the federal case against Trump: Unlike other criminal defendants in America, he could soon possess the power to exonerate himself. If he becomes president, he can order the Justice Department to drop the case against him. He could pardon himself. That’s a legal distinction in this case that separates it from ordinary prosecutions of politicians.

On the one hand, it’s important that Trump be granted all the due process protections of any ordinary defendant. The urgency of the timing shouldn’t prejudice his defense. At the same time, however, to the extent that his case is unique, judges and justices should tailor their rulings to address the case’s singular nature. And, to be fair to the court, there is a strong argument that the Justice Department should have filed charges sooner. But the court has to deal with the case before it, no matter when it is brought.

As a practical matter, that should have meant hearing his immunity argument — which raises issues unique to Trump — while denying any further effort to delay the case, which would also address issues unique to Trump. Instead, we wait.

While there is an outside chance that federal prosecutors can still try Trump before the election, in all likelihood we have to wait to see whether politics (namely, the 2024 election) will grant our nation the opportunity to apply the rule of law to a president.

We got another inflation report this morning, on the personal consumption expenditure price index, which is the number the Federal Reserve targets. And it was substantially hotter than it has been in recent months — hot enough to cause a visible bump even in six-month measures of core inflation, which many of us think give a more up-to-date reading than annual measures:

So I was watching financial markets and following economics-nerd social media, and the response to the new data was a resounding “Meh.” Nobody got excited. Why?

Part of the answer is that many economists believed that the data was partly spurious. As Mark Zandi of Moody’s Analytics wrote, it was “juiced by problematic seasonals.” (How to write like an economist.) What Zandi means is that we’re probably looking in part at the “January effect,” in which many companies raise prices at the start of the calendar year. Official data are supposed to correct for seasonal factors, but many experts believe that in this case the adjustment is inadequate.

The idea that this bump was spurious is given extra credibility by the fact that we aren’t seeing any signs of an inflation surge in business surveys. The Atlanta Fed’s survey of business inflation expectations is basically unchanged. The S&P Global survey of purchasing managers actually showed cost pressures dissipating. If there were a serious outbreak of inflation in progress, you’d expect businesses to notice; they haven’t.

In short, the consensus seems to be to move along, nothing to see here. This still looks like a soft landing, with inflation at worst marginally above the Fed’s 2 percent target.

The “Iliad.” The “Odyssey.” “Beowulf.” “Who TF Did I Marry”?

If you’re not on TikTok, that last epic may be unfamiliar to you. It’s a 50-part series by a woman who goes by Reesa Teesa that chronicles her relationship with a man she calls a pathological liar, the many ways he tricked and conned her and how she eventually learned of his duplicity. Like many unverified social media stories, it’s unclear how much of Reesa Teesa’s story is even real. But real or not, it’s a good story.

On its face, the series looks like many others on TikTok: A person speaks into a front-facing phone camera, narrating some aspect of her life for viewers online. She isn’t an influencer — or wasn’t before millions watched her posts. Yet Reesa Teesa’s twisting, turning epic has kept viewers on the edges of our seats. Her husband lied about his job, his family, his very name, and over the course of the last few days Reesa Teesa has regaled us with his web of lies as she commutes in her car or sits at her desk in her Atlanta home. It’s nearly impossible not to feel a rush of endorphins as she announces part whatever of “Who TF Did I Marry” at the start of each video.

It’s easy to dismiss “Who TF Did I Marry” as the latest mind-numbing content on offer on platforms like TikTok and Instagram. But epic, multipart tales are a tradition that extends beyond social media, transcending culture and time. Reesa Teesa has merely adapted the oldest form of storytelling for the medium of the day. Instead of sitting around a fire in a cave or on the steps of an ancient Greek amphitheater, we listen to her tell us a story through our smartphones.

The story sprawls — with an editor, it might have been 20 parts instead of 50. But that’s part of the reason it’s so good. The story is raw, direct from the source, and the audience as well as the storyteller react to one another in real time. The narrative morphs as a result of that collective experience.

There’s a lot of hand-wringing about the isolating effects of modernity, social media in particular. But, as lonely and isolating many aspects of modernity can be, we’re the same as our ancient ancestors: suckers for a good story.

Not even the Grim Reaper rules forever.

Mitch McConnell, the Senate Republican leader — who earned his macabre nickname for his steadfast commitment to killing any and all legislation favored by the opposition — announced on Wednesday that he will step down from his leadership post at year’s end.

It was time. McConnell, 82, has had some disturbing health events this past year. (Freezing during a news conference is never a good thing.) He is increasingly out of step with his conference on national security. (The Ukraine funding mess has been humiliating for him.) More generally, he is facing growing abuse from his fractious MAGA members — and that’s without a vengeful Donald Trump back in the White House. Which could very well happen.

Besides, McConnell has already achieved his personal goal of becoming the longest-serving conference leader in Senate history. And his legacy of cementing a solidly conservative Supreme Court majority, leading to the end of abortion rights, will long endure.

Having covered the leader for what feels like forever, I greeted his announcement with both delight and dread, and with just a splash of bittersweetness.

The delight part is the most straightforward. McConnell is a political cynic and raging opportunist who cares above all else about amassing power, for his conference and himself. He has twisted norms and rules beyond recognition, without an ounce of shame. And he has been brutally effective. This is, in part, why his political opponents hate him so — just as her Republican foes despised Nancy Pelosi.

But McConnell also has been upfront about who he is and what motivates him. He doesn’t bother pretending he has high-minded principles beyond winning. He doesn’t try to be a nice guy. He rules by fear, not love. He didn’t even bother trying to ingratiate himself with Trump. For this, I have long had a grudging respect for him. As opposed to someone like Kevin McCarthy, who waffled and weaseled and butt-smooched his way to the top of the House, only to get booted back down almost immediately.

And the dread? It’s lovely to imagine that whoever succeeds McConnell will be a less cynical, more productive kind of leader. But with the way the G.O.P. is headed these days …

Let’s just say that while I don’t expect to miss McConnell, I’m more than a little anxious about who and what comes next.

One reason American companies are addicted to mergers is that eliminating competitors makes it easier to charge higher prices. What gets less attention is that companies also rely on mergers to hold down wages.

Kroger and Albertsons, the nation’s two largest supermarket operators, are proposing to become one large supermarket company. For New Yorkers, the death of Flaco — the beloved Central Eurasian eagle-owl that escaped from the Central Park Zoo a year ago after someone cut the mesh wires to his small cage — is a painful ending to a glorious year of free flight.

Day after day, he hooted, fluffed and spread his magnificent wings and flew above our heads, resisting all the naysayers. Flaco kept attracting crowds not just because he was such a glorious bird, with his marvelous ear tufts and splendid six-foot wingspan, but also because we were touched by his yearning to fly free. We didn’t want to see him on exhibit; we wanted him to soar and thrive.

Goodbye, Flaco. You will be missed. We will remember you as a bird that flew free in a city that survives and flourishes despite all the challenges and attempts to stifle its spirit.
