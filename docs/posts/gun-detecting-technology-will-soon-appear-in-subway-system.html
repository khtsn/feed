Gun-Detecting Technology Will Soon Appear in Subway System

A set of weapon-screening devices will be deployed at various stations over the course of a month.

July 25, 2024, 3:00 a.m. ET

New York City officials will begin testing gun-detecting scanners inside subway stations this week in what they say is an effort to address riders’ concerns about crime.

The weapon-detection devices, produced by Evolv Technology, a Massachusetts-based start-up, roughly resemble the metal detectors often found at the entrances of courthouses and concerts. Representatives for Mayor Eric Adams, who announced the pilot, said that a single set of roving scanners would be used to search for weapons at various stations throughout the subway system for one month beginning Thursday or Friday.

It is the latest high-tech solution that Mr. Adams has championed to address public safety concerns. Since taking office, the mayor, who describes himself as a tech geek, has unveiled a robot to patrol the Times Square subway station, expanded the use of drones and boasted about the city’s use of a robotic dog to assist in emergencies.

Critics have argued that Mr. Adams’s latest experiment with the gun detectors is dabbling in expensive and largely untested technology. Surveillance experts have said that leasing a single unit can run about $125,000 over a four-year contract. By comparison, conventional metal detectors can typically be purchased outright for less than $10,000 each.

City officials declined to say where the Evolv devices would be placed because they did not want to tip off people who intended to bring weapons into the system illegally.

The Metropolitan Transportation Authority, which operates the subway, referred questions about the pilot to City Hall. The Police Department did not respond to inquiries about the experiment.

It was not immediately clear how the city would gauge the pilot’s efficacy and whether there were plans to deploy the gadgets more widely. A representative for City Hall said that the city had not entered into a contract with Evolv and that it was not spending any money on the gadgets for the pilot. Officials have said that they are only experimenting with Evolv and that they are still seeking proposals from other companies with similar products.

“We invite companies that specialize in weapons detection technology to contact us as we look to expand how we are keeping New Yorkers safe when riding the subways,” said Amaris Cockfield, a spokeswoman for the city.

Evolv, which was founded in 2013, says that the devices are programmed with the “signatures” of certain items, allowing them to detect weapons. According to the company’s website, its clients include the Niagara Falls City School District, the Lincoln Center for the Performing Arts in New York City and the Boch Center in Boston.

But the company has been sued by investors and users of the technology who have questioned the efficacy of its products. It has also been the source of federal investigations into its marketing practices.

A spokeswoman for Evolv did not immediately respond to a request for comment late Wednesday.

Two years ago, the company’s own leaders expressed concerns to City Hall that the technology could cause bottlenecks if used in the subway system.

Besides slowing down people’s commutes, the scanners will “do absolutely nothing for public safety,” said Albert Fox Cahn, the executive director of the Surveillance Technology Oversight Project, a privacy and civil rights group based in New York.

“It’s going to last a matter of days, not months, before the mayor reverses course and pulls back,” he said. Mr. Fox Cahn questioned the effectiveness of the technology and said that it was not practical to scan every passenger in a transportation system as dynamic as the subway.

The Legal Aid Society has called the technology “invasive”, and on Wednesday, the organization criticized the Adams administration for not publicly posting a final policy on how the scanners would be implemented, as required by law before deploying them in the subway. As of Wednesday afternoon, only a draft policy — which was posted in March — was publicly available.

Although the transit system is relatively safe, high-profile crimes in the subway can unsettle New Yorkers. The pilot is part of an effort to increase the sense of security in the subway system after a series of random attacks and mirrors several similar surges over the last two years. The new weapon-detection initiative was first announced in March, days after a man was shoved in front of a train in East Harlem and killed.

Police officers had already been working an extra 1,200 daily overtime shifts within the subway system when officials roughly doubled their presence by deploying an additional 1,000 officers in February. An additional 1,000 National Guardsmen, state troopers and transit officers, as well as 800 more police officers, were added in March.

Teams of health care workers have been sent to help homeless people and remove them from the subways, sometimes forcibly. And thousands of cameras have been installed in the system over the past two years, adding up to a total of about 20,000, according to the M.T.A. By the end of this year, cameras will be installed inside every train car.

Transit leaders have also installed structural features in an effort to make riders feel safe. Officials are testing new fare gates to stop turnstile jumpers and metal platform barriers to prevent riders from falling onto tracks, and they plan to add brighter lights in the system to make it feel less claustrophobic.

Subway leaders have struggled to win back the riders the system lost during the pandemic, largely because of the popularity of remote work but also because some riders have reported feeling unsafe. Today, ridership hovers at about 70 percent of prepandemic levels.
