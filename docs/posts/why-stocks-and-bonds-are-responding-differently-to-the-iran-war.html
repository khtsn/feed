Why Stocks and Bonds Are Responding Differently to the Iran War

Strategies

Stock investors are betting that companies will make enormous profits, despite the war. But investors in bonds, including U.S. Treasuries, have other concerns.

May 7, 2026Updated 12:37 p.m. ET

The U.S. stock market has been splendid lately, while the bond market has wobbled. These two barometers of the global financial world have responded quite differently to the higher oil prices and increased economic risks induced by the war in Iran.

After a rough stretch in March, the U.S. stock market has regularly shrugged off risk — not only recovering its losses since the start of the war but going on to new highs, as investors bet that publicly traded U.S. companies would keep reaping enormous profits, regardless of what happened in the war.

Other international stock markets, which had performed marvelously before the war and took heavy losses in March, have also rebounded stoutly. International stock markets overall are ahead of the U.S. stock market since the start of the year.

But the bond market is another matter. Bond traders have maintained a much sharper focus on risk. Yields remain correlated with shifts in the price of oil. As oil prices have spiked and inflation has risen, yields have risen and bond prices, which move in the opposite direction, have fallen.

Diversified long-term investors holding both stocks and bonds have quite done well overall since the start of the year, but that’s because of stocks, not bonds, which have barely held their own.

This divergence could continue for some time. Many strategists believe it will.

The Resilient Stock Market

The stock market continues to fluctuate, of course, but it has been persistently buoyant in the face of intermittent violence in the Persian Gulf. Wall Street analysts have become increasingly positive about S&P 500 stocks.

“The ‘Teflon market’ has proved resilient despite lingering concerns, driven by strong corporate profits,” Keith Lerner, chief investment officer at Truist Advisory, a unit of Truist, the commercial bank, wrote in a note to clients this week.

By April 30, analysts’ estimates for S&P 500 companies’ profits in the next 12 months had risen 11 percent since the start of the year. The boom in artificial intelligence company stocks propelled much of this optimism, despite the war in the Middle East, tariffs, the partial government shutdown, the continuing war in Ukraine and many other disruptions. This month, reports of breakthroughs in the conflict between the United States and Iran have been setting off strong rallies, while the stock market lately has minimized the significance of setbacks.

Consider the astonishing rapidity with which the S&P 500 put the war behind it. That benchmark index peaked on Jan. 27 and then fell 9.1 percent through March 30. By April 15, a mere 16 days later, the market had returned to a new high.

Kevin Khang, senior global economist at Vanguard, examined every market decline of at least 9 percent since 1950. There were 32 of them, according to data he shared with me, and the recent Iran-war rebound has been the fastest of all. For these rebounds, the median length — the point in the middle — was 107 days. The average rebound took much longer, 309 days. The stock market’s recovery this time occurred in the comparative blink of an eye.

Yet many economists warn that oil and other fossil fuel prices will remain elevated for weeks — and perhaps months, if the war is not definitively ended soon. That could produce stagflation, an ugly blend of inflation and flagging economic growth. Despite that risk, stock market analysts, for the most part, have been unconcerned, not just about U.S. stocks, but about those of many other countries, too.

Estimates for a global stock index, the MSCI World ex USA Index — essentially, a measurement of publicly traded stocks outside the United States — are soaring, too. Through Wednesday, both the MSCI World and the MSCI USA (which measures the U.S. stock market) were up about 4 percent this year.

A global bull market in stocks is underway, and it is being propelled by corporate earnings, especially in the United States. said Edward Yardeni, an independent economist and strategist based on Long Island. The U.S. market’s low on March 30 was probably “the low for the year,” he said. But, he added, a straight-ahead rally isn’t a slam dunk. “I also recognize that the war isn’t over in the Middle East,” he said.

Nevertheless, despite many market reversals, he said “the U.S. economy and the stock market have been remarkably resilient” for years, and he expects that to continue.

A Subdued Bond Market

There is far less exuberance in the bond market. The Bloomberg US Aggregate Index is probably the most widely followed benchmark for the investment-grade bond market. Investors in index funds that track the benchmark had solid gains of almost 2 percent for the first two months of the year.

Then the war started, oil prices soared and interest rates in the bond market rose, too, driving down bond prices. The index has recovered somewhat, but it’s close to zero for the year.

It’s been a rougher ride for long-term bonds. Treasuries are often described as the safest bonds in the marketplace, but long-term bonds, whether Treasuries or not, decline further in price than shorter duration bonds do when interest rates rise. The iShares 20+ Year Treasury Bond E.T.F. (sometimes known by its ticker, TLT) tracks the long-term Treasury market. It soared, with a total return of 4.6 percent through Feb. 27 — before the surge in oil prices set off increases in both inflation and interest rates (or yields). The E.T.F. plummeted as yields rose, dropping to a small loss for the year.

Of course, that’s nothing to complain about, compared with the 31 percent loss inflicted on investors in that fund in 2022, the year when inflation at one point exceeded 9 percent and the Bloomberg US Aggregate lost 13 percent. But the sober state of the bond market is a reminder that if inflation rises much higher, bond yields are likely to rise with it and investors will register paper losses — along with real ones if they sell their bonds or funds at inopportune times.

The Status of Treasuries

Perhaps of greater gravity, the status of U.S. Treasuries as the linchpin of the global financial system is no longer rock solid. Fitch Ratings, which downgraded U.S. debt one notch from the highest AAA level in 2023, said in a new report late last month that the debt burden of the United States is “far higher” than that of other countries that hold the same AA credit rating.

The unique global status of the U.S. dollar and financial markets, and the strength of the U.S. economy, have enabled the government to retain its current rating. “A large, dynamic economy, the dollar’s reserve-currency role and the depth and liquidity of U.S. capital markets are key sovereign rating strengths,” Fitch said. But a variety of “governance” issues under the Trump administration, as well as the conflict in the Middle East, along with persistent and widening budget deficits, have challenged that credit rating.

Nonetheless, U.S. Treasuries have attracted global investors as a “safe haven” during the conflict. Other countries, like Britain, don’t have that status now. British 30-year government bonds, known as gilts, have reached their highest level since 1998. And Britain’s benchmark 10-year bond yield was close to 5 percent, a premium of more than 0.6 percentage points above the equivalent Treasury.

Major world central banks have responded defensively to these financial storms. As I wrote last week, the Bank of Japan, European Central Bank, Bank of England and Federal Reserve have all decided to take no action on their key interest rates because of the dual risks posed by rising oil prices resulting from the war with Iran: There are heightened risks of both runaway inflation and throttled economic growth.

That dilemma continues. Kevin M. Warsh, nominated to succeed Jerome H. Powell as Federal Reserve chair, has spoken frequently of the need to trim interest rates but the markets are skeptical. They project no Fed action on rates through December 2027 as the most likely outcome, with a greater possibility of interest rate increases than of reductions, according to futures prices tracked by CME FedWatch.

In short, central banks, which control the shortest-duration interest rates, and the bond market, which sets longer rates, view the economic environment with a jaundiced eye. There is a range of possibilities, from prosperity in many developed markets to chaos if the conflict in the Middle East widens. Fixed-income markets tend to focus on risks more than on the potential for windfall profits that the stock market cherishes.

The big two markets are behaving differently, which may be fine for now, but watch out if it continues for a long while. The optimism in the stock market will be tested if interest rates rise and the economy falters.

Jeff Sommer writes Strategies, a weekly column on markets, finance and the economy.
